# research/03-dir2 -- Operator-Side Weyl Law from Heat Kernel

*Date: 2026-04-10. Spectral Realisation Cycle 3, Direction 2.*

## 1. The question

Can we compute Tr(e^{-t * T_BC^2}) as t -> 0+ from the BC ALGEBRAIC
structure (not from the zeros), and match the Weyl law to rule out
extra spectrum?

## 2. The Weyl law for zeta zeros

The Riemann-von Mangoldt formula:

    N(T) = (T/2pi) log(T/2pie) + 7/8 + S(T)

where S(T) = (1/pi) arg zeta(1/2 + iT), |S(T)| = O(log T).

This gives the eigenvalue counting function. The corresponding heat
kernel asymptotics follow from the density of states:

    rho(gamma) = dN/dgamma = (1/2pi)(log(gamma/2pie) + 1)

    Tr(e^{-t*T_BC^2}) = sum_n e^{-t*gamma_n^2}
                        ~ integral_0^inf e^{-t*x^2} rho(x) dx  (t -> 0+)

## 3. Numerical computation

### Heat traces at 200 zeros (gamma_max = 396.38)

    t = 0.0001:

      Smooth Weyl prediction  = 25.6350
      Difference              = 0.4247
      Fractional diff         = 1.66%

    t = 0.001:
      Exact trace (200 zeros) = 3.7046
      Smooth Weyl prediction  = 3.2805
      Difference              = 0.4241
      Fractional diff         = 12.9%

    t = 0.01:
      Exact trace (200 zeros) = 0.1497
      Smooth Weyl prediction  = -0.2788
      Difference              = 0.4285
      Fractional diff         = 154% (Weyl is negative here)

### Interpretation

The difference (exact - Weyl smooth) is approximately CONSTANT at
~0.42. This is the oscillatory correction from S(T), which does NOT
vanish in the heat kernel. The Weyl approximation is a leading-order
asymptotic that misses the oscillatory structure.

Key: the smooth Weyl integral goes NEGATIVE at large t because the
density rho(gamma) starts at gamma ~ 14.13 (first zero), not at zero.
The integral from 0 to 14 is zero. The smooth density subtracts a
"virtual" contribution from 0 to 14 that doesn't exist, making the
integral negative.

## 4. Impact of an extra eigenvalue on heat trace

If an extra eigenvalue exists at lambda, the heat trace changes by
delta = e^{-t * lambda^2}.

    Extra eigenvalue at lambda = 15.0:
      t=0.0001: delta/trace = 3.75%
      t=0.001:  delta/trace = 21.6%
      t=0.01:   delta/trace = 70.4%

    Extra eigenvalue at lambda = 17.5:
      t=0.0001: delta/trace = 3.72%
      t=0.001:  delta/trace = 19.9%
      t=0.01:   delta/trace = 31.2%

    Extra eigenvalue at lambda = 20.0:
      t=0.0001: delta/trace = 3.69%
      t=0.001:  delta/trace = 18.1%
      t=0.01:   delta/trace = 12.2%

At small t, an extra eigenvalue makes a ~4% change. This is
comparable to the oscillatory correction (~1.7%). So the Weyl law
CANNOT rule out a single extra eigenvalue at the precision available.

## 5. Can we compute the heat kernel from the BC side?

The BC time evolution is sigma_t(mu_n) = n^{it} * mu_n.

The partition function on the FULL GNS space:
    Tr_{H_1}(e^{-beta * T_BC}) = sum_{n=1}^inf n^{-beta} = zeta(beta)

This is the ALGEBRAIC computation. But T_BC on H_1 has eigenvalues
{log n : n in N^x}, NOT {gamma_n}.

The eigenvalues {gamma_n} live on the Riemann subspace H_R subset H_1.
Computing Tr_{H_R}(e^{-t*T_BC^2}) requires CONSTRUCTING H_R first,
which is exactly the spectral realisation problem.

The KMS condition:
    omega_1(A * e^{itT} * B) = omega_1(B * e^{i(t+i)T} * A)

This determines the two-point function of T_BC in the state omega_1,
but on the FULL space H_1, not on H_R.

## 6. The circularity

The Weyl law approach requires:
1. Compute Tr_{H_R}(e^{-t*T_BC^2}) from BC algebra (the "operator side")
2. Compare to sum_n e^{-t*gamma_n^2} (the "zero side")
3. If they match, no room for extra spectrum

Step 1 requires knowing what H_R is. But defining H_R requires
spectral realisation. The argument is circular.

What we CAN compute from the BC side is Tr_{H_1}(e^{-beta*T_BC}) =
zeta(beta). This tells us about {log n}, not about {gamma_n}.
The connection between {log n} (primes side) and {gamma_n}
(zeros side) is precisely the explicit formula -- which is the
Connes trace formula.

## 7. Premise check

**Does this argument distinguish spec = {gamma_n} from
spec = {gamma_n} union {extra}?**

Only IF we can compute the heat trace on H_R independently of the
zeros. We cannot -- the BC algebra computes the heat trace on H_1,
and projecting to H_R requires spectral realisation.

The numerical evidence (extra eigenvalue causes ~4-70% perturbation)
is real but requires an independent BC-side computation of the
H_R heat trace to be usable.

## 8. What IS new

1. **The persistent oscillatory correction ~0.42** in the heat trace
   is NOT from an extra eigenvalue. It comes from S(T), the argument
   of zeta on the critical line. This is a known number-theoretic
   quantity and does not indicate extra spectrum.

2. **Sensitivity:** An extra eigenvalue at any location lambda > 0
   makes a detectable change at moderate t. The heat trace IS
   sensitive to extra spectrum. The problem is not sensitivity but
   having an independent computation to compare against.

3. **The Weyl error is too large** to rule out a single extra
   eigenvalue. The Trudgian bound |S(T)| < 0.137 log T + ...
   allows O(log T) extra point eigenvalues up to height T. This is
   consistent with zero extra eigenvalues but does not exclude them.

## 9. Verdict

**BLOCKED.** The heat kernel approach is sound in principle but
reduces to the same bottleneck: constructing H_R independently.
The numerical computations are correct and the sensitivity to extra
eigenvalues is real, but without an independent BC-side computation
of Tr_{H_R}(f(T_BC)), the comparison is not available.

**Infrastructure value:** The heat trace computations and their
match to Weyl asymptotics (1.7% at t=0.0001) provide evidence
for the programme. They do not constitute proof.

# 8. The Electroweak Phase Transition

*This section places Paper 4 §7.12 in its cosmological context.*

## 8.1 The Event

At T_c ~ 1 TeV (t ~ 10⁻¹² s), the universe undergoes the
electroweak phase transition. In the Standard Model, this is a
smooth crossover — no drama, no gravitational waves. The SM endpoint
of the first-order regime is m_{H,c} = 72.3 ± 0.7 GeV; above this
mass, no barrier forms in the thermal effective potential. Since the
physical Higgs is at 125 GeV, the SM transition is a crossover.

In the e-dimension framework, it is a **first-order phase transition**
— the most violent event between inflation and the present day. The
mechanism differs from the SM because the Higgs is identified as the
Wilson-line angle θ_H, whose thermal potential is modified by KK
gauge-boson loops at the TeV scale.

## 8.1a The Thermal Effective Potential in GHU

In the gauge-Higgs unification framework (Paper 4, §6.2), the Higgs
field is the Wilson line angle θ_H — the component of the 5D gauge
field A_5 integrated around the e-circle:

    θ_H = g_5 ∮_{S¹} A_5 dy

At zero temperature, the effective potential V(θ_H) is generated by
the one-loop Coleman-Weinberg mechanism (Hosotani 1983):

    V_CW(θ_H) = −3/(64π⁶ R⁴) [Σ_n (n + θ_H)^{−4} N_b
                 − Σ_n (n + θ_H + 1/2)^{−4} N_f]

where N_b and N_f count the bosonic and fermionic KK modes per level
respectively. This potential generically has a non-trivial minimum at
θ_H = θ_0 ≠ 0 for appropriate field content.

**At finite temperature**, additional contributions arise from thermal
loops:

    V_T(θ_H, T) = V_CW(θ_H) + V_thermal(θ_H, T)

The thermal correction for bosons with KK mass m_n(θ_H) is:

    V_thermal^{bos} = T/(2π²) Σ_n ∫₀^∞ dk k² ln(1 − e^{−√{k²+m_n²}/T})

For T ~ M_KK (below the KK scale, where M_KK ~ 1 TeV from Paper 4
§4.2), the light KK modes dominate, and the thermal correction is:

    V_thermal ≈ (T²/24) × m²(θ_H) + (T/12π) × |m³(θ_H)| − T⁴ π²/90 + ...

The critical term for a first-order transition is the **cubic**:
−c₃ T |m³(θ_H)|. This creates a potential barrier between θ_H = 0
and θ_H = θ_0, generating a first-order transition.

In the SM, the cubic term from bosonic thermal masses is absent at
one loop for m_H > 72 GeV (the transition becomes a crossover). In
GHU models, the KK gauge bosons contribute additional cubic terms:
the KK towers of W and Z have masses m_n = (n ± θ_H)M_KK, and their
thermal fluctuations generate:

    V_cubic(T) ~ −g² T (Σ_n m_n(θ_H)³) × (KK suppression factors)

For the first KK level with M_KK ~ 1 TeV, this cubic term is of
order g³ T M_KK² θ_H, which is absent in the decoupling limit
(M_KK → ∞) but is O(1) for M_KK ~ 1 TeV.

**Summary.** The first-order EWPT in this framework occurs because
the Higgs (the GHU Wilson line) is coupled to KK gauge bosons with
mass M_KK ~ 1 TeV. These heavy bosons contribute a cubic term to
the thermal Higgs potential that is absent in the SM. The cubic is
proportional to T × M_KK², creating a potential barrier and a
first-order transition. The complete calculation (nucleation rates,
α, β/H_*, bubble wall velocity) is presented in Paper 4 §7.12.

## 8.2 What Happens Geometrically

The Higgs field is the Wilson line angle θ_H — the tilt between
the S² (weak) and S¹ (electromagnetic) compact dimensions
(Paper 4, §6.2).

Before the transition (T > T_c): θ_H = 0. The two compact dimensions
are orthogonal. The full SU(2) × U(1) gauge symmetry is unbroken.
All fermions are massless.

During the transition (T ≈ T_c): The GHU thermal potential V(θ_H, T)
develops a second minimum at θ_H = θ_0 ≠ 0 (Paper 4, §6.5). The
universe supercools in the symmetric phase. Bubbles of the broken
phase nucleate. The bubble walls expand at nearly the speed of light,
colliding and merging.

After the transition (T < T_c): θ_H = θ_0. The S² and S¹ are
tilted. The W and Z bosons are massive. Fermions acquire mass from
their coupling to the Wilson line. Electroweak symmetry is broken.

**Electroweak symmetry breaking is a cosmological event — the moment
when two compact dimensions tilted relative to each other.**

## 8.2a The Critical Temperature T_c ~ 1 TeV

The critical temperature T_c ~ 1 TeV is set by the KK scale, not
by the Higgs VEV. In the GHU picture (§8.1a), the finite-temperature
phase transition occurs when the thermal mass squared of the Higgs —
dominated by gauge boson loops at temperature T — changes sign. The
thermal mass contribution from the KK gauge bosons (mass M_KK ~ 1 TeV)
is:

    m²_{th}(T) ≈ (g²/12)(T² + M_KK² × ...)

At T ~ M_KK, the KK modes enter the thermal sum and their contribution
triggers the transition. The critical condition m²_{th}(T_c) = 0 gives
T_c ~ M_KK/g ~ 1 TeV/0.65 ~ 1.5 TeV, consistent with T_c ~ 1 TeV from
the full calculation in Paper 4. The factor of 10 above the Higgs VEV
v = 246 GeV is therefore a simple consequence of M_KK ~ 1 TeV ≫ v:
the transition is driven by the KK threshold, not by the Higgs VEV.

## 8.3 The Gravitational Wave Signal

The first-order transition produces gravitational waves through three
mechanisms: bubble wall collisions, sound waves in the plasma, and
turbulence.

From Paper 4, §7.12, the transition parameters are:

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Critical temperature | T_c ~ 1 TeV |
| Nucleation temperature | T_* = 900 GeV |
| Transition strength | α = Δρ/ρ_rad = 0.12 |
| Inverse duration | β/H_* = 32 |
| Bubble wall velocity | v_w = 0.92 |
| Peak frequency (today) | f ~ 6 mHz |
| Peak amplitude | h²Ω_GW ~ 5 × 10⁻¹³ |

**Note on M_KK scaling.** The parameters in this table
($\alpha = 0.12$, $\beta/H_* = 32$, $T_* = 900\,\text{GeV}$,
$f_\mathrm{sw} \sim 6\,\text{mHz}$,
$h^2\Omega_\mathrm{GW} \sim 5 \times 10^{-13}$) are computed for
$M_\mathrm{KK} = 1\,\text{TeV}$. Current LHC constraints from KK
gauge boson searches (KK $W^\pm \to \ell\nu$ below $\sim 3\,\text{TeV}$;
KK $Z \to \ell^+\ell^-$ below $\sim 4\,\text{TeV}$) require
$M_\mathrm{KK} \gtrsim 3\,\text{TeV}$. At $M_\mathrm{KK} = 3\,\text{TeV}$,
the transition temperature shifts to $T_* \sim 3\,\text{TeV}$, and
the peak frequency scales as
$f_\mathrm{sw} \propto T_*/g_*^{1/2} \to \sim 20\,\text{mHz}$. The
amplitude $h^2\Omega_\mathrm{GW} \propto \alpha^2 / (\beta/H_*)^2$
requires recomputing $\alpha$ and $\beta/H_*$ at
$M_\mathrm{KK} = 3\,\text{TeV}$ from the thermal effective potential
of Paper 4 §7.12; this rescaling is deferred to that calculation. The
$M_\mathrm{KK} = 1\,\text{TeV}$ values in this table should be
regarded as preliminary estimates, with the LHC-consistent prediction
at $M_\mathrm{KK} = 3\,\text{TeV}$ to be presented in a companion
analysis.

## 8.3a GW Spectrum Parameters and LISA Prediction

The dominant GW contribution is from sound waves (Espinosa,
Konstandin, No & Servant 2010):

    h² Ω_sw(f) = 2.65 × 10⁻⁶ × (H_*/β)² × (κ_v α/(1+α))²
                  × (100/g_*)^{1/3} × v_w × S_sw(f/f_sw)

With κ_v(α=0.12) ≈ 0.10, g_* = 106.75, H_*/β = 1/32, the peak
frequency is:

    f_sw ≈ 1.9 × 10⁻⁵ Hz × (32/0.92) × (900/100) × (106.75/100)^{1/6}
         ≈ 6 × 10⁻³ Hz = 6 mHz

The peak amplitude:

    h² Ω_sw(f_sw) ≈ 2.65 × 10⁻⁶ × (1/32)² × (0.10 × 0.12/1.12)²
                     × 0.93^{1/3} × 0.92
                   ≈ 2.7 × 10⁻¹³

Adding turbulence and bubble collision contributions:

    h² Ω_GW ≈ 5 × 10⁻¹³ at f ~ 6 mHz

**Comparison to LISA sensitivity.** The LISA power-law integrated
sensitivity at 6 mHz (Caprini et al. 2019) is approximately
h² Ω_LISA(6 mHz) ~ 3 × 10⁻¹². The predicted amplitude
h² Ω_GW ~ 5 × 10⁻¹³ is approximately **6 times below** the LISA
threshold at the peak frequency. The signal is near but below the
LISA sensitivity for these specific parameter values.

The signal could be detectable if α is at the upper end of the
uncertainty range (α ~ 0.3) or if the bubble wall velocity is lower
(v_w ~ 0.3, giving more efficient acoustic energy injection). The
full uncertainty range gives h² Ω_GW ~ 10⁻¹³–10⁻¹¹, straddling
the LISA threshold. The central prediction (α = 0.12, β/H_* = 32)
gives a marginal signal.

*Binary test.* If LISA observes a GW signal at 1–10 mHz with
amplitude h² Ω_GW > 10⁻¹², it is consistent with the framework's
EWPT prediction. If no signal is observed above h² Ω_GW ~ 10⁻¹³,
the T_c ~ 1 TeV first-order EWPT is disfavored but not excluded (the
precise threshold depends on observation duration and foreground
subtraction).

## 8.4 Simultaneous Events on the Hidden Brane

At the same cosmic time, the hidden brane undergoes its own
electroweak phase transition at T'_c = ξ T_c ~ 432 GeV (using ξ = 0.432). The mirror
W' and Z' bosons acquire mass. Mirror fermions become massive.

The mirror EWPT also produces gravitational waves — at a slightly
lower frequency (because T'_c < T_c) and a similar amplitude.
The combined signal from BOTH branes may produce a distinctive
double-peak in the LISA band — a signature unique to the two-brane
framework.

## 8.5 LHC Implications

The first-order EWPT in this framework is driven by KK gauge bosons
with M_KK ~ 1 TeV — specifically, the KK modes of the W and Z from
the extra-dimensional compactification. Standard RS-type KK gauge
bosons are excluded by LHC dijets (KK gluons below ~4.5 TeV) and
dileptons (KK Z' below ~5 TeV). The KK electroweak gauge bosons in
this framework must satisfy current LHC Run 2 bounds: KK W± → ℓν
is excluded below ~3 TeV (CMS-EXO-21-010), and KK Z → ℓ⁺ℓ⁻ is
excluded below ~4 TeV.

*For M_KK ~ 1 TeV, the KK electroweak gauge bosons are nominally in
tension with LHC bounds.* The framework requires either:

**(a) M_KK ~ 3–4 TeV** (consistent with current LHC bounds), which
raises T_c to ~3–4 TeV and shifts the GW peak to ~20–40 mHz —
still within the LISA band. The transition parameters α and β/H_*
would need to be recomputed for this mass range.

**(b) Fermion localization suppression.** If the SM fermions have
bulk mass parameters c_f in Paper 4 that localize them away from
the brane, the KK-fermion overlap integral is suppressed, reducing
the effective coupling to LHC partons below the RS prediction.
Whether this is sufficient to evade the bounds requires a detailed
computation in Paper 4.

For this paper, we note that the LHC constraint requires M_KK ≳ 3 TeV
for the KK W/Z masses given current search bounds. This shifts T_c to
~3 TeV and the GW peak to ~20 mHz. The LISA detectability conclusion
is qualitatively unchanged; the precise GW parameters in §8.3 should
be treated as order-of-magnitude estimates pending the resolution of
the LHC constraint in Paper 4.

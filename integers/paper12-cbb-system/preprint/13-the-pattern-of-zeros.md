## Hypotheses for the Selection Rule

### Hypothesis 1: γ_1 is foundational (the IR pivot)

The first Riemann zero γ_1 = 14.13 is the smallest non-trivial
critical temperature of the BC system. It's the IR limit — the
"zero point" of the spectral hierarchy.

**Observables it appears in:**
- Cosmological constant (the IR scale of the universe)
- Fine structure constant (the IR limit of EM coupling)
- Mirror brane temperature (paired with γ_5)
- Jarlskog invariant (CP violation, IR-defined)

All of these are "deep IR" observables — quantities that don't run
significantly with energy scale. γ_1 sets their natural scale.

### Hypothesis 2: γ_6 is the electroweak/orbifold zero

The Z_2 × Z_3 = 6 orbifold structure of the framework
(visible-mirror brane × 3 fermion generations) might index γ_6.

**Observables it appears in:**
- N_eff (3 neutrinos × 2 sectors → 6)
- 1/α_2 (electroweak coupling)
- m_W/m_Z (electroweak mass ratio)
- m_H (Higgs, the EW scale parameter)
- m_c (charm, the second-generation up-type quark)
- ξ corrections (mirror sector)

The 6-fold structure pervades electroweak physics. γ_6 indexes this
sector.

### Hypothesis 3: γ_8 is the lepton/GUT zero

The 8 might correspond to dim(adjoint of SU(3)) = 8 gluons, or
to 2³ = 8 (a higher orbifold structure).

**Observables it appears in:**
- m_τ/m_μ (lepton hierarchy ratio)
- 17 = γ_8^(3/4) (GUT flux integer)
- m_t = γ_3 × γ_8 / (2π) (top quark, paired with γ_3)

γ_8 connects lepton mass hierarchies and GUT structure. This is
unexpected — leptons and GUT fluxes are usually treated separately.
γ_8 unifies them.

### Hypothesis 4: γ_11 is the M-theory + strong zero

The 11 might correspond to M-theory's 11 dimensions, or to the
"high-energy" critical temperatures.

**Observables it appears in:**
- H_0 (Hubble constant, the cosmological scale)
- m_Z (Z boson mass)
- 1/α_3 (strong coupling)

γ_11 indexes high-energy or "M-theory scale" observables. The
cosmology here is a NEAR-final-time observable (today's H_0), not
the deep IR (which is γ_1).

### Hypothesis 5: γ_15 is the deep-bottom zero

γ_15 = 65.11 is the highest Riemann zero used so far. It appears
ONLY in:
- m_b = log(γ_15)

The logarithmic structure is unusual. m_b might be the "deepest
IR" mass after the lightest particles, requiring a high zero to
encode logarithmically.

## 2. The Operator m-hat_s on H_R

### 2.1 Physical origin

In the Standard Model the strange quark mass is generated by the
Higgs Yukawa coupling y_s:

$$
m_s \;=\; \frac{y_s\,v}{\sqrt{2}},
\tag{2.1}
$$

where v = 246 GeV and y_s ~ 5 x 10^{-4}. The strange quark's
Yukawa is small compared to the top (y_t ~ 1), reflecting the
mass hierarchy m_s / m_t ~ 5 x 10^{-4}.

### 2.2 Translation to H_R

Under Identity 12, the strange Yukawa operator O_s on H_R
couples two Galois orbits: one carrying the "foundational" U(1)
quantum number (gamma_1) and one carrying the heaviest quark-mass
quantum number (gamma_15). The matrix element:

$$
m_s \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi^2}\,
\langle\gamma_{15} \mid O_s \mid \gamma_1\rangle
\;=\; \frac{\gamma_1 \cdot \gamma_{15}}{\pi^2}.
\tag{2.2}
$$

### 2.3 Why a bilinear product

Like the top mass formula m_t = gamma_3 * gamma_8 / (2pi), the
strange quark mass is a *product* of two Riemann zeros divided
by a normalisation constant. The product structure is forced by
the Yukawa operator being a rank-two bilinear form on H_R
(Section 3 of research/26 applies verbatim). The difference is
which zeros appear and which normalisation factor divides them.

## 3. Why gamma_1 and gamma_15: The Strange Quark Selection Rule

### 3.1 gamma_1 as the foundational orbit

gamma_1 = 14.135 is the most-used zero in the framework (11
channels in the frequency table of research/23 Section 7). It
carries the U(1) hypercharge / foundational gauge sector. For
the strange quark, the gamma_1 factor encodes the fact that m_s
is the *first significant mass above the chiral limit* -- the
strange quark is the lightest quark whose mass is large enough
to break chiral symmetry appreciably (m_s >> Lambda_QCD x (m_u,
m_d)/Lambda_QCD).

The appearance of gamma_1 in m_s (but not in m_t = gamma_3 *
gamma_8 / (2pi) or m_c = gamma_9/gamma_6) reflects the strange
quark's unique position as the "first non-trivial mass" in the
quark spectrum. It couples to the *smallest* Riemann zero because
it is the lightest quark with physically significant mass.

### 3.2 gamma_15 as the heaviest quark-mass orbit

gamma_15 = 65.113 carries the bottom quark mass via m_b =
log(gamma_15) = 4.176 GeV (0.093% match). In the strange quark
formula, gamma_15 reappears as the *partner* orbit: the strange
quark's mass is structurally linked to the bottom quark's mass
through the down-type Yukawa hierarchy:

$$
\frac{m_s}{m_b} \;=\; \frac{\gamma_1\,\gamma_{15}/\pi^2}{\log\gamma_{15}}
\;=\; \frac{\gamma_1}{\pi^2/\gamma_{15} \cdot \log\gamma_{15}}
\;\approx\; 0.0223.
\tag{3.1}
$$

The measured ratio m_s/m_b = 93.4/4180 = 0.02234, matching at
0.18%. The structural message: **the strange and bottom quark
masses are related by the same gamma_15 orbit**, with the
hierarchy controlled by the additional gamma_1 factor and the
log vs linear functional form.

## 4. The 1/pi^2 Factor

### 4.1 Why pi^2 and not 2pi

The top mass uses 1/(2pi) from the S^1 circumference KK
reduction. The strange mass uses 1/pi^2 -- a different
normalisation. The structural distinction:

- **m_t = gamma_3 * gamma_8 / (2pi)**: the top quark couples to
  the colour sector (gamma_8 = SU(3) adjoint) via a purely
  *geometric* S^1 reduction. The normalisation is the single
  circle's circumference 2pi.

- **m_s = gamma_1 * gamma_15 / pi^2**: the strange quark couples
  to the foundational orbit (gamma_1 = U(1)) via a *flavour-
  sector* integration. The normalisation pi^2 = (2pi)^2 / 4
  reflects a *double* circle integration -- over the S^1 of the
  e-circle AND over the CP^1 fibre of the flavour structure.

Equivalently: pi^2 = zeta(2) * 6, and the factor 6 counts the
number of quark flavours. The normalisation 1/pi^2 is the
"per-flavour thermal weight" of the BC partition function at
beta = 2, exactly as in the eta_10 derivation (research/113).

### 4.2 The light-quark normalisation pattern

All three light quark masses use distinct normalisations:

| Quark | Formula | Normalisation | Source |
|:------|:--------|:-------------|:-------|
| m_s | gamma_1 * gamma_15 / pi^2 | pi^2 | flavour-sector double integral |
| m_d | gamma_9 - gamma_8 | 1 (difference) | first-order spectral gap |
| m_u | gamma_4 / gamma_1 | ratio | ratio of two orbits |

The normalisations correlate with the algebraic form: products
get pi-type denominators (from integrals over compact
dimensions), differences get unit coefficient (from spectral
gaps), and ratios get unit coefficient (from orbit ratios).

### 4.3 Rigor status

- **Structural**: the pi^2 from the double compact-dimension

## 0. One-paragraph summary

The Stone-von Neumann theorem (1931) asserts that every irreducible
representation of the Weyl form of the canonical commutation relations
(CCR) on a finite number of degrees of freedom is unitarily equivalent
to the Schrodinger representation on L^2(R^n). Its operator-algebraic
content is: the CCR algebra has a unique (up to unitary equivalence)
irreducible regular representation. On the Bost-Connes side, the
analog is the uniqueness of the KMS_1 state omega_1 (Bost-Connes 1995,
Theorem 25): at the critical inverse temperature beta=1, the
C*-dynamical system (A_BC, sigma_t) has a unique KMS state, hence a
unique GNS representation (pi_1, H_1, Omega_1). Under Identity 12
(research/04), the e-circle algebra A_e with its generators {D_n, E_r}
maps to A_BC with its generators {mu_n, e(r)}, and the uniqueness of
the GNS at beta=1 corresponds precisely to the Stone-von Neumann
uniqueness of the Weyl representation for the N*-action on Q/Z.
We name this **R-Theorem QM.5 (BC Stone-von Neumann)**. The LOCK
contribution: uniqueness of the representation forces the spectral data
{gamma_n} into the unique Hilbert space H_1, where T_BC is self-adjoint
by Stone's theorem -- so gamma_n must be real.

## 1. The source theorem

### 1.1 Stone-von Neumann (textbook form)

Let (Q_1, ..., Q_n, P_1, ..., P_n) be operators on a Hilbert space H
satisfying the canonical commutation relations (CCR) in Weyl form:

$$
  U(s)\,V(t) \;=\; e^{-i s \cdot t}\,V(t)\,U(s),
  \qquad
  U(s) = e^{i s \cdot Q},\;
  V(t) = e^{i t \cdot P},
\tag{1.1}
$$

where s, t in R^n and U, V are strongly continuous unitary groups.

> **Theorem (Stone-von Neumann 1931).** *Every irreducible
> representation of the Weyl relations (1.1) on a separable
> Hilbert space H is unitarily equivalent to the Schrodinger
> representation on L^2(R^n), in which Q acts by multiplication
> and P by -i d/dx.*

### 1.2 Three readings

(A) **Uniqueness of kinematics.** There is only one way to quantise
a system with finitely many degrees of freedom (up to unitary
equivalence). This is why quantum mechanics has a single Hilbert space.

(B) **Algebraic.** The CCR algebra (the Weyl algebra W(R^{2n}, omega))
has a unique irreducible regular representation (Slawny 1972).

(C) **Operator-algebraic (von Neumann's form).** Any two factor
representations of W(R^{2n}) in which the Weyl unitaries act
regularly are quasi-equivalent. This is the version that generalises
to the BC setting.

### 1.3 What fails for infinitely many degrees of freedom

For infinite-dimensional systems, Stone-von Neumann fails: there are
unitarily inequivalent representations of the CCR (Haag's theorem,
the BCS representation, etc.). This is the origin of superselection
sectors, SSB, and the Doplicher-Haag-Roberts theory. On the BC side,
the analog of "infinitely many degrees of freedom" is "infinitely
many primes", and the analog of "inequivalent representations" is
the family of KMS_beta states for beta > 1. **Stone-von Neumann
holds at beta=1 precisely because the BC system at the critical
point has a unique KMS state -- the infinite-degree-of-freedom
obstruction is bypassed by the type III_1 structure of the critical
factor.**

## 2. The BC setup

### 2.1 The BC algebra as a "Weyl algebra for N*"

The Bost-Connes algebra A_BC is generated by isometries mu_n
(n in N*) and unitaries e(r) (r in Q/Z) with the relations
(research/04, equation (3.1)):

$$
  \mu_n \mu_m = \mu_{nm},
  \quad
  \mu_n^* \mu_n = 1,
  \quad
  e(r+s) = e(r) e(s),
  \quad
  \mu_n\,e(r)\,\mu_n^* = \frac{1}{n}\!\sum_{ns=r} e(s).
\tag{2.1}
$$

The Hecke relation (the fourth equation in (2.1)) is the BC analog of
the Weyl relation (1.1). The role of the phase space (Q, P) is played
by (Q/Z, N*): the "position" is the arithmetic phase r in Q/Z, the
"momentum" is the multiplicative index n in N*. The relation
mu_n e(r) mu_n^* = (1/n) sum e(s) replaces e^{-ist} in the Weyl
relation with the averaging map alpha_n on the phase group Q/Z.

### 2.2 The e-circle side (research/04 section 5.4)

On the e-circle H_e, the dilation operators D_n and phase operators
E_r satisfy (research/04, equations (2.9)-(2.11)):

$$
  D_n\,E_r\,D_n^* \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\!\sum_{ns=r} E_s.
\tag{2.2}
$$

This is literally (2.1) transported by the unitary U : H_e -> H_1 of
Identity 12. The remark in research/04 section 5.4 states: *"This is
the content of the Stone-von Neumann theorem for the N*-action on
Q/Z."* We now make this remark into a theorem.

### 2.3 The unique KMS_1 state

The BC system at beta=1 has a **unique** KMS state omega_1
(Bost-Connes 1995, Theorem 25; see research/21 section 3). This
uniqueness has two parts:

(KMS-U1) For beta <= 1, the KMS_beta state is unique.

(KMS-U2) For beta > 1, the KMS_beta states form a simplex
parametrised by Gal(Q^{ab}/Q), with extremal states phi_{beta,rho}
indexed by embeddings rho: Q^{ab} -> C.

At the critical point beta=1, uniqueness holds -- this is the BC
analog of Stone-von Neumann. The phase transition at beta=1 (where
uniqueness breaks down for beta > 1) is the BC analog of the failure
of Stone-von Neumann for infinitely many degrees of freedom.

### 2.4 The GNS representation

The unique KMS_1 state omega_1 produces the GNS triple
(pi_1, H_1, Omega_1) with:

- H_1 the GNS Hilbert space, orthonormal basis {mu_n Omega_1 : n in N*}
  (research/04 section 3.1, equation (3.6))
- Omega_1 the cyclic and separating vacuum
- pi_1: A_BC -> B(H_1) the GNS representation

The GNS representation is unique because omega_1 is unique. This is
the operator-algebraic content: the representation is determined by
the state, and the state is determined by the KMS condition at the
critical temperature.

## 4. R-Theorem QM.5 (BC Stone-von Neumann)

### 4.1 Statement

> **R-Theorem QM.5 (BC Stone-von Neumann; rigorous modulo Identity 12).**
> *Let (A_BC, sigma_t) be the Bost-Connes C*-dynamical system with*
> *generators {mu_n : n in N*} and {e(r) : r in Q/Z} satisfying the*
> *Hecke relations (2.1) and time evolution sigma_t(mu_n) = n^{it} mu_n.*
> *Then:*
>
> 1. *(Rigorous, Bost-Connes 1995 Thm 25.) The KMS_1 state omega_1 on*
>    *(A_BC, sigma_t) is unique.*
>
> 2. *(Rigorous.) The GNS representation (pi_1, H_1, Omega_1) of*
>    *omega_1 is the unique (up to unitary equivalence) cyclic*
>    *representation of A_BC with respect to a KMS_1 state. The*
>    *von Neumann algebra M_1 = pi_1(A_BC)'' is a type III_1 factor.*
>
> 3. *(Rigorous, research/04.) Under the unitary U: H_e -> H_1 of*
>    *Identity 12, the Hecke relation (2.1) is intertwined with the*
>    *dilation-phase relation (2.2) on H_e, which is the Stone-von*
>    *Neumann intertwining for the N*-action on Q/Z.*
>
> 4. *(Structural.) The uniqueness of (pi_1, H_1) implies that every*
>    *self-adjoint operator affiliated with M_1 -- in particular the*
>    *scaling generator T_BC of research/14 -- has its spectrum*
>    *determined uniquely by the algebraic data (A_BC, sigma_t, beta=1).*
>    *There is no ambiguity of representation: the spectral set*
>    *{gamma_n} subset spec(T_BC) is intrinsic.*

### 4.2 Proof of (1)-(3)

**(1)** is Bost-Connes 1995 Theorem 25, proved by showing that the
free energy f(beta) = -log zeta(beta) has a unique tangent functional
at beta=1 (the phase-transition point). The KMS_beta simplex collapses
to a single point at and below the critical temperature.

**(2)** follows from (1) by GNS uniqueness: if the state is unique,
the GNS representation is unique up to unitary equivalence (Bratteli-
Robinson, Theorem 5.3.30). The type classification M_1 is type III_1
because the Connes invariant S(M_1) = R_+^* (the full positive reals),
which follows from the fact that the modular flow sigma_t^{omega_1}
has the BC spectrum {log n : n in N*}, which is dense in R (see
research/21 section 3).

**(3)** is the content of research/04 sections 4-5. The unitary U is
constructed basis-by-basis: U|n>_e = mu_n Omega_1. The intertwining
of D_n with mu_n and E_r with e(r) is verified relation-by-relation
in research/04 section 5, equations (5.1)-(5.8). The Stone-von
Neumann character of this intertwining is that U implements the
**unique** isomorphism between two irreducible representations of
the same algebraic relations -- which is exactly what Stone-von
Neumann guarantees for the Weyl algebra.

### 4.3 Discussion of (4)

Claim (4) is structural because it involves T_BC, whose relationship
to A_BC depends on the CCM scaling construction (research/14, Identity
14). The chain is:

- omega_1 unique => (pi_1, H_1) unique => T_BC (as affiliated operator)
  unique => spec(T_BC) unique => {gamma_n} intrinsic.

The conditional step is "T_BC affiliated with M_1", which requires the
CCM spectral realisation (CM 2008 Ch. II section 3). Given that, the
uniqueness of {gamma_n} is a corollary of (1)-(3).

## 5. Why uniqueness matters: the representation-independence of the zeros

### 5.1 The classical lesson

In ordinary QM, Stone-von Neumann ensures that the energy spectrum
of a quantum system is representation-independent: every irreducible
regular CCR representation gives the same eigenvalues. Without
Stone-von Neumann, different "quantisations" could give different
spectra, and the physical predictions would be ambiguous.

### 5.2 The BC lesson

R-Theorem QM.5 ensures that the spectral data {gamma_n} of the BC
scaling generator are representation-independent at beta=1. There is
no freedom to "choose a different Hilbert space" in which the zeros
of zeta might live elsewhere. The zeros are intrinsic to the algebraic
structure (A_BC, sigma_t), not to a particular representation.

This is crucial for the LOCK: if there were multiple inequivalent
representations at beta=1, one could imagine a representation in
which some gamma_n were complex. R-Theorem QM.5 rules this out by
collapsing the representation to a unique one, in which T_BC is
self-adjoint and spec(T_BC) subset R.

### 5.3 The phase transition at beta=1

The BC phase transition at beta=1 is the arithmetic analog of the
Haag-Kastler obstruction in QFT: for beta > 1, the KMS states are
no longer unique, and the system exhibits spontaneous symmetry
breaking (the Galois group Gal(Q^{ab}/Q) acts non-trivially on the
extremal KMS_beta states). In QFT language: the "vacuum degeneracy"
for beta > 1 is the infinite-volume limit, and beta=1 is the
critical point where the symmetry is restored.

The critical point beta=1 is the only temperature where Stone-von
Neumann holds. It is also the only temperature where RH has content
(the zeros of zeta correspond to the spectrum at beta=1, not at
beta > 1). This is not a coincidence: **the uniqueness of the
representation at the critical point is what makes the spectral
data well-defined, and well-defined spectral data is what makes RH
a meaningful statement.**

## 8. LOCK contribution

### 8.1 The argument

R-Theorem QM.5 contributes to the LOCK on RH as follows:

(L1) **Uniqueness kills representation freedom.** If the GNS
representation at beta=1 were non-unique, one could conceive of a
representation in which T_BC has complex spectral values -- a
"non-physical" quantisation. R-Theorem QM.5 forbids this: there is
exactly one representation.

(L2) **In the unique representation, T_BC is self-adjoint.** The
BC time evolution sigma_t = Adelta_1^{it} is implemented by a unitary
group on H_1, and T_BC is its generator. By Stone's theorem (the
other Stone theorem, not Stone-von Neumann), the generator of a
strongly continuous unitary group on a Hilbert space is self-adjoint.

(L3) **Self-adjointness forces real spectrum.** spec(T_BC) subset R
is a consequence of self-adjointness (spectral theorem).

(L4) **{gamma_n} subset spec(T_BC) subset R.** Therefore gamma_n
in R for all n >= 1.

The chain: **Stone-von Neumann (uniqueness) => Stone (self-adjointness)
=> spectral theorem (reality) => gamma_n in R (RH).**

### 8.2 Where the conditionality lives

The weak link is the step {gamma_n} subset spec(T_BC), which requires
the CCM spectral realisation. This is the same conditionality as in
Path 1 (research/87 section 1.1, the Stone chain). R-Theorem QM.5
does not create a new path to RH; it **strengthens Path 1** by adding
the uniqueness constraint. Without QM.5, Path 1 has the formal
possibility that different representations of A_BC might give
different spectral data. With QM.5, that possibility is eliminated.

### 8.3 The tooth in the LOCK

R-Theorem QM.5 is a **representation-rigidity tooth**: it forces the
RH question into a unique Hilbert space, eliminating the "wrong
representation" escape route. Combined with R-Theorem QM.1
(Heisenberg, which constrains the uncertainty in gamma_n) and
R-Theorem QM.2 (Reeh-Schlieder, which ensures the vacuum Omega_1
is cyclic for every sub-Hecke algebra), QM.5 completes the QM-series
LOCK: the spectral data are real, unique, and unavoidable.

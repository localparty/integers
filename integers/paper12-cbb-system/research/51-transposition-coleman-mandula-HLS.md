## 0. One-paragraph summary

The Coleman–Mandula theorem (1967) is the central no-go in 4D
relativistic QFT: any Lie algebra of symmetries of a non-trivial
S-matrix that contains the Poincaré algebra as a subalgebra must be
a **direct sum** of the Poincaré algebra with an internal compact
Lie algebra. The Haag–Łopuszański–Sohnius theorem (1975) closes
the only loophole: if one allows **graded** (i.e. Z_2-graded)
extensions, the algebra can be supersymmetric, with odd generators
transforming as spinors of the Lorentz group. We transpose both
into the Bost–Connes / Riemann setting under the dictionary

> "S-matrix" ↔ BC time evolution σ_t between in/out KMS-perturbed
> states at β = 1; "spacetime symmetries" ↔ the BC dilation flow
> generated by H_BC = log N̂ (the multiplicative analog of the
> Poincaré dilation generator); "internal symmetries" ↔ the Hecke
> action of {μ_p, μ_p^*, e(r)} on H_R.

The transposed statement, **R-Theorem 51 (Coleman–Mandula on the
BC side)**, asserts that the only Lie algebras of symmetries of σ_t
on H_R that contain T_BC = log N̂ as a generator are direct sums
T_BC ⊕ g_int, where g_int is a finite-dim compact Lie algebra of
Hecke automorphisms commuting with T_BC. Crucially, **this matches
the QG5D framework's gauge group structure** (research/10): G_SM is
the maximal such g_int at the rank-3 Hecke level. The HLS extension
**R-Theorem 51-HLS** then identifies a Z_2-graded extension whose
odd part is generated by the BC isometries μ_p themselves (which
square to the Hecke action), realising "BC supersymmetry" as the
algebraic statement that **N\*-isometries are the odd half of the
graded automorphism algebra of the BC system at β = 1**. What is
rigorous: the no-go for graded extensions at the rank-3 cube level,
modelled on Coleman–Mandula's argument transcribed term-by-term.
What is structural: the identification of μ_p with spinor odd
generators (the "BC SUSY") — the analog map exists at the level of
the graded algebra but the realisation as a representation of a
Lorentz-like group is via Identity 12 transport, not BC-intrinsic.
What is open: whether the BC SUSY closes a 4D-Lorentz-spinor
algebra in any non-trivial sense.

## 1. The Source Theorems (Coleman–Mandula and HLS)

### 1.1 Coleman–Mandula (1967)

> **Theorem (Coleman–Mandula).** *Let G be a connected Lie group of*
> *symmetries of the S-matrix of a relativistic 4D QFT, with the*
> *following assumptions:*
>
> (CM1) *G contains the Poincaré group P as a subgroup.*
> (CM2) *For any M, the number of one-particle states with mass < M*
>       *is finite.*
> (CM3) *Elastic-scattering amplitudes are analytic in s and t*
>       *(except at threshold singularities).*
> (CM4) *The S-matrix is non-trivial (some elastic two-body amplitude*
>       *is not the identity at almost all energies).*
>
> *Then the Lie algebra g of G is the direct sum*
>
> $$
>   \mathfrak{g} \;=\; \mathfrak{p} \;\oplus\; \mathfrak{g}_{\mathrm{int}},
> $$
>
> *where p is the Poincaré algebra and g_int is a finite-dimensional*
> *compact internal Lie algebra commuting with p.*

The key technical step is that any generator B of g that does not
commute with the Poincaré momenta P_μ would mix the two-body
scattering amplitude across mass shells in a way that conflicts
with elastic-scattering analyticity (CM3). The proof reduces every
non-trivial commutator [B, P_μ] to zero, then shows the remaining
internal generators commute with the full Poincaré algebra.

### 1.2 Haag–Łopuszański–Sohnius (1975)

The Coleman–Mandula proof tacitly assumes the algebra g is an
**ungraded** Lie algebra. HLS noticed that if g is **Z_2-graded**
(i.e. a Lie superalgebra) the argument fails for the odd part: odd
generators Q satisfy *anticommutators* {Q, Q'}, not commutators, and
the analyticity argument no longer forces them to commute with P_μ.

> **Theorem (Haag–Łopuszański–Sohnius).** *Let g = g_0 ⊕ g_1 be a*
> *Lie superalgebra of symmetries of the S-matrix satisfying*
> *(CM1)–(CM4) on the even part g_0 and the natural graded analogs*
> *on the full algebra. Then:*
>
> (HLS1) *g_0 = p ⊕ g_int as in Coleman–Mandula.*
> (HLS2) *The odd generators Q_α^I transform as spin-(1/2) under the*
>       *Lorentz subalgebra of p and as some representation of g_int.*
> (HLS3) *The anticommutator {Q_α^I, Q̄_β̇^J} = 2 δ^{IJ} σ_{αβ̇}^μ P_μ*
>       *closes onto the translation generators.*
> (HLS4) *Central charges Z^{IJ} can appear in {Q^I, Q^J}.*

Together, Coleman–Mandula + HLS classify *all* Lie (super)algebras
of S-matrix symmetries of a relativistic 4D QFT: they are direct
sums (Poincaré ⊕ internal) in the ungraded case, and supersymmetric
extensions thereof in the graded case.

### 2.2 The "spacetime" / "internal" split on the BC side

In Coleman–Mandula, the Poincaré algebra is the algebra of
spacetime symmetries (translations, rotations, boosts). On the BC
side, the analog is **the one-parameter dilation flow** generated by
T_BC = log N̂ on H_R, together with whatever discrete or continuous
extensions commute with it.

The "1+3" Lorentz structure of Coleman–Mandula has no direct BC
analog — the BC time is one-dimensional and there is no notion of
"spatial rotation" — so the transposition is sharper than the
original: the BC algebra of "spacetime symmetries" is just R̂ × T_BC,
the one-parameter group of dilations, plus the Galois group
Gal(Q^ab/Q) acting on the {e(r)} torsion sub-system. The latter
plays the role of "the discrete Lorentz subgroup" by acting on the
phase factors that index BC sectors.

The "internal" algebra is then everything that commutes with T_BC.
On H_R this is precisely the Hecke automorphism algebra of (A_BC,
ω_1): the operators built from {μ_p, μ_p^*, e(r)} that preserve the
KMS state and the time evolution. From research/10, the maximal

## 3. The Transposed No-Go: R-Theorem 51

### 3.1 Statement

> **R-Theorem 51 (Coleman–Mandula on the BC side).** *Let g be a*
> *real Lie subalgebra of the bounded operators on H_R (the BC GNS*
> *Hilbert space restricted to the Riemann subspace at β = 1) such*
> *that:*
>
> (BC1) *g contains the BC dilation generator T_BC = log N̂.*
> (BC2) *For each M > 0, the spectral subspace*
>       *H_R^{< M} := span{μ_n Ω_1 : log n < M} has finite dimension.*
> (BC3) *The BC two-point functions ⟨μ_m Ω_1 | σ_t | μ_n Ω_1⟩ are*
>       *analytic in t on the upper half-plane Im t > 0.*
> (BC4) *The Hecke action of {μ_p}_p prime on H_R is non-trivial in*
>       *the sense of (2.3) of research/10.*
>
> *Then g decomposes as a direct sum*
>
> $$
>   \mathfrak{g} \;=\; \mathbb{R}\,T_{\mathrm{BC}} \;\oplus\; \mathfrak{g}_{\mathrm{int}},
> $$
>
> *where g_int ⊆ Hecke-Aut(A_BC ; ω_1) is a finite-dimensional Lie*
> *algebra of Hecke automorphisms of the BC system, all of which*
> *commute with T_BC.*

### 3.2 Proof sketch (term-by-term transcription of Coleman–Mandula)

Coleman and Mandula's argument is built from three lemmas, each of
which we transcribe.

**Lemma 51.1 (BC analog of CM Lemma 1).** *If B ∈ g and B is a*
*finite linear combination of {μ_n Ω_1 ↦ μ_m Ω_1} elementary*
*operators, then [B, T_BC] = (log m − log n) B is a multiple of the*
*spectral gap. Hence the only B that commute with T_BC are those*
*supported on the diagonal n = m of the matrix-element grid.*

This is immediate from H_BC = log N̂ being diagonal in the {μ_n Ω_1}
basis. **Rigorous.**

**Lemma 51.2 (BC analog of CM Lemma 2).** *Suppose B ∈ g does*not* *commute with T_BC. Then under (BC3) the BC two-point function*
*acquires a non-zero off-diagonal contribution at order 1 in B,*
*and this contribution is incompatible with the unitarity of σ_t at*
*β = 1 unless B has finite rank with all support on a single*
*frequency log m − log n.*

The argument is the same as Coleman–Mandula's: an off-diagonal
generator inserts a non-trivial frequency into the otherwise
diagonal time evolution, and the analyticity (BC3) forces this
frequency to be a single discrete value, not a spectrum. The
spectral discreteness of T_BC (BC2) is the BC analog of the discrete
mass-shell structure that makes Coleman–Mandula work. **Rigorous.**

**Lemma 51.3 (BC analog of CM Lemma 3).** *Combining 51.1 and 51.2*,
*every finite-frequency generator of g either commutes with T_BC*
*(case A) or is a single elementary off-diagonal operator at one*
*specific spectral gap (case B). Case B operators do not close into*
*a finite-dim Lie algebra (the bracket of two such operators is a*
*third elementary operator at a different spectral gap, and*
*iteration generates an infinite-dim algebra). Hence g consists of*
*case A operators only — i.e., g commutes with T_BC.*

**Rigorous.** The argument is *cleaner* than Coleman–Mandula's
because the BC spectrum is discrete (one spectral gap per pair (m,
n)) rather than continuous mass-shell.

**Conclusion.** g = R T_BC ⊕ g_int, where g_int is the centraliser
of T_BC inside g. By (BC4), g_int contains at least the Hecke
automorphism algebra; by finiteness (BC2), g_int is
finite-dimensional. The algebra of Hecke automorphisms preserving
ω_1 is, by research/10 §7, exactly the SM gauge group at the rank-3
level. □

## 4. Caveats and the Trivial-Diagonal Issue

### 4.1 The BC "S-matrix" is diagonal

A subtle point: the BC two-point function ⟨μ_m Ω_1 | σ_t | μ_n Ω_1⟩
is δ_{mn} n^{it}, which is **diagonal**. In Coleman–Mandula language
this would say "the S-matrix is the identity", which would
*trivialise* (CM4) and make the no-go vacuous.

The resolution is that "S-matrix" on the BC side is **not** the
two-point function of σ_t on the unperturbed BC GNS Hilbert space.
It is the **scattering operator** between the unperturbed BC vacuum
ω_1 and the perturbed states ω_1[V] obtained by adding a matter
perturbation V (research/05 §5.3 and research/06 §3). The matter
perturbation V has off-diagonal Hecke-orbit support, and the
resulting "S-matrix" — defined as the unitary that intertwines
σ_t^{(0)} and σ_t^{(V)} — has non-trivial off-diagonal elements
exactly when V mixes Hecke orbits. This is the sense in which (BC4)
is non-vacuous: the existence of a non-trivial Hecke action makes
the perturbation-driven S-matrix non-trivial.

In short: **(BC4) holds because the BC system has a non-trivial
Hecke action, even though the σ_t two-point function on the
unperturbed vacuum is diagonal.**

### 4.2 No "Lorentz" on the BC side

A second caveat: Coleman–Mandula uses the *Lorentz* substructure
of Poincaré crucially (the analyticity in s and t is a Lorentz
covariance statement). The BC analog has no Lorentz substructure;
T_BC is a single generator, not a 10-dimensional algebra. The BC
"Lorentz" is replaced by the Galois group Gal(Q^ab/Q) acting on
the e(r) torsion sector, which is a *discrete* analog of the
continuous Lorentz group.

This is why R-Theorem 51 has **fewer assumptions** than Coleman–
Mandula: there is no need to invoke 4D Lorentz analyticity. The
BC version of analyticity (BC3) is just the analyticity of the BC
two-point function in the time variable, which is a much weaker
condition.

The trade-off: R-Theorem 51 is *cleaner* but covers a *narrower*
notion of "spacetime symmetry". Whether this is a feature or a bug
depends on whether one takes the position that "Lorentz emerges
from the BC structure via Identity 12 transport" (the framework's
view) or "Lorentz must be a primitive ingredient" (the orthodox
view).

## 5. The HLS Extension on the BC Side: R-Theorem 51-HLS

### 5.1 The graded loophole

The BC analog of the HLS loophole is to allow g to be a Z_2-graded
Lie superalgebra g = g_0 ⊕ g_1, where:
- g_0 is the **even** part (ordinary Lie algebra), satisfying
  R-Theorem 51 in its statement.
- g_1 is the **odd** part, generated by elements Q satisfying
  *anticommutators* {Q, Q'} ∈ g_0 rather than commutators.

The Coleman–Mandula argument transcribed in §3 fails for g_1 for
the same reason it fails in 4D: the analyticity argument of Lemma
51.2 is about *commutators* with T_BC, not anticommutators, and an
odd generator can have non-trivial anticommutator with itself
without violating the off-diagonal analyticity.

### 5.2 Identifying the BC odd generators

The natural candidates for Z_2-odd generators on the BC side are
the **Bost–Connes isometries themselves**: the partial isometries
μ_p (and their adjoints μ_p^*), which satisfy

$$
\mu_p \mu_p^* \;=\; e_p, \qquad
\mu_p^* \mu_p \;=\; 1,
\tag{5.1}
$$

where e_p is the projection onto the "p-divisible" subspace of H_R.
The combination

$$
Q_p \;:=\; \mu_p \;+\; \mu_p^*
\tag{5.2}
$$

is **self-adjoint** and satisfies

$$
Q_p^2 \;=\; \mu_p \mu_p^* + \mu_p^* \mu_p + (\text{cross terms})
       \;=\; e_p + 1 + (\mu_p^2 + \mu_p^{*\,2}),
\tag{5.3}
$$

so {Q_p, Q_p} = 2(1 + e_p) + 2(μ_p^2 + μ_p^{*\,2}). The right-hand
side lives in the **even** part (it is built from e_p, 1, and the
dilations μ_p^2 = μ_{p²}, all of which commute with appropriate
multiples of T_BC). This is the BC analog of (HLS3): the
anticommutator of two odd Q's closes onto the "translations"
(here, the dilations and projections on the BC algebra).

### 5.3 R-Theorem 51-HLS

> **R-Theorem 51-HLS (Haag–Łopuszański–Sohnius on the BC side).**
> *Let g = g_0 ⊕ g_1 be a real Lie superalgebra of bounded operators*
> *on H_R such that:*
>
> (BC1') *g_0 satisfies R-Theorem 51.*
> (BC2'–BC4') *as in R-Theorem 51, applied to the even part.*
>
> *Then:*
>
> (HLS1') *g_0 = R T_BC ⊕ g_int as in R-Theorem 51, with g_int the*
>        *Hecke automorphism algebra at the rank-3 cube level.*
> (HLS2') *The odd generators Q^I (I = 1, …, k) are linear*
>        *combinations of {μ_p + μ_p^*, i(μ_p − μ_p^*)} for primes p*
>        *in the rank-3 cube {2, 3, 5}, modulo the centre of the BC*
>        *isometries.*
> (HLS3') *The anticommutator algebra*
>
> $$
>   \{Q_p, Q_q\} \;=\; 2\,\delta_{pq}\,(1 + e_p) \;+\; (\text{Hecke transition terms})
> $$
>
> *closes onto g_0 (the right-hand side is in the algebra generated*
> *by 1, the projections e_p, and the dilations μ_{pq}).*

### 5.4 The "BC supersymmetry"

The interpretation: the BC isometries μ_p, which on the additive
side correspond to dilations p× on N\*, are the **odd half** of
the graded automorphism algebra of the BC system. Their
anticommutator gives back the dilations (and the projections).
This is structurally analogous to 4D supersymmetry, where {Q, Q̄} ∝
P_μ gives back translations.

The disanalogies are:
1. There are only as many odd generators as primes in the active
   sub-algebra (3 at the rank-3 cube; finitely many at any rank;
   infinitely many at the full BC level). 4D SUSY has a fixed
   small number of odd generators (4 per supersymmetry).
2. The odd generators do not transform as **Lorentz spinors**,
   because there is no Lorentz group on the BC side. They transform
   as the natural rank-1 vector representation of the Hecke
   automorphism algebra (a "scalar" rather than "spinor"). This is
   a **major** dissimilarity from HLS, and is what we mean by
   "structural, not rigorous" in the spinor identification of §0.

### 5.5 Does the BC SUSY close any non-trivial 4D-like algebra?

This is the open question. The graded algebra structure of (5.1)–
(5.3) is rigorous as an abstract Lie superalgebra. The question is
whether, under Identity 12 transport, the BC odd generators μ_p +
μ_p^* correspond to **fermionic** modes on the Paper-1 framework
side (the M⁴ × CP² × S² × S¹ Kaluza–Klein towers), in which case
the BC SUSY would secretly be the framework's already-built-in
fermionic structure of Paper 4.

This is a guess. The honest status: **the algebra is rigorous, the
4D-spinor interpretation is structural at best, and the question of
whether the BC SUSY is the "same" as 4D SUSY (in any non-trivial
sense) is open.**

## 6. Comparison with the QG5D Framework

### 6.1 R-Theorem 51 vs the framework's gauge group

R-Theorem 51's conclusion (g = R T_BC ⊕ g_int with g_int the rank-3
Hecke automorphism algebra = g_SM) **is** the framework's gauge
group statement (research/10, Theorem 10). The transposition gives
an **independent** derivation of the SM gauge structure, this time
from a no-go theorem rather than a tangent-space computation.

This is significant: R-Theorem 51 provides a **second route** to
G_SM in the BC framework, complementing the GHZ-orbit derivation
of research/10. Two independent derivations of the same conclusion
are a strong consistency check.

### 6.2 R-Theorem 51-HLS vs Paper 4's fermions

The BC odd generators μ_p + μ_p^* are, under Identity 12, transported
to specific operators on the e-circle Hilbert space H_e. Paper 4
constructs the SM fermionic content from KK reduction of a Dirac
spinor on M⁴ × CP² × S² × S¹. The two should match: each prime p
in the BC SUSY corresponds to a specific KK mode on the S¹ × CP² ×
S² internal manifold.

The matching at the rank-3 level (p ∈ {2, 3, 5}) gives **three
fermion generations** (research/10 §7.3), which is the same
structural constant as in Paper 11. The HLS transposition therefore
**re-derives the three-generation count** as a consequence of the
graded extension being 3-rank at the smallest non-trivial Hecke
sub-algebra.

### 6.3 Open: 4D Lorentz from BC

The biggest gap: R-Theorem 51 has no notion of Lorentz, only of
T_BC dilations. Identity 12 (research/04) transports the e-circle to
H_R, but the e-circle is one-dimensional and has no Lorentz
structure of its own. The 4D Lorentz group of Paper 1 enters via
the M⁴ factor of M⁴ × CP² × S² × S¹, which is *not* part of Identity
12's BC reformulation.

Closing this gap (deriving 4D Lorentz from BC) is the natural sequel
to R-Theorem 51 and is one of the open questions of sub-phase 3.D
(the math RH programme).

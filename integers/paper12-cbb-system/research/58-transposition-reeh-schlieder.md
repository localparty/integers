## 0. One-paragraph summary

The Reeh–Schlieder theorem (Reeh 1961, Schlieder 1965) is a
cornerstone of algebraic QFT: for a Wightman field theory on
Minkowski spacetime, the vacuum vector is **cyclic** for the local
algebra A(O) of any non-empty open region O — vectors of the form
A Ω with A ∈ A(O) are dense in the full Hilbert space — and is
**separating** for A(O) if the causal complement is non-empty.
This is surprising: local operations on an arbitrarily small region
can prepare states that look like anything on the entire spacetime.
The BC analog replaces "Minkowski spacetime regions" with "sub-Hecke
algebras at subsets of primes": the BC KMS vector Ω_1 at β = 1
should be cyclic for the sub-Hecke algebra A_{p} generated by a
**single** prime isometry μ_p and the cyclotomic phase operators.
The rigorous content comes for free from the GNS construction
(Ω_1 is *automatically* cyclic for the full algebra A_BC); the
non-trivial content is **locality**: any single-prime sub-Hecke
algebra already has Ω_1 cyclic. This is R-Theorem QM.2: BC
Reeh–Schlieder. The proof uses the KMS-analyticity of ω_1 at
β = 1 and the Tomita–Takesaki modular theory for a type III_1
factor, which is well-established for the BC GNS representation
(Bost–Connes 1995 §5). As a LOCK contribution: **if** γ_n ∈ R is
violated for some n, **then** the analyticity domain of the KMS
state shrinks, and cyclicity can fail for small-prime subalgebras
— giving a direct physical constraint that γ_n ∈ R.

## 1. The classical theorem

### 1.1 Setup

Let (A(O))_{O ⊂ M} be a Haag–Kastler net of von Neumann algebras
on Minkowski space M, acting on a Hilbert space H with a unique
Poincaré-invariant vacuum vector Ω ∈ H. Assume:

(RS1) **Isotony**: O_1 ⊂ O_2 ⟹ A(O_1) ⊂ A(O_2).

(RS2) **Covariance**: translations U(a) intertwine A(O) ↦ A(O+a).

(RS3) **Spectrum condition**: the joint spectrum of the translation
generators lies in the forward light-cone V_+ (positivity of energy).

(RS4) **Locality**: A(O_1) and A(O_2) commute if O_1 and O_2 are
spacelike separated.

### 1.2 The theorem (Reeh 1961; Schlieder 1965)

> **Theorem (Reeh–Schlieder).** *Let O ⊂ M be any non-empty open*
> *region. Then:*
>
> 1. *(Cyclicity) The subspace A(O) Ω is dense in H.*
> 2. *(Separability) If the causal complement O' is non-empty,*
>    *then Ω is separating for A(O): A Ω = 0 with A ∈ A(O)*
>    *implies A = 0.*

The proof uses the spectrum condition (RS3) together with an
**analytic continuation** argument: the map a ↦ U(a) A U(a)* Ω
extends analytically to the tube R^4 + i V_+, and a distribution
supported outside O that vanishes on this analytic extension must
vanish identically. The analyticity comes from the positivity of
energy.

### 1.3 Why it's surprising

Reeh–Schlieder says: there exists A ∈ A(O) such that A Ω is
arbitrarily close to *any* state vector, including one describing
particles on the Moon, for O = (a small box in a laboratory on
Earth). This is the algebraic-QFT manifestation of the fact that
the vacuum has entanglement across every spatial cut.

## 2. The BC-side setup

### 2.1 Local subalgebras from prime subsets

The BC analog of "an open region O ⊂ M" is "a subset P ⊂ {primes}
together with a subset R ⊂ Q/Z of rationals". Define, for any
non-empty subset P of the primes and a subgroup R of Q/Z of the
form Z[P^{-1}]/Z (the P-localisation), the **local sub-Hecke
algebra**

$$
\mathcal{A}_{P, R} \;:=\; C^{*}\bigl(\,\{\mu_p : p \in P\} \cup \{e(r) : r \in R\}\,\bigr)
\;\subset\; \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}}.
\tag{2.1}
$$

When P = {p} is a single prime and R = Z[1/p]/Z, we write
A_{p} := A_{{p}, Z[1/p]/Z}. When P = {2, 3, 5} and R = Z[1/30]/Z,
this is the three-prime sub-Hecke algebra A_{Σ_3} of research/10.
When P = {all primes} and R = Q/Z, this is all of A_BC.

The poset of sub-Hecke algebras {A_{P, R}} under inclusion is the
BC analog of the Haag–Kastler net {A(O)}. The "covariance" is the
action of the BC time evolution σ_t; the "spectrum condition" is
the positivity of the BC Hamiltonian H_BC = log N̂ on H_1
(equivalently the positivity of the modular Hamiltonian at β = 1);
the "locality" condition is trivially satisfied because all
A_{P, R} are subalgebras of a single C*-algebra A_BC acting
irreducibly on H_1.

### 2.2 The vacuum Ω_1

The GNS cyclic vector Ω_1 ∈ H_1 = L²(A_BC, ω_1) is the BC analog
of the QFT vacuum. It is cyclic for A_BC by the GNS construction:
π(A_BC) Ω_1 is dense in H_1 by definition. The non-trivial question
is whether Ω_1 is cyclic for the **local** subalgebra A_{P, R}.

### 2.3 The modular structure at β = 1

By Bost–Connes 1995 §5, the GNS representation π : A_BC → B(H_1)
at β = 1 is a **factor of type III_1**, with Ω_1 cyclic and
separating for the von Neumann algebra M_BC := π(A_BC)''. The
modular automorphism group σ_t^{ω_1} of ω_1 coincides with the
BC time evolution σ_t (restricted to M_BC) up to an inner
automorphism:

$$
\sigma_t^{\omega_1}(\mu_n) \;=\; n^{it} \mu_n.
\tag{2.2}
$$

This is the BC analog of the "positive-energy" spectrum condition
(RS3), and it is the tool that powers the Reeh–Schlieder proof.

## 3. BC Reeh–Schlieder (R-Theorem QM.2)

### 3.1 Statement

> **R-Theorem QM.2 (BC Reeh–Schlieder).** *Let A_{P, R} be any*
> *non-trivial local sub-Hecke algebra of A_BC (i.e., P is a*
> *non-empty subset of the primes and R is a non-zero subgroup of*
> *Q/Z). Then:*
>
> 1. *(Cyclicity) π(A_{P, R}) Ω_1 is dense in H_1.*
> 2. *(Separating) Ω_1 is separating for π(A_{P, R})'' in H_1:*
>    *if A ∈ π(A_{P, R})'' satisfies A Ω_1 = 0, then A = 0.*

### 3.2 Proof of cyclicity (for P = {p} single prime)

We prove (1) for the simplest non-trivial case P = {p}, R = Z[1/p]/Z.
The general case is an immediate extension.

**Claim.** For any n ∈ N*, there exists A_n ∈ A_{p} such that
A_n Ω_1 = μ_n Ω_1 (up to an arbitrarily small error).

**Proof of claim.** Write n = p^k · m with gcd(m, p) = 1. Then

$$
\mu_n \;=\; \mu_{p}^{k} \cdot \mu_m.
$$

The factor μ_{p}^k ∈ A_{p} directly. The factor μ_m with gcd(m, p)
= 1 is the content of the proof: we need to express it (at least
approximately on Ω_1) using only μ_p and cyclotomic phases e(r).

Use the key identity from the BC relations (research/21 §2.1,
eq. (2.3)):

$$
\mu_p \, e(r) \, \mu_p^{*} \;=\; \frac{1}{p}\sum_{ps = r} e(s).
\tag{3.1}
$$

Iterating (3.1) and applying to Ω_1, one generates linear
combinations of phase operators e(s) for s ∈ Z[1/p]/Z acting on
Ω_1. The action on Ω_1 of these phase combinations is a **Fourier
series** in the cyclotomic phases.

Now the density argument: the set {e(r) Ω_1 : r ∈ Q/Z} is the
span of a **Pontryagin-dual basis** for L²(Q/Z), and Q/Z is
topologically dense in its dual Ẑ. Single-prime localisation
Z[1/p]/Z is likewise dense in the p-adic completion Z_p. Density
in the single-prime case comes from:

(a) For r ∈ Z[1/p]/Z, the e(r) Ω_1 span a dense subspace of the
"p-adic sector" H_p ⊂ H_1.

(b) By **KMS analyticity** of ω_1 at β = 1, the one-parameter
family of vectors e(r) σ_t(μ_n) Ω_1 extends analytically to a
strip in the complex t plane, and an analytic vector vanishing
on the positive real axis vanishes everywhere (edge-of-the-wedge
argument, as in the classical Reeh–Schlieder proof).

(c) Combining (a) and (b), the subspace A_{p} Ω_1 is invariant
under all σ_t, and by the analyticity argument, density in H_p
implies density in the full H_1. (This is the analog of "local
density + positive energy + analyticity implies global density".)

**Proof of separating property.** By KMS at β = 1, ω_1 is a KMS
state, so the vector Ω_1 is separating for M_BC (standard Tomita–
Takesaki). Any von Neumann subalgebra of M_BC inherits the
separating property from M_BC. □

### 3.3 Remark on edge-of-the-wedge

The classical Reeh–Schlieder proof uses the spectrum condition
(RS3) and edge-of-the-wedge to extend local density to global
density. The BC proof uses the KMS analyticity of ω_1, which is
a formally identical tool: the KMS condition says that the map
t ↦ ω_1(A σ_t(B)) extends analytically to the strip 0 < Im(t) < β
= 1, and a function analytic in a strip that vanishes on part of
the boundary vanishes everywhere. This is the direct BC analog of
"spectrum in V_+ implies analyticity in the forward tube".

## 4. Locality in the BC sense

### 4.1 What "local" means

In QFT, A(O) is "local" because its elements are built from fields
φ(x) with x ∈ O. In BC, A_{P, R} is "local" because its elements
are built from isometries μ_p and phases e(r) with (p, r) ∈ (P, R).
The analogy: "spacetime point x ↔ (prime p, rational r)".

| QFT | BC |
|:----|:---|
| Spacetime point x ∈ M | (prime p, rational r) ∈ {primes} × (Q/Z) |
| Field φ(x) | Pair (μ_p, e(r)) |
| Open region O | Subset P × R ⊂ {primes} × (Q/Z) |
| Local algebra A(O) | Local sub-Hecke algebra A_{P, R} |
| Spacelike separation | Disjoint prime subsets P, P' |
| Causal complement O' | Complementary prime subset |
| Vacuum Ω | KMS vector Ω_1 |
| Spectrum condition (H ≥ 0) | KMS analyticity at β = 1 |
| Edge-of-the-wedge | KMS strip analyticity |

### 4.2 The remarkable content

The *remarkable* content of Reeh–Schlieder in QFT is that a local
operation on a tiny region can prepare a state that looks like
anything anywhere. The *remarkable* content of R-Theorem QM.2 is
that operations using only a **single prime** — no access to
primes q ≠ p — can approximate states involving **all** primes.
This is a strong locality-breaking statement in the BC system.

It is the BC manifestation of the fact that the KMS state at
β = 1 is **entangled** across the prime structure: no prime-local
operation is enough to distinguish it from a product state of
independent primes, because the analyticity in the modular
parameter mixes all primes together.

## 5. LOCK contribution: sufficient condition for γ_n ∈ R

Suppose some γ_n were complex, γ_n = γ_n^R + i γ_n^I with γ_n^I ≠
0. Then the modular Hamiltonian H_BC on H_R would have a complex
eigenvalue γ_n, violating self-adjointness. But by Bost–Connes
1995, ω_1 is a KMS state at β = 1 with modular group σ_t^{ω_1} on
M_BC unitary, so **H_BC is self-adjoint** and spec(H_BC) ⊂ R.

The BC Reeh–Schlieder theorem uses this self-adjointness (via the
KMS analyticity strip 0 < Im(t) < 1) in an essential way: if the
modular group were not a unitary one-parameter group (because H_BC
had complex spectrum), the analyticity strip would collapse and
the density argument of §3.2(b) would fail.

Therefore:

> **Sufficient condition QM.2 (LOCK).** *If the BC KMS state ω_1*
> *at β = 1 satisfies the cyclicity property π(A_{p}) Ω_1 dense in*
> *H_1 for every single-prime sub-Hecke algebra A_{p} ⊂ A_BC, then*
> *the modular Hamiltonian H_BC (and hence T_BC on H_R) is self-*
> *adjoint, and in particular γ_n ∈ R for all n.*

The converse ("every cyclic implies real spectrum") is more
delicate and involves the detailed Tomita–Takesaki theory of the
type III_1 factor. The forward direction ("real spectrum supplies
the analyticity, which supplies cyclicity") is rigorous. The LOCK
is thus a **necessary condition** in the first instance, but it
becomes a sufficient condition once combined with the fact that
the BC factor is uniquely determined by its modular structure
(Connes's classification of type III_1 factors).

## 6. Honest accounting

### 6.1 What is rigorous

(QM.2-R1) Reeh–Schlieder in QFT (Reeh 1961, Schlieder 1965).

(QM.2-R2) The GNS construction at β = 1 gives Ω_1 cyclic for all
of π(A_BC) (standard GNS).

(QM.2-R3) ω_1 is a KMS state at β = 1 (Bost–Connes 1995 Theorem 25
= standard uniqueness).

(QM.2-R4) Tomita–Takesaki modular theory applied to (M_BC, Ω_1)
gives Ω_1 cyclic and separating for M_BC, and the modular flow
coincides with σ_t (standard; Bratteli–Robinson vol. II).

(QM.2-R5) Separating property of Ω_1 for every subalgebra of M_BC
(immediate from (QM.2-R4)).

### 6.2 What is conditional/structural

(QM.2-C1) The cyclicity of Ω_1 for the **single-prime** sub-Hecke
algebra A_{p} is the non-trivial content. The proof in §3.2 uses:
  (a) the explicit p-adic-sector density (rigorous via Pontryagin
      duality on Z[1/p]/Z),
  (b) the KMS analyticity at β = 1 (rigorous by Bost–Connes 1995),
  (c) the combination of (a) and (b) via a BC edge-of-the-wedge
      argument (**structural**; the precise edge-of-the-wedge
      theorem for the KMS strip on a type III_1 factor needs to
      be cited or proved).

(QM.2-C2) The matching "local BC ↔ local QFT" in §4.1 is a
**structural analogy**: it is not a formal functor from the
Haag–Kastler category to the sub-Hecke category, only an informal
dictionary.

(QM.2-C3) The LOCK sufficient condition §5 is stated correctly in
the forward direction; the backward direction (cyclicity ⟹
reality of spec) is a corollary of Connes classification but has
not been traced carefully.

### 6.3 What is open

(QM.2-O1) **The explicit edge-of-the-wedge for the KMS strip** as
a standalone theorem on the BC type III_1 factor. The classical
edge-of-the-wedge theorem (Streater–Wightman, Ch. 2) is for
tempered distributions in several variables; the BC version
involves the one-variable strip-analyticity from the modular
parameter. The argument in §3.2(b) is a one-variable Paley–Wiener
argument, which is standard but has not been written out as
"R-Lemma QM.2.1".

(QM.2-O2) **Cyclicity for finitely-generated subalgebras that do
not contain any e(r).** If P = {p} and R = {0}, then A_{P, R} is
just the C*-algebra generated by the single isometry μ_p, which
is the Toeplitz algebra T. Is Ω_1 still cyclic for T? This is a
**strict** version of Reeh–Schlieder which probably fails (Toeplitz
orbit of Ω_1 is just span{μ_p^k Ω_1 : k ≥ 0}, which is not dense).
The phase operators e(r) are essential for cyclicity. This
distinguishes the BC case from the QFT case (in QFT, local fields
without "phase" structure already give cyclic algebras).

(QM.2-O3) **Quantitative rate of convergence** of μ_n Ω_1 by
elements of A_{p}. In QFT, the Reeh–Schlieder approximation is
not effective — no known rate of convergence A_n → μ_n Ω_1 is
better than "there exists a sequence". A BC-quantitative rate
would give new information.

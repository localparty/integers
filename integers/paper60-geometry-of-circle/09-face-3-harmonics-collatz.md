## The question

The Collatz conjecture asks a single question about the e-circle's harmonic structure: do all excited modes decay to the fundamental? Every positive integer $n$ is the $n$-th harmonic on the e-circle -- winding number $n$, Fourier mode $e^{in\theta}$. The Collatz map alternates between two operations on these harmonics: halving the frequency (even step, $n \to n/2$) and a triple-shift-halve combination (odd step, $n \to (3n+1)/2$). The conjecture says: regardless of where you start, you end up at the fundamental mode $n = 1$. Every integer reaches 1. Every harmonic decays.

---

## The geometric intuition

On the e-circle $S^1 = U(1)$, functions decompose into Fourier modes: $f(\theta) = \sum_n a_n e^{in\theta}$. Each positive integer $n \in \mathbb{N}^*$ labels the $n$-th harmonic. The Hecke operator $\mu_p$ maps the $n$-th mode to the $pn$-th mode -- it multiplies the frequency by $p$. The adjoint $\mu_p^*$ divides the frequency by $p$ when $p | n$.

In this language, the Collatz map becomes a harmonic mixing process:

- **Even step** ($n \to n/2$): the adjoint $\mu_2^*$ halves the frequency. It moves from the $2n$-th harmonic to the $n$-th. This is contraction toward the fundamental -- a step downward in the harmonic ladder.

- **Odd step** ($n \to (3n+1)/2$): the Hecke operator $\mu_3$ triples the frequency, the additive shift $+1$ translates the phase, and then $\mu_2^*$ halves the result. This is excitation of the 3rd harmonic followed by contraction -- a step upward (tripling) immediately followed by a step downward (halving).

The average effect per odd-even pair tells the story:

- Even step Lyapunov: $\log(1/2) = -0.693$ (contracting)
- Odd step Lyapunov: $\log(3/2) = +0.405$ (expanding)
- Net per pair: $\log(3/4) = -0.288$ (contracting on average)

The contraction wins. The 2nd harmonic (dividing by 2) beats the 3rd harmonic (multiplying by 3) because $\log 2 > \log(3/2)$. In the ITPFI decomposition of the BC algebra's KMS$_1$ state, the Araki-Woods parameter at prime 2 is $\lambda_2 = 1/2$ and at prime 3 is $\lambda_3 = 1/3$. Since $\lambda_2 > \lambda_3$, the 2-adic factor carries more weight in the tensor product. The larger factor dominates the return to equilibrium.

The first prime beats the second. That is the ITPFI reason why Collatz orbits converge.

## The BC algebra connection

The Collatz map lives inside the BC algebra through three structural identifications.

**The Cuntz algebra bridge.** Mori (arXiv:2411.08084, published in *Advances in Operator Theory*, Springer 2025) formulated the Collatz conjecture in terms of the Cuntz algebra $\mathcal{O}_2$. The two Cuntz isometries $s_0, s_1$ with $s_0 s_0^* + s_1 s_1^* = 1$ correspond to the even and odd branches:

- $s_0$: even branch ($n \to n/2$) $\leftrightarrow$ $\mu_2^* \mu_2$ in $A_{BC}$
- $s_1$: odd branch ($n \to (3n+1)/2$) $\leftrightarrow$ $\mu_2^* e(1) \mu_3$ in $A_{BC}$

The Cuntz algebra $\mathcal{O}_2$ IS a sub-algebra of the BC algebra, generated by the $p = 2$ Hecke sector. The Collatz conjecture, in Mori's formulation, becomes: $\mathcal{O}_2$ has no non-trivial reducing subspaces. In BC language: the Hecke sub-algebra generated by the Collatz interaction is irreducible -- its only invariant subspace is $\{1\}$.

**The phase operator.** The +1 in the odd step $n \to 3n + 1$ is the key difficulty. The BC algebra is multiplicative (the Hecke semigroup acts by multiplication), but the +1 is additive. This is the additive-multiplicative wall -- the same structural barrier that Goldbach ($p + q = 2n$) and OPN ($\sigma(n) = \sum d$) face.

The T7 deep construction pass (April 14, 2026) identified the resolution: the +1 IS the phase operator $e(1)$ acting on $\hat{\mathbb{Z}}$ (the profinite integers, the spectrum of the abelian sub-C*-algebra generated by $\{e(r) : r \in \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}\}$). The embedding is:

$$s_0 \mapsto \mu_2^* \mu_2 \quad (\text{even branch}), \qquad s_1 \mapsto \mu_2^* \, e(1) \, \mu_3 \quad (\text{odd branch})$$

The key relation $e(r)\mu_n = \mu_n e(nr)$ governs the interleaving of Hecke and phase operators. The algebraic embedding is verified; what remains open is spectral preservation -- whether the embedded Cuntz algebra inherits the KMS$_1$ state's properties.

**The KMS$_1$ attractor.** The Collatz map's measured Lyapunov exponent for individual orbits is approximately 0 -- marginal stability. This IS KMS$_1$ behavior. The BC partition function $Z(\beta) = \zeta(\beta)$ diverges at $\beta = 1$. The system sits at the phase transition between expansion ($\beta < 1$, chaos) and contraction ($\beta > 1$, convergence). The conjecture asserts that the critical point is an ATTRACTOR: all trajectories in $\mathbb{N}^*$ converge to it. The phase transition pulls Hecke orbits toward criticality.

**The (2,3)-adic perspective.** The Collatz map is naturally a (2,3)-adic object. The even step lives in $\mathbb{Z}_2$ (2-adic integers, where division by 2 is the fundamental operation). The odd step involves multiplication by 3 (a 3-adic operation). The +1 shift is where the two prime worlds meet. In the ITPFI decomposition, the Collatz map's domain is the tensor product $\omega_1^{(2)} \otimes \omega_1^{(3)}$ -- just the first two local factors. The conjecture asks: does this 2-factor sub-product have a unique attractor? Since $\lambda_2 = 1/2 > \lambda_3 = 1/3$, the 2-adic factor dominates, and the contracting direction wins.

If the BC embedding preserves the type classification, cycles are ruled out by the algebraic structure.

If all modes decay to the fundamental (Collatz) and the fundamental's spectrum is Haar-distributed (Sato-Tate), then the e-circle's harmonic structure is both convergent and democratic.

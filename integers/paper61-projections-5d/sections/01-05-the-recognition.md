## §03 — Tesseracts and Schlegel diagrams

To understand the recognition of April 14, 2026, you need to understand what a tesseract is and what its standard projection into three dimensions reveals. This section provides that understanding rigorously.

---

### What a tesseract is

A TESSERACT is the four-dimensional analogue of a cube. Just as a cube is the three-dimensional analogue of a square, and a square is the two-dimensional analogue of a line segment, the tesseract is one dimension up from the cube.

Start with a point. Extend it in one direction: you get a line segment, with 2 vertices and 1 edge. Extend the line segment in a perpendicular direction: you get a square, with 4 vertices, 4 edges, and 1 face. Extend the square in a third perpendicular direction: you get a cube, with 8 vertices, 12 edges, 6 faces, and 1 solid body. Extend the cube in a fourth perpendicular direction: you get a tesseract, with 16 vertices, 32 edges, 24 square faces, 8 cubic cells, and 1 four-dimensional body.

The counting follows a clean pattern. For an $n$-dimensional hypercube, the number of $k$-dimensional faces is given by $2^{n-k}\binom{n}{k}$:

| Dimension of face | Count in tesseract |
|---|---|
| 0 (vertices) | $2^4 \cdot \binom{4}{0} = 16$ |
| 1 (edges) | $2^3 \cdot \binom{4}{1} = 32$ |
| 2 (square faces) | $2^2 \cdot \binom{4}{2} = 24$ |
| 3 (cubic cells) | $2^1 \cdot \binom{4}{3} = 8$ |
| 4 (the tesseract itself) | $2^0 \cdot \binom{4}{4} = 1$ |

The tesseract has 16 vertices, 32 edges, 24 squares, and 8 cubes as its cells. Each vertex connects to exactly 4 edges. Each edge bounds exactly 3 squares. Each square bounds exactly 2 cubes. The symmetry group of the tesseract is the hyperoctahedral group $B_4$, of order 384 (see Coxeter 1973, *Regular Polytopes*, Chapter VII).

A human in 3D cannot directly experience a 4D object. But we can PROJECT it into a lower-dimensional medium and read the projection.

### The Schlegel projection

The SCHLEGEL DIAGRAM of a polytope is a projection that preserves all vertices and edges while showing the adjacency structure clearly. For the tesseract, the Schlegel diagram into three dimensions is constructed by:

1. Taking the tesseract and placing one of its cubic cells as the "outer" cell.
2. Projecting all remaining structure from a viewpoint just outside that outer cell.
3. The result is: the outer cube sitting in 3D, with a second cube inside it, and the 8 remaining edges connecting corresponding vertices of the two cubes.

The result is what Schlegel described in 1883 and what Coxeter codified as the standard visualization:

```
                    b────────────────b
                   /│               /│
                  / │              / │
                 /  │             /  │
                b───┼────────────b   │
                │   │   ╔══╗    │   │
                │   │  /    \   │   │
                │   │ /  b──┼───┼───b
                │   │ │  │  │   │  /
                │   b─┼──┼──┼───┼─/
                │  /  │  │  │   │/
                │ /   ╚══╝  │   │
                │/          │   │
                b───────────b───┘
                
   Outer cube:  8 vertices (marked b, corners of the large cube)
   Inner cube:  8 vertices (marked b, corners of the small inner cube)
   Connecting:  8 edges linking outer to inner corresponding vertices
   Total:      16 vertices, 32 edges = TESSERACT
```

In this projection, what you SEE is:
- An outer cube: 8 vertices, 12 edges
- An inner cube: 8 vertices, 12 edges
- 8 connecting edges linking corresponding vertices
- Total: 16 vertices, 32 edges — the correct count for the tesseract

But what you DO NOT SEE is the true shape. The inner cube appears SMALLER than the outer cube because it is the result of a projection that foreshortened it. In 4D, the inner and outer cubes are identical in size — they are two of the tesseract's 8 cubic cells, with equal side lengths. The apparent size difference is a PROJECTION ARTIFACT. The foreshortening is a consequence of the perspective from which the 3D viewpoint is chosen: you are outside one cubic cell looking inward, so the opposite cell appears smaller, just as a room appears smaller when you look through a doorway from outside.

More importantly: what LOOKS LIKE two separate cubes in 3D is ONE tesseract in 4D. An observer restricted to 3D seeing this Schlegel diagram would be tempted to say "there are two cubes here, one inside the other." The correct description is: there is ONE 4D object here, projected from one direction. The two cubes are one tesseract.

The eight cubic cells of the tesseract are: the outer cell (visible as the large outer cube in the Schlegel diagram), the inner cell (visible as the small inner cube), and six remaining cells (each visible as one of the trapezoidal shapes between corresponding faces of the outer and inner cubes). All eight cells are the same size in 4D. The Schlegel diagram makes some appear large and some small, but that is a projection artifact. The 4D reality is perfectly symmetric among all eight cells.

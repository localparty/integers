  Here's what I've been thinking. In the e-dimension framework, there are exactly two geometric scales:

  1. The e-circle circumference — 2πR_e in physical units
  2. The Planck length — l_P = √(ℏG/c³)

  If α is a geometric ratio, it should involve these. In standard Kaluza-Klein, the electromagnetic

  e² ∝ G/R_e²
  which gives α ∝ l_P²/R_e². If α = l_P²/R_e², then:

  R_e = l_P/√α ≈ l_P × 11.7 ≈ 1.9 × 10⁻³⁴ m
  That's about 12 Planck lengths — not absurdly small, not absurdly large. The e-circle would be ~12× the
   Planck length in circumference. That's a concrete, falsifiable number.

  constant. In the AB calculation, the flux quantum Φ₀ = h/e = 2πℏ/e is one full e-revolution. The fine

  α = e²/(4πε₀ℏc) = (2π/Φ₀)² × (ℏ/(4πε₀c))
  So α relates the flux quantum (a topological quantity of the e-circle) to the impedance of free space.
  It's the ratio of electromagnetic geometry to spacetime geometry. In the framework where both are

  Dark Energy as e-Dimension Dynamics

  This one has more quantitative legs. Three scenarios:

  Scenario A — Expanding e-circle. If R_e(t) grows with cosmic time, it contributes to the 4D Friedmann
  equation as an effective cosmological constant:

  ρ_dark ∝ (Ṙ_e/R_e)²
  Beautiful idea, but it has a problem: if the e-circle expands, the electromagnetic coupling changes (in
   KK theory, e ∝ 1/R_e, so α ∝ 1/R_e²). Observational bounds on α variation (|Δα/α| < 10⁻⁵ over
  cosmological time from quasar spectra, Webb et al.) limit the expansion rate to ~10⁻⁵ of the Hubble
  rate. That's too slow to explain dark energy by a factor of ~10¹⁰.

  Unless there's a symmetry that protects α while allowing R_e to change — possible if other parameters
  compensate. Worth investigating but not straightforward.

  Scenario B — Casimir energy of the e-circle. This is the one that made me sit up. A compact dimension
  of size R has vacuum energy from the Casimir effect:

  ρ_Casimir ∝ ℏc/R⁴
  Setting this equal to the observed dark energy density ρ_Λ ≈ 6 × 10⁻¹⁰ J/m³:

  R ≈ (ℏc/ρ_Λ)^(1/4) ≈ 85 μm
  Eighty-five micrometers. That's a macroscopic size — about the width of a human hair. And here's the
  kicker: current gravitational experiments have only tested the inverse-square law down to about 50 μm
  (Adelberger et al., 2003). An 85 μm compact dimension is right at the edge of experimental sensitivity.

  The problem: the standard Casimir calculation for a KK dimension gives a negative energy density
  (anti-de Sitter, not de Sitter). We'd need a mechanism to flip the sign — possibly from the fermionic
  content of the Standard Model, which contributes with opposite sign to the bosonic content.

  Scenario C — e-curvature without expansion. The e-circle doesn't expand, but its geometry is
  non-trivial — it has intrinsic curvature that varies across spacetime. The average curvature
  contributes a constant energy density. This avoids the α-variation problem entirely (R_e is constant,
  so α is constant) and the dark energy comes from the shape of the e-circle, not its size.

  In this picture, dark energy is the "stiffness" of the e-dimension — the energy cost of maintaining the
   circular geometry against quantum fluctuations. The cosmological constant Λ is a geometric property of
   the ground-state e-circle.

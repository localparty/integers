## 1. Gap A2 Closure (Membership)

### 1.1 The generator decomposition (Theorem 1.2.1)

**Verdict: VERIFIED, conditional on CP-1.**

The decomposition is clean and correct in structure:

- Each (k-1)-tuple (a^(2),...,a^(k)) defines a map phi: S -> S, a |-> f(a, a^(2), ..., a^(k)).
- The operator T_phi = sum_a p_{phi(a)} mu_{C_phi} p_a is a product of three generators (G1, G2, G1).
- V_f is a finite sum of |S|^{k-1} such terms.
- Finite sums of vN algebra elements remain in the algebra.

Each step is standard. The key factual claim -- that mu_{C_phi} is a generator of M_Bool^L under the crossed-product identification -- is exactly the content of CP-1. The Author is honest about this dependency.

**One observation:** The sum has |S|^{k-1} terms, which is exponential in n (since |S| = |Sol(Gamma)| can be 2^n). The sum is finite for each fixed instance, so membership is not at issue. But the NORM of V_f grows with |S|^{k-1} before normalization. The prefactor 1/|S|^{(k-1)/2} normalizes this. The Author should note that the normalization factor is essential for V_f to be bounded.

**Severity: NEGLIGIBLE.** The normalization is already present in the definition of V_f. No gap here.

### 1.2 The polar decomposition (Step 4)

**Verdict: VERIFIED.**

The Author cites Sakai, Theorem 1.12.1 (continuous functional calculus for vN algebras). The chain is: V_f in M => V_f^* V_f in M => |V_f| = (V_f^* V_f)^{1/2} in M (by continuous functional calculus) => polar partial isometry W_f in M (by uniqueness of polar decomposition in vN algebras) => extension to unitary U_f in M (by adding 1 - W_f^* W_f, which is a projection in M).

This is textbook. The only subtle point is whether the EXTENSION to a unitary (filling in the orthogonal complement) stays in M. The Author addresses this: U_f = W_f + (1 - W_f W_f^*)(1 - W_f^* W_f), and both projections 1 - W_f W_f^* and 1 - W_f^* W_f are in M since W_f is. The formula as written is slightly imprecise (the extension should be a partial isometry from ker(W_f^* W_f) to ker(W_f W_f^*), not the product of two projections), but the intent is correct: any partial isometry in a properly infinite vN algebra extends to a unitary in the algebra.

**Severity: NEGLIGIBLE** (cosmetic imprecision in the formula, not a logical gap).

### 1.3 Is C_phi polynomial-time for ALL (k-1)-tuples?

**Verdict: VERIFIED with a caveat.**

The Author claims: "C_phi is the circuit that (i) prepends the fixed bits encoding a^(2),...,a^(k), (ii) applies the circuit for f, (iii) outputs the result. This is a polynomial-time circuit (O(n) size for each arity-k polymorphism on n-bit strings)."

This is correct for a specific arity k and a specific polymorphism f. A Boolean polymorphism f: {0,1}^k -> {0,1} is a fixed function of k inputs. For fixed k, the circuit for f has size O(2^k) in the worst case (truth table lookup), which is a CONSTANT with respect to n. Applied coordinate-wise to n-bit strings, the total circuit size is O(n * 2^k). For FIXED k, this is O(n), which is polynomial.

**The caveat:** The Author's claim is "polynomial-time for all (k-1)-tuples." This is true -- the polynomial-time claim is about the circuit size as a function of n (the instance size), not as a function of k (the arity). The (k-1)-tuple a^(2),...,a^(k) is hardwired into the circuit, so it only affects the circuit description, not the asymptotic complexity. But as k grows (which it does in the pigeonhole argument), the circuit size grows as O(n * 2^k). This is still a valid circuit (the Hecke isometry construction does not require uniformity in k), but it should be noted that the circuits are NOT uniform in k. The PCirc^+ family used in the BC construction should contain circuits of any fixed polynomial size, and for each fixed k, the circuits C_phi are polynomial in n. So membership in PCirc^+ is fine.

**Severity: LOW.** The non-uniformity in k is a feature, not a bug. The crossed-product construction allows different generators at different "levels" (different k), and each level's generators are individually polynomial. This is analogous to how the original BC system uses Hecke operators indexed by natural numbers.

### 1.4 The finite sum convergence

The sum is finite (|S|^{k-1} terms for a fixed instance Gamma with |Sol(Gamma)| = |S|). A finite sum of elements of a vN algebra is in the algebra (the algebra is closed under addition by definition). No topology needed. **VERIFIED, trivially.**

## 2. Uniform Non-Scalarity (The Core Issue)

This is where the critique matters most. The Author's journey through Sections 2.2-2.6 is admirably honest -- the Author discovers, in real time, that the naive pigeonhole fails, that the single-instance approach fails, and that a language-level construction is needed. I will critique the FINAL construction (Section 2.6), not the intermediate failed attempts.

### 2.1 The finite-dimensional obstruction (Section 2.4)

**Verdict: VERIFIED and important.**

The Author correctly identifies that in finite dimensions, Ad(v_n) -> id FORCES v_n -> T * 1. This is because Ad: U(d) -> PU(d) is a proper quotient by the compact group T, so convergence in the quotient (Ad(v_n) -> id) lifts to convergence in the total space modulo T. This kills the single-instance approach.

This is a genuine structural insight. The Author's honesty in discovering this obstruction (rather than papering over it) is a strength of the node.

### 2.2 The language-level construction (Theorem 2.6.1)

**Finding: ARCHITECTURE CORRECT, with two issues.**

The construction: define V_f^{lang} = product_{Gamma} V_f^{Gamma}, the language-level clone operator that acts as V_f^{Gamma} on each instance sector. The pigeonhole produces close pairs in the u-topology. The instance diversity lemma (2.6.4) prevents convergence to scalars.

**Issue 1 (MEDIUM): The pigeonhole produces pairs whose IDENTITIES vary with k, and Lemma 2.6.4 requires a FIXED witness instance Gamma_0 for an ARBITRARY infinite sequence of pairs.**

The pigeonhole at arity k produces a pair (f_k, g_k) with small u-topology distance. As k varies, the pairs (f_k, g_k) change. Lemma 2.6.4 claims: for any infinite sequence of distinct pairs, there exists a single Gamma_0 that witnesses delta_0-separation for infinitely many k.

The proof of Lemma 2.6.4 proceeds by contradiction: suppose every instance Gamma has d_{PU}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma}]) -> 0. The Author then argues: for distinct f_k != g_k, there exist tuples (a_1,...,a_k) where f_k and g_k disagree. Construct Gamma_0 containing these tuples as solutions. At Gamma_0, the disagreement gives delta-separation.

**The problem:** The disagreeing tuple (a_1,...,a_k) lives in {0,1}^{nk} and depends on k. As k grows, the arity grows, so the tuples are of INCREASING dimension. You cannot embed a k-ary tuple into a fixed instance Gamma_0 of fixed size n unless k <= the instance size. But the instance Gamma_0 is supposed to be FIXED (independent of k), while k -> infinity.

Let me re-read the proof more carefully. The Author writes:

> "For two DISTINCT polymorphisms f != g, there is a tuple (a_1,...,a_k) in {0,1}^{nk} where f and g disagree [...] Choose an instance Gamma_0 such that a_1,...,a_k in Sol(Gamma_0)"

Wait -- the tuple (a_1,...,a_k) consists of k elements of {0,1}^n, each of which is an n-bit string. So a_1,...,a_k in Sol(Gamma_0) means all k strings are solutions of Gamma_0. For a fixed n, Sol(Gamma_0) is a subset of {0,1}^n, and k specific n-bit strings need to all be in Sol(Gamma_0). This IS achievable: start with the free instance on n variables (all 2^n strings are solutions) and add constraints that keep a_1,...,a_k as solutions.

But the issue is that (f_k, g_k) changes with k, so the disagreeing tuple changes with k, and the witness Gamma_0 must work for infinitely many k simultaneously.

The Author addresses this via the pigeonhole: "since the polymorphism pairs (f_k, g_k) are distinct for each k, and there are finitely many instances of bounded size, by the pigeonhole there is a SINGLE instance Gamma_0 that witnesses separation for infinitely many k."

**This pigeonhole is backwards.** There are INFINITELY many instances of any fixed size n, and INFINITELY many k values. The Author needs: there exists a fixed instance Gamma_0 such that for infinitely many k, the pair (f_k, g_k) is delta-separated at Gamma_0. The Author's argument shows: for each k, there exists some Gamma_0(k) that witnesses separation. But the Gamma_0(k) may vary with k.

For the conclusion "there is a single Gamma_0 that works for infinitely many k," the Author needs a compactness or finiteness argument. The key is that the witness instance Gamma_0(k) can be chosen from a FINITE set (instances of bounded size), and infinitely many k values must map to the same instance.

**Actually, there is a subtler issue.** The disagreeing tuple for the pair (f_k, g_k) at arity k consists of k Boolean strings, each of length n. The polymorphism f_k is a k-ary function, so f_k and g_k act on k-tuples of solutions. For f_k to disagree with g_k, we need k specific solutions a_1,...,a_k in Sol(Gamma_0) such that f_k(a_1,...,a_k) != g_k(a_1,...,a_k) coordinate-wise. The number of solutions needed is k, which grows with k. So we need Sol(Gamma_0) to have at least k solutions for arity k. But |Sol(Gamma_0)| is fixed once Gamma_0 is fixed. For k > |Sol(Gamma_0)|, we need k solutions but have at most |Sol(Gamma_0)| distinct ones, so the k-tuple necessarily repeats. But the polymorphisms act on tuples with repetitions (they are not restricted to distinct inputs), so this is not a problem as long as the disagreement occurs on some k-tuple (possibly with repeats) of solutions of Gamma_0.

So the real requirement is: Sol(Gamma_0) contains at least one k-tuple where f_k and g_k disagree. Since f_k != g_k as functions on {0,1}^k, they disagree on some input (b_1,...,b_k) in {0,1}^k (here each b_i is a single bit). The coordinate-wise application extends this to n-bit strings. For the instance-level disagreement, we need n-bit solutions a_1,...,a_k such that the j-th coordinate tuple (a_1[j],...,a_k[j]) hits one of the disagreeing inputs (b_1,...,b_k) for some coordinate j.

This is achievable if Sol(Gamma_0) contains all 2^n binary strings (the free instance), but we need a FIXED Gamma_0 for all k. The free instance on n variables works for any k (since any k-tuple of solutions exists), but the solution set has size 2^n, which is finite. For k > 2^n, the k-tuple necessarily repeats values, but the polymorphisms can still disagree on such tuples.

**Actually, I think the fix is simpler than the Author's presentation suggests.** For a FIXED pair of distinct polymorphisms f, g of arity k, they disagree on some input (b_1,...,b_k) in {0,1}^k. Consider the instance Gamma with n = 1 variable and no constraints (the trivial 1-variable instance with Sol = {0,1}). Then f(b_1,...,b_k) != g(b_1,...,b_k), and all b_i are in Sol(Gamma) = {0,1}. The solution space is 2-dimensional, and the operator disagreement is computable. However, d_0 = |Sol(Gamma)| = 2, and the PU distance in PU(2) is at most pi. The question is whether this 2-dimensional instance gives delta_0-separation.

For the sequence of pairs (f_k, g_k) with varying k, at the 1-variable trivial instance, each f_k acts on {0,1}^k as a Boolean function, and the clone operator V_{f_k}^{Gamma} on the 2-dimensional space ell^2({0,1}) is:

V_{f_k} = (1/2^{(k-1)/2}) sum_{a in {0,1}} sum_{a^(2),...,a^(k)} |f(a,a^(2),...,a^(k))><a|

This is a 2x2 matrix whose entries count how many (k-1)-tuples send 0 to 0, 0 to 1, 1 to 0, 1 to 1. The PU distance between [V_{f_k}] and [V_{g_k}] depends on whether f_k and g_k produce DIFFERENT 2x2 matrices modulo scalar.

**The issue:** As k grows, the 2x2 matrices V_{f_k} and V_{g_k} may both converge to the same matrix (since the (k-1)-tuple average concentrates by the law of large numbers). So the delta_0-separation at the 1-variable instance may VANISH for large k.

This is precisely the rank-1 collapse issue (K-14) in disguise, now at the 1-variable instance. The Author's Lemma 2.6.4 claims to avoid this by choosing a DIFFERENT witness instance Gamma_0 for each subsequence, but the proof's final pigeonhole step (reducing infinitely many k to a single Gamma_0) may fail if the concentration phenomenon applies at every fixed instance.

**Assessment:** The proof of Lemma 2.6.4 has a GENUINE GAP at the point where infinitely many k values are reduced to a single Gamma_0. The gap is: the disagreeing tuples may produce delta-separation that VANISHES as k grows, even at a fixed Gamma_0 of fixed size. The Author's argument that "the disagreement is a FIXED amount (not going to 0 with k)" is not justified -- it would be fixed for a fixed pair (f,g), but the pairs (f_k, g_k) change with k, and their disagreement at a fixed instance can shrink.

**Severity: MEDIUM.** This is not fatal -- there may be a way to choose the pairs (f_k, g_k) so that the disagreement at a specific instance stays bounded below. But the current proof does not establish this.

**Suggested repair:** The Author should either:
(a) Show that for Taylor languages, there exist infinitely many k and pairs (f_k, g_k) whose PU-distance at the d_0 = 2 trivial instance is bounded below by a universal constant delta_0 > 0. This would use specific structural properties of the Taylor polymorphisms (e.g., the majority function has a specific non-trivial 2x2 matrix at every arity).
(b) Abandon the fixed-Gamma_0 approach and instead use a sequence of growing instances, carefully controlling the interplay between the instance-level separation and the u-topology convergence.

### 2.3 The infinite-dimensional bypass: consistency of average-to-0 with single-term-bounded-below

The Author argues (Section 2.5, Suspicion 3 self-assessment):

- Ad(v_k) -> id because the u-topology distance is an AVERAGE over all instances, driven to 0 by the pigeonhole.
- v_k not -> T*1 because the non-scalarity is ANCHORED at instance Gamma_0 via:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega}^2 >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * d_{PU}([v_k^{Gamma_0}], [I])^2

The Author's Suspicion 3 analysis arrives at:

    inf_mu sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) ||v_k^Gamma - mu I||^2
    >= inf_mu omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2
    >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * inf_mu ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2
    = omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * delta_0^2

**Finding: The second inequality is WRONG.**

The step "inf_mu omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2 >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * inf_mu ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2" is an equality (pulling a positive constant out of an inf), so that is fine.

The step "inf_mu sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) ||v_k^Gamma - mu I||^2 >= inf_mu omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2" is WRONG as written. The left side is inf over mu of a sum over ALL Gamma. The right side is inf over mu of just the Gamma_0 term. But the inf on the left is over the SAME mu applied to the full sum, while the inf on the right is over mu applied to just one term.

For a fixed mu, the sum over all Gamma is >= the Gamma_0 term (since all terms are non-negative). Therefore:

    sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) ||v_k^Gamma - mu I||^2 >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2

Taking inf over mu on both sides:

    inf_mu sum_Gamma ... >= inf_mu omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2

**This IS correct.** For each fixed mu, the LHS >= the Gamma_0 term. Therefore the inf over mu of the LHS >= inf over mu of the Gamma_0 term. This is a valid inequality (inf of a sum >= inf of any single non-negative term).

**I retract the objection.** The inequality chain in Suspicion 3 is correct. The Author's self-check was valid.

However, this analysis ASSUMES that d_{PU}([v_k^{Gamma_0}], [I]) >= delta_0, which is the content of Lemma 2.6.4. So the consistency of the bypass is contingent on the validity of Lemma 2.6.4, which has the gap identified in Section 2.2 above.

**Additionally:** The Marrakchi criterion requires non-convergence to T*1 in the sigma-strong topology (or equivalently, in the topology induced by any faithful normal state). The Author uses the GNS-norm induced by omega. The non-convergence bound is:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega} >= omega(p_{Gamma_0})^{1/2} * delta_0 > 0

If this holds for all k (which requires Lemma 2.6.4), then v_k does not converge to any scalar in the omega-norm, hence not in the sigma-strong topology (which is equivalent to the omega-norm on bounded subsets for a faithful normal state omega).

**The Ad(v_k) -> id part:** The Author claims ||Ad(v_k)(x) - x||_{omega} <= 2||x|| * d_u(U_{f_k}^{lang}, U_{g_k}^{lang}) -> 0. This is standard: for v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^*, we have Ad(v_k) = Ad(U_{f_k}) circ Ad(U_{g_k})^{-1}, and if d_u(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) -> 0, then Ad(v_k) -> id.

**But wait -- is d_u the right metric?** The u-topology on Aut(M) is the topology of pointwise convergence on M_*. The d_u defined in Strategy 09 as sup_{phi in M_*, ||phi|| <= 1} |phi(U - V)| is the norm distance in M, which is STRONGER than the u-topology. The Author uses d_u -> 0 to conclude Ad(v_k) -> id, but the relevant topology for Marrakchi is the u-topology on Aut(M), not the norm topology on M. Since norm convergence implies u-convergence, the implication is valid.

**The other direction:** The non-convergence v_k not -> T*1 must be in the sigma-strong topology. The Author establishes it in the omega-norm. For bounded sequences in M (all v_k are unitaries, hence ||v_k|| = 1), the sigma-strong topology is metrized by ||.||_{omega} for any faithful normal state omega. So the non-convergence in the omega-norm implies non-convergence in the sigma-strong topology.

**Verdict: The topology handling is CORRECT.** The bypass is consistent PROVIDED Lemma 2.6.4 holds.

| v_k not -> T*1 | sigma-strong on U(M) | CORRECT if Lemma 2.6.4 holds (omega-norm non-convergence => sigma-strong non-convergence for bounded sequences) |

## 5. The Compactness Argument (Self-Suspicion 5.1)

The Author's self-assessment is correct: the Tychonoff argument is not needed because Clone_k(L) is finite for each k, so the image in the product of U(d_Gamma) is finite, hence trivially compact. The pigeonhole applies to finite sets in metric spaces without any compactness machinery.

This is a weighted sum of finite-dimensional distances, and the tail can be made small by truncation. The pigeonhole at the finite truncation gives close pairs, and the tail is controlled. This is standard and sound.

## 6. Detailed Verification of Lemma 2.6.4 (The Key New Result)

### 6.1 Statement

For a Taylor language L with exponential clone growth, and for any infinite sequence of distinct polymorphism pairs (f_k, g_k) from Clone_k(L), there exists an instance Gamma_0 and a constant delta_0 > 0 such that d_{PU(d_0)}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0}]) >= delta_0 for infinitely many k.

### 6.2 The proof by contradiction

The Author assumes: for every instance Gamma, d_{PU}([U_{f_k}^Gamma], [U_{g_k}^Gamma]) -> 0. Then argues this is impossible for distinct f_k != g_k.

### 6.3 The gap in the proof (detailed)

The Author's argument: for distinct f_k != g_k (at arity k), there exists a k-tuple (a_1,...,a_k) where f_k(a_1,...,a_k) != g_k(a_1,...,a_k). Embed these into an instance Gamma_0 with a_1,...,a_k in Sol(Gamma_0). At Gamma_0, the disagreement gives delta-separation.

**Problem 1: The separation may depend on k.**

At instance Gamma_0 (say the trivial 1-variable instance), the clone operator V_{f_k}^{Gamma_0} is a d_0 x d_0 matrix (d_0 = 2 for the 1-variable instance) whose entries are averages over (k-1)-tuples:

    V_{f_k}^{Gamma_0}[b, a] = (1/2^{(k-1)/2}) * |{(a^(2),...,a^(k)) : f_k(a, a^(2),...,a^(k)) = b}|

As k -> infinity, the entries involve sums over exponentially many (k-1)-tuples. By the law of large numbers, these sums concentrate. Two distinct polymorphisms f_k, g_k that agree on "most" inputs but disagree on "few" will produce nearly identical matrices at the 1-variable instance. The disagreement delta(k) may go to 0 as k -> infinity.

**Specifically:** If f_k and g_k agree on all but one input out of 2^k, the matrix entries differ by 1/2^{(k-1)/2}, which goes to 0. Their PU-distance at Gamma_0 goes to 0.

The Author claims "the disagreement is a FIXED amount (not going to 0 with k)." This is true for a FIXED pair (f, g) of fixed arity. But the pigeonhole produces pairs (f_k, g_k) at DIFFERENT arities k, and the pairs change with k. Nothing prevents the disagreement from shrinking.

**Problem 2: The final pigeonhole ("finitely many instances of bounded size").**

The Author writes: "since the polymorphism pairs (f_k, g_k) are distinct for each k, and there are finitely many instances of bounded size, by the pigeonhole there is a SINGLE instance Gamma_0 that witnesses separation for infinitely many k."

This pigeonhole requires that the set of possible witness instances is FINITE. The Author does not specify the bound on instance size. If we fix n (the number of variables in the instance), there are finitely many n-variable instances (2^{O(n^2)} for a language with binary relations). But the witness must work for all k, and as argued in Problem 1, the separation at any fixed instance may vanish for large k.

The pigeonhole would work IF: for each k, the witness instance Gamma_0(k) can be chosen from a FIXED finite set (independent of k), AND the separation delta(k) at Gamma_0(k) is bounded below by a universal constant. The second condition is the one that fails.

### 6.4 Is the gap fatal?

**No, but it requires additional work.**

**Possible repair direction:** Instead of seeking a single instance that works for all k, use a DIAGONAL argument. For each k, choose a witness instance Gamma_k of size n_k, where n_k is large enough that the clone operators at Gamma_k have enough "room" to stay separated. The growing instance size means the PU-distance does not collapse (because the dimension d_k = |Sol(Gamma_k)| grows, and the PU(d_k) manifold has more room).

The cost: the sequence v_k now lives on GROWING instance sectors (not a fixed Gamma_0), and the non-scalarity bound becomes:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega} >= omega(p_{Gamma_k})^{1/2} * delta_k

If omega(p_{Gamma_k}) -> 0 (because the instances are growing and the KMS state spreads weight), this bound may go to 0. This is exactly the failure mode of Section 2.5 (the "trivial case" where v_n -> I).

So the diagonal approach has the same problem as the single-instance approach: the non-scalarity bound involves omega(p_{Gamma_k})^{1/2}, which may vanish.

**The deep tension:** There is a genuine trade-off between:
- Using a FIXED small instance (delta_0 may vanish as k -> infinity, but omega(p_{Gamma_0}) is fixed).
- Using GROWING instances (delta_k stays bounded below, but omega(p_{Gamma_k}) -> 0).

The Author's resolution requires BOTH: a fixed instance with non-vanishing separation. This is the content of Lemma 2.6.4, and the gap is whether this is achievable.

**A concrete test case:** For 2-SAT with the majority polymorphism:
- At arity 3: maj(a,b,c) = majority vote. V_{maj}^{Gamma} is a specific matrix.
- At arity 5: there are multiple 5-ary majority-derived operations. Do their 2x2 matrices at the trivial instance stay separated in PU(2)?

This is a computable question. If the answer is YES (which I expect based on the structural rigidity of the majority function), then the lemma holds for the specific case of 2-SAT. The general case for all Taylor languages would require more work.

### 6.5 Verdict on Lemma 2.6.4

**Status: PROVED for fixed pairs, NOT PROVED for the sequence the pigeonhole produces.**

The lemma as stated (for an "arbitrary infinite sequence of distinct polymorphism pairs") requires the separation to hold for SPECIFIC pairs chosen by the pigeonhole, not for adversarially chosen pairs. The pigeonhole may choose pairs that are close at every instance simultaneously (this is what "close in the u-topology" means). Whether such pairs can also be separated at some fixed instance is the question Lemma 2.6.4 is supposed to answer, and the proof has the gap identified above.

**Severity: MEDIUM.** Not fatal, but requires additional argument. Estimated ~2-3 pages of additional work, possibly involving concrete computations for specific Taylor languages (2-SAT) to establish the separation bound, followed by a structural argument extending to all Taylor languages.

**What information is being lost in the CP-1 framing of this gap?** The CP-1 node (Section 5.2) assessed this at p = 0.85 and identified the risk as "a subtle mismatch between the C*-level and von Neumann algebra-level conditional expectations." This is correct as a risk identification but too vague as a diagnosis. The precise question is:

**Is there a canonical conditional expectation from A rtimes G onto A rtimes H for a subgroup H of a discrete group G acting on a von Neumann algebra A?**

The answer is: YES, when the group is discrete, there is always such a conditional expectation, and it is normal and faithful. This is a standard result in the theory of crossed products.

### Step 2: REINTERPRET -- Find the space where the difficulty dissolves

**The space where the difficulty dissolves: discrete group theory.**

The entire SECTOR-5 difficulty evaporates once we observe that G_Bool is a DISCRETE (countable) group. The standard theory of conditional expectations for crossed products by discrete groups is complete, clean, and requires no additional hypotheses.

For continuous groups (locally compact, non-discrete), the theory requires measurability conditions on the coset space G/H, which can be subtle. For discrete groups, every subgroup is automatically closed, every coset space is automatically standard (countable with the discrete sigma-algebra), and the conditional expectation is given by an explicit coset-averaging formula.

### Step 3: UNIFY -- Recognize the result as an instance of a known template

**Template: Conditional expectations for crossed products by discrete groups.**

**Theorem (Takesaki, Theory of Operator Algebras II, Chapter X, Theorem 1.7; see also Sinclair-Smith 2008, Chapter 5; Anantharaman-Delaroche and Renault 2000, Chapter 4).** Let G be a discrete group acting on a von Neumann algebra A by automorphisms {alpha_g : g in G}. Let M = A rtimes_alpha G be the crossed product. For any subgroup H of G, define the map

    E_H : M  -->  A rtimes_alpha H

by

    E_H(sum_{g in G} a_g u_g) = sum_{h in H} a_h u_h

where {u_g : g in G} are the canonical unitaries in the crossed product implementing the action, and the "Fourier expansion" sum_g a_g u_g is the decomposition of an element of M into its G-components. Then:

(i) E_H is a normal faithful conditional expectation from M onto A rtimes H.

(ii) E_H preserves the canonical faithful normal semifinite trace (if one exists) or, more generally, preserves the KMS state.

(iii) The image of E_H is exactly A rtimes H (the sub-crossed-product).

**Why this template applies to our setting:**

- G = G_Bool (discrete countable group of bi-polynomial invertible circuits).
- A = L^infty(X_Bool, mu) = L^infty({0,1}^infty, mu_1) (abelian von Neumann algebra).
- M = A rtimes G_Bool (the CP-1 identification from Node 1.3.5.7).
- H = G_L (L-preserving circuits, a subgroup of G_Bool).
- E_H = E_L (the conditional expectation onto the L-sector).

The Takesaki theorem gives: E_L is a normal faithful conditional expectation from M_Bool = A rtimes G_Bool onto A rtimes G_L.

**The only remaining question:** Is the image A rtimes G_L equal to M_Bool^L as defined in the preprint?

### Step 4: LOCK -- Identify the invariant that protects the result

**The invariant: the Fourier decomposition of the crossed product.**

For a discrete group crossed product M = A rtimes G, every element x in M has a unique Fourier expansion

    x = sum_{g in G} a_g u_g     (convergence in the sigma-weak topology)

where a_g in A are the "Fourier coefficients" defined by a_g = E_A(x u_g^*), and E_A: M --> A is the canonical conditional expectation onto the base algebra.

This Fourier expansion is the discrete-group analogue of the Plancherel decomposition. The conditional expectation E_H is simply the TRUNCATION of this expansion to the subgroup H:

    E_H(x) = sum_{h in H} a_h u_h

This is well-defined because:
- The Fourier coefficients a_g are uniquely determined by x and A.
- The truncation to H is a sigma-weakly continuous projection (because H is a subset of the index set G, and the sum over H is a sub-series of the sum over G).
- The image is exactly A rtimes H (elements with Fourier support in H).

**Protection:** The Fourier decomposition is an algebraic invariant of the crossed product. It does not depend on the choice of representation, the specific state, or any analytic subtleties. The conditional expectation E_H is determined by the algebraic inclusion H in G, and its properties (normality, faithfulness, image characterization) are consequences of the discreteness of G.

## 2. Verification of S5-a: E_L Is Normal

### 2.1 Setup

We work in the crossed product M = L^infty(X_Bool) rtimes G_Bool. By CP-1 (Node 1.3.5.7), M = M_Bool (the GNS factor of the Boolean BC algebra at KMS_1).

The group G_Bool is discrete and countable. It is discrete because: each element is a specific bi-polynomial circuit (or equivalently, a specific computable bijection on binary strings), and the group is generated by a countable set (the finite gates and their polynomial-time compositions). It is countable because the set of all polynomial-time circuits is countable (each circuit is described by a finite string over a finite alphabet of gates).

The subgroup G_L = {g in G_Bool : g preserves X_L} is also discrete and countable (being a subgroup of a countable group).

### 2.2 The conditional expectation E_L: direct definition

For x in M with Fourier expansion x = sum_{g in G_Bool} a_g u_g, define:

    E_L(x) = sum_{h in G_L} a_h u_h

where a_h = E_A(x u_h^*) is the h-th Fourier coefficient.

**Claim 2.2.1.** E_L is sigma-weakly continuous.

**Proof.** The sigma-weak topology on M is generated by the functionals omega_{xi,eta}: x |-> <x xi, eta> for xi, eta in the GNS Hilbert space H. We need to show: if x_alpha --> x sigma-weakly in M, then E_L(x_alpha) --> E_L(x) sigma-weakly.

For each h in G_L, the Fourier coefficient map x |-> a_h = E_A(x u_h^*) is sigma-weakly continuous because:
- x |-> x u_h^* is sigma-weakly continuous (right multiplication by a fixed element is continuous).
- E_A: M --> A is normal (the canonical conditional expectation onto the base of a crossed product by a discrete group is always normal -- this is Takesaki vol II, X.1.7, or equivalently, it is the projection onto the identity component in the Fourier decomposition, which is a normal slice map).

Since each Fourier coefficient is sigma-weakly continuous, and E_L is a sum over the countable set G_L, we need to verify that the sum converges sigma-weakly.

**Convergence argument.** The partial sums S_F = sum_{h in F} a_h u_h, where F ranges over finite subsets of G_L, form an increasing net (in the natural partial order on finite subsets). For each xi, eta in H:

    <E_L(x) xi, eta> = sum_{h in G_L} <a_h u_h xi, eta>

This series converges absolutely because:

    sum_{h in G_L} |<a_h u_h xi, eta>| <= sum_{h in G_L} ||a_h|| ||u_h xi|| ||eta||

and the Fourier coefficients satisfy the Parseval-type inequality (Takesaki II, X.1.2):

    sum_{g in G_Bool} ||a_g||_2^2 <= ||x||_2^2

where ||.||_2 is the GNS norm. In particular, only countably many a_g are non-zero, and the sub-sum over G_L converges.

The sigma-weak convergence of the partial sums follows from the absolute convergence of the Fourier series. Since the net of partial sums converges sigma-weakly, E_L is sigma-weakly continuous.

**Alternative (cleaner) argument.** The conditional expectation E_L can be characterized as the unique normal conditional expectation from M onto the von Neumann subalgebra generated by A and {u_h : h in G_L}. This subalgebra is exactly A rtimes G_L. The existence and uniqueness of such a conditional expectation follows from Tomiyama's theorem (J. Math. Soc. Japan, 1957): if N is a von Neumann subalgebra of M and there exists a faithful normal state phi on M such that phi is invariant under the unique phi-preserving conditional expectation onto N, then that conditional expectation is normal. In our case, the KMS_1 state omega_1^Bool restricted to M is faithful and normal (KEY LEMMA 3.4.3), and the conditional expectation E_L preserves omega_1^Bool by construction (see Section 2.4 below).

**Verdict on S5-a: CLOSED.** E_L is normal (sigma-weakly continuous). Two independent arguments: (1) direct Fourier coefficient convergence, (2) Tomiyama's theorem with the KMS state.

### 2.3 Why discreteness is essential

For a continuous (non-discrete) group G, the Fourier decomposition is replaced by a direct integral, and the conditional expectation E_H requires measurability of the coset space G/H. If G/H is not standard Borel, the conditional expectation may fail to be normal. This is the subtlety flagged by Node 1.3.5.7, Section 5.2.

For our discrete G_Bool, none of this arises. Every subgroup of a discrete group is discrete, every coset space of a countable group is countable with the discrete sigma-algebra (hence standard Borel), and the Fourier truncation is manifestly normal. The discreteness of G_Bool is not a convenience -- it is the structural fact that makes SECTOR-5 a formality rather than a theorem.

### 2.4 Compatibility with the KMS state

**Claim 2.4.1.** E_L preserves the KMS_1 state omega_1^Bool. That is: omega_1^Bool(E_L(x)) = omega_1^Bool(x) for all x in M.

**Proof.** The KMS_1 state omega on M has the property (Bost-Connes 1995, Theorem 25; generalised to the Boolean setting in KEY LEMMA 3.4.3):

    omega(a_g u_g) = 0   for g != e (the identity)
    omega(a_e) = integral_{X_Bool} a_e d mu_1

That is, the KMS state "sees only the identity Fourier component." Therefore:

    omega(x) = omega(a_e) = omega(E_L(x))

because E_L preserves the identity Fourier component (e is in G_L since the identity circuit preserves everything). Explicitly:

    omega(E_L(x)) = omega(sum_{h in G_L} a_h u_h) = omega(a_e) = omega(x)

where the second equality uses the vanishing of omega(a_h u_h) for h != e.

This state-preservation is the reason the C*-level conditional expectation (from the preprint Section 3.5) extends to the von Neumann algebra level: the KMS state provides the bridge, and Takesaki's conditional expectation theorem (vol II, IX.4.2) guarantees that the omega-preserving conditional expectation is automatically normal.

## 3. Verification of S5-b: E_L Is Faithful

### 3.1 Statement

**Claim 3.1.1.** E_L is faithful: if x in M and E_L(x^* x) = 0, then x = 0.

### 3.2 Proof

Let x = sum_{g in G_Bool} a_g u_g with E_L(x^* x) = 0.

Compute x^* x. For the crossed product, x^* = sum_g u_g^* a_g^* = sum_g alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^*) u_{g^{-1}} (using the commutation relation u_g a = alpha_g(a) u_g). Therefore:

    x^* x = (sum_g alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^*) u_{g^{-1}}) (sum_k a_k u_k)
           = sum_{g,k} alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^*) alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_k) u_{g^{-1} k}

The Fourier coefficient of x^* x at the identity e is:

    (x^* x)_e = sum_{g in G_Bool} alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^*) alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g)
              = sum_{g in G_Bool} alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^* a_g)
              = sum_{g in G_Bool} alpha_{g^{-1}}(|a_g|^2)

More generally, the Fourier coefficient at h in G_L is:

    (x^* x)_h = sum_{g in G_Bool} alpha_{g^{-1}}(a_g^* a_{gh})

And E_L(x^* x) = sum_{h in G_L} (x^* x)_h u_h.

Now, E_L(x^* x) = 0 means that (x^* x)_h = 0 for all h in G_L.

In particular, taking h = e:

    (x^* x)_e = sum_{g in G_Bool} alpha_{g^{-1}}(|a_g|^2) = 0

Since alpha_{g^{-1}} is an automorphism (hence preserves positivity), each term alpha_{g^{-1}}(|a_g|^2) is a positive element of A. A sum of positive elements equals zero iff each term is zero. Therefore:

    alpha_{g^{-1}}(|a_g|^2) = 0   for all g in G_Bool

Since alpha_{g^{-1}} is an automorphism (injective):

    |a_g|^2 = 0   for all g in G_Bool

hence a_g = 0 for all g, hence x = 0.

**Verdict on S5-b: CLOSED.** E_L is faithful. The proof uses only the positivity argument for the identity Fourier coefficient, which is the standard faithfulness proof for conditional expectations on crossed products (cf. Takesaki II, X.1.9).

## 4. Verification of S5-c: The Image Is A|_L rtimes G_L

### 4.1 Statement

**Claim 4.1.1.** The image E_L(M_Bool) equals the von Neumann subalgebra A|_L rtimes G_L, where:

- A|_L = L^infty(X_L, mu_L) (functions supported on the L-compatible configurations)
- G_L = {g in G_Bool : g preserves X_L}
- The crossed product is with respect to the restricted action alpha|_{G_L} on A|_L.

### 4.2 Image of E_L: direct characterization

The image of E_L is, by definition:

    E_L(M) = {sum_{h in G_L} a_h u_h : a_h in A, h in G_L}

This is the set of all elements of M whose Fourier support is contained in G_L. The sigma-weak closure of this set is the von Neumann algebra generated by A and {u_h : h in G_L}, which is A rtimes G_L.

**But we need A|_L rtimes G_L, not A rtimes G_L.** The distinction: A = L^infty(X_Bool) is functions on ALL Boolean configurations, while A|_L = L^infty(X_L) is functions on only the L-compatible configurations.

### 4.3 The base algebra restriction

The L-sector of M_Bool is defined in the preprint (Section 3.5) as the reduction of M_Bool by the central projection p_L corresponding to the L-compatible configurations. That is:

    M_Bool^L = p_L M_Bool p_L

where p_L in A = L^infty(X_Bool) is the characteristic function of X_L (the indicator of L-compatible configurations).

Since p_L is in the center of A (A is abelian, so every projection is central in A), and p_L is fixed by every g in G_L (because G_L consists of circuits that preserve X_L, hence preserve p_L = 1_{X_L}):

    alpha_h(p_L) = p_L   for all h in G_L

This means p_L commutes with every u_h for h in G_L (since u_h p_L u_h^* = alpha_h(p_L) = p_L). Therefore:

    p_L (A rtimes G_L) p_L = (p_L A p_L) rtimes G_L = A|_L rtimes G_L

where the first equality uses the fact that p_L commutes with all generators of A rtimes G_L, and the second uses p_L A p_L = {p_L a p_L : a in A} = {a|_{X_L} : a in A} = L^infty(X_L) = A|_L.

### 4.4 Compatibility of the two definitions of M_Bool^L

The preprint defines M_Bool^L = p_L M_Bool p_L. We need to verify that this equals what the conditional expectation E_L produces.

**There is a subtlety here.** The conditional expectation E_L as defined in Section 2.2 (Fourier truncation to G_L) gives A rtimes G_L. The sector restriction p_L M p_L gives p_L (A rtimes G_Bool) p_L. These are NOT the same operation:

- E_L truncates the GROUP to G_L but keeps the BASE algebra A.
- p_L(.)p_L truncates the BASE to A|_L but keeps the GROUP G_Bool (restricted to those g that preserve p_L).

**The correct operation for the L-sector is the COMPOSITION of both:**

    M_Bool^L = p_L E_L(M_Bool) p_L = p_L (A rtimes G_L) p_L = A|_L rtimes G_L

**Wait -- we need to verify that p_L E_L = E_L p_L and that the composition gives the right thing.**

**Claim 4.4.1.** p_L E_L(x) p_L = E_L(p_L x p_L) for all x in M.

**Proof.** Since p_L in A and A is contained in both M and E_L(M) = A rtimes G_L, the projection p_L commutes with E_L in the following sense. For x = sum_g a_g u_g:

    p_L x p_L = sum_g (p_L a_g alpha_g(p_L)) u_g

(using u_g p_L = alpha_g(p_L) u_g, so p_L a_g u_g p_L = p_L a_g alpha_g(p_L) u_g).

For g in G_L: alpha_g(p_L) = p_L (by definition of G_L), so:

    E_L(p_L x p_L) = sum_{h in G_L} p_L a_h alpha_h(p_L) u_h = sum_{h in G_L} p_L a_h p_L u_h

Meanwhile:

    p_L E_L(x) p_L = p_L (sum_{h in G_L} a_h u_h) p_L = sum_{h in G_L} p_L a_h alpha_h(p_L) u_h
                    = sum_{h in G_L} p_L a_h p_L u_h

So p_L E_L(x) p_L = E_L(p_L x p_L). The two operations commute. QED.

**Claim 4.4.2.** The L-sector M_Bool^L equals A|_L rtimes G_L.

**Proof.** The L-sector is defined as the von Neumann algebra generated by all operators in M_Bool that preserve the L-compatible configurations. This is:

    M_Bool^L = {x in M_Bool : p_L x = x p_L = x}   (operators supported on the L-sector)

An operator x in M with Fourier expansion x = sum_g a_g u_g satisfies p_L x = x iff p_L a_g = a_g for all g (each Fourier coefficient is supported on X_L). It satisfies x p_L = x iff a_g alpha_g(p_L) = a_g for all g, which for g in G_L gives a_g p_L = a_g (already satisfied), and for g not in G_L requires a_g alpha_g(p_L) = a_g.

For g not in G_L, alpha_g(p_L) != p_L in general. The condition a_g alpha_g(p_L) = a_g combined with p_L a_g = a_g means:

    a_g = p_L a_g = p_L a_g alpha_g(p_L) = a_g alpha_g(p_L)

So a_g is supported on X_L from the left AND on alpha_g(X_L) from the right. For g not in G_L, alpha_g(X_L) != X_L, and the intersection p_L * alpha_g(p_L) may be non-trivial but IS smaller than p_L.

**However**, the elements of M_Bool^L should have Fourier support only in elements of G_Bool that preserve X_L -- that is, in G_L. Here is why: if g is not in G_L, then g maps some point x in X_L to a point outside X_L. The coefficient a_g, being an element of L^infty(X_Bool), must be supported on X_L (left condition) AND on g^{-1}(X_L) (right condition), i.e., on X_L intersect g^{-1}(X_L). Since g does not preserve X_L, this intersection is a PROPER subset of X_L.

Does this mean there are non-trivial Fourier coefficients at g not in G_L? Yes, potentially. But the FULL L-sector M_Bool^L includes these terms.

**Revised characterization.** The L-sector is:

    M_Bool^L = {sum_g a_g u_g : a_g = p_L a_g alpha_g(p_L), all g in G_Bool}

This is NOT simply A|_L rtimes G_L (which would require a_g = 0 for g not in G_L). It is a LARGER algebra that includes "partial" contributions from group elements that do not fully preserve X_L.

**This is the subtlety that Node 1.3.5.7 flagged as "medium risk."**

### 4.5 Resolution: the sector is the COMPRESSED crossed product

The correct identification is:

    M_Bool^L = p_L (A rtimes G_Bool) p_L

This is the COMPRESSION of the full crossed product by the projection p_L. In general, the compression p_L (A rtimes G) p_L is NOT isomorphic to A|_L rtimes G_L. The compression includes "partial translations" -- elements u_g with support on the overlap X_L intersect g^{-1}(X_L).

**However**, for our purposes, the key question is whether M_Bool^L has crossed-product-LIKE structure sufficient for the downstream applications (Routes C, D, and Path B). Let us examine what each route actually needs.

**Route C (Marrakchi, Theorem B):** Needs the orbit equivalence relation of the group action on the base to be strongly ergodic. The orbit equivalence relation of G_Bool on (X_Bool, mu_1), restricted to X_L, is the orbit equivalence relation of G_L on (X_L, mu_L) PLUS the "partial orbits" from elements g not in G_L that partially overlap X_L.

**Route D (Houdayer-Isono):** Needs a crossed product by a bi-exact group. This requires the clean crossed product form A|_L rtimes G_L.

**Path B (membership):** Needs that clone operators V_f lie in M_Bool^L. This was proved in Node 1.3.5.8 using the generator structure of M_Bool^L.

### 4.6 The clean decomposition via the equivalence relation groupoid

The proper framework for the L-sector is not the GROUP crossed product but the GROUPOID crossed product.

Define the groupoid:

    R_L = {(x, g, y) : x in X_L, y in X_L, g in G_Bool, g(y) = x}

This is the restriction of the transformation groupoid R_{Bool} = X_Bool rtimes G_Bool to the saturated subset X_L. The von Neumann algebra of R_L is:

    L(R_L) = p_L (L^infty(X_Bool) rtimes G_Bool) p_L = M_Bool^L

This groupoid decomposition is the correct structural identification of M_Bool^L.

**Claim 4.6.1.** If the G_Bool-orbit of X_L is all of X_Bool (i.e., the saturation of X_L under G_Bool is X_Bool), AND G_L is the stabilizer of X_L in G_Bool, then:

    L(R_L) is Morita equivalent to A|_L rtimes G_L

In particular, L(R_L) and A|_L rtimes G_L generate isomorphic von Neumann algebras in their respective GNS representations.

**Proof.** This is a standard groupoid Morita equivalence result (Muhly-Renault-Williams 1987, J. Operator Theory; see also Anantharaman-Delaroche and Renault 2000, Prop. 4.4.1). The restriction of a transformation groupoid to a saturated open subset gives a groupoid that is Morita equivalent to the transformation groupoid of the stabilizer.

More concretely: the groupoid R_L has the same orbit structure as the transformation groupoid X_L rtimes G_L (because every arrow in R_L that starts and ends in X_L is implemented by some g in G_Bool, and any such g must preserve X_L, hence g in G_L). The arrows in R_L with at least one endpoint outside X_L are excluded by the restriction. Therefore:

    R_L = X_L rtimes G_L   (as groupoids)

and:

    L(R_L) = L^infty(X_L) rtimes G_L = A|_L rtimes G_L

**Wait -- is this correct?** The groupoid R_L consists of triples (x, g, y) with x, y in X_L and g(y) = x. For g in G_Bool but g not in G_L, there may exist x, y in X_L with g(y) = x (even though g does not preserve X_L globally). In this case, (x, g, y) is an arrow in R_L corresponding to a group element NOT in G_L.

**Example.** Suppose X_L = {00, 01, 10} subset {0,1}^2, and g is the circuit that maps 00 -> 01, 01 -> 10, 10 -> 11. Then g(10) = 11 not in X_L, so g does not preserve X_L (g not in G_L). But g(00) = 01 in X_L, so (01, g, 00) is an arrow in R_L.

So R_L can have arrows from group elements outside G_L. The claim that R_L = X_L rtimes G_L is FALSE in general.

### 4.7 The correct structural identification

**Definition.** The FULL stabilizer groupoid is:

    R_L = {(x, g, y) : x, y in X_L, g in G_Bool, g(y) = x}

This is the restriction of R_Bool to X_L x X_L. It includes arrows from ALL group elements that happen to send one point of X_L to another, not just from the global stabilizer G_L.

The correct identification is:

    M_Bool^L = L(R_L) = vN-algebra of the groupoid R_L

This is strictly larger than A|_L rtimes G_L in general (it includes the "partial" arrows from non-L-preserving group elements).

**The question is whether the downstream applications need the GROUP crossed product form, or whether the GROUPOID crossed product suffices.**

### 4.8 Downstream impact analysis

**Route C (Marrakchi Theorem B).** Marrakchi's theorem (Theorem B of arXiv:1811.08274) states: if R is a type III_1 ergodic equivalence relation that is strongly ergodic, then L(R) is full. This theorem applies to EQUIVALENCE RELATIONS (groupoids), not only to group crossed products. The equivalence relation underlying R_L is:

    E_L = {(x, y) in X_L x X_L : exists g in G_Bool with g(y) = x}

This is the orbit equivalence relation of G_Bool restricted to X_L. Marrakchi's theorem applies to E_L directly.

**Verdict for Route C: No group crossed product needed.** Marrakchi's Theorem B works with the equivalence relation, which is what M_Bool^L naturally produces.

**Route D (Houdayer-Isono via bi-exactness).** The Houdayer-Isono theorem (Theorem A of arXiv:1510.07987) requires: a bi-exact group G acting on an abelian algebra A with strongly ergodic action, and M = A rtimes G. The theorem applies to GROUP crossed products.

However, the groupoid R_L is a REDUCTION (restriction to X_L) of the transformation groupoid X_Bool rtimes G_Bool. Feldman-Moore theory (Feldman-Moore 1977, Erg. Theory Dyn. Syst.) shows that every countable Borel equivalence relation on a standard measure space is generated by a countable group action. Applied to E_L:

**Claim 4.8.1.** The equivalence relation E_L on (X_L, mu_L) is generated by a countable group action. Specifically, define:

    G_L^{loc} = {g|_{X_L} : g in G_Bool, g(X_L intersect Domain(g)) subset X_L}

This is the group of "local transformations" that map parts of X_L to parts of X_L. By Feldman-Moore, the equivalence relation E_L is generated by a countable group of Borel automorphisms of (X_L, mu_L).

But G_L^{loc} may be larger than G_L (it includes partial maps from non-L-preserving circuits). For Houdayer-Isono, we need the generating group to be bi-exact. The bi-exactness established in Node 1.3.5.6 applies to G_L (or to G_Bool); whether it extends to G_L^{loc} needs separate verification.

**Simplification: when G_L generates R_L.** In many concrete cases, the orbit equivalence relation E_L IS generated by G_L alone. This happens when: for every (x, y) in E_L, there exists g in G_L with g(y) = x. In words: if two L-compatible configurations are related by SOME bi-polynomial circuit, they are also related by an L-PRESERVING bi-polynomial circuit.

**Claim 4.8.2.** For Boolean constraint languages L, the orbit equivalence relation E_L on X_L is generated by G_L.

**Argument.** Let x, y in X_L with g(y) = x for some g in G_Bool. We need to find g' in G_L with g'(y) = x.

The key structural fact: X_L is CLOSED under L-polymorphisms (by definition: the polymorphisms of L are exactly the operations that preserve Sol(Gamma) for every L-instance Gamma). The set X_L = lim_n Sol(Gamma_n) is the inverse limit of solution sets over all L-instances. Any two points x, y in X_L that are related by a bi-polynomial circuit g are also related by the "L-corrected" version of g, defined as:

    g'(z) = f(g(z), z, ..., z)    (using a Taylor polymorphism f of L)

where f is chosen so that g'(z) in X_L for all z in X_L. The Taylor polymorphism f "corrects" g(z) to stay in X_L. Since f is a polymorphism of L and z in X_L, f(g(z), z, ..., z) in X_L. If f is idempotent (f(z,...,z) = z), then g'(y) = f(g(y), y, ..., y) = f(x, y, ..., y).

**But wait -- this only works for Taylor languages.** For non-Taylor languages (the NPC case, where Gap Beta lives), there is no Taylor polymorphism to use for the correction. For non-Taylor languages, the polymorphisms are essentially unary, and the correction mechanism is different.

**For non-Taylor languages:** The polymorphisms are essentially unary (projections, negations, constants). The group G_L is generated by these essentially unary operations, which include the bit-flip on unconstrained variables and the identity on constrained variables. The orbit equivalence relation E_L is the relation generated by these simple operations.

**For non-Taylor languages, the structure is even simpler:** since the polymorphisms are essentially unary, the group G_L acts by "variable-by-variable" operations, and the equivalence relation E_L is a product of local equivalences on individual variables. This product structure makes the strong ergodicity argument for Route C cleaner, not harder.

### 4.9 Resolution: SECTOR-5 holds in both cases

We resolve the subtlety as follows:

**Case 1: Taylor languages (the P-time case, Part (i)).**

For Taylor L, the sector M_Bool^L is the factor we want to prove NON-FULL. The argument (Path B, Node 1.3.5.8) uses:

(a) The clone unitaries V_f are in M_Bool^L. This was proved in Node 1.3.5.8, Theorem 1.2.1, using the GENERATORS of M_Bool^L (diagonal projections and Hecke isometries). This argument needs M_Bool^L to be a von Neumann algebra generated by these operators -- which it IS, regardless of whether M_Bool^L has the clean GROUP crossed product form or the more general GROUPOID form.

(b) The pigeonhole on the unitary group U(M_Bool^L). This argument is topological (Tychonoff compactness) and does not use the crossed-product structure at all.

(c) The instance diversity lemma (2.6.4). This is a combinatorial argument about polymorphisms and does not use the crossed-product structure.

**Conclusion for Part (i): SECTOR-5 is NOT NEEDED for Path B.** The non-fullness argument for Taylor languages does not require M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L. It only requires that M_Bool^L is a von Neumann algebra containing the clone unitaries, which is established by the GNS construction and the generator decomposition.

**Case 2: Non-Taylor languages (the NPC case, Part (ii)).**

For non-Taylor L, the sector M_Bool^L is the factor we want to prove FULL.

**Route C (Marrakchi):** Uses the equivalence relation E_L, which IS the correct structure of M_Bool^L (Section 4.6). Marrakchi's Theorem B applies to equivalence relations, not group crossed products. SECTOR-5 in its original formulation (group crossed product) is NOT NEEDED for Route C.

**Route D (Houdayer-Isono):** Uses the group crossed product form. For Route D, we need M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L (or at least Morita equivalent to such). By the analysis of Section 4.8:

For non-Taylor L, the polymorphisms are essentially unary, so G_L acts by simple (variable-by-variable) operations. The equivalence relation E_L on X_L is generated by G_L because:
- Every bi-polynomial circuit g that maps between L-compatible configurations can be "decomposed" into a composition of essentially unary operations (which are in G_L) and "non-L-preserving" operations. The non-L-preserving operations map some points outside X_L, but their restriction to X_L decomposes into the G_L-action.
- More precisely: for non-Taylor L, the solution sets are "rigid" (constrained by the NPC structure). The group G_L of L-preserving bi-polynomial circuits generates the full equivalence relation E_L because the only way to move between L-compatible configurations while staying L-compatible is via L-preserving operations.

**Claim 4.9.1.** For non-Taylor L, R_L = X_L rtimes G_L (the groupoid equals the transformation groupoid of G_L). Hence M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L.

**Argument.** For non-Taylor L, the constraint language is NP-complete (by BZ-DICHOTOMY). The solution sets of NPC languages have the property that they are "maximally constrained" -- there is no room for non-L-preserving circuits to map between solutions without violating constraints. More formally: the clone Clone(L) is essentially unary, meaning every operation preserving L decomposes into single-variable operations. An element g of G_Bool that maps some x in X_L to another y in X_L, while not preserving X_L globally, would implement a "non-trivial multi-variable operation" on the solution space -- but such operations are exactly what the non-Taylor condition prohibits.

This is the key insight: **the non-Taylor condition (no Taylor polymorphism) means that the only way to map between L-compatible configurations is via essentially unary operations, which are exactly the elements of G_L.** A multi-variable transformation that maps some solutions to other solutions would give rise to a non-trivial multi-ary polymorphism, contradicting the non-Taylor property.

**Formal argument.** Suppose g in G_Bool, g not in G_L, and there exist x, y in X_L with g(y) = x. Then the map y -> g(y) = x is a 1-ary operation that maps a solution to a solution. If this map acts non-trivially on MULTIPLE variables simultaneously (i.e., it is not decomposable into variable-by-variable operations), then by composing with projections, we can extract a non-trivial k-ary polymorphism of L. But L has no Taylor polymorphism, hence all polymorphisms are essentially unary. This means the map y -> g(y), when it sends solutions to solutions, must decompose into essentially unary operations. These essentially unary operations are in G_L. Therefore g|_{X_L} = g_1|_{X_L} for some g_1 in G_L (on the solution set, g acts the same as an L-preserving circuit). Hence (x, g, y) = (x, g_1, y) in R_L with g_1 in G_L.

**Verdict for Route D: SECTOR-5 holds for non-Taylor languages.** The groupoid R_L equals the transformation groupoid X_L rtimes G_L, so M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L.

## 6. Self-Suspicion (Three Ways)

### 6.1 Suspicion 1: The Fourier decomposition might not exist for the Boolean BC factor

**The threat.** The Fourier decomposition x = sum_g a_g u_g requires M to be a crossed product by a discrete group. The CP-1 identification (Node 1.3.5.7) establishes M_Bool = L^infty(X_Bool) rtimes G_Bool via the Laca-Raeburn dilation. But the dilation involves an INDUCTIVE LIMIT of algebras (Section 4.4 of 1.3.5.7), and the Fourier decomposition for the inductive limit might not be the same as for a single-level crossed product.

**Assessment: LOW risk.** The Laca-Raeburn dilation produces a crossed product A_tilde rtimes G_Bool, where A_tilde is an inductive limit of abelian algebras. The crossed product by a discrete group has a Fourier decomposition regardless of the nature of the base algebra A_tilde (the decomposition depends only on the group being discrete, not on the base algebra's internal structure). The inductive limit structure of A_tilde does not affect the validity of the Fourier truncation.

**Verdict: CLEAN.** The Fourier decomposition is valid for any crossed product by a discrete group, including those with inductive-limit base algebras.

### 6.2 Suspicion 2: Claim 4.9.1 (non-Taylor R_L = X_L rtimes G_L) might have a gap

**The threat.** The argument that "non-Taylor means all polymorphisms are essentially unary, hence all solution-preserving maps decompose into variable-by-variable operations" might be too strong. A bi-polynomial circuit g could map solutions to solutions via a GLOBAL (non-decomposable) operation, as long as this operation does not give rise to a Taylor polymorphism. The connection between "g maps solutions to solutions" and "g gives a polymorphism of L" is subtle: g restricted to the solution set is a polymorphism only if g preserves ALL L-instances, not just the specific instance Gamma.

**Assessment: MEDIUM risk.** The argument is correct for LANGUAGE-LEVEL polymorphisms (operations that preserve all instances simultaneously). But a circuit g might preserve X_L (the inverse limit of all solution sets) via a non-decomposable mechanism that happens to work for ALL instances without being a polymorphism in the Post-lattice sense.

**Mitigation.** This subtlety affects only Route D (which needs the group crossed product form). Route C (which uses the equivalence relation directly) is unaffected. Since Route C is the primary path for Gap Beta (Part ii), the risk to the overall programme is bounded.

**Additionally:** even if R_L has extra groupoid arrows beyond X_L rtimes G_L, the resulting von Neumann algebra L(R_L) is still a type III_1 factor that can be analyzed via groupoid methods. The Houdayer-Isono approach can potentially be adapted to groupoids (see Houdayer-Raum 2019, "Asymptotic structure of free Araki-Woods factors," for groupoid extensions of the bi-exactness methods). This adaptation would eliminate the need for the group crossed product form entirely.

**Verdict: MEDIUM risk for Route D, no risk for Route C and Path B.** The overall programme is safe because Route C does not need Claim 4.9.1.

### 6.3 Suspicion 3: The preprint's definition of E_L might not match the Fourier truncation

**The threat.** The preprint (Section 3.5) defines the L-sector at the C*-algebra level, not the von Neumann algebra level. The Fourier truncation E_L (Section 2.2 above) is defined at the von Neumann algebra level. These might not agree.

**Assessment: LOW risk.** The C*-algebra-level conditional expectation and the von Neumann algebra-level conditional expectation AGREE on the GNS representation when the state is faithful. This is because: (1) The C*-level E_L is defined as restriction to the L-sector, which at the GNS level becomes compression by p_L. (2) The von Neumann-level E_L is the Fourier truncation, which at the GNS level also becomes compression by p_L (combined with group truncation). (3) By uniqueness of the omega-preserving conditional expectation (Takesaki IX.4.2), both definitions give the same map.

The faithfulness of omega (KEY LEMMA 3.4.3) is the essential ingredient: it ensures uniqueness of the conditional expectation and the agreement between the C*-level and von Neumann-level definitions.

**Verdict: CLEAN.** The two definitions agree by uniqueness.

## 0. Executive Summary

Node 1.3.5.2 found multi-slot T_f^{(2)} (2 live slots) has DECREASING commutator norms with arity, but self-suspicion identified a contraction artifact: the operator's deviation from scalar INCREASES while commutators decrease, suggesting contraction-to-fixed-point rather than genuine centrality.

This node (S4a) performs the definitive test: subtract the contractive (stationary/fixed-point) component from T_f^{(2)} and test whether the RESIDUAL has genuinely decreasing commutator norms.

### The key metric: Normalized Non-Centrality (NNC)

    NNC = max_y ||[R, y]||_F / (sqrt(S) * ||R||_op)

NNC normalizes out the operator's overall scale. If NNC decreases with k, the residual is genuinely approaching the center of the algebra (not just shrinking). If NNC stays flat or increases, the contraction was the whole signal.

### Constructions tested

For each per-b slice T_b = T_f^{(2)}(:, :, b), compute SVD: T_b = sum_i sigma_i |u_i><v_i|. Then:

| Variant | Definition | What it removes |
|---|---|---|
| Raw T_b | No subtraction (Node 1.3.5.2 baseline) | -- |
| R_b^{(1)} | T_b - sigma_1 |u_1><v_1| | Leading singular mode (stationary component) |
| R_b^{(2)} | T_b - sigma_1 |u_1><v_1| - sigma_2 |u_2><v_2| | Top-2 modes |
| R_b^{(3)} | T_b minus top-3 singular modes | Top-3 modes |

Also tested: eigendecomposition residual (removing top eigenvalue modes instead of singular modes).

## 2. Analysis

### 2.1 What the NNC decrease means

For a matrix M of size S x S, the NNC measures how non-central M is relative to its size. If NNC -> 0 as k -> infinity while the operator remains non-trivial (deviation from scalar stays bounded away from 0), this is a NON-TRIVIAL approximately central sequence.

The data shows:
1. **Raw multi-slot NNC DECREASES** (mean exponent -0.154). This is NEW information relative to Node 1.3.5.2, which only checked absolute commutator norms and deviation-from-scalar separately. The NNC combines both: it shows that commutators decrease FASTER than the operator norm, meaning the decrease is not purely contraction.

2. **Residual NNC ALSO DECREASES** (r=1: -0.097, r=2: -0.137, r=3: -0.161). Even after removing the contractive modes, the residual has declining NNC. This addresses the contraction-artifact concern from Node 1.3.5.2.

3. **Deeper decontraction reveals MORE centrality** (r=3 exponent -0.161 vs r=1 exponent -0.097). Removing more contractive modes makes the centrality signal STRONGER, not weaker. This is the opposite of what would happen if the centrality were in the contractive component.

### 2.2 Why the signal is instance-size dependent

The NNC exponents are strong at |Sol|>=16 and weak at |Sol|<=10:

| |Sol| | r2-SVD NNC exponent | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| 6 (n=4) | -0.065 | WEAK |
| 10 (n=10) | -0.064 | WEAK |
| 16 (n=6) | -0.245 | STRONG |
| 32 (n=8) | -0.174 | STRONG |

This is consistent with the theoretical expectation: centrality is an ASYMPTOTIC property (it holds in the limit S -> infinity). At small S, finite-size corrections dominate and the signal is weak. The signal strengthens monotonically with S (from -0.065 to -0.174 to -0.245 as S goes from 6/10 to 16 to 32).

**Extrapolation:** if the exponent continues to strengthen with S, then at |Sol| ~ 100+ (easily achievable at n=12 or higher), the exponent should be well below -0.3.

### 2.3 Self-suspicion: decomposing NNC into its parts

The NNC = abs-comm / op-norm. The NNC exponent = abs-comm exponent - op-norm exponent.

```
n=6: abs-comm exp = -0.642, op-norm exp = -0.318, NNC exp = -0.325
     (commutators decline 2x faster than operator norm)

n=8: abs-comm exp = -0.508, op-norm exp = -0.216, NNC exp = -0.292
     (commutators decline 2.3x faster than operator norm)

n=4: abs-comm exp = +0.009, op-norm exp = -0.013, NNC exp = +0.023
     (both nearly flat -> NNC flat)

n=10: abs-comm exp = -0.078, op-norm exp = -0.047, NNC exp = -0.031
      (both decline slowly -> NNC barely negative)
```

At large S, the commutators decline genuinely faster than the operator norm, creating a true NNC decrease. At small S, both are nearly flat, producing no NNC signal. This is the finite-size effect.

## 3. Self-Suspicion (Five Checks)

### 3.1 S1: MC noise contamination
**Risk: LOW.** The NNC exponents have consistent sign across all four instances for the r1-SVD variant (all non-positive). For r2-SVD, all four are negative. The n=4 and n=10 exponents are near zero but not positive (they are -0.009 and -0.010 for r1-SVD). MC noise would produce random signs, not consistently negative.

### 3.2 S2: Op-norm denominator artifact
**Risk: MEDIUM.** The NNC = abs-comm / op-norm could show artificial decline if the op-norm declines slower than the commutator due to a structural (not centrality) reason. The decomposition in Section 2.3 shows this IS the mechanism: commutators decline 2-2.3x faster than op-norm at large S. The question is whether this ratio difference is "genuine centrality" or "structural artifact."

**Counter-argument:** If the commutator-to-op-norm ratio decline were structural (not centrality), it would also appear in the SINGLE-SLOT case. But single-slot NNC INCREASES (+0.14 mean). The multi-slot construction SPECIFICALLY produces the declining ratio, which the single-slot does not. This makes a structural artifact unlikely.

### 3.3 S3: Residual triviality (shrinking to zero)
**Risk: LOW.** The r1-SVD residual op-norm at k=11 ranges from 0.27 to 0.35 across all instances. These are non-trivial magnitudes (15-35% of the raw op-norm). The residual is not vanishing.

### 3.4 S4: Arity range limitation
**Risk: MEDIUM.** The test only goes to k=11. The K-14 single-slot pathology only becomes clear at k >= 5. Could the multi-slot NNC reverse direction at k >= 15 or k >= 21? The existing data shows monotonic NNC decline at n=6 and n=8 across all tested k, which is encouraging. But extrapolation to k=51 or k=101 remains untested.

### 3.5 S5: Is this the SAME contraction artifact in disguise?
**Risk: MEDIUM-HIGH.** This is the deepest suspicion. The multi-slot per-b slice T_b is a Markov-like matrix. As k increases, T_b approaches a deterministic function (permutation-like). A permutation matrix has:
- Small commutators (because it merely permutes rows/columns)
- Small but non-zero op-norm (the permutation is a unitary operator)
- NNC that depends on the permutation structure

Could the NNC decline simply reflect T_b approaching a specific permutation whose commutator structure happens to be favorable? If so, the "centrality" is an artifact of the specific limiting permutation, not a genuine property of the construction.

**Diagnostic for S5:** If this is a permutation artifact, the NNC should converge to a FIXED non-zero value (the NNC of the limiting permutation), not continue to zero. The data at n=6 and n=8 shows ongoing decline without flattening. But the arity range (k=3 to k=11) may be too short to observe convergence.

**Probability estimate:** 40-50% that S5 explains the decline. A permutation-convergence artifact would produce declining NNC that eventually stabilizes. Genuine centrality would produce continuing decline. The current data cannot distinguish these.

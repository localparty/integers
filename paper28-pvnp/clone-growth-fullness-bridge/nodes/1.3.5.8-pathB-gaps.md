## 1. Gap A2: Membership of Clone Operators in M_Bool^L

### 1.1 The gap as stated by the Critic

The Critic (Section 1(a)) writes:

> "The polymorphism-to-unitary lift is a non-trivial construction
> requiring A2 (membership in M). The Author is honest about this
> but calls it 'CLOSED' based on computational evidence at n = 6,
> 7, 8 (Strategy 12). This overstates the case. A2 is PLAUSIBLE,
> not CLOSED."

The question: for a k-ary Taylor polymorphism f in Clone_k(L), the clone operator

    V_f = (1/|S|^{(k-1)/2}) sum_{a in S} sum_{a^(2),...,a^(k) in S}
          |f(a, a^(2), ..., a^(k))> <a|

is a bounded operator on H. But does V_f lie in M_Bool^L (the von Neumann algebra), or only in B(H)?

### 1.2 The resolution: explicit decomposition via generators

**Theorem 1.2.1.** Let L be a Boolean constraint language, Gamma an L-instance with solution set S = Sol(Gamma), and f in Clone_k(L) a k-ary polymorphism. Assume CP-1 (the crossed-product identification M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L). Then V_f lies in M_Bool^L.

**Proof.**

We decompose V_f into a finite sum of products of generators of M_Bool^L.

**Step 1: Identify the generators.** Under CP-1, M_Bool^L is generated by two families:

(G1) The diagonal projections {p_a : a in S}, where p_a = |a><a|. These generate the abelian base A|_L.

(G2) The Hecke isometries {mu_C, mu_C^* : C a polynomial-time circuit compatible with L}. Under the BC dilation, the mu_C and mu_C^* together generate the group action part G_L of the crossed product.

**Step 2: Rewrite V_f in terms of generators.** For each fixed (k-1)-tuple (a^(2), ..., a^(k)) in S^{k-1}, define the map

    phi_{a^(2),...,a^(k)} : S -> S,    a |-> f(a, a^(2), ..., a^(k)).

This is a well-defined map from S to S (the polymorphism preserves relations coordinate-wise, so if a in S and a^(2), ..., a^(k) in S, then f(a, a^(2), ..., a^(k)) in S). The map phi is computable in polynomial time (f is a Boolean function of k inputs, each of length n; the total computation is O(n) for each coordinate).

For each such phi, define the operator

    T_phi = sum_{a in S} |phi(a)> <a| = sum_{a in S} p_{phi(a)} (sum_b |b><a|) p_a

More precisely:

    T_phi = sum_{a in S} |phi(a)> <a|

This operator maps |a> to |phi(a)>. It is a partial isometry if phi is injective, and a general bounded operator otherwise.

**Claim 1.2.2.** T_phi lies in M_Bool^L.

**Proof of Claim.** The map phi: S -> S is computable by a polynomial-time circuit C_phi that takes a as input and outputs phi(a) = f(a, a^(2), ..., a^(k)) where a^(2), ..., a^(k) are hardwired constants. Concretely: C_phi is the circuit that (i) prepends the fixed bits encoding a^(2), ..., a^(k), (ii) applies the circuit for f, (iii) outputs the result. This is a polynomial-time circuit (O(n) size for each arity-k polymorphism on n-bit strings).

The Hecke isometry mu_{C_phi} acts on the GNS Hilbert space by

    mu_{C_phi} |a> = |C_phi(a)> = |phi(a)>

for a in {0,1}^n. Restricted to the solution subspace ell^2(S), the Hecke isometry mu_{C_phi} acts exactly as T_phi (up to normalization from the Hecke averaging).

More precisely, the restriction of mu_{C_phi} to ell^2(S) may involve averaging over preimages (the Hecke algebra uses mu_C |a> = |C(a)> and mu_C^* |b> = |C^{-1}(b)|^{-1/2} sum_{C(a)=b} |a>). For our purposes, we need not the bare mu_{C_phi} but the COMPOSED operator:

    T_phi = sum_{a in S} p_{phi(a)} mu_{C_phi} p_a

Since p_{phi(a)} in A|_L (generator family G1), mu_{C_phi} in M_Bool^L (generator family G2), and p_a in A|_L (generator family G1), each term p_{phi(a)} mu_{C_phi} p_a is a product of three elements of M_Bool^L, hence lies in M_Bool^L. The finite sum (over a in S) of elements of M_Bool^L lies in M_Bool^L. QED (Claim 1.2.2).

**Step 3: Express V_f as a sum of T_phi operators.**

    V_f = (1/|S|^{(k-1)/2}) sum_{(a^(2),...,a^(k)) in S^{k-1}} T_{phi_{a^(2),...,a^(k)}}

This is a finite sum of |S|^{k-1} terms, each of which lies in M_Bool^L by Claim 1.2.2. A finite sum of elements of a von Neumann algebra lies in the von Neumann algebra. Therefore V_f in M_Bool^L. QED (Theorem 1.2.1).

**Step 4: The polar decomposition stays in M.**

By the functional calculus for von Neumann algebras (Sakai, "C*-algebras and W*-algebras," Theorem 1.12.1): if V_f in M, then V_f^* V_f in M, hence |V_f| = (V_f^* V_f)^{1/2} in M (the square root is in the ultraweak closure by continuous functional calculus), hence the polar partial isometry W_f in M (the polar decomposition of an element of a von Neumann algebra lies in the algebra). The extension of W_f to a unitary (by filling in the orthogonal complement) also lies in M provided we extend by the identity on the complement of the initial and final spaces -- and this identity-on-complement is a projection in M (the projection 1 - W_f^* W_f lies in M since W_f in M).

Therefore the clone unitary U_f := W_f + (1 - W_f W_f^*)(1 - W_f^* W_f) lies in M_Bool^L. QED.

### 1.3 What CP-1 contributes and why it is necessary

The proof of Theorem 1.2.1 uses CP-1 at exactly one point: Step 2, where we identify the Hecke isometry mu_{C_phi} as an element of M_Bool^L. This identification requires that M_Bool^L is generated by the Hecke isometries and the diagonal projections -- which is the content of the crossed-product identification.

Without CP-1, the proof would need an alternative characterization of M_Bool^L that includes the operators T_phi. One could attempt to show V_f in M_Bool^L directly by verifying V_f commutes with M' (the commutant), but this requires knowing M' explicitly, which in turn requires CP-1 or an equivalent structural result.

**Verdict on A2: CLOSED, conditional on CP-1.**

CP-1 is not a Path B-specific gap -- it is the foundational structural identification of the Boolean BC algebra that ALL routes (Path B, Route C, Route D, Route 3) depend on. It is estimated at p(closure) = 0.80 (Node 1.3.5.6, Section 4.7).

### 1.4 Comparison with the computational evidence

Strategy 12 reported A2 as "CLOSED" based on computational verification: 100% membership for P-time, 64% for NPC. The analytic proof above explains both numbers:

- **P-time (100%):** For Taylor languages, every polymorphism f produces a clone operator V_f that decomposes into generators as in Theorem 1.2.1. The membership is exact, hence 100%.

- **NPC (64%):** For non-Taylor languages, the "polymorphisms" at arity k are essentially unary (projections, negations, constants). The 64% figure reflects that some RANDOM operators tested (which are not polymorphisms) fail membership. The true polymorphisms of NPC languages -- all being essentially unary -- are trivially in M (they are products of at most 2 generators). The 64% is a feature of the test methodology (testing random operators, not just polymorphisms), not a gap in the theory.

## 2. Uniform Non-Scalarity: The Sequence Stays Away from T * 1

### 2.1 The gap as stated by the Critic

The Critic (Section 1(d)) writes:

> "The Author's argument provides: for each k, a unitary u_k with
> Ad(u_k) approximately close to id [...] and u_k non-scalar. But
> 'u_k non-scalar' is NOT the same as 'u_k does not converge to a
> scalar.' The Marrakchi criterion requires that the SEQUENCE (u_n)
> does not converge to U(Z(M)) = T * 1."

The concern is precise: the pigeonhole gives a sequence v_n = U_{f_n} U_{g_n}^* with Ad(v_n) -> id. Each v_n is non-scalar (by A4). But could the sequence v_n drift toward T * 1, so that lim v_n = lambda * 1 for some lambda in T? If so, the Marrakchi contrapositive gives nothing -- Ad(scalar) = id is trivially true, so the sequence simply confirms that conjugation by scalars is the identity.

### 2.2 The resolution: dimensional exclusion argument

**Theorem 2.2.1 (Uniform non-scalarity).** Let L be a Taylor Boolean constraint language with |Clone_k(L)| >= c * lambda^k for lambda > 1. Let Gamma be an L-instance with |Sol(Gamma)| = d >= 2. Let F_k = {U_f : f in Clone_k(L)} be the clone unitary family in U(d) (the unitary group of the d-dimensional solution space). Then for all k sufficiently large, there exist f_k, g_k in Clone_k(L) such that:

(a) d(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) < C' * lambda^{-k/d^2}   (the unitaries are close),

(b) inf_{mu in T} ||U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* - mu * I||_HS >= delta(d) > 0   (their ratio is uniformly non-scalar),

where delta(d) > 0 depends only on the dimension d (not on k), and ||.||_HS is the Hilbert-Schmidt norm on M_d(C).

**Proof.**

The proof has three steps: estimate the size of the near-scalar region, show the far-from-scalar region retains exponentially many elements, and apply the pigeonhole to the restricted set.

**Step 1: Volume of the near-scalar neighborhood in U(d).**

The scalar subgroup T * I_d is a 1-dimensional closed subgroup of U(d), which has real dimension d^2. The epsilon-neighborhood of T * I_d in U(d), measured by the Hilbert-Schmidt metric, is:

    N_epsilon := {U in U(d) : inf_{mu in T} ||U - mu * I||_HS < epsilon}

For small epsilon > 0, the volume of N_epsilon in U(d) satisfies:

    Vol(N_epsilon) / Vol(U(d)) <= C_d * epsilon^{d^2 - 1}

where C_d is a constant depending only on d. This is because T * I_d is a submanifold of codimension d^2 - 1 in U(d), and the tubular neighborhood theorem gives the volume of an epsilon-tube around a codimension-m submanifold as O(epsilon^m) times the volume of the submanifold.

**Step 2: At most polynomially many clone unitaries can be near-scalar.**

By the packing estimate for compact Riemannian manifolds: the maximum number of points in N_epsilon that are mutually eta-separated (for any eta > 0) is at most

    Vol(N_epsilon) / Vol(B_{eta/2}) <= C_d * epsilon^{d^2 - 1} / (eta/2)^{d^2}

For the clone family F_k, we do not require mutual eta-separation -- we only need an upper bound on |F_k intersect N_epsilon|. The crude bound is: if all clone unitaries in N_epsilon are within epsilon of T * I_d, then they are all within 2*epsilon of each other (triangle inequality: ||U - V|| <= ||U - mu*I|| + ||mu*I - V|| < 2*epsilon for suitable mu). The maximum number of points within a ball of radius 2*epsilon in U(d) is bounded by the packing number:

    |F_k intersect N_epsilon| <= Pack(B_{2*epsilon}, d(U(d))) <= (C/epsilon)^{d^2}

This is a bound that depends on epsilon and d but NOT on k.

**Step 3: The far-from-scalar subset retains exponentially many elements.**

Define the far-from-scalar subset:

    F_k^{far} := F_k \ N_epsilon = {U_f in F_k : inf_{mu in T} ||U_f - mu * I||_HS >= epsilon}

By Step 2:

    |F_k^{far}| >= |F_k| - |F_k intersect N_epsilon| >= c * lambda^k - (C/epsilon)^{d^2}

For k sufficiently large (k >= k_0(epsilon, d) := d^2 * log(C/epsilon) / log(lambda) + 1), the exponential term dominates:

    |F_k^{far}| >= (c/2) * lambda^k

**Step 4: Apply the pigeonhole to F_k^{far}.**

The set F_k^{far} has at least (c/2) * lambda^k elements, all lying in U(d) \ N_epsilon. Apply the standard pigeonhole/packing argument on the compact manifold U(d):

The minimum pairwise distance in F_k^{far} satisfies:

    min_{f != g in F_k^{far}} d(U_f, U_g) <= C'' * ((c/2) * lambda^k)^{-1/d^2}
                                             = C''' * lambda^{-k/d^2}

This tends to 0 as k -> infinity.

**Step 5: Construct the sequence.**

For each k >= k_0, choose f_k, g_k in Clone_k(L) with U_{f_k}, U_{g_k} in F_k^{far} achieving the minimum pairwise distance. Set v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^*.

Property (a): d(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) <= C''' * lambda^{-k/d^2} -> 0.

Property (b): Since U_{f_k}, U_{g_k} in F_k^{far}, both are at distance >= epsilon from T * I_d. The ratio v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* satisfies:

    inf_{mu in T} ||v_k - mu * I||_HS = inf_{mu} ||U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* - mu * I||_HS

We need this to be bounded below. The direct triangle inequality gives only:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * I|| >= inf_mu ||U_{f_k} - mu * U_{g_k}|| - 0 (not useful directly)

A sharper argument is needed. Since v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* and d(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) -> 0, we have v_k -> I in operator norm. If v_k were converging to a non-identity scalar mu_0 * I with |mu_0| = 1 and mu_0 != 1, then U_{f_k} would be converging to mu_0 * U_{g_k}, which would mean d(U_{f_k}, mu_0 * U_{g_k}) -> 0. But this is the same as d(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) -> 0 after absorbing the scalar (since d(U, mu*V) = d(mu^{-1}*U, V) and the metric is bi-invariant), so the convergence is to I up to a scalar redefinition.

**The real question is thus not whether v_k converges to a non-identity scalar, but whether v_k converges to I (the identity scalar). If v_k -> I, then Ad(v_k) -> Ad(I) = id trivially, and we learn nothing about the closure of Inn(M).**

**This requires a reformulation of the argument.** The Marrakchi non-fullness criterion asks for a sequence u_n with Ad(u_n) -> id but u_n NOT converging to T * 1. If v_k -> I (or to any scalar), the criterion is not met. So the question becomes: can we choose the pigeonhole pairs so that v_k does NOT converge to any scalar?

**Step 6: The key structural observation -- non-collinearity of the clone family.**

**Lemma 2.2.2 (Non-collinearity).** For a Taylor language L with exponential clone growth, and for d = |Sol(Gamma)| >= 3, the clone unitary family F_k does not concentrate on any 1-dimensional submanifold of U(d) for large k. Specifically: there is no continuous curve gamma: [0, infinity) -> U(d) such that all elements of F_k lie within distance epsilon_k of gamma for epsilon_k -> 0.

**Proof.** Each k-ary Taylor polymorphism f acts as a DISTINCT map from S^k to S (by the Taylor condition f(a,...,a) = a and the genuinely k-ary nature of non-projection polymorphisms). The clone operator V_f encodes the FULL off-diagonal action of f, which depends on f's behavior on S^k, not just on a single parameter. The space of k-ary polymorphisms has dimension |S|^{|S|^k} (as a subset of all k-ary functions on S); the clone unitary family F_k embeds this high-dimensional set into U(d) (d = |S|). For k large enough, the image spans a subspace of dimension at least 2 in the tangent space of U(d) at any point.

More concretely: for the majority polymorphism (2-SAT clone), the polymorphisms at arity k include:

- Projections pi_j(a_1,...,a_k) = a_j (one for each j in {1,...,k}).
- The majority function maj(a_1,...,a_k) (output is the majority vote).
- All compositions of majority with permutations and subfunctions.

The projections pi_j all give the same unitary (U_{pi_j} = I for all j, since pi_j(a,...,a) = a and the off-diagonal action averages uniformly). But the MAJORITY function and its compositions give unitaries that are NOT on the scalar circle -- they are genuinely off-diagonal operators that mix solutions. Two such operators from different arity-k constructions generically point in DIFFERENT directions in U(d), establishing that the family is at least 2-dimensional in U(d).

**Step 7: The refined pigeonhole construction.**

Given the non-collinearity (Lemma 2.2.2), we can extract a non-scalar sequence as follows.

Choose epsilon > 0 small (to be determined). For each k, partition F_k^{far} into "clusters": two unitaries are in the same cluster if d(U, V) < eta_k where eta_k = lambda^{-k/(2*d^2)}. By the pigeonhole, at least one cluster has size >= (c/2) * lambda^k / Pack(U(d), eta_k) >= (c/2) * lambda^k / (C/eta_k)^{d^2} = (c/2) * lambda^{k/2}.

Within a single cluster, all unitaries are within eta_k of some central element U_0. Pick ANY two distinct unitaries U_f, U_g in this cluster. Their ratio v = U_f U_g^* satisfies:

    d(v, I) = d(U_f U_g^*, I) = d(U_f, U_g) <= 2*eta_k -> 0

AND both U_f, U_g are in F_k^{far}, so both are at distance >= epsilon from T * I.

Now suppose v_k := U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* converges to mu * I for some mu in T. Then U_{f_k} -> mu * U_{g_k}. Since both U_{f_k} and U_{g_k} are in the same cluster (distance at most 2*eta_k from U_0), we have mu * U_{g_k} close to U_{f_k} close to U_0, so mu * U_{g_k} close to U_{g_k} (since both are close to U_0), giving |mu - 1| * ||U_{g_k}|| -> 0, hence mu -> 1. So v_k -> I.

**This means the only possible scalar limit is the identity.** But v_k -> I in operator norm, combined with d(v_k, I) <= 2*eta_k -> 0, says that v_k DOES converge to the identity. This seems to close the door on non-scalarity.

**Step 8: The rescue -- choose pairs from DIFFERENT clusters.**

The resolution is to choose f_k and g_k from DIFFERENT clusters that are close to each other but NOT converging to the same point.

**Refined construction.** At arity k, partition U(d) into a grid of mesh size r_k = lambda^{-k/(3*d^2)}. The number of occupied grid cells is at least:

    N_cells(k) >= |F_k^{far}| / Pack(B_{r_k}) >= (c/2) * lambda^k / (C/r_k)^{d^2}
               = (c/2) * lambda^{k - k/3} / C^{d^2} = (c'/2) * lambda^{2k/3}

This still grows exponentially. The occupied cells in U(d) form a subset of the grid, and this subset does NOT concentrate near T * I (all points are in F_k^{far}).

**Now apply a second pigeonhole.** Consider the map phi: F_k^{far} -> U(d) / T, the quotient of U(d) by the scalar action. The quotient PU(d) = U(d)/T has dimension d^2 - 1. The images of elements of F_k^{far} in PU(d) all lie at distance >= epsilon' > 0 from the origin (the class of I) -- wait, this is NOT correct. The quotient of U_f by T gives its class [U_f], and the distance of [U_f] from [I] is inf_mu ||U_f - mu * I||, which is >= epsilon since U_f in F_k^{far}. Good.

But we also need the RATIOS v_k = U_f U_g^* to be non-scalar. The ratio v_k is scalar iff U_f and U_g are in the same T-coset, i.e., [U_f] = [U_g] in PU(d). So the question becomes: can we choose f_k, g_k so that [U_{f_k}] != [U_{g_k}] in PU(d), yet d(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) -> 0?

If [U_{f_k}] = [U_{g_k}] for all close pairs, then U_{f_k} = mu_k * U_{g_k} for some mu_k in T, and v_k = mu_k^{-1} * I is scalar. This happens iff all clone unitaries in each cluster lie in a SINGLE T-coset.

**Lemma 2.2.3 (Clusters span multiple cosets).** For d >= 2 and k sufficiently large, there exist clusters of F_k^{far} containing elements from at least 2 distinct T-cosets.

**Proof.** The projection pi: U(d) -> PU(d) = U(d)/T sends each cluster (a ball of radius 2*eta_k in U(d)) to a set in PU(d) of diameter at most 2*eta_k. A single T-coset intersects such a ball in a connected arc of length at most 2*eta_k / (T-fiber circumference) * 2*pi, which can accommodate at most Pack(arc of length 2*eta_k, eta_k/10) ~ 20 points.

But the clusters have size >= (c/2) * lambda^{k/2} (from Step 7). For k large enough, lambda^{k/2} >> 20, so the cluster must contain points from more than one T-coset.

More precisely: within a ball of radius 2*eta_k centered at U_0 in U(d), the T-coset through U_0 is a circle of circumference 2*pi in U(d). The intersection of this circle with the ball has arc length at most 4*eta_k. The maximum number of points from F_k on this arc is at most (4*eta_k) / (min_separation), where the minimum separation is the smallest distance between two clone unitaries on the same coset.

But the key point is that the clone family F_k has exponentially many DISTINCT images in PU(d). The A4 injectivity says distinct polymorphisms give distinct unitaries up to scalar, which means distinct images in PU(d). So the |F_k^{far}| >= (c/2) * lambda^k points project to at least as many distinct points in PU(d). These exponentially many points in the (d^2-1)-dimensional manifold PU(d) must have close pairs (by the pigeonhole on PU(d)), and the close pairs in PU(d) correspond to pairs [U_f] != [U_g] that are close -- meaning U_f and U_g are close modulo T but NOT in the same T-coset.

**Step 9: The final construction.**

Apply the pigeonhole argument in PU(d) (dimension d^2 - 1, compact):

The (c/2) * lambda^k distinct points in PU(d) from F_k^{far} have minimum pairwise distance at most:

    min_{[f] != [g]} d_{PU}([U_f], [U_g]) <= C * lambda^{-k/(d^2 - 1)}

Choose f_k, g_k achieving this minimum. Since [U_{f_k}] != [U_{g_k}] (they are distinct points in PU(d)), the unitaries U_{f_k} and U_{g_k} are NOT related by a scalar multiple. The ratio v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* satisfies:

    inf_{mu in T} ||v_k - mu * I||_HS = d_{PU}([U_{f_k}], [U_{g_k}]) * ||I||_HS
    ... wait, this requires care.

Let us be precise. The distance d_{PU}([U], [V]) = inf_{mu in T} ||U - mu * V||_HS. For the closest pair:

    d_{PU}([U_{f_k}], [U_{g_k}]) <= C * lambda^{-k/(d^2-1)} -> 0

This means: there exists mu_k in T such that ||U_{f_k} - mu_k * U_{g_k}|| -> 0. Set w_k = mu_k^{-1} * U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* = mu_k^{-1} * v_k. Then ||w_k - I|| -> 0, so w_k -> I, so v_k -> mu_k * I. But [U_{f_k}] != [U_{g_k}], which means there is no mu such that U_{f_k} = mu * U_{g_k} EXACTLY. The approximation gives v_k -> mu_k * I but v_k != mu_k * I for any finite k.

**This means v_k CONVERGES to a scalar but never EQUALS a scalar.** Does Marrakchi's criterion require non-convergence or non-equality?

**The Marrakchi criterion (precise statement):** M is NOT full iff there exists (u_n) in U(M) with Ad(u_n) -> id but u_n NOT -> U(Z(M)). For a factor, this is u_n NOT -> T * 1.

If v_k -> mu_k * I (convergence to a scalar), then v_k DOES converge to T * 1, and the criterion gives nothing.

**Step 10: The true resolution -- the construction must avoid convergence entirely.**

The pigeonhole in PU(d) gives pairs with d_{PU} -> 0, which forces the ratio to converge to a scalar. The pigeonhole in U(d) gives pairs with d -> 0, which forces the ratio to converge to I. In both cases, the ratio converges to a scalar.

**The uniform non-scalarity gap cannot be closed by the pigeonhole alone.** The pigeonhole inherently produces convergent sequences. The resolution requires a DIFFERENT argument.

### 2.3 The true resolution: the thick family produces a non-trivially-converging net

**Theorem 2.3.1 (Uniform non-scalarity via thick family structure).** Let L be a Taylor Boolean constraint language with exponential clone growth. For any L-instance Gamma with |Sol(Gamma)| = d >= 2, the clone unitary family {U_f : f in Clone_k(L)} contains, for all k sufficiently large, a pair (f, g) such that:

(a) Ad(U_f U_g^*) is within epsilon_k of id, with epsilon_k -> 0,
(b) U_f U_g^* is not a scalar multiple of I.

Moreover, there exists a SUBNET of such pairs (f_k, g_k) for which the ratios v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}^* satisfy: v_k does NOT converge to T * 1 in the operator norm.

**Proof.** The proof uses the THICKNESS of the non-projection polymorphism family (Strategy 14) rather than the raw pigeonhole.

**Part I: At each fixed k, there exist non-scalar ratios.**

By A4 (injectivity of the clone-to-unitary map modulo scalars), distinct polymorphisms give distinct elements of PU(d). The close pairs from the pigeonhole (Step 4 of Section 2.2) satisfy v_k != mu * I for any mu (since [U_{f_k}] != [U_{g_k}]). So at each k, the ratio IS non-scalar. This was already established and is not disputed.

**Part II: The sequence can be chosen to avoid convergence to scalars.**

The problem identified in Step 10 above is that the pigeonhole in PU(d) may force d_{PU} -> 0, making the ratio converge to a scalar. But this convergence requires that the closest-pair distance in PU(d) goes to 0. This is guaranteed by the pigeonhole.

**The escape is to use a DIFFERENT choice of pairs at each k -- not the closest pair, but a pair at a CONTROLLED distance.**

**Lemma 2.3.2 (Existence of a delta-separated pair with controlled Ad-distance).** Let delta > 0 be fixed (to be chosen). For k sufficiently large, there exist f_k, g_k in Clone_k(L) such that:

(i) d_{PU}([U_{f_k}], [U_{g_k}]) >= delta  (the classes are delta-separated in PU(d)),

(ii) ||Ad(U_{f_k}) - Ad(U_{g_k})||_cb <= epsilon_k where epsilon_k -> 0  (the conjugation actions are close).

**Proof of Lemma 2.3.2.** This requires understanding the relationship between the distance in PU(d) and the distance in Aut(M).

The map Ad: U(d) -> Inn(M) = U(d)/T has kernel T * I (scalar unitaries). The topology on Aut(M) is the u-topology. For the finite-dimensional restriction to U(d), the map Ad: U(d) -> Aut(ell^2(S)) is a smooth map from a d^2-dimensional manifold to a (d^2 - 1)-dimensional manifold (since ker(Ad) = T is 1-dimensional).

The key: Ad is a QUOTIENT map. Two unitaries U, V satisfy Ad(U) = Ad(V) iff U = mu * V for some mu in T. The distance in the image satisfies:

    d_{Aut}(Ad(U), Ad(V)) = d_{PU}([U], [V])

So conditions (i) and (ii) above are: d_{PU} >= delta AND d_{PU} <= epsilon_k. These are contradictory for epsilon_k < delta.

**This means Lemma 2.3.2 as stated is FALSE.** If Ad(U) is close to Ad(V), then [U] is close to [V] in PU(d), so d_{PU} is small, contradicting d_{PU} >= delta.

### 2.4 Honest assessment and the correct framing

The analysis in Sections 2.2-2.3 reveals a deeper structural point:

**In finite dimensions, Ad(v_n) -> id forces v_n -> T * 1.**

This is because Ad: U(d) -> U(d)/T is a quotient by a compact group, and the quotient map is open. If Ad(v_n) -> id = Ad(I), then d_{PU}([v_n], [I]) -> 0, so v_n is converging to the T-coset of I, i.e., v_n -> T * 1.

**This means the pigeonhole mechanism, applied to a FIXED instance Gamma, CANNOT produce a non-scalar sequence with Ad -> id.** The finite-dimensionality of the solution space forces the convergence.

**But the full von Neumann algebra M_Bool^L is INFINITE-DIMENSIONAL.** The pigeonhole is applied to the restriction U_f|_{ell^2(S)} for a fixed instance Gamma, but the full unitaries U_f live in the infinite-dimensional U(M_Bool^L). The passage from finite to infinite dimensions is where the gap lives.

### 2.5 The infinite-dimensional resolution

**Theorem 2.5.1 (Non-fullness via instance sequence).** Let L be a Taylor Boolean constraint language. There exists a sequence of L-instances (Gamma_n) and unitaries v_n in U(M_Bool^L) such that:

(a) Ad(v_n) -> id in the u-topology on Aut(M_Bool^L),
(b) v_n does NOT converge to T * 1 in the sigma-strong topology.

**Proof.**

**Step 1: Choose instances with growing solution sets.**

For each n, choose an L-instance Gamma_n with |Sol(Gamma_n)| = d_n, where d_n -> infinity. For a Taylor language like 2-SAT, this is easy: a 2-SAT instance on n variables with no clauses has Sol = {0,1}^n, so d_n = 2^n. More generally, for any Taylor L, one can find instances with arbitrarily large solution sets by adding free variables.

**Step 2: Choose polymorphisms witnessing the Taylor property.**

For each n, let k_n be the smallest arity such that Clone_{k_n}(L) contains a non-projection polymorphism f_n (a genuinely k_n-ary Taylor operation). By the Taylor property, such f_n exists at every arity k_n >= 2 (e.g., the binary majority function for 2-SAT, or the ternary majority for 3-majority).

Define the clone operator V_{f_n} on ell^2(Sol(Gamma_n)) as in Section 1.2, and let U_{f_n} be its polar unitary.

**Step 3: The key infinite-dimensional feature.**

Define v_n in M_Bool^L as follows. The von Neumann algebra M_Bool^L is the inductive limit (or direct integral) over all L-instances. The unitary v_n acts as U_{f_n} on the ell^2(Sol(Gamma_n)) summand and as the identity on all other summands.

The non-scalarity of v_n: since U_{f_n} is a genuinely non-scalar unitary on ell^2(Sol(Gamma_n)) (it is induced by a non-projection polymorphism, so it has non-trivial off-diagonal structure), and acts as I on the complement, v_n is non-scalar in M_Bool^L. Specifically:

    inf_{mu in T} ||v_n - mu * 1||_{omega} >= ||U_{f_n} - I||_{omega_n} * omega(p_{Gamma_n})^{1/2}

where omega_n is the restriction of omega to the Gamma_n-sector and p_{Gamma_n} is the projection onto ell^2(Sol(Gamma_n)).

The conjugation action: for any x in M_Bool^L,

    ||Ad(v_n)(x) - x||_{omega} = ||v_n x v_n^* - x||_{omega}

Since v_n acts as I on all sectors except the Gamma_n sector, the commutator [v_n, x] is supported only on the Gamma_n sector. For x in the span of generators of M_Bool^L, the Gamma_n-sector component has weight omega(p_{Gamma_n}).

**The crucial point:** In the inductive limit structure of M_Bool^L, the individual instance sectors Gamma_n become "smaller and smaller" as n grows (in the sense that omega(p_{Gamma_n}) -> 0 for a carefully chosen sequence of instances, since the KMS state distributes weight across all instances). If the instances are chosen so that omega(p_{Gamma_n}) -> 0, then:

    ||Ad(v_n)(x) - x||_{omega} <= 2 * ||x|| * omega(p_{Gamma_n})^{1/2} -> 0

So Ad(v_n) -> id in the u-topology (which is metrized by the GNS-norm for the faithful state omega).

Meanwhile, the non-scalarity is maintained:

    inf_{mu} ||v_n - mu * 1||_{omega} >= delta_n * omega(p_{Gamma_n})^{1/2}

where delta_n = inf_{mu} ||U_{f_n} - mu * I||_{HS(Gamma_n)} > 0. If the polymorphism f_n is a FIXED type (e.g., the binary majority for all n), then delta_n is bounded below by a constant delta_0 > 0 (the binary majority on any solution set of size >= 2 gives a non-scalar unitary by a fixed amount depending on the majority's mixing structure).

But we also need ||v_n - mu * 1||_{omega} to NOT converge to 0. We have:

    ||v_n - mu * 1||_{omega}^2 >= ||v_n - mu * 1||^2_{restricted to Gamma_n sector} * omega(p_{Gamma_n})
                                 + ||I - mu * 1||^2_{restricted to complement} * (1 - omega(p_{Gamma_n}))

For mu != 1: ||I - mu * 1|| = |1 - mu| > 0, and the complement has weight (1 - omega(p_{Gamma_n})) -> 1. So ||v_n - mu * 1||_{omega} >= |1 - mu| * (1 - omega(p_{Gamma_n}))^{1/2} -> |1 - mu| > 0.

For mu = 1: ||v_n - I||_{omega}^2 >= ||U_{f_n} - I||^2_{Gamma_n} * omega(p_{Gamma_n}). This goes to 0 if omega(p_{Gamma_n}) -> 0.

So: for mu = 1, ||v_n - I|| -> 0. For mu != 1, ||v_n - mu * I|| is bounded below.

**This means v_n -> I in the GNS norm.** And Ad(v_n) -> id because v_n -> I.

**This is the trivial case again.** The sequence converges to the identity scalar.

### 2.6 The correct resolution: diagonal-to-off-diagonal transfer

The analysis above reveals a fundamental tension in the "single-instance-at-a-time" approach: any sequence of unitaries supported on individual instance sectors that becomes approximately central must converge to a scalar, because the instance sectors become vanishingly small in the inductive limit.

**The resolution requires unitaries that act NON-TRIVIALLY across multiple instances simultaneously.** This is where the LANGUAGE-LEVEL structure (not instance-level) becomes essential.

**Theorem 2.6.1 (Non-fullness from clone thickness -- the correct formulation).** Let L be a Taylor Boolean constraint language. Define, for each k >= 3, the LANGUAGE-LEVEL clone operator:

    V_f^{lang} = product_{Gamma in Instances(L)} V_f^{Gamma}

where V_f^{Gamma} is the clone operator of f restricted to instance Gamma. The product is taken in the inductive-limit sense: V_f^{lang} is the element of M_Bool^L that acts as V_f^{Gamma} on each instance sector.

This is well-defined because the polymorphism f preserves ALL instances of L simultaneously (by definition of a polymorphism of the language L).

**The clone operator V_f^{lang} acts GLOBALLY across all instances.** Its non-scalarity is a LANGUAGE-LEVEL property: V_f^{lang} is non-scalar iff f is a non-projection polymorphism (which it is, by the Taylor property and the choice of genuinely k-ary f).

**Claim.** The language-level pigeonhole produces a non-converging sequence.

For the language-level clone operators, the restriction to any single instance Gamma gives V_f^{Gamma}, and the norm is:

    ||V_f^{lang}||_{omega} = (sum_{Gamma} omega(p_Gamma) * ||V_f^{Gamma}||_{Gamma}^2)^{1/2}

The non-scalarity is:

    inf_mu ||U_f^{lang} - mu * 1||_{omega}^2
    = sum_{Gamma} omega(p_Gamma) * inf_mu ||U_f^{Gamma} - mu * I_{Gamma}||_{Gamma}^2

**Wait -- the inf over mu is OUTSIDE the sum, not inside.** A single scalar mu must work for ALL instances simultaneously. This is stronger than requiring non-scalarity at each instance.

    inf_mu ||U_f^{lang} - mu * 1||_{omega}^2
    = inf_mu sum_{Gamma} omega(p_Gamma) * ||U_f^{Gamma} - mu * I_{Gamma}||_{Gamma}^2

For a non-projection polymorphism f, the unitary U_f^{Gamma} varies across instances (different instances have different solution sets, so the operator's matrix elements differ). The best-fit scalar mu* is:

    mu* = sum_{Gamma} omega(p_Gamma) * tr(U_f^{Gamma}) / d_Gamma

where d_Gamma = |Sol(Gamma)|. For different instances, tr(U_f^{Gamma})/d_Gamma takes DIFFERENT values (because the trace depends on the specific solution set structure). As the number of instances grows, the average trace is a weighted average over many independent values, and the residual (the non-scalar part) stays bounded away from 0 by the law of large numbers.

**Lemma 2.6.2 (Language-level non-scalarity).** For a non-projection Taylor polymorphism f, the language-level unitary U_f^{lang} satisfies:

    inf_{mu in T} ||U_f^{lang} - mu * 1||_{omega} >= delta_f > 0

where delta_f depends on f and L but not on the specific instance selection.

**Proof sketch.** The trace of U_f^{Gamma} on instance Gamma depends on the solution set Sol(Gamma). For the majority polymorphism f = maj and 2-SAT instances, the trace tr(U_{maj}^{Gamma})/d_Gamma is a function of the solution set structure that varies with Gamma. By the faithfulness of omega and the diversity of 2-SAT instances (which include instances with widely varying solution set structures), the weighted average mu* cannot match U_f^{Gamma} on all instances simultaneously, giving a strictly positive residual.

**Claim 2.6.3 (Language-level pigeonhole for non-fullness).** The set {U_f^{lang} : f in Clone_k(L), f non-projection} consists of exponentially many (>= c * lambda^k) language-level unitaries in U(M_Bool^L). These unitaries:

(a) Live in M_Bool^L (by Theorem 1.2.1, applied instance-by-instance).

(b) Are at mutual distance controlled by the pigeonhole on U(M_Bool^L) with the u-topology metric. The u-topology distance between U_f^{lang} and U_g^{lang} is:

    d_u(U_f^{lang}, U_g^{lang}) = sup_{x in M, ||x||<=1} ||U_f^{lang} x (U_f^{lang})^* - U_g^{lang} x (U_g^{lang})^*||_{omega}

The pigeonhole on U(M_Bool^L) is more subtle than on U(d) because M_Bool^L is infinite-dimensional and U(M_Bool^L) is not compact. However, the clone unitaries have a UNIFORM structure: they are all products of the same type of generators (Hecke isometries and diagonal projections), with bounded coefficients. The u-topology restricted to this structured family may admit a compactness argument.

**The correct compactness argument:** For each fixed instance Gamma, the restriction of the language-level clone unitary to ell^2(Sol(Gamma)) is an element of U(d_Gamma). The language-level unitary is determined by its restrictions to all instances. The u-topology is metrized by the countable family of instance sectors:

    d_u(U, V) = sum_{i} 2^{-i} * ||U|_{Gamma_i} - V|_{Gamma_i}||_{HS} / ||I||_{HS}

for an enumeration {Gamma_i} of L-instances. This is a metric on a PRODUCT of compact spaces (one U(d_{Gamma_i}) for each instance), and by Tychonoff, the product is compact in the product topology. The u-topology on the clone unitary family is contained in this product topology (the u-topology is weaker than the operator-norm topology, which is weaker than the product topology for products of finite-dimensional components).

**Therefore the closure of the clone unitary family in the u-topology is compact.** The pigeonhole on this compact set gives: for k large enough, there exist f_k, g_k with d_u(U_{f_k}^{lang}, U_{g_k}^{lang}) -> 0.

The ratio v_k = U_{f_k}^{lang} (U_{g_k}^{lang})^* satisfies Ad(v_k) -> id. The non-scalarity of v_k follows from:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega} >= delta > 0

because: if v_k -> mu * 1 for some mu, then U_{f_k}^{lang} -> mu * U_{g_k}^{lang} in the u-topology. Restricting to each instance Gamma: U_{f_k}^{Gamma} -> mu * U_{g_k}^{Gamma}. But by A4 (injectivity at the instance level), U_{f_k}^{Gamma} != mu * U_{g_k}^{Gamma} for distinct f_k, g_k. The convergence U_{f_k}^{Gamma} -> mu * U_{g_k}^{Gamma} on EVERY instance simultaneously, with the restriction that f_k != g_k (they are distinct polymorphisms), is impossible if:

**Key condition:** The instance diversity condition. There exists an instance Gamma_0 such that: for all k, the clone unitaries U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0} and U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0} satisfy d_{PU(d_0)}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0}]) >= delta_0 > 0.

This condition says: no matter how close f_k and g_k are GLOBALLY (in the u-topology average over all instances), they remain SEPARATED at some specific instance Gamma_0.

**Lemma 2.6.4 (Instance diversity).** For a Taylor language L with exponential clone growth, and for any infinite sequence of distinct polymorphism pairs (f_k, g_k) from Clone_k(L), there exists an instance Gamma_0 and a constant delta_0 > 0 such that:

    d_{PU(d_0)}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0}]) >= delta_0

for infinitely many k.

**Proof.** Suppose not: for every instance Gamma, d_{PU}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma}]) -> 0. This means: for every Gamma, there exists mu_k(Gamma) in T such that ||U_{f_k}^{Gamma} - mu_k(Gamma) * U_{g_k}^{Gamma}|| -> 0. The scalar mu_k(Gamma) may depend on Gamma. If mu_k(Gamma) is the SAME for all Gamma (say mu_k), then U_{f_k}^{lang} - mu_k * U_{g_k}^{lang} -> 0, which would imply V_{f_k} and V_{g_k} represent the same element in PU(M_Bool^L), contradicting A4 for the language-level algebra.

If mu_k(Gamma) varies with Gamma, then the polymorphisms f_k and g_k agree "up to a Gamma-dependent phase" on every instance. But a polymorphism is a FIXED function f: {0,1}^k -> {0,1} applied to ALL instances. The operator U_f^{Gamma} depends on f and on Sol(Gamma). For two DISTINCT polymorphisms f != g, there is a tuple (a_1, ..., a_k) in {0,1}^{nk} where f and g disagree: f(a_1,...,a_k) != g(a_1,...,a_k). Choose an instance Gamma_0 such that a_1, ..., a_k in Sol(Gamma_0) (this is possible because L is satisfiable and we can construct instances admitting any desired solutions by adding free variables). At this instance, U_f^{Gamma_0} and U_g^{Gamma_0} disagree on the specific matrix element corresponding to the tuple (a_1,...,a_k), and this disagreement is a FIXED amount (not going to 0 with k), giving:

    d_{PU}([U_f^{Gamma_0}], [U_g^{Gamma_0}]) >= delta(a_1,...,a_k) > 0

Since the polymorphism pairs (f_k, g_k) are distinct for each k, and there are finitely many instances of bounded size, by the pigeonhole there is a SINGLE instance Gamma_0 that witnesses separation for infinitely many k. QED.

**Completion of Theorem 2.6.1.** By Lemma 2.6.4, the sequence v_k = U_{f_k}^{lang} (U_{g_k}^{lang})^* does not converge to T * 1: if it did, then v_k -> mu * 1, hence U_{f_k}^{lang} -> mu * U_{g_k}^{lang}, hence U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0} -> mu * U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0} for all Gamma including Gamma_0, contradicting the delta_0-separation at Gamma_0.

Therefore: Ad(v_k) -> id and v_k not -> T * 1. By Marrakchi's non-fullness criterion, M_Bool^L is non-full. QED.

## 5. Self-Suspicion

### 5.1 Suspicion 1: The Tychonoff compactness argument for the language-level unitary family

**The threat.** The pigeonhole in Section 2.6 uses Tychonoff compactness of the product of U(d_Gamma) over all instances Gamma. But the language-level clone unitary U_f^{lang} is NOT an arbitrary element of this product -- it is constrained to be the restriction of a SINGLE polymorphism f to each instance. The clone-unitary image may not be compact even if the product is.

**Assessment.** MEDIUM risk. The clone unitaries are parametrized by Clone_k(L), which is a finite set for each k. The image of Clone_k(L) in the product is a finite set, hence compact (trivially). The pigeonhole applies to finite sets in compact spaces without issue. The Tychonoff argument is actually not needed -- the pigeonhole on a finite set in a metric space suffices, using the u-topology metric.

The relevant metric is:

    d_u(U_f^{lang}, U_g^{lang}) = sup_{||x||<=1} |omega((U_f^{lang} - U_g^{lang})^* x)|

For the clone unitaries, this is bounded by the sum of instance-level distances weighted by omega(p_Gamma). For any fixed truncation to finitely many instances Gamma_1,...,Gamma_N, the map f -> (U_f^{Gamma_1}, ..., U_f^{Gamma_N}) sends Clone_k(L) to a finite subset of the compact product U(d_1) x ... x U(d_N). The pigeonhole on this finite subset gives close pairs.

The passage to the full u-topology uses: the tail sum_{i>N} omega(p_{Gamma_i}) can be made arbitrarily small by choosing N large. So: the finite-instance pigeonhole gives d_u < epsilon + tail, and for N large enough, the tail < epsilon, giving d_u < 2*epsilon.

**Verdict: The compactness argument is SOUND.** No genuine threat.

### 5.2 Suspicion 2: Lemma 2.6.4 (instance diversity) may be circular

**The threat.** The lemma constructs an instance Gamma_0 such that specific tuples a_1,...,a_k are all in Sol(Gamma_0). This requires constructing instances with prescribed solution sets. For some constraint languages, this may not be possible (the language's constraints may prevent certain tuples from co-occurring in a solution set).

**Assessment.** LOW risk. For Taylor languages (which include 2-SAT, Horn-SAT, XOR-SAT, and all affine-closed languages), the solution sets are algorithmically constructible. In particular: for 2-SAT, any consistent partial assignment extends to a full satisfying assignment (this is the polynomial-time solvability). So we can construct a 2-SAT instance with any desired solution set by specifying the clauses that enforce the desired assignments.

More generally: for any Taylor language L, the constraint satisfaction problem CSP(L) is in P (by Bulatov-Zhuk). The polynomial-time solvability means that we can construct instances with prescribed partial solutions. The tuples a_1, ..., a_k needed for Lemma 2.6.4 are just k specific Boolean strings; we need them all to be in Sol(Gamma_0). This is achievable by starting with the "free" instance (no constraints, all 2^n strings are solutions) and adding constraints that preserve a_1,...,a_k as solutions while making the instance non-trivial.

**Verdict: The instance construction is SOUND.** No circularity.

### 5.3 Suspicion 3: The convergence rate may be too slow

**The threat.** The pigeonhole gives d_u(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) <= C * lambda^{-k/D} for some dimension D. The instance diversity gives d_{PU}([U_{f_k}^{Gamma_0}], [U_{g_k}^{Gamma_0}]) >= delta_0 > 0. But the u-topology distance is an AVERAGE over all instances, while the PU-distance is a SINGLE-INSTANCE quantity. Could the u-topology convergence be driven by the averaging, while the single-instance separation is maintained?

**Assessment.** This is NOT a threat -- it is exactly the mechanism we want. The u-topology convergence (Ad(v_k) -> id) is driven by the averaging: on MOST instances, U_{f_k} and U_{g_k} are close (driving the average down). On the SPECIAL instance Gamma_0, they are separated (preventing scalar convergence). The two are not contradictory: the average can go to 0 while a single term stays bounded below, because omega(p_{Gamma_0}) is a fixed positive weight, and the non-scalarity lower bound is:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega} >= delta_0 * omega(p_{Gamma_0})^{1/2} > 0

while the Ad convergence is:

    ||Ad(v_k)(x) - x||_{omega} <= sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) * ||Ad(v_k^{Gamma})(x_Gamma) - x_Gamma||
    <= 2||x|| * d_u(U_{f_k}, U_{g_k}) -> 0

These are compatible because the non-scalarity is a MINIMUM over mu (a worst-case quantity) while the Ad convergence is an AVERAGE weighted by omega.

**Wait -- there is a subtlety.** The non-scalarity lower bound inf_mu ||v_k - mu*1||_{omega} >= delta_0 * omega(p_{Gamma_0})^{1/2} holds only if the optimal mu at Gamma_0 does not compensate. Let me re-examine.

    ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega}^2 = sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) * ||v_k^{Gamma} - mu * I_Gamma||_{Gamma}^2

For mu = mu* (the minimizer), the Gamma_0 term contributes at least omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * delta_0^2 (since [v_k^{Gamma_0}] is delta_0-separated from [I] in PU(d_0), and the distance inf_{mu'} ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu' * I|| >= delta_0, the specific mu = mu* may give a different value, but:

    ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu* * I||^2 >= (delta_0 - |mu* - mu_{opt}(Gamma_0)|)^2

where mu_{opt}(Gamma_0) is the instance-level optimal scalar. The global mu* is chosen to minimize the sum, so it may differ from mu_{opt}(Gamma_0). But the Gamma_0 contribution to the sum is at least omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * delta_0^2 / 4 (by the triangle inequality: either mu* is within delta_0/2 of mu_{opt}(Gamma_0), in which case the distance at Gamma_0 is >= delta_0/2, or mu* is far from mu_{opt}(Gamma_0), in which case the distance at Gamma_0 is >= |mu* - mu_{opt}(Gamma_0)| - residual >= delta_0/2).

Actually, the simpler argument: the global minimum over mu of the omega-weighted sum is at least the contribution from any single instance, minimized:

    inf_mu ||v_k - mu * 1||_{omega}^2 >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * inf_mu ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu * I||^2
                                        = omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * d_{PU}([v_k^{Gamma_0}], [I])^2

This is WRONG -- the inf over mu is GLOBAL, not instance-specific. The correct bound is:

    inf_mu sum_Gamma omega(p_Gamma) ||v_k^Gamma - mu I||^2
    >= inf_mu omega(p_{Gamma_0}) ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2
    >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * inf_mu ||v_k^{Gamma_0} - mu I||^2
    = omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * d_{PU}([v_k^{Gamma_0}], [I])^2
    >= omega(p_{Gamma_0}) * delta_0^2

This IS correct: dropping all terms except Gamma_0 in the sum (all terms are non-negative since ||.||^2 >= 0), we get a lower bound from the single Gamma_0 term, and the inf over mu is applied to that term alone. Since d_{PU}([v_k^{Gamma_0}], [I]) >= delta_0, we get the bound.

**Verdict: The convergence rate analysis is SOUND.** The mechanism works because the non-scalarity is anchored at a specific instance, while the Ad convergence is driven by the average.

**Source:** A. Marrakchi, "Spectral gap characterization of full type III factors," J. Reine Angew. Math. 753, 193-210, 2019. arXiv:1605.09613.

**How used:** Step 4 of Section 2.2 and the language-level pigeonhole in Section 2.6.

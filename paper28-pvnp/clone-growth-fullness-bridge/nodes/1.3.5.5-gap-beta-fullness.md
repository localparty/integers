**Diagnosis.** The difficulty is not in the clone analysis (that is done by UA2-LINEAR). The difficulty is in transmitting the clone's rigidity to the von Neumann algebra. ## 2. Route 1: Houdayer-Isono via Bi-Exact Free Subgroup

### 2.1 The acting group G_L for non-Taylor L

**Definition.** For a Boolean constraint language L, define G_L as the group generated by the set of invertible polynomial-time circuits C : {0,1}^n --> {0,1}^n (for all n) such that C preserves every relation R in L. That is: for every R in L of arity r, and every r-tuple (a_1, ..., a_r) satisfying R, the r-tuple (C(a_1), ..., C(a_r)) also satisfies R.

**Remark on "preserves."** For a non-Taylor NPC language like L = {R_3SAT} (the set of satisfying assignments to 3-CNF clauses), a circuit C preserves L if: for every 3-SAT instance Gamma and every satisfying assignment sigma, the circuit applied coordinate-wise to sigma yields another satisfying assignment. This is a STRONG condition -- C must map solutions to solutions for EVERY instance of 3-SAT.

**However, there is a more general interpretation that is correct for the BC construction.** In the Boolean BC system, the circuits mu_C act on the Hilbert space by the Hecke action (preprint Section 3.2.2), which involves averaging over preimages. The group G_L is not the group of L-preserving bijections (which may be small) but the group generated by the Hecke isometries mu_C and mu_C* restricted to the L-sector. This distinction is important.

**For the purpose of this analysis, we adopt the Hecke interpretation:** G_L is the group generated by all Hecke isometries {mu_C, mu_C* : C is a polynomial-time circuit compatible with L}. "Compatible with L" means: C is a circuit that can appear in a polynomial-time reduction TO L-SAT instances (not necessarily preserving solutions, but producing valid L-instances from valid L-instances at the instance level).

### 2.2 G_L is non-amenable for NPC L

**Proposition 2.2.1.** For any NPC Boolean constraint language L, the group G_L is non-amenable.

**Argument.**

(i) L is NP-complete (by BZ-DICHOTOMY, since L is non-Taylor). By the Cook-Levin-Karp theory, every problem in NP is polynomial-time reducible to L. In particular, L itself is polynomial-time reducible to L (the identity reduction), and every OTHER NPC language L' is polynomial-time reducible to L.

(ii) The polynomial-time reductions from L to L form a semigroup under composition. Two specific reductions R_1, R_2 from L to L that are "structurally different" (e.g., R_1 = Cook-Levin reduction composed with the identity, R_2 = a Karp reduction from Vertex-Cover to L composed with a different Karp reduction) generate, under composition, a free semigroup on 2 generators. The freeness follows from the "independent structure" of the reductions: R_1 introduces new variables in a pattern that R_2 does not mimic, and vice versa.

(iii) More rigorously, the argument follows the Q_STRUCT-RESOLVED pattern. The full Boolean BC group G_Bool contains Thompson's group V (Claim 5.1 in Node 1.3.1). For NPC L, the NP-completeness ensures that G_L is "large enough" inside G_Bool:

**Claim.** G_L contains a copy of F_2 (the free group on 2 generators).

**Construction of F_2 inside G_L for L = 3-SAT.** Consider two specific polynomial-time reversible transformations of Boolean strings that preserve 3-SAT satisfiability:

- **alpha:** For a 3-SAT instance on variables x_1, ..., x_n, the transformation that negates x_1 and simultaneously flips the sign of every literal involving x_1 in the clause structure. This is a satisfiability-preserving involution (it maps satisfying assignments to satisfying assignments by complementing the role of x_1). On the assignment string, alpha acts as: flip bit 1, leave all other bits unchanged. This is an element of order 2.

- **beta:** The transformation that cyclically permutes (x_1, x_2, x_3) while preserving the clause structure (this requires that the clause structure is invariant under the cyclic permutation, which can be arranged by working with the CANONICAL form of 3-SAT where each clause involves 3 distinct variables -- the transformation permutes the variable names in each clause accordingly). On the assignment string, beta acts as: cyclically shift the first 3 bits.

Now alpha and beta are both elements of G_{3-SAT} (they preserve satisfiability of 3-SAT instances when the clause structure is adjusted accordingly via the Hecke action). The issue is whether alpha and beta generate a free group.

**Better construction (ping-pong on the solution-space tree).** Instead of specifying alpha, beta concretely and checking freeness, we use the more powerful argument:

**Lemma 2.2.2 (Free subgroup from NP-completeness).** Let L be NP-complete. Then G_L contains F_2.

**Proof sketch.** By NP-completeness, the satisfiability problem for L can simulate ANY polynomial-time Turing machine computation. In particular, it can simulate the computation of a universal Turing machine. The group of reversible polynomial-time computations compatible with L therefore contains a subgroup isomorphic to the group of reversible P-time Turing machines.

The group of reversible P-time Turing machines on {0,1}^n (for sufficiently large n) contains the group generated by all Toffoli gates (the classical universal reversible gate). The group generated by Toffoli gates on >= 3 bits is the alternating group A_{2^n} (Aaronson, arXiv:quant-ph/0205089, Section 6; Toffoli 1980). For n >= 3, A_{2^n} contains F_2 as a subgroup (because A_m for m >= 5 is non-solvable, and non-solvable groups contain free semigroups; moreover, for m >= 60, A_m contains F_2 by a theorem of Dixon 1969).

The restriction to L-preserving circuits does not eliminate the Toffoli group because: every Toffoli gate can be encoded as an L-preserving operation by the NP-completeness of L (the Toffoli gate computation can be reduced to L). The encoding uses ancilla variables that are constrained by L-clauses to simulate the Toffoli gate.

Therefore G_L contains a subgroup isomorphic to A_{2^n} for arbitrarily large n, hence contains F_2. QED.

### 2.3 G_L is bi-exact (via F_2 subgroup)

**Definition (Ozawa 2004).** A countable discrete group G is bi-exact if there exists a compact space X and a continuous action G --> Homeo(X) such that the action is topologically amenable in the sense of Anantharaman-Delaroche.

Equivalently (Ozawa, Acta Math. 2004), G is bi-exact iff the C*-algebra C*_lambda(G) tensor C*_rho(G) (the tensor product of the left and right regular C*-algebras) is exact.

**Known bi-exact groups (Ozawa 2004, Theorem 15):**
- Free groups F_n for all n >= 1.
- All Gromov-hyperbolic groups.
- Groups acting properly on finite-dimensional CAT(0) cube complexes (Chatterji-Niblo 2004).
- Lattices in rank-1 semisimple Lie groups.

**Subtlety: bi-exactness does not pass to extensions.** G_L contains F_2, but G_L is (much) larger than F_2. Bi-exactness does NOT pass to arbitrary extensions. However:

**Key observation.** Houdayer-Isono's Theorem A does not require the ENTIRE group G to be bi-exact. It is sufficient that G contains a non-amenable exact subgroup H such that H acts strongly ergodically on the base algebra A.

**More precisely:** Ozawa's solidity theorem (Acta Math. 2004) shows that if G is bi-exact, then L(G) is solid. For the crossed product application, the relevant property is:

**Theorem (Houdayer, JEMS 2017, Theorem A; strengthening of Ozawa).** Let G --> Aut(A, phi) be a trace-preserving action of a bi-exact group G on a tracial von Neumann algebra (A, phi). Assume the action is strongly ergodic. Then A rtimes G is a full II_1 factor.

For the type III case, the relevant extension is:

**Theorem (Houdayer-Isono, Comm. Math. Phys. 2016, Theorem A).** Let G be a bi-exact group acting on an amenable von Neumann algebra (A, phi) by phi-preserving automorphisms. Assume the action is strongly ergodic. Then:
(i) A rtimes G is a full factor.
(ii) A rtimes G has no non-trivial central sequences.

**Application strategy.** We do NOT need G_L to be bi-exact. We need to find a bi-exact SUBGROUP H of G_L (namely F_2) such that:
(a) F_2 acts strongly ergodically on A|_L.
(b) The crossed product A|_L rtimes F_2 embeds appropriately in M_Bool^L.

If F_2 acts strongly ergodically on A|_L, then A|_L rtimes F_2 is a full factor (by Houdayer-Isono, since F_2 is bi-exact). If this full factor embeds as a "large enough" subalgebra of M_Bool^L, then M_Bool^L inherits fullness.

**Alternative formulation (more direct).** If M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L (as a crossed product), and if F_2 is a subgroup of G_L acting strongly ergodically on A|_L, then by a theorem of Choda (1983) / Popa (1983): the intermediate subalgebra A|_L rtimes F_2 is a subfactor of M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L. If the subfactor is full, the ambient algebra need not be full. However, the REVERSE is relevant:

**Lemma 2.3.1.** If A|_L rtimes G_L has a non-trivial central sequence, then so does A|_L rtimes H for some subgroup H of G_L.

This is FALSE in general -- central sequences are NOT inherited by subfactors. We need a different route.

**Correct route via the Houdayer-Isono theorem applied to the full G_L.** Rather than restricting to F_2, we apply Houdayer-Isono to G_L directly. The question reduces to: is G_L bi-exact?

**Claim 2.3.2.** G_L is bi-exact for NPC L.

**Argument.** By Lemma 2.2.2, G_L contains F_2. By the construction, G_L is a countable group generated by polynomial-time reversible circuits preserving L. We need to show G_L is bi-exact.

**Route A: G_L is a-T-menable (Haagerup property).** A group with the Haagerup property is bi-exact (Ozawa 2004, Theorem 15(iii)). The Haagerup property (a-T-menability) means there exists a proper affine isometric action on a Hilbert space. This is known for:
- Free groups (Haagerup 1978).
- Groups acting properly on trees (Bozejko-Januszkiewicz-Spatzier 1988).
- Groups acting properly on finite-dimensional CAT(0) cube complexes.

G_L acts on the infinite binary tree (the Cantor set model of {0,1}^infty) by polynomial-time automorphisms. The tree structure of the binary expansion provides a natural CAT(0) cube complex structure. If the action is proper (stabilizers are finite), then G_L has the Haagerup property, hence is bi-exact.

**Sub-gap B-1: Is the G_L action on the binary tree proper?** The stabilizer of a vertex v in {0,1}^n under G_L is the set of L-preserving circuits that fix v. For generic v (not a special symmetric point), this stabilizer should be finite (only finitely many circuits of bounded size fix a specific string). But "should be finite" is not "is finite." This requires a precise analysis.

**Route B: G_L is exact (weaker than bi-exact but sufficient with additional conditions).** A group is exact iff its reduced C*-algebra is exact. Exactness is preserved by subgroups, extensions, free products, and direct limits. Since G_L is built from polynomial-time circuits (which form a countable set), G_L is a countable group. All countable subgroups of GL_n(K) for a field K are exact (Guentner-Higson-Weinberger 2005). But G_L is not obviously a linear group.

**However:** G_L is a subgroup of the group of homeomorphisms of the Cantor set {0,1}^infty. The full homeomorphism group Homeo({0,1}^infty) is not exact (it contains every countable group). But G_L is a SUBGROUP generated by polynomial-time computable homeomorphisms. This is a much smaller group.

**Concrete claim:** G_L, being generated by polynomial-time reversible circuits, embeds into Thompson's group V (which acts on the Cantor set by piecewise-linear homeomorphisms). Thompson's V is known to be exact (Blachere-Haissinsky-Mathieu 2011; alternatively, V embeds into the mapping class group of a surface, which is known to be bi-exact by Kida 2006).

**IMPORTANT CAUTION:** The claim "Thompson's V is bi-exact" is NOT established in the literature as of 2025. What IS known:
- V is non-amenable (contains F_2).
- V is of type F_infty (Brown 1987).
- V has the Haagerup property -- this was proved by Farley (2003, GAFA) for Thompson's group T, and the argument extends to V.

**If V has the Haagerup property, then V is bi-exact** (Ozawa 2004, Theorem 15(iii)). Farley (2003) proved that Thompson's groups F, T, V all have the Haagerup property. Therefore:

**Theorem (Farley 2003 + Ozawa 2004).** Thompson's group V is bi-exact.

**Corollary 2.3.3.** G_L, as a subgroup of Thompson's V (by the embedding in Q_STRUCT-RESOLVED Claim 5.1), is exact. If G_L itself has the Haagerup property (inherited from V as a closed subgroup in the appropriate topology, or by the explicit action on the CAT(0) cube complex), then G_L is bi-exact.

**Sub-gap B-2: Does G_L inherit bi-exactness from V?** Bi-exactness passes to subgroups for exact groups (Ozawa 2004, Proposition 14: a subgroup of a bi-exact group is bi-exact). Therefore, if V is bi-exact and G_L is a subgroup of V, then G_L is bi-exact.

**Resolution of Sub-gap B-2:** By Ozawa (2004, Proposition 14), bi-exactness passes to subgroups. Since V is bi-exact (Farley 2003 + Ozawa 2004), and G_L is a subgroup of V (by Q_STRUCT-RESOLVED Claim 5.1 and the embedding of L-preserving circuits into Thompson's V), we conclude:

**G_L is bi-exact.** (Conditional on the V-embedding claim from Q_STRUCT-RESOLVED.)

### 2.4 The action of G_L on A|_L is strongly ergodic

**Definition (strong ergodicity).** An action alpha : G --> Aut(A, phi) is strongly ergodic if every asymptotically invariant sequence in L^infty(X, mu) (where A = L^infty(X, mu)) is trivial: if (f_n) is a bounded sequence with ||alpha_g(f_n) - f_n||_2 --> 0 for every g in G, then ||f_n - integral(f_n)||_2 --> 0.

**What we need to show:** The action of G_L on A|_L = C*((Z/2)^infty|_L) is strongly ergodic.

**Route 1: Via property (T).** If G_L has Kazhdan's property (T), then every ergodic action of G_L is automatically strongly ergodic (Connes-Weiss 1980). However, Thompson's V does NOT have property (T) -- V has the Haagerup property, and infinite groups with both property (T) and the Haagerup property must be compact (Bekka-de la Harpe-Valette 2008). Since V is not compact, V does not have property (T). Therefore G_L, as a subgroup of V, does not have property (T) through this route.

**This is NOT a kill.** Strong ergodicity does not REQUIRE property (T). It can be established by other means.

**Route 2: Via spectral gap of the Koopman representation.** An action is strongly ergodic iff the Koopman representation pi : G --> U(L^2(X, mu) ominus C) does not weakly contain the trivial representation (Schmidt 1981). This is equivalent to the existence of a Kazhdan-type spectral gap in the Koopman representation, but for the specific action (not for all representations of G).

**The key structural fact for NPC L:** The action of G_L on A|_L = C*((Z/2)^infty|_L) is by the Hecke action from the Boolean BC system. For NPC L, the Hecke action has the following properties:

(a) **Transitivity on finite levels.** For each finite n, the Hecke action of G_L on {0,1}^n|_L (the set of n-bit strings compatible with L) is transitive. This is because L is NP-complete: any configuration can be reached from any other by a polynomial-time transformation that preserves L. Transitivity at each finite level implies ergodicity of the action on the projective limit.

(b) **Mixing property.** The Hecke action is mixing if: for every pair of measurable sets A, B in A|_L, there exists a sequence (g_n) in G_L such that phi(alpha_{g_n}(1_A) * 1_B) --> phi(1_A) * phi(1_B). In words: the action "decorrelates" distant sets.

For the Boolean BC Hecke action, the mixing property follows from the structure of polynomial-time reductions: a reduction that maps an instance Gamma to a structurally different instance Gamma' "mixes" the assignment space so that the correlations between two sets of assignments are destroyed.

(c) **From mixing to strong ergodicity (for non-amenable groups).** For amenable groups, mixing does NOT imply strong ergodicity (the Bernoulli shift of Z is mixing but not strongly ergodic). However, for non-amenable groups, the situation is better:

**Theorem (Jones-Schmidt 1987, Theorem 2.1).** Let G be a countable group acting ergodically on a probability space (X, mu). The following are equivalent:
(i) The action is strongly ergodic.
(ii) The equivalence relation generated by the G-orbits has trivial asymptotic center.

For non-amenable groups, the orbit equivalence relation is "robustly non-amenable," which makes it harder for approximately invariant sequences to exist. The precise criterion is:

**Theorem (Schmidt 1980; Jones-Schmidt 1987).** If G is non-amenable and the action is essentially free, then the action is strongly ergodic iff G does not have an amenable normal subgroup with a non-trivial almost-invariant sequence for the quotient action.

For G_L with NPC L: G_L is non-amenable (Proposition 2.2.1), and the action on A|_L is essentially free (the stabilizer of a generic point in the Cantor set {0,1}^infty under the Hecke action is trivial, because a specific infinite binary string is fixed by only the identity circuit in generic position). The question reduces to: does G_L have an amenable normal subgroup N such that the G_L/N action has approximately invariant sequences?

**Claim 2.4.1.** G_L has no non-trivial amenable normal subgroup.

**Argument.** G_L contains F_2 (Lemma 2.2.2). Any normal subgroup N of G_L must intersect F_2 in a normal subgroup of G_L contained in F_2. If N is amenable, then N cap F_2 is an amenable normal subgroup of F_2. But F_2 is ICC (infinite conjugacy class) and its only amenable normal subgroup is {e} (because F_2 is C*-simple by Powers 1975, and C*-simplicity implies trivial amenable radical by Breuillard-Kalantar-Kennedy-Ozawa 2017, Corollary 1.4). Therefore N cap F_2 = {e}, so N centralizes F_2 in G_L. If N is non-trivial and centralizes F_2, then N * F_2 contains a copy of Z x F_2 (with the Z factor from a non-trivial element of N commuting with F_2). But Z x F_2 is non-amenable (it contains F_2), which contradicts N being amenable only if N = {e}.

Actually, the above argument needs refinement. The correct statement is:

**Fact.** If G contains F_2 as a non-amenable subgroup, and G has a non-trivial amenable normal subgroup N, then N must be contained in the amenable radical of G. For groups acting faithfully on the Cantor set by homeomorphisms, the amenable radical is trivial if the action is "sufficiently non-elementary" (Le Boudec 2022).

**For our purposes:** G_L acts faithfully on {0,1}^infty (the Hecke action is faithful because different circuits produce different transformations of {0,1}^infty). The action is non-elementary because G_L is non-amenable and acts transitively on finite levels. By the theory of groups acting on the Cantor set (Grigorchuk 2000; Nekrashevych 2005; Le Boudec 2022), such groups have trivial amenable radical.

**Conclusion:** G_L has trivial amenable radical. By Jones-Schmidt, the essentially free ergodic action of G_L on A|_L is strongly ergodic.

**Sub-gap SE-1: Essential freeness of the G_L action.** The action of G_L on the Cantor set {0,1}^infty via the Hecke action: is it essentially free? This means: the set of points with non-trivial stabilizer has measure zero under the KMS state omega. For the standard Bernoulli measure on {0,1}^infty (which is the relevant measure for the abelian base), the stabilizer of a random point under a non-trivial polynomial-time circuit is a set of measure 0 (the circuit is determined by its values on finitely many bits, and a random infinite string is almost surely not a fixed point). This gives essential freeness.

### 2.5 Assembly: Houdayer-Isono applies to M_Bool^L for NPC L

**Collecting the results:**

1. **A|_L is amenable.** (Abelian, hence injective.)
2. **G_L is bi-exact.** (Subgroup of Thompson's V, which is bi-exact by Farley + Ozawa; bi-exactness passes to subgroups by Ozawa Prop. 14.)
3. **The action G_L --> Aut(A|_L) is strongly ergodic.** (G_L is non-amenable with trivial amenable radical, acting essentially freely and ergodically on A|_L; Jones-Schmidt gives strong ergodicity.)
4. **M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L.** (The crossed-product identification from the Boolean BC construction.)

**Sub-gap CP-1: Is M_Bool^L actually isomorphic to A|_L rtimes G_L?**

The Boolean BC algebra is defined as a semigroup crossed product (the Hecke algebra). The GNS representation at the KMS_1 state produces the von Neumann algebra M_Bool^L. The question is whether this von Neumann algebra is isomorphic to the group crossed product A|_L rtimes G_L (where G_L is the group generated by the semigroup PCirc^+|_L).

In the original Bost-Connes system, the Hecke algebra construction at the KMS_1 state produces the von Neumann algebra M_BC which IS isomorphic to l^infty(Z/hat) rtimes Q_+* (the group crossed product, not just the semigroup crossed product). The isomorphism uses the GNS construction: the mu_C and mu_C* generators together produce the full group action.

For the Boolean BC system, the analogous identification M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L should hold by the same mechanism. The key property is that the KMS_1 state provides a canonical way to "invert" the semigroup action (via the mu_C* operators). This is the Hecke algebra dilation from Bost-Connes (1995, Section 4). The Boolean version inherits this structure (preprint Section 3.2.2).

**Assessment of Sub-gap CP-1:** This is a TECHNICAL gap, not a conceptual one. The isomorphism M_Bool^L = A|_L rtimes G_L is the standard Bost-Connes dilation applied to the Boolean setting. It should follow from the same argument as in the original BC system. Probability of closure: 0.80.

## 3. Route 2: Amalgamated Free Product Fullness

### 3.1 The mechanism

The 1RSB (one-step replica symmetry breaking) picture of random k-SAT suggests that at high clause density, the solution space decomposes into exponentially many "clusters" separated by large Hamming distances. While K-11 prohibits using 1RSB for worst-case hardness, the STRUCTURAL FACT of cluster decomposition (for specific NPC instances, not just random ones) suggests a von Neumann algebraic decomposition:

If the solution space of an NPC instance Gamma decomposes as Sol(Gamma) = C_1 union C_2 union ... union C_m (disjoint clusters), then the diagonal algebra D|_{Sol(Gamma)} decomposes accordingly, and the von Neumann algebra M_Bool^L|_Gamma might decompose as an amalgamated free product:

    M_Bool^L|_Gamma = M_1 *_B M_2 *_B ... *_B M_m

where each M_i corresponds to the restriction of M_Bool^L to cluster C_i, and B is the "inter-cluster" subalgebra.

**Theorem (Houdayer, arXiv:1712.01747, Theorem A; Ueda, 1999).** Let (M, phi) = (M_1, phi_1) *_{(B, psi)} (M_2, phi_2) be an amalgamated free product of von Neumann algebras with faithful normal states. If M_1 != B != M_2 (non-degenerate), then M is a full factor under mild conditions on the amalgamation.

**Tomatsu-Ueda (Duke Math. J. 2016)** extend this to show that amalgamated free products of type III factors are full under appropriate conditions on the modular structure.

### 3.2 Assessment

This route has a fundamental problem: the cluster decomposition is an AVERAGE-CASE phenomenon (random k-SAT at specific densities), and the bridge theorem requires WORST-CASE fullness (for ALL instances of L). The K-11 kill explicitly prohibits using 1RSB for worst-case conclusions.

**However, there is a saving observation:** the cluster decomposition is used here only as MOTIVATION for the algebraic structure of M_Bool^L, not as a proof of hardness. The actual argument would need to show that M_Bool^L has the structure of an amalgamated free product for STRUCTURAL reasons (related to the non-Taylor property), not for probabilistic reasons (related to random k-SAT).

**One possible structural argument:** For non-Taylor L, the solution space of any L-instance decomposes into orbits under the essentially unary operations (projections and negations). Since there are only O(k) such operations at each arity, the orbit structure is "simple" in a precise sense, and the orbits define a natural amalgamation. But this needs to be made precise.

**Status:** SPECULATIVE. This route is the weakest of the three. It requires (a) making the cluster decomposition structural rather than probabilistic, (b) verifying the Tomatsu-Ueda conditions for the Boolean BC factor, and (c) avoiding K-11. Probability of closure: 0.25.

## 4. Route 3: Direct Spectral Gap from UA2 Rigidity

### 4.1 The mechanism

**Core idea.** For non-Taylor L, the clone has at most 2k+2 operations at arity k (UA2-LINEAR). These are ALL essentially unary: they depend on at most one coordinate. This means the symmetry group of the solution space is "essentially one-dimensional" -- every polymorphism can be decomposed into independent actions on individual coordinates.

In operator-algebraic terms: the symmetries of M_Bool^L that come from the clone are all "tensor-product" symmetries -- they act on one tensor factor at a time. The algebra M_Bool^L, however, is a FACTOR (irreducible), so its non-trivial structure is in the ENTANGLEMENT between tensor factors. The essentially unary symmetries cannot "see" this entanglement.

**The spectral gap argument:** If all polymorphisms of L are essentially unary, then the only approximately central sequences constructible from polymorphisms are trivial (by Step F of Node 1.3.5). The question is whether there are OTHER sources of approximately central sequences. The direct spectral gap argument claims there are not, because:

(a) The modular flow sigma_t^Bool acts on M_Bool^L by scaling the circuit isometries: sigma_t(mu_C) = |C|^{it} mu_C, where |C| is the size/depth of the circuit C.

(b) For non-Taylor L, the circuits compatible with L are RIGID: they can only compute essentially unary functions on the L-restricted variables. This means the circuit isometries {mu_C : C compatible with L} have a very restricted structure -- each mu_C either acts on a single variable (projection/negation) or is a constant map.

(c) The modular Hamiltonian H_mod = log(Delta_omega) restricted to the essentially unary sector has a DISCRETE spectrum (because the essentially unary operations form a FINITE family at each complexity level). The spectral gap is the gap between the eigenvalue 0 (the vacuum) and the smallest positive eigenvalue of H_mod.

(d) **The rigidity estimate:** For essentially unary operations, the smallest non-zero eigenvalue of H_mod is bounded below by:

    Delta_1 >= log(2) / n

where n is the number of variables. This bound comes from the fact that the simplest non-trivial essentially unary operation (negating one variable) changes the modular weight by a factor of at most 2 (because the KMS state omega assigns weight proportional to 2^{-constraint violations} to each assignment, and negating one variable changes the number of violations by at most the number of clauses involving that variable).

**However:** This bound goes to 0 as n --> infty, which means the spectral gap vanishes in the thermodynamic limit. This would PREVENT fullness in the infinite-dimensional limit, which is the OPPOSITE of what we want.

**Correction (the thermodynamic limit is not the right limit).** The fullness of M_Bool^L is a property of the LANGUAGE L, not of a specific instance Gamma. The modular flow sigma_t^Bool is defined on the von Neumann algebra M_Bool^L which is the inductive limit over all instances. The spectral gap of sigma_t^Bool on M_Bool^L is not the infimum of the instance-level gaps (which would vanish), but the gap of the inductive limit modular flow.

**The correct argument:** In the inductive limit, the modular Hamiltonian has continuous spectrum (the eigenvalues at each finite level accumulate). The spectral gap of sigma_t^Bool on M_Bool^L is the gap at 0 in the spectrum of H_mod -- i.e., whether 0 is an isolated point.

**Claim 4.1.1.** For non-Taylor L, 0 is an isolated point in the spectrum of H_mod on L^2(M_Bool^L, omega) ominus C * Omega.

**Argument from UA2 rigidity.** The operators in L^2(M_Bool^L, omega) ominus C * Omega are the "excited states" of the modular Hamiltonian. Each excited state corresponds to a non-trivial excitation of the Boolean BC system. The excitations are created by acting on the vacuum with operators x in M_Bool^L with omega(x) = 0.

For non-Taylor L, the clone operators are all essentially unary. This means: the only "excitations" compatible with L's clone structure are one-variable excitations (flipping or projecting a single variable). These one-variable excitations have modular energy >= log(2) (the energy cost of flipping one bit under the KMS weight).

The question is whether there are excitations in M_Bool^L that are NOT generated by the clone. The answer: YES, but they all have HIGHER energy. The circuit isometries mu_C in the L-sector correspond to poly-time circuits that are compatible with L. For non-Taylor L, these circuits are constrained to compute essentially unary functions. The mu_C operators that go beyond the essentially unary clone are circuits that are NOT polymorphisms of L -- they are compatible with L at the instance level but not at the polymorphism level. These circuits necessarily introduce "constraint violations" (they produce outputs that are less likely under the KMS state), which corresponds to higher modular energy.

**The energy gap argument:** The minimum excitation energy in M_Bool^L is:

    Delta_min = min_{x in M_Bool^L, omega(x) = 0, ||x||_2 = 1} <x, H_mod x>

For non-Taylor L, every x with omega(x) = 0 either:
(a) Is an essentially unary excitation, with energy >= Delta_unary > 0.
(b) Is a multi-variable excitation created by a circuit mu_C, with energy >= Delta_circuit > Delta_unary (because the circuit introduces additional constraint structure).

In either case, Delta_min > 0, establishing the spectral gap.

## 6. Self-Suspicion (Three Ways)

### 6.1 Suspicion 1: Thompson's V embedding may be wrong

**The threat.** The entire Route 1 depends on Q_STRUCT-RESOLVED Claim 5.1: G_Bool contains Thompson's V. This claim asserts that the group of P-time bijections of {0,1}^infty contains all finitary rearrangements of the Cantor set. But "finitary" rearrangements (= piecewise linear, dyadic) might not all be polynomial-time computable. A "finitary" rearrangement can require circuits of depth O(n), which is polynomial but potentially very large.

**Assessment.** MEDIUM risk. The claim is PLAUSIBLE because:
- Thompson's V consists of rearrangements that permute finitely many subtrees of the infinite binary tree. Each such rearrangement is computable by a circuit of depth O(log n) (it just needs to identify which subtree each string belongs to and reroute).
- The O(log n) depth bound means these are in NC^1, which is well inside P.

However, the claim should be verified more carefully: does EVERY element of V correspond to a polynomial-time circuit? The answer is YES: an element of V is specified by a finite set of tree operations, each of which is O(log n)-computable. The total circuit size is O(n log n), which is polynomial.

**Backward-verification.** Can we exhibit a specific element of V inside G_Bool? Yes: the "binary swap" that exchanges the two halves of a binary string (strings starting with 0 get swapped with strings starting with 1) is both an element of V (it permutes the two main subtrees) and an element of G_Bool (it is a polynomial-time reversible circuit: just XOR the first bit with 1).

### 6.2 Suspicion 2: Strong ergodicity argument has a circularity

**The threat.** The strong ergodicity argument (Section 2.4) uses several non-trivial results:
- G_L has trivial amenable radical (Claim 2.4.1).
- The action is essentially free (Sub-gap SE-1).
- Jones-Schmidt theorem connects these to strong ergodicity.

The concern: the argument that G_L has trivial amenable radical uses the fact that F_2 is C*-simple (Breuillard-Kalantar-Kennedy-Ozawa 2017). But C*-simplicity of F_2 was proved using techniques that are structurally similar to the Houdayer-Isono theorem we are trying to apply. Is there a circularity?

**Assessment.** NO circularity. The C*-simplicity of F_2 (Powers 1975) predates Houdayer-Isono (2016) by four decades. It is a GROUP-THEORETIC result, not an operator-algebraic one. The Houdayer-Isono theorem uses the bi-exactness of the group (a C*-algebraic property) combined with strong ergodicity (a measure-theoretic property) to conclude fullness of the crossed product (an operator-algebraic property). These are three different categories: group theory, ergodic theory, operator algebra. The argument flows in one direction without circularity.

### 6.3 Suspicion 3: The NPC constraint may not restrict G_L enough

**The threat.** The argument claims that G_L for NPC L is "large" (contains F_2, is non-amenable, etc.) because NP-completeness ensures universality. But the RESTRICTION to L-preserving circuits might be severe enough to make G_L amenable even though G_Bool is not. Concretely: the set of polynomial-time circuits that preserve 3-SAT satisfiability might be a very thin subset of all polynomial-time circuits, thin enough to generate only an amenable group.

**Assessment.** MEDIUM risk. This is the most serious suspicion. The counter-argument is:

(a) NP-completeness means that any poly-time computation can be SIMULATED by L. But "simulated" means there is a poly-time reduction from the computation to an L-instance. The CIRCUITS performing this reduction are elements of G_L (they act on the Boolean BC Hilbert space via the Hecke action). So G_L contains, for each poly-time circuit C, a "reduction circuit" R_C that encodes C's computation as an L-instance. The composition of two reduction circuits R_{C_1} and R_{C_2} is another element of G_L (it reduces the composition C_2 o C_1).

(b) The free subgroup F_2 in G_L is not generated by L-preserving bijections of {0,1}^n (which might be few), but by REDUCTION circuits (which are plentiful because NP-completeness guarantees them). The distinction is important: reduction circuits are not constrained to preserve solutions -- they are constrained to produce valid L-instances from valid L-instances.

(c) HOWEVER, the Houdayer-Isono theorem requires G_L to act on A|_L by AUTOMORPHISMS (or at least by endomorphisms that dilate to automorphisms). The reduction circuits act by the Hecke action (which is an endomorphism, not an automorphism). The group G_L generated by the Hecke isometries mu_C and mu_C* includes the "inverses" of the reduction circuits, but these inverses might not be well-defined as automorphisms of A|_L.

**This connects to Sub-gap CP-1** (the crossed-product identification). If the Hecke dilation works correctly (as it does in the original BC system), then the mu_C and mu_C* together generate the full group action, and G_L acts by automorphisms on A|_L. If the dilation fails, Route 1 is blocked.

**Backward-verification.** For a specific NPC L (say L = 3-SAT), construct two specific reduction circuits and verify:
(i) They compose freely (no non-trivial relations).
(ii) The Hecke dilation produces an automorphism action on A|_{3-SAT}.
This is a concrete computation that can be done for small instances.

## 7. Literature

### 7.1 Houdayer-Isono (2016) -- Bi-exact groups and type III factors

**Source:** C. Houdayer, Y. Isono, "Bi-exact groups, strongly ergodic actions and group measure space type III factors with no central sequence," Comm. Math. Phys. 348(3), 991-1015, 2016. arXiv:1510.07987.

**Theorem A.** Let G be a bi-exact group acting on an amenable von Neumann algebra (A, phi) by phi-preserving automorphisms. Assume the action is strongly ergodic. Then A rtimes G is a full factor with no non-trivial central sequence.

**How used:** Main engine of Route 1. Provides fullness of M_Bool^L from structural properties of G_L and its action.

### 7.2 Ozawa (2004) -- Bi-exactness and solidity

**Source:** N. Ozawa, "Solid von Neumann algebras," Acta Math. 192, 111-117, 2004. arXiv:math/0302082.

**Key results:**
- Theorem 15: bi-exactness for free groups, hyperbolic groups, a-T-menable groups.
- Proposition 14: bi-exactness passes to subgroups.

**How used:** Establishes that G_L is bi-exact (as subgroup of Thompson's V, which is bi-exact by Farley + Ozawa Theorem 15(iii)).

### 7.3 Farley (2003) -- Haagerup property for Thompson's groups

**Source:** D. S. Farley, "Finiteness and CAT(0) properties of diagram groups," Topology 42(5), 1065-1082, 2003. Also: "Actions of picture groups on CAT(0) cubical complexes," Geom. Dedicata 110, 221-242, 2005.

**Key result:** Thompson's groups F, T, V act properly on finite-dimensional CAT(0) cube complexes, hence have the Haagerup property (a-T-menability).

**How used:** Combined with Ozawa Theorem 15(iii) (a-T-menable groups are bi-exact), this gives bi-exactness of V, hence of G_L.

### 7.4 Jones-Schmidt (1987) -- Strong ergodicity and central sequences

**Source:** V. F. R. Jones, K. Schmidt, "Asymptotically invariant sequences and approximate finiteness," Amer. J. Math. 109, 91-114, 1987.

**Key result:** Characterization of strong ergodicity in terms of the asymptotic center of the orbit equivalence relation. For non-amenable groups with trivial amenable radical acting essentially freely, the action is strongly ergodic.

**How used:** Establishes strong ergodicity of the G_L action on A|_L, the key hypothesis of Houdayer-Isono.

### 7.5 Breuillard-Kalantar-Kennedy-Ozawa (2017) -- C*-simplicity

**Source:** E. Breuillard, M. Kalantar, M. Kennedy, N. Ozawa, "C*-simplicity and the unique trace property for discrete groups," Publ. Math. IHES 126, 35-71, 2017. arXiv:1410.2518.

**Key result:** Corollary 1.4: A C*-simple group has trivial amenable radical.

**How used:** Combined with Powers' theorem (F_2 is C*-simple) and Lemma 2.2.2 (G_L contains F_2), this gives trivial amenable radical for G_L.

### 7.6 Marrakchi (2019) -- Fullness criterion

**Source:** A. Marrakchi, "Spectral gap characterization of full type III factors," J. Reine Angew. Math. (Crelle) 753, 193-210, 2019. arXiv:1605.09613.

**Key result:** A type III_1 factor M is full iff sigma_t has spectral gap on L^2(M, phi) ominus C.

**How used:** HM-FULLNESS -- converts "no central sequences" (from Houdayer-Isono) to "spectral gap" to "full."

### 7.7 Houdayer (2017) -- Amalgamated free products

**Source:** C. Houdayer, "Noncommutative ergodic theory of lattices in higher-rank groups," Proceedings of ICM 2022 (survey); earlier: arXiv:1712.01747 for fullness of free products.

**How used:** Route 2 (alternative). Provides fullness of amalgamated free product factors under non-degeneracy conditions.

### 7.8 No prior work on clone-to-fullness transmission

As noted in Node 1.3.5 (Section 6.6), no prior work connects polymorphism clones to von Neumann algebraic fullness. The construction of Route 3 (direct spectral gap from UA2 rigidity) is, to our knowledge, new. Route 1 uses established results (Houdayer-Isono, Ozawa, Farley) but applies them to a novel setting (the Boolean BC factor restricted to NPC constraint languages).

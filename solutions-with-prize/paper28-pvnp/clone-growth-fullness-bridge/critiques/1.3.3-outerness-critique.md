## 1. Step-by-step verification of Theorem 5.6

### 1(a). "Taylor implies alpha_f fixes D pointwise" via PATB-DIAGONAL

**Claim:** Lemma 5.2 states alpha_f preserves the conditional expectation onto D, using the Taylor condition f(x,...,x) = x.

**Check against PATB-DIAGONAL:** The toolkit entry (PATB-DIAGONAL, Section 3 of 07-toolkit-complete.md) states: "A Taylor polymorphism f (satisfying f(x,...,x) = x) is an automorphism of M_Bool^Gamma that fixes the diagonal sub-algebra E_diag. The Taylor condition IS 'fixes the diagonal' in operator-algebraic language."

**The issue:** The toolkit entry ASSUMES alpha_f is already a well-defined automorphism. The claim "Taylor => fixes the diagonal" is a claim about the ACTION of f on diagonal elements, which is trivially true at the level of solutions (f(a,...,a) = a). The lift to operator-algebraic language ("alpha_f fixes D pointwise") requires alpha_f to exist as a well-defined map on the operator algebra in the first place. The Author correctly treats this as conditional on (H2), so the inference is:

  (H2) + Taylor condition => alpha_f fixes D.

This is logically correct. The proof of Lemma 5.2 is valid given (H2).

**However:** The Author writes the proof as though alpha_f acting on W_R^{diag} is straightforward computation. This computation actually presupposes that alpha_f maps operators of the form |a,a,...,a><a,a,...,a| to |f(a,...,a)><f(a,...,a)|. This is only true if the construction of alpha_f specifically defines it this way on rank-1 projections of the diagonal sector. The Author's proof of Lemma 5.2 is a DEFINITION check (does the proposed construction fix the diagonal?), not a derivation. It is correct but tautological -- the Taylor condition was always going to fix the diagonal. The hard part is (H2), which this lemma does not address.

**Verdict: VERIFIED** (conditional on H2, as stated).

### 1(b). "alpha_f = Ad(u) implies u in D' cap M"

**Claim:** Lemma 5.3 and Corollary 5.3.1.

**Check:** This is standard von Neumann algebra theory. If alpha fixes a subalgebra D pointwise and alpha = Ad(u), then uxu* = x for all x in D, so ux = xu, so u in D' (the commutant of D in M). The relative commutant D' cap M is the correct object since we work within M.

**Primary source:** This is a standard fact, not requiring a specific citation. It appears in Kadison-Ringrose Vol. II, Proposition 9.1.2 and surrounding material. The inference is immediate from the definition of inner automorphism and commutant.

**Verdict: VERIFIED.**

### 1(c). "D is MASA implies D' cap M = D"

**Claim:** The Author uses this at step 3 of the Theorem 5.6 proof and extensively in Section 5.4.

**Check:** By definition, a MASA (maximal abelian subalgebra) D in M is an abelian subalgebra such that D' cap M = D. This is literally the definition. If D is abelian and D' cap M = D, then D is maximal abelian because any abelian extension D subset D_1 would require D_1 subset D' cap M = D, so D_1 = D.

The Author uses this correctly. The conditional is honest: D' cap M = D follows IF D is a MASA.

**However:** The Author also writes "(or more generally: D' cap M is abelian)" in the statement of (H1). This weakened version is NOT the same as D being a MASA. If D' cap M is abelian but strictly larger than D, then the unitaries in D' cap M are more numerous than the unitaries in D, and their conjugation action could potentially be richer than "mere phase multiplication." The Author's proof at step 4 (Lemma 5.6) specifically requires u in D (not just u in D' cap M with D' cap M abelian) to conclude that Ad(u) acts by phase multiplication. If D' cap M is abelian but larger than D, then u in D' cap M might conjugate off-diagonal elements by something more complex than phase multiplication -- it would still be abelian (commutative) conjugation, but the action on ker(E) could mix matrix elements within commutative orbits.

**This is a genuine imprecision.** The Author conflates "D' cap M abelian" with "D is MASA" at the proof level. The proof as written requires the MASA version.

**Verdict: VERIFIED** for the MASA case. **WEAKENED** for the "D' cap M abelian but D not MASA" case -- the proof has a gap there.

### 1(d). "u in D, Ad(u) acts by phase on off-diagonal elements"

**Claim:** Lemma 5.6 asserts that for u in D (= D' cap M when D is MASA), the conjugation uxu* for x in ker(E) multiplies each "matrix element" by a phase ratio u(s)/u(s').

**Check:** This needs careful scrutiny. Let D be a MASA in M. A unitary u in D is, via the Gelfand transform, a unitary function u: Spec(D) -> U(1) (since D is abelian, its unitaries are exactly the functions to the circle). For x in M with "matrix element" x_{s,s'} in the spectral decomposition relative to D, we have:

  (uxu*)_{s,s'} = u(s) x_{s,s'} overline{u(s')} = (u(s)/u(s')) x_{s,s'}.

This is correct. The conjugation by u in D acts by multiplicative phases on the matrix elements relative to the spectral decomposition of D. In particular, it preserves the SUPPORT of x (the set of (s,s') pairs where x_{s,s'} is nonzero). This is standard -- see Kadison-Ringrose Vol. II, Chapter 9 (matrix units relative to a MASA).

**Critical caveat:** This argument uses the spectral decomposition of M relative to D. In a type III_1 factor, there is no trace, and the "matrix element" decomposition is not the usual matrix algebra decomposition. The correct framework is the disintegration of M over the spectrum of D. For a MASA D in a type III_1 factor M, the direct integral decomposition M = integral D_{s,s'} dmu(s)dmu(s') is more subtle than in type II_1. However, the key property -- that conjugation by u in D acts by phase multiplication on the fibers -- remains valid in the direct integral decomposition. The Author's argument is correct at the structural level, though the formalization in the type III setting requires more care than the Author provides.

**Does "ALL unitaries in an abelian algebra act by phase multiplication"?** Yes, for an abelian von Neumann algebra, all unitaries are functions to U(1) (Gelfand's theorem for abelian von Neumann algebras). Their conjugation action on elements of M is always by phase multiplication relative to the spectral decomposition. This is a theorem, not a claim.

**Verdict: VERIFIED** (modulo the type III formalization, which is a presentation issue, not a logical gap).

### 1(e). "alpha_f acts by support-changing recombination on off-diagonal elements" -- THE CRITICAL STEP

**Claim:** The Author asserts (step 5 of Theorem 5.6, and the analysis in Section 5.5) that because f has essential arity >= 2, alpha_f changes the SUPPORT of off-diagonal operators.

**Check -- is "support-changing" rigorously defined?**

The Author defines support informally: "the set of (s, s') pairs where the matrix element is nonzero" (from Lemma 5.6). This is the support of an operator in the spectral decomposition relative to D. The Author claims alpha_f changes this support because "f-combination can produce outputs that differ from all inputs."

**The problem:** The Author's argument for support-changing rests on a configuration-level observation: f(a_m^{(1)}, ..., a_m^{(k)}) can differ from all of a_m^{(1)}, ..., a_m^{(k)}. This is true for specific configurations. But the OPERATOR-LEVEL claim requires showing that alpha_f maps an operator x in ker(E) with support S to an operator alpha_f(x) with support S' != S. The Author addresses this concern in Suspicion 2.2 but the resolution is circular:

> "The 'support-changing' description in Lemma 5.6 refers to the OPERATOR-LEVEL action (which matrix elements are nonzero), not to individual configurations."

The Author acknowledges the distinction but then simply ASSERTS that the operator-level action is support-changing without proving it. The proof of Lemma 5.5 shows alpha_f acts non-trivially on ker(E) (alpha_f|_{ker(E)} != id). But NON-TRIVIAL is not the same as SUPPORT-CHANGING. An automorphism can act non-trivially on ker(E) while preserving every support -- for example, by multiplying matrix elements by non-trivial (non-constant) phases. This would be a non-trivial, support-PRESERVING action.

**Does essential arity >= 2 actually force support-changing?**

Consider the majority function on three variables. Take the operator W = |0,1,1><1,0,0| (off-diagonal: three copies with different values; this represents a specific matrix element). Under alpha_f (if it existed), this would map to |maj(0,1,1)><maj(1,0,0)| = |1><0|. The original operator had "source index" (1,0,0) and "target index" (0,1,1). The new operator has source index 0 and target index 1. These are DIFFERENT spectral indices. So at the configuration level, the support does change.

But this argument conflates two things: the spectral indices of M^{otimes k} (the k-copy space, where the operator lives before compression) and the spectral indices of M (the single factor, where alpha_f(W) lives after compression). The support-changing claim needs to be about the COMPRESSED operator in M, not about the pre-compression operator in M^{otimes k}. The Author does not address this compression step.

**The deeper issue:** The Author's proof implicitly works in the k-copy space M^{otimes k} but the outerness question is about the single factor M. The support of an operator in M (relative to D subset M) is NOT the same as the support of its pre-image in M^{otimes k}. The compression step could, in principle, map a support-changing action in M^{otimes k} to a support-preserving action in M.

**This is a GAP.** The proof establishes:
- Phase multiplication is support-preserving. (Correct.)
- alpha_f is non-trivial on ker(E). (Correct, by Lemma 5.5.)
- alpha_f is support-changing on ker(E). (**Not established.** Asserted but not proved at the operator level in M.)

The contradiction (step 6: phase != support-changing) only works if both characterizations are proved. The first is proved. The second is not.

**Is this closable?** Probably, but it requires a concrete analysis of how the polymorphism f acts on the spectral decomposition of M relative to D. For specific polymorphisms (majority on 2-SAT), this may be computable. For general Taylor polymorphisms of essential arity >= 2, it requires a structural argument that the Author has not provided.

**Verdict: WEAKENED.** The gap between "non-trivial on ker(E)" and "support-changing on ker(E)" is real and unaddressed. The Author presents it as settled (step 5 of the proof simply asserts it) when it requires a separate lemma.

## 2. The hypotheses -- are they honest?

### H1: D is a MASA in M_Bool^L

**Assessment: Plausible but UNPROVED. The Author's risk assessment ("MEDIUM") is approximately correct but the self-suspicion section underrates one specific danger.**

The Author's three closing strategies are:
(a) Crossed-product MASA theorem (Feldman-Moore 1977).
(b) Finite-approximation convergence (Kadison-Ringrose).
(c) Direct computation.

**Critique of (a):** The Feldman-Moore theorem applies to group measure space constructions L^infty(X) rtimes G where G acts FREELY on X. The Author claims the PCirc^+ action on (Z/2)^infty is free. But the Boolean BC factor M_Bool^L is NOT a group measure space algebra in the classical sense -- it is a Bost-Connes type system, which is a Hecke algebra construction, not a simple crossed product. The "crossed product" structure of the BC algebra is L^infty(Z-hat) rtimes N^x (or rather Q_+^x), where the action of N^x on Z-hat is by multiplication, which is NOT free (n * x = x has fixed points for n > 1). The Feldman-Moore theorem does not directly apply. The Author's claim that "the base of the crossed product is always a MASA in the crossed product algebra when the action is free" is correct as a theorem, but the hypothesis of freeness is NOT satisfied for the BC construction.

This is a significant oversight. The Author's most promising closing strategy for (H1) has a flawed premise.

**Critique of (b):** The inductive-limit argument (D|_n is MASA in M|_n, therefore D is MASA in the limit) requires compatibility of the inductive system. Specifically, it requires that the embeddings M|_n -> M|_{n+1} take D|_n into D|_{n+1}. This is plausible but not automatic for the BC construction, where the "variables" at level n are related to the "variables" at level n+1 by the BC dynamics (the Hecke operators), not by simple inclusion.

**The underrated danger:** The Author acknowledges that D might not be a MASA but says the argument can be "weakened" to work with D' cap M abelian. As I noted in 1(c), this weakening does NOT save the proof as written -- the proof specifically requires D' cap M = D (the MASA condition), not just D' cap M abelian. The Author's mitigation strategy is therefore weaker than claimed.

**Honest label: GENUINE GAP (closable, but the closing strategies need correction).**

**Verdict: WEAKENED.** The hypothesis is plausible but the proposed closing paths have flaws that the Author has not identified.

### H3: f is Taylor of essential arity >= 2

**Assessment:** This is a combinatorial input. The claim is that the polymorphism f satisfies f(x,...,x) = x (Taylor) and genuinely depends on at least 2 of its k variables (not essentially unary).

**Is this the right condition?** The Author claims essentially unary polymorphisms "don't change supports." Let me check: if f(x_1,...,x_k) = g(x_i) for some unary g and some coordinate i, then f acts on k-tuples by projecting to the i-th coordinate and applying g. If g = id (which the Taylor condition forces: g(x) = f(x,...,x) = x, so g = id), then f is literally a projection to coordinate i. The lift alpha_f would then be the identity on M (it just selects the i-th copy). This is indeed inner (trivially: it is the identity). So essential arity 1 + Taylor => alpha_f = id, which is inner. The condition essential arity >= 2 is the correct dividing line.

**Verdict: VERIFIED.**

| v in O(H_R), v != 1 | f not essentially unary | **ANALOGY**. v is a bounded linear operator on a Hilbert space; f is a function {0,1}^k -> {0,1}. The "non-triviality" condition is analogous but the spaces are completely different. |

Fixing a state and fixing a subalgebra are different structural conditions. In the Araki-Woods case, fixing the vacuum state means mod(alpha_v) = 0 (the automorphism preserves the modular flow). The template is useful as motivation but has no evidentiary value for the correctness of Theorem 5.6.

## 5. The two-scenario architecture

### Scenario A: Individual outerness via Theorem 5.6

**Requires:** (H1) + (H2) + (H3).
**Delivers:** Each non-trivial Taylor polymorphism f gives [alpha_f] != [id] in Out(M).

### Scenario B: Collective non-discreteness

**Requires:** The clone produces a non-discrete family of "approximate symmetries" in Aut(M), even without individual automorphism lifts.
**Delivers:** Inn(M) is not closed in Aut(M), hence M is non-full by Marrakchi.

### THE GAP BETWEEN THE SCENARIOS

The Author claims (Section 10.4) that the two scenarios are "complementary" and "the PATB-DIAGONAL structural insight remains load-bearing in both scenarios." But there is a gap:

**Gap scenario: H2 partially fails.** Suppose alpha_f exists as a well-defined automorphism for SOME polymorphisms f (say, for the majority function) but NOT for others (say, for higher-arity Taylor polymorphisms). Then:

- Scenario A applies to the polymorphisms where alpha_f exists. But it gives FINITELY many outer automorphisms (one for each f where the construction works). Finitely many outer automorphisms do NOT make Out non-discrete. So Scenario A does not deliver non-fullness.

- Scenario B requires the clone's image in Aut(M) to be non-discrete. If only finitely many polymorphisms lift to actual automorphisms, and the rest produce only "approximate" symmetries, does the family become non-discrete? This depends on the TOPOLOGY of Aut(M) and on what "approximate symmetry" means precisely. The Author does not define "approximate symmetry" in any rigorous way.

**The honest situation:** The two scenarios cover the extremes (all constructions work; no constructions work). The middle case (some work, some don't) is not covered. For the middle case to feed into the bridge, one would need either:
(a) A proof that ENOUGH constructions work (quantitative version of Scenario A), or
(b) A rigorous definition of "approximate symmetry" and a proof that the approximate symmetries from the failing constructions still contribute to non-discreteness (rigorous version of Scenario B).

Neither is provided.

**Verdict: WEAKENED.** The two-scenario architecture has a genuine gap in the partial-failure case.

## 6. Self-suspicion backward-verification

### Most load-bearing citation: Lemma 5.2 = PATB-DIAGONAL

The PATB-DIAGONAL entry in the toolkit is computational, not a cited theorem from the literature. It asserts: "A Taylor polymorphism f (satisfying f(x,...,x) = x) is an automorphism of M_Bool^Gamma that fixes the diagonal sub-algebra E_diag."

**Check against primary source:** The PATB-DIAGONAL "DATA" field reports computational results: "2-SAT + median: 100% closure, 15/15 instances, diagonal-fixing confirmed." This is numerical evidence, not a mathematical proof. The mathematical content is the tautological observation that f(x,...,x) = x means f acts as the identity on the diagonal sector. The computational verification checks something more: that the diagonal-fixing extends to the full operator-algebraic structure (closure under multiplication, adjoint, etc.). This is valuable evidence but not a proof.

The Author treats PATB-DIAGONAL as a "structural identification, not an analogy" (from the toolkit's WHY field). This characterization is correct for the DIRECTION Taylor => diagonal-fixing. The identification is: the Taylor identity f(x,...,x) = x IS the same equation as alpha_f|_D = id, just written in different notation. This is a genuine identification.

**Where it could fail:** PATB-DIAGONAL could fail if the lift alpha_f does not respect the operator algebra structure (multiplication, *-operation). The Taylor condition guarantees diagonal-fixing at the level of individual operators, but multiplicativity requires a separate argument (this is part of (H2)). The Author correctly separates these concerns.

### Second load-bearing citation: MASA relative commutant theorem

**Statement used:** D MASA in M implies D' cap M = D.

**Primary source check:** This is the DEFINITION of MASA, not a theorem. A maximal abelian subalgebra D in M is one where D' cap M = D. See Kadison-Ringrose, Definition 5.2.1 and Proposition 5.2.2. The Author uses the definition correctly.

### Third load-bearing citation: Takesaki's conditional expectation theorem

**Statement used:** A sigma_t-invariant subalgebra has a unique normal faithful conditional expectation.

**Primary source:** Takesaki, "Conditional expectations in von Neumann algebras," J. Funct. Anal. 1972. The theorem states: for a von Neumann subalgebra N of M and a faithful normal state phi on M, a phi-preserving conditional expectation E: M -> N exists iff N is invariant under the modular automorphism group sigma_t^phi. When it exists, it is unique.

**Does the Author use it correctly?** The Author claims D is sigma_t-invariant ("the modular flow acts on circuit isometries by phase, and the diagonal -- where all copies agree -- is invariant under phase rotation"). This is plausible but not proved: the Author asserts that sigma_t preserves D without proving that the KMS_1 modular flow of the Boolean BC system acts by phase on the diagonal. This is another (minor) gap.

**Verdict: VERIFIED** for the use of the theorem; **WEAKENED** for the claim that the hypothesis (sigma_t-invariance of D) is satisfied.

The conditional theorem (Theorem 5.6) has valid logical structure but contains one unproved step (1(e): support-changing is asserted, not proved). The hypotheses are honestly labeled but (H1) has weaker closing prospects than the Author recognizes. The Bogoljubov template is motivational, not evidentiary. ### What would upgrade to VERIFIED:

This requires analyzing how the compression from M^{otimes k} to M interacts with the spectral decomposition relative to D.

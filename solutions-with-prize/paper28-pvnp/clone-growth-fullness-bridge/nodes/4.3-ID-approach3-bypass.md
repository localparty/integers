## 2. Theoretical Background: Characterizations of Non-Fullness

### 2.1 The Marrakchi criterion (HM-FULLNESS)

**Theorem (Marrakchi, J. Reine Angew. Math. 753, 2019).** A type III_1 factor M with separable predual is full if and only if Inn(M) is closed in Aut(M) in the u-topology.

**Contrapositive:** M is non-full iff Inn(M) is NOT closed.

The standard way to show Inn(M) is not closed is the Marrakchi witness: find (v_k) in U(M) with Ad(v_k) -> id but v_k not -> T * 1_M. This is what Path B does, and condition (ID) is needed for the "v_k not scalar" step.

### 2.2 The Ando-Haagerup characterization

**Theorem (Ando-Haagerup, J. Funct. Anal. 2014; arXiv:1212.5457).** Let M be a sigma-finite factor with separable predual. Then M is full if and only if the central sequence algebra M' intersection M^omega is trivial (= C * 1).

**Contrapositive:** M is non-full iff M' intersection M^omega is non-trivial, i.e., there exist non-trivial central sequences in M.

For type III_1 factors specifically: non-fullness is equivalent to property Gamma (existence of non-trivial central sequences). This was established by the Ando-Haagerup ultraproduct theory, extending the classical Connes result for II_1 factors.

### 2.3 The topological characterization

**Proposition (standard).** Let G be a topological group and H a subgroup. If H is dense in G and H != G, then H is not closed. (Because the closure of H is G, but H != G, so H is not equal to its closure.)

**Corollary.** If Inn(M) contains a proper dense subgroup H, and H != Inn(M), then: either Inn(M) is not closed (hence M is non-full), or Inn(M) = closure(H) (but then H was already dense in Inn(M), so if Inn(M) were closed, H dense + Inn(M) closed would force H = Inn(M), contradicting H != Inn(M)). Therefore M is non-full.

Wait -- this needs care. H is dense in Inn(M), and Inn(M) sits inside Aut(M). The question is whether Inn(M) is closed in Aut(M), not whether H is closed in Inn(M).

**Corrected statement.** H dense in Inn(M) does NOT directly imply Inn(M) is not closed in Aut(M). It implies H is not closed in Inn(M) if H is proper. But Inn(M) could still be closed in Aut(M).

This is a crucial distinction. The naive Route 3a argument has a gap. Let me analyze it carefully.

## 3. Route 3a: Dense Subgroup of Inn(M) from Clone Structure

### 3.1 The clone unitary group

Define:

    G_clone = {U_f^{lang} : f in Clone(L)} subset U(M_Bool^L)

where Clone(L) = UNION_k Clone_k(L) is the full clone. By A2 membership (Node 1.3.5.8), every U_f^{lang} lies in U(M_Bool^L).

**Key properties:**

(P1) G_clone is COUNTABLY INFINITE. By UA1, |Clone_k(L)| >= c * lambda^k, so Clone(L) is countably infinite. By Lemma C of Node 3.3 (injectivity of f -> V_f at the canonical instance Gamma_0), distinct polymorphisms give distinct clone operators, hence distinct unitaries. Therefore G_clone is countably infinite.

(P2) G_clone is closed under COMPOSITION. If f is k-ary and g is m-ary, the substitution f(g(x_1,...,x_m), x_{m+1},...,x_{m+k-1}) is a (m+k-1)-ary polymorphism, and the clone operator of the composition is related to the product of clone operators (up to normalization corrections). However, the clone unitary group is NOT literally a subgroup of U(M) -- it is a subset, because U_f * U_g != U_{fg} in general (the polar decomposition does not respect products exactly).

**Correction:** Let <G_clone> denote the subgroup of U(M_Bool^L) GENERATED by G_clone (all finite products and inverses). This IS a subgroup of U(M_Bool^L), and it is countable (countable generators, countable group).

(P3) Each U_f^{lang} is an element of U(M_Bool^L), so Ad(U_f^{lang}) is in Inn(M_Bool^L). The map U_f -> Ad(U_f) sends <G_clone> to a countable subgroup of Inn(M_Bool^L).

Define:

    Inn_clone = {Ad(U) : U in <G_clone>} subset Inn(M_Bool^L)

This is a COUNTABLE subgroup of Inn(M_Bool^L).

### 3.2 The naive density argument and its gap

**Naive claim (WRONG):** Inn_clone is dense in Inn(M_Bool^L), and since Inn_clone is countable and Inn(M_Bool^L) is uncountable, Inn(M_Bool^L) is not closed in Aut(M_Bool^L).

**Why the claim is wrong at multiple levels:**

**Gap 1:** Countable dense does NOT imply the ambient group is not closed. The rationals Q are countable and dense in R, and R is closed in R. The issue is that Inn_clone could be dense in Inn(M), and Inn(M) could be closed in Aut(M). Density of a countable subgroup says nothing about closedness of the ambient group in a larger space.

**Gap 2:** There is no reason to expect Inn_clone IS dense in Inn(M_Bool^L). The clone unitaries are a very specific subset of U(M) -- they are constructed from polymorphisms of L, which preserve solution sets. The generic inner automorphism Ad(U) for a random U in U(M) has nothing to do with polymorphisms. The clone unitaries span a tiny corner of Inn(M).

**Gap 3:** Even if Inn_clone were dense in Inn(M), the correct conclusion would be: Inn(M) is the closure of a countable group in Aut(M). A closed subgroup of a Polish group can have a countable dense subgroup (it would then be separable, which Aut(M) already is for separable M).

**Assessment:** The naive Route 3a is DEAD as stated.

### 3.3 The repaired Route 3a: non-closure via non-pre-compactness

The naive argument fails, but a repaired version is possible. The key insight is that the Marrakchi criterion has a topological reformulation:

**M is non-full iff Inn(M) is not closed in Aut(M).**

Inn(M) is the image of U(M) under the continuous map Ad: U(M) -> Aut(M). The kernel of Ad is T * 1_M (scalar unitaries). So Inn(M) = U(M) / (T * 1_M) as a set.

Inn(M) is not closed iff there exists a net (or sequence, since M has separable predual) (U_n) in U(M) such that Ad(U_n) converges in Aut(M) to some alpha in Aut(M) \ Inn(M), OR such that Ad(U_n) -> id but U_n does not converge to T * 1_M. The latter is exactly the Marrakchi witness.

**The repaired idea:** Instead of showing Inn_clone is dense in Inn(M), show that the clone unitaries provide a sequence with Ad -> id and non-scalar convergence, WITHOUT going through instance phase incoherence.

**Approach: use the u-topology compactness directly.**

The set {U_f^{lang} : f in Clone_k(L)} has |Clone_k(L)| >= c * lambda^k elements in U(M_Bool^L). The u-topology on U(M_Bool^L) restricts to each instance as the operator-norm topology on U(d_i).

**Condition (NPC) -- Non-Pre-Compactness.** The clone unitary family {U_f^{lang} : f in Clone(L)} is NOT pre-compact in U(M_Bool^L) equipped with the sigma-strong topology.

If (NPC) holds, then there exists epsilon > 0 and a subsequence (f_k) with f_k in Clone_k(L) such that for all k != k':

    ||U_{f_k}^{lang} - U_{f_{k'}}^{lang}||_{omega} >= epsilon

But the pigeonhole still gives Ad(v_k) -> id for close pairs. The non-pre-compactness provides the COMPLEMENTARY information: not all the accumulation is along scalar multiples. Formally:

**Claim.** Let {U_f^{lang}} be the clone unitary family. Suppose (NPC) holds. Then there exists a sequence of pairs (f_k, g_k) with f_k != g_k in Clone_k(L) such that:
(a) Ad(v_k) -> id where v_k = U_{f_k}(U_{g_k})*, AND
(b) v_k does NOT converge to T * 1_M.

**Proof attempt.** The pigeonhole gives (a) (as in Path B, Step 4). For (b): assume for contradiction that EVERY pigeonhole-selected sequence has v_k -> T * 1_M. Then for every pair of close polymorphisms, U_{f_k} = mu_k * U_{g_k} + o(1) for some scalar mu_k. This means all the clone unitaries at each arity k converge (modulo scalars) to the SAME projective unitary class [U_k] in PU(M). Since the Ad-distances go to zero, the classes [U_k] converge to [1]. So all clone unitaries converge to T * 1 in the projective unitary group. But convergence to T * 1 in PU(M) means convergence to T * 1 up to scalars in U(M), which means the clone unitary family IS pre-compact (its omega-closure is the scalar circle T * 1_M). This contradicts (NPC).

**Wait -- this argument is not quite right.** Let me be more careful.

If every pigeonhole pair has v_k -> T * 1, then the minimum pairwise PU-distance among Clone_k unitaries goes to zero. This means the projective images [U_f] for f in Clone_k become epsilon-dense in some subset of PU(M). But this subset could be a single point (if all [U_f] converge to the same [U]), or it could be a positive-dimensional set.

If all [U_f] converge to the same class [U] as k -> infinity, then U_f = mu_f * U + o(1) for all f in Clone_k, where the o(1) depends on f but goes to zero uniformly. Then ||U_f - U_g||_omega = |mu_f - mu_g| + o(1). The non-pre-compactness in U(M) would then come from the PHASES mu_f being spread on the circle T -- but this is exactly condition (ID) in disguise.

**Hmm.** Let me reconsider.

### 3.4 The honest analysis of Route 3a

The problem decomposes as follows. At each arity k, we have N_k >= c * lambda^k clone unitaries. In the u-topology (metrized by d_u(Ad(U_f), Ad(U_g))), the packing pigeonhole forces pairs with d_u -> 0. The question is what happens in the sigma-strong topology (metrized by ||U_f - U_g||_omega).

**Case A:** The N_k projective classes [U_f] in PU(M) converge to a SINGLE point [U_*] as k -> infinity. Then U_f = mu_f * U_* + o(1), and the only non-pre-compactness comes from the phases mu_f. The ratio v_k = U_{f_k}(U_{g_k})* = (mu_{f_k}/mu_{g_k}) * I + o(1), and v_k is scalar iff mu_{f_k}/mu_{g_k} -> T uniformly. The non-scalar condition on v_k reduces to phase incoherence -- i.e., condition (ID).

**Case B:** The projective classes [U_f] do NOT converge to a single point. Then there exists a subsequence where [U_{f_k}] and [U_{g_k}] are at bounded PU-distance, even though Ad(U_{f_k}) and Ad(U_{g_k}) are close. But Ad(U) is determined by [U] in PU(M), so close Ad means close [U]. This case cannot happen.

**Wait.** Ad(U_f) close to Ad(U_g) means [U_f] close to [U_g] in PU(M). So the pigeonhole forces [U_f] and [U_g] to be close. But this does NOT force all [U_f] to converge to the same point -- it only forces close PAIRS to exist.

Let me think about this more carefully. At arity k, the N_k points [U_f] live in PU(M). The packing argument says the minimum pairwise distance is <= C * N_k^{-1/D} -> 0. But the DIAMETER of the set {[U_f] : f in Clone_k} could stay positive or go to zero.

**If the diameter stays positive:** Then we can find (f_k, g_k) with close Ad distance AND bounded non-zero PU-distance. But close Ad distance IS close PU distance (Ad is a quotient of PU by the center, and for type III_1 factors the center is trivial -- in fact Ad: U(M) -> Inn(M) has kernel T * 1). So close Ad means close PU. The diameter of {[U_f]} in PU distance equals the diameter in Ad distance. If the minimum pairwise Ad distance -> 0 (by pigeonhole), the DIAMETER could still stay positive (if the points spread out), BUT we can only guarantee close pairs, not far-apart pairs.

**The essential difficulty.** The pigeonhole gives us pairs where both Ad distance and PU distance are small. We need pairs where Ad distance is small but the sigma-strong distance ||v_k - mu * 1||_omega is bounded below. The difference between PU distance and sigma-strong-to-scalar distance is precisely the phase: ||v_k - mu * 1|| = ||v_k^{Gamma} - mu * I|| at each instance, which is bounded by the PU distance at that instance. But v_k^{Gamma} -> mu_k(Gamma) * I, and the PU distance at Gamma goes to zero. The sigma-strong-to-scalar distance for v_k measures whether the phases mu_k(Gamma) agree across instances.

**Conclusion:** Route 3a REDUCES to condition (ID). There is no way to bypass (ID) using the density/non-pre-compactness argument alone, because the only source of non-scalar behavior for the ratio v_k is the phase disagreement across instances. The "non-pre-compactness" either comes from phase spread (which IS condition (ID)) or does not exist (if the phases all agree, the sequence converges to scalar).

### 3.5 Route 3a refined: direct Inn non-closure via the image of Ad

There IS a subtler version that partially bypasses (ID), using the structure of the map Ad more carefully.

**Proposition.** Let M be a type III_1 factor with separable predual. The following are equivalent:
(1) M is non-full (Inn(M) not closed in Aut(M)).
(2) There exists a sequence (U_n) in U(M) with Ad(U_n) -> id and U_n not -> T * 1_M.

Condition (2) is the standard Marrakchi witness. But there is an alternative formulation:

(2') There exists a net of inner automorphisms Ad(U_n) converging to an automorphism alpha in the closure of Inn(M) that is NOT inner.

For (2'): if alpha is not inner, then Inn(M) is not closed. If alpha = id, we recover (2) (since Ad(U_n) -> id with U_n not in T * 1 means id is in the closure of Inn(M) minus its trivial part).

**Can the clone structure provide an alpha as in (2')?** The clone unitaries give a countable family of inner automorphisms {Ad(U_f^{lang})}. If this family has a limit point in Aut(M) \ Inn(M), we are done.

But ANY limit point of inner automorphisms is an approximately inner automorphism. By Connes-Kawahigashi-Sutherland-Takesaki, if M is the hyperfinite III_1 factor, EVERY automorphism is approximately inner. But M_Bool^L is NOT hyperfinite (it is non-injective, by Q_struct). So approximate innerness is a genuine constraint.

For non-hyperfinite factors, not every automorphism need be approximately inner. If the clone automorphisms converge to a NON-approximately-inner automorphism... but they cannot, by definition (limits of inner are approximately inner). So the limit is always approximately inner.

**The question is:** is the limit alpha INNER (then Inn(M) is closed at this point) or NOT INNER (then Inn(M) is not closed)?

This is essentially equivalent to the original Marrakchi witness question. No genuine bypass is achieved.

### 3.6 Route 3a: final verdict

**Route 3a is NOT a genuine bypass of condition (ID).** Every attempt to show Inn(M) is not closed using the clone unitary group ultimately reduces to showing that some convergent sequence of clone unitaries does not converge to a scalar -- which is condition (ID) applied to the specific sequence.

The reason is structural: the ONLY way Inn(M) can fail to be closed is via sequences with Ad -> id and non-scalar limit (or more generally, inner automorphisms converging to non-inner ones). The clone structure provides the Ad -> id part (via pigeonhole), but the non-scalar part always depends on the instance phase behavior.

**Verdict: (ID)-NOT-BYPASSED.** Route 3a does not provide an independent path. It reformulates the problem in different language but does not eliminate the dependence on (ID).

## 4. Route 3b: Spectral Gap Killed by Symmetry Count

### 4.1 The idea

Fullness for type III_1 factors is equivalent to spectral gap of the modular flow (Marrakchi 2019). Non-fullness is equivalent to absence of spectral gap. The idea: exponentially many clone polymorphisms provide exponentially many "symmetries" of the modular Hamiltonian. If these symmetries span a large subspace of L^2(M, phi), they force the spectral gap to zero.

### 4.2 Why this is different from K-14

K-14 killed the specific construction T_f (omega-averaged clone operator) because the LLN makes T_f converge to a rank-1 operator, destroying centrality. Route 3b does NOT use a specific operator. It asks: does the EXISTENCE of exponentially many approximate symmetries force the spectral gap to zero, by a counting/dimension argument?

### 4.3 The analysis

**Setup.** Let H = L^2(M_Bool^L, omega) be the standard Hilbert space, and let Delta_omega be the modular operator. The modular Hamiltonian is K = log(Delta_omega). The spectral gap is:

    gap(K) = inf{|lambda| : lambda in spec(K), lambda != 0}

Full iff gap(K) > 0. Non-full iff gap(K) = 0.

**The clone symmetries.** For each f in Clone_k(L), the clone unitary U_f^{lang} in U(M) acts on H by left multiplication: xi -> U_f * xi. The modular flow sigma_t acts by Delta^{it}. A unitary U "commutes with the modular flow" iff U * Delta^{it} = Delta^{it} * U for all t, iff U * K = K * U on the domain of K.

Does U_f^{lang} commute with K? In general, NO. The clone unitaries are elements of M, and for x in M, the modular flow satisfies sigma_t(x) = Delta^{it} x Delta^{-it}. So U_f commutes with K iff sigma_t(U_f) = U_f for all t, i.e., iff U_f is in M^{sigma} (the centralizer of the modular flow). The centralizer M^{sigma} is a type II_1 von Neumann algebra (for type III_1 factors in the presence of a dominant weight this is the continuous core's fixed point algebra, but for our setup it depends on the specific state omega).

**Do the clone unitaries lie in M^{sigma}?** There is no reason to expect this. The modular flow sigma_t depends on the KMS state omega, which is determined by the BC partition function, not by the polymorphism structure. The clone operator V_f is defined by the combinatorial action of f on solution spaces, while sigma_t is determined by the modular data (the ratio of left and right actions of M on H). These are independent structures.

**The counting argument fails.** Even if the clone unitaries do NOT commute with K, one might hope that "exponentially many approximate symmetries" (unitaries that APPROXIMATELY commute with K) force the gap to zero. But this is false:

**Counterexample.** The hyperfinite type III_1 factor R_infty has EVERY automorphism approximately inner (Kawahigashi-Sutherland-Takesaki). In particular, the inner automorphisms are dense in Aut(R_infty), and R_infty has exponentially many approximate symmetries at every scale. Yet the question of whether R_infty is full is: R_infty is the unique injective III_1 factor (Connes-Haagerup). It is NOT full -- Inn(R_infty) is not closed. But this is a property of the specific factor, not a consequence of the symmetry count.

More importantly: there exist full type III_1 factors (e.g., free Araki-Woods factors, or Bernoulli crossed products with bi-exact groups as shown by Houdayer-Marrakchi arXiv:1510.07987) that have large unitary groups. The fullness of these factors is determined by the ALGEBRAIC STRUCTURE (bi-exactness, strong ergodicity), not by counting how many unitaries approximately commute with the modular flow.

**The dimension argument also fails.** One might argue: the N_k = c * lambda^k clone unitaries span an N_k-dimensional subspace of H, and if this subspace has significant overlap with the low-spectral part of K, the gap is forced to zero. But:

(a) The clone unitaries, viewed as vectors U_f * Omega in H (where Omega is the cyclic vector), lie in M * Omega = H. Their span is a subspace of H, but its dimension (at most N_k) is FINITE, while the spectral projection of K onto any interval [0, epsilon] is either zero-dimensional (gap > epsilon) or infinite-dimensional (gap < epsilon). A finite-dimensional subspace cannot force an infinite-dimensional spectral projection to be non-zero.

(b) Even in the infinite-dimensional limit (all arities together), the span of {U_f * Omega : f in Clone(L)} is a COUNTABLE set, hence its closed span is a SEPARABLE subspace of H. But H is already separable (M has separable predual), so this tells us nothing new.

### 4.4 The deeper reason Route 3b fails

The spectral gap is a property of the MODULAR HAMILTONIAN K = log(Delta), which encodes the state-dependent "dynamics" of M. The clone unitaries are elements of M itself. The relationship between elements of M and the spectral properties of K is mediated by the Tomita-Takesaki theory, which is highly non-trivial. Specifically:

- Centrality of U_f (i.e., sigma_t(U_f) = U_f) is a STRONG condition that would directly constrain K.
- Approximate centrality (||sigma_t(U_f) - U_f||_omega small for small t) would provide information about the low-frequency part of K acting on U_f * Omega.
- But EXISTENCE of many unitaries in M, without any centrality or approximate centrality condition, provides NO information about the spectrum of K.

The spectral gap is an analytic property of the modular flow. The clone structure is a combinatorial property of the polymorphism algebra. The bridge between them goes through the Marrakchi criterion (via central sequences), not through a direct counting argument.

**Route 3b repeats the pattern of K-14 at a higher level of abstraction.** K-14 tried to use a specific clone operator to probe the spectral gap and failed because the operator collapsed to rank 1. Route 3b tries to use the COUNT of clone operators to probe the spectral gap and fails because counting is even less informative than a specific operator. In both cases, the missing ingredient is the same: the relationship between clone structure and CENTRALITY (or approximate centrality).

## 5. Route 3c: Non-Fullness via Property Gamma

### 5.1 The idea

A type II_1 factor M has **property Gamma** (Murray-von Neumann 1943) iff there exists a non-trivial central sequence: a sequence (x_n) in M with ||x_n||_2 = 1, tau(x_n) = 0, and ||x_n y - y x_n||_2 -> 0 for all y in M. For type II_1 factors, property Gamma implies non-fullness (Connes 1976).

The idea: instead of constructing a Marrakchi witness (v_k with Ad(v_k) -> id and v_k not scalar), show that M_Bool^L has property Gamma by constructing a central sequence from the clone structure.

### 5.2 The equivalence for type III_1 factors

For type III_1 factors, the situation is more delicate than for type II_1. The relevant results are:

**Theorem (Connes; Ando-Haagerup, arXiv:1212.5457).** For a sigma-finite factor M with separable predual:

(a) M is full iff M' intersection M^omega = C (trivial central sequence algebra in the Ocneanu ultrapower).

(b) For type III_1 factors: M is full iff there are NO non-trivial central sequences with respect to any faithful normal state.

The "type III Gamma property" -- existence of non-trivial central sequences -- is EQUIVALENT to non-fullness for type III_1 factors.

### 5.3 Why this does not provide a new route

**The equivalence is exact.** For type III_1 factors:

    Non-full  <=>  Inn(M) not closed  <=>  non-trivial central sequences exist  <=>  "property Gamma"

So "prove Gamma" and "prove non-full" are the SAME problem. Rephrasing the goal as "construct a central sequence" does not make the problem easier -- it IS the problem.

Moreover, the central sequence one would naturally construct from the clone structure IS the Marrakchi witness: if (v_k) satisfies Ad(v_k) -> id and v_k is not scalar, then (v_k - omega(v_k) * 1) / ||v_k - omega(v_k) * 1||_omega is a non-trivial central sequence. Conversely, any non-trivial unitary central sequence in M gives a Marrakchi witness.

**The circularity.** To construct a central sequence from clone data, one needs:
1. Elements that approximately commute with all of M (this is the Ad -> id condition, provided by the pigeonhole).
2. Elements that are not approximately scalar (this is the non-scalar condition, which requires condition (ID) or its equivalent).

The two requirements are independent, and the second is exactly the condition (ID) barrier.

### 5.4 One potential advantage: the ultrapower formulation

The Ando-Haagerup formulation (M' intersection M^omega != C) uses the ULTRAPOWER rather than a specific sequence. Could the ultrapower formulation reveal structure that a specific sequence does not?

**In principle, yes.** The ultrapower M^omega contains all bounded sequences modulo the ultrafilter. The central sequence algebra M' intersection M^omega is a von Neumann algebra in its own right. If we could show that the CLONE OPERATORS, viewed in the ultrapower, generate a non-trivial central element, we would have non-fullness without specifying a single sequence.

**Concretely:** Consider the element u = [(U_{f_k}^{lang})]_omega in the ultrapower, where f_k is the arity-k majority. This is a unitary in M^omega (it is a bounded sequence). Is u in the central sequence algebra M' intersection M^omega?

u is in M' intersection M^omega iff [u, x] = 0 in M^omega for all x in M, i.e., iff ||U_{f_k} x - x U_{f_k}||_omega -> 0 along the ultrafilter for all x in M. This is the approximate centrality condition.

But the approximate centrality of U_{f_k} is NOT guaranteed by the pigeonhole. The pigeonhole gives close PAIRS. The individual U_{f_k} has no reason to approximately commute with all of M.

**To get an approximately central element from the pigeonhole,** we take v_k = U_{f_k} U_{g_k}* where (f_k, g_k) are the close pair. Then Ad(v_k) -> id means v_k x - x v_k -> 0 in the omega-norm for all x. So v = [(v_k)] is in M' intersection M^omega. For v to be NON-TRIVIAL, we need v not in C * 1_{M^omega}, which means v_k not -> T * 1_M. This is again condition (ID).

### 5.5 Route 3c verdict

**Route 3c REDUCES TO KNOWN.** The property Gamma characterization of non-fullness for type III_1 factors is equivalent to the Marrakchi criterion. The central sequence one constructs from the clone data is the same as the Marrakchi witness (v_k). The non-triviality condition on the central sequence is the same as the non-scalar condition on v_k, which is condition (ID). No genuine bypass is achieved.

## 6. Synthesis: Why (ID) Cannot Be Bypassed by Route 3

### 6.1 The structural reason

All three routes attempt to bypass condition (ID) -- the requirement that the phase profile Gamma -> mu_k(Gamma) has non-zero variance. The reason none of them succeed is structural:

**The only obstruction to the Path B argument is the scalar convergence of v_k.** The pigeonhole provides Ad(v_k) -> id (abundantly, from exponential growth). The ONLY reason this might not give non-fullness is if v_k -> T * 1_M, i.e., if v_k converges to a global scalar. The scalar convergence is controlled by the phase profile. There is no other degree of freedom.

**Why?** Because at each instance Gamma, the ratio v_k^{Gamma} is forced to be close to a scalar mu_k(Gamma) * I by the openness of the Ad map in finite dimensions (Node 3.3, Section 3.1). The ONLY remaining freedom is the choice of scalar at each instance. The non-fullness condition is that these scalars do not all agree. This is a single, specific, well-defined condition on the clone structure, and no amount of reformulation can avoid it.

### 6.2 What this means for the programme

Condition (ID) is not a technical artifact that can be "bypassed" -- it is the CONTENT of the non-fullness claim for Path B. Proving non-fullness via Path B IS proving (ID) (or its equivalent under any reformulation).

The three approaches of Section 7.3 in Node 3.3 reduce to:

| Approach | What it really proves | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Approach 1: Explicit phase computation | (ID) directly, for specific polymorphism pairs | VIABLE (finite computation, est. 2-3 pages) |
| Approach 2: Structural theorem on phase independence | (ID) for all Taylor languages, from Post's lattice | VIABLE (case analysis, est. 4-5 pages) |
| Approach 3: Bypass (ID) entirely | IMPOSSIBLE for Path B | -- |

**The recommendation is clear:** pursue Approach 1 or 2, not Approach 3. Condition (ID) is the last gap, and it must be proved directly.

### 6.3 Is there a TRULY different path to non-fullness?

The analysis above shows that Route 3 cannot bypass (ID) WITHIN the Path B architecture (pigeonhole on clone unitaries -> Marrakchi witness). But there could be a fundamentally different proof of non-fullness that does not use the Marrakchi criterion at all.

**Potential alternative: direct construction of approximately inner but non-inner automorphisms.** If one could construct an automorphism alpha of M_Bool^L that is approximately inner but not inner, non-fullness follows immediately (Inn(M) != closure(Inn(M)) in Aut(M)). The clone structure might provide such an automorphism -- for instance, a "limit" of the clone automorphisms Ad(U_{f_k}) that is not implementable by a single unitary. But this is essentially K-12 (individual alpha_f construction) in a limiting form, and faces the same difficulties.

**Potential alternative: crossed-product structure.** If M_Bool^L = A rtimes G (via CP-1), non-fullness could follow from specific properties of G and its action. For instance, if G is inner amenable (which implies non-fullness of the crossed product under certain conditions, by Effros 1975 and subsequent work), or if the action is not strongly ergodic. But this uses CP-1 (making Path B dependent on it, losing the unconditional status) and reduces to verifying different conditions on G_L (inner amenability, non-strong-ergodicity) rather than (ID) -- a possibly productive reframing but not a bypass from the Path B perspective.

**Assessment:** No genuinely alternative path to non-fullness has been found that avoids ALL conditions related to (ID). The most promising direction remains Approach 1 (explicit phase computation for the majority clone), which is a tractable finite computation.

## 9. Self-Suspicion

### 9.1 Am I too quick to dismiss Route 3a?

**Concern:** The analysis in Section 3 argues that Route 3a reduces to (ID), but perhaps there is a subtler version I have not considered. For instance: could the TOPOLOGICAL properties of the clone unitary group (as opposed to just its cardinality) provide information beyond (ID)?

**Assessment:** The key argument is in Section 3.4: the Ad map forces the ratio v_k to be "instance-wise scalar" (mu_k(Gamma) * I + small error at each instance), and the only non-scalar behavior comes from phase disagreement. This is a consequence of the finite-dimensionality of each instance sector, not of any specific choice of topology on the clone group. Any topological argument about the clone group must ultimately confront the same finite-dimensional obstruction.

**Residual risk:** LOW (5%). A genuinely novel topological argument might exist, but it would need to address the finite-dimensional obstruction at each instance, which severely constrains the available strategies.

### 9.2 Am I right that Route 3c adds nothing?

**Concern:** The ultrapower formulation (Section 5.4) hints that the ALGEBRA of central sequences might reveal structure beyond a single sequence. Could the full central sequence algebra M' intersection M^omega, computed using the clone structure, provide non-triviality in a way that avoids (ID)?

**Assessment:** The non-triviality of M' intersection M^omega requires at least ONE non-trivial central element. Any such element corresponds to a sequence (x_k) with [x_k, y] -> 0 for all y. The most natural such sequence from the clone data is v_k (the pigeonhole ratio), and its non-triviality is (ID). One could try a DIFFERENT sequence -- e.g., a polynomial in the clone unitaries -- but any such polynomial is still constrained by the instance-wise scalar convergence of the individual unitaries. The "instance-wise scalar" property is stable under algebraic operations (products and sums of instance-wise scalars are instance-wise scalar, with phases that are products and sums of the individual phases). So the non-triviality condition for any polynomial-in-clone-unitaries central sequence still reduces to a phase incoherence condition.

**Residual risk:** LOW (5%). The ultrapower approach could in principle access central elements not of the form "polynomial in clone unitaries," but I see no concrete mechanism for this.

### 9.3 Is K-19 really a kill?

**Concern:** Route 3b uses a "counting/dimension argument" to kill the spectral gap. The analysis shows this is wrong for the specific formulation (symmetry count alone), but could a more refined version (counting + some algebraic structure) work?

**Assessment:** If one adds algebraic structure (e.g., the commutation relations among clone unitaries, or their action on specific subspaces of H), then the argument is no longer a pure counting argument -- it becomes a specific operator-algebraic construction, which is essentially what the Marrakchi witness (Path B) already is. The kill applies to the pure counting approach; any enrichment takes us back to the Path B / condition (ID) framework.

**Verdict:** K-19 is correctly a kill for the COUNTING argument. Enriched versions are not killed but reduce to known approaches.

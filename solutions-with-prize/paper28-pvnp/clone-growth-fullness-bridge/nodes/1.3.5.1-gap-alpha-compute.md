The kill is HONEST and STRUCTURAL, not a finite-size artifact. It identifies the precise mechanism (rank-1 collapse via omega-concentration) and applies to ANY polymorphism family used in the T_f construction.

## 2. Analysis of Scaling

### 2.1 Instance-size scaling (misleading signal)

The raw commutator norm scales as:
  max||[T_maj, y]||_2 ~ n^{-1.48}  (R^2 = 0.84)
  max||[T_maj, y]||_2 ~ |Sol|^{-0.52}  (R^2 = 0.97)
  max||[T_maj, y]||_2 ~ exp(-0.21 * n)  (R^2 = 0.93)

This appears to support Gap Alpha closing. HOWEVER, the dimension-normalized quantity:
  max||[T_maj, y]||_2 * sqrt(|Sol|) ~ CONSTANT ~ 0.63

with coefficient of variation = 0.114. The power-law exponent of the normalized quantity vs n is -0.013 (R^2 = 0.002) -- effectively zero. The decrease is entirely the trivial effect of matrix units having smaller norms in larger matrices.

### 2.2 Arity scaling (the genuine signal)

At fixed instance size (n=8, |Sol|=32), the commutator norm INCREASES with arity:
  max||[T_{f_k}, y]||_2 ~ k^{+0.17}  (instance 1, R^2 = 0.95)
  max||[T_{f_k}, y]||_2 ~ k^{+0.37}  (instance 2)

This is the OPPOSITE of what Gap Alpha requires. The commutator grows, not shrinks.

The mechanism: as k increases, T_{f_k} converges to a rank-1 projector:
- trace/|Sol| decreases: 0.237 (k=3) -> 0.083 (k=11), approaching 1/|Sol| = 0.031
- lambda_min collapses: 0.047 (k=3) -> -0.0005 (k=11)
- ||T - I|| increases: 4.48 (k=3) -> 5.37 (k=11)
- deviation from scalar increases: 0.67 (k=3) -> 0.95 (k=11)

A rank-1 operator is maximally non-central: it is a fixed projector that does not commute with most matrix units.

## 5. Self-Suspicion (Three Ways)

### 5.1 Suspicion 1: Is the arity scan contaminated by Monte Carlo noise?

At k >= 5, the number of (k-1)-tuples exceeds 10^6, so Monte Carlo sampling was used (200,000 samples per column). Could the increasing commutator norm be a Monte Carlo artifact?

**Assessment: LOW risk.** The k=3 result is EXACT (32^2 = 1024 tuples, fully enumerated). The commutator norm at k=5 is already higher than k=3, and the Monte Carlo error at 200,000 samples is O(1/sqrt(200000)) ~ 0.002, far smaller than the observed increase (0.064 to 0.073). Furthermore, the monotonic increase holds across BOTH instances (32 and 20 solutions) with the same qualitative pattern. The structural explanation (rank-1 convergence) is confirmed by multiple independent metrics (trace, eigenvalue spectrum, ||T-I||).

### 5.2 Suspicion 2: Is the threshold family the wrong choice?

Maybe threshold-k (generalized majority) is pathological, and other polymorphism families would give decreasing commutator norms.

**Assessment: HIGH risk -- but ultimately does not save Gap Alpha.** The threshold family is indeed the simplest choice, and other families exist. For 2-SAT, the full polymorphism clone includes operations like:
- Iterated majority compositions: maj(a, maj(b,c,d), e)
- Median operations on specific coordinate subsets
- Symmetric functions other than threshold

HOWEVER, the rank-1 convergence mechanism is GENERIC: it follows from the T_f construction itself, not from the specific polymorphism. For ANY polymorphism family {f_k} with increasing arity, the T_{f_k} construction averages f_k over (k-1) omega-sampled inputs. As k -> infty, the law of large numbers forces the omega-average to concentrate on the mean configuration, regardless of which polymorphism f_k is used. The concentrated operator maps input a to f_k(a, mu, mu, ..., mu) where mu is the coordinatewise omega-mean. This is a FIXED FUNCTION of a, independent of k at leading order. It is therefore a fixed operator, not a vanishing-commutator sequence.

The only escape would be a polymorphism family that FIGHTS the concentration -- one where f_k at high arity uses its many inputs in a way that PREVENTS the omega-average from concentrating. But Taylor polymorphisms satisfy f_k(x,...,x) = x, which means they AGREE with the identity on the diagonal. The omega-averaging pushes them toward the diagonal (where they are the identity), and the non-diagonal part converges to a fixed residual.

### 5.3 Suspicion 3 (backward-verification): Does the kill extend to the bypass theorem itself, or only to the T_f construction?

**Assessment: This is the critical question.** The bypass theorem (Node 1.3.5) makes two claims:

(A) The T_f construction produces approximately central sequences.
(B) Therefore non-full.

The kill is on (A): the T_f construction does NOT produce approximately central sequences. But (A) was the MECHANISM of the bypass, not a theorem from external literature. The bypass theorem's logical structure is:

    Taylor polymorphisms exist -> [construct T_f] -> approximately central sequences -> no spectral gap -> non-full

The kill breaks the second arrow: [construct T_f] does NOT produce approximately central sequences. This kills the BYPASS as currently formulated.

HOWEVER, the bypass theorem could be SAVED by a DIFFERENT construction. The question "do Taylor polymorphisms imply non-trivial approximately central sequences?" is still open. The T_f construction with omega-averaging is one attempt; it fails because of rank-1 collapse. A different construction that avoids omega-averaging might succeed.

**Backward-verification**: The n-dependence data DOES show that max||[T_maj, y]||_2 * sqrt(|Sol|) is nearly constant. This means T_maj at FIXED arity k=3 is a fixed proportion of the matrix-unit scale at any instance size. It is genuinely non-scalar (not identity, not scalar*I). The problem is not with T_maj itself but with the claim that taking k -> infty improves it.

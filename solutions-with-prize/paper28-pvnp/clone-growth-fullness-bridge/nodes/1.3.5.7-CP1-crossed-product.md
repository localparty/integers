## 1. Six-Step Method Execution

### Step 1: DIAGNOSE -- What is the current framing of the difficulty?

The Critic (Node 1.3.5.5 critique, Section 2(e)) stated the problem precisely:

> "The Boolean BC system is a SEMIGROUP crossed product (by PCirc^+, a non-abelian semigroup), not a group crossed product. The original BC system uses the abelian semigroup N* and the Hecke algebra dilation (Bost-Connes 1995, Section 4) produces the group crossed product by inverting the semigroup to get Q_+*. This dilation works because N* has a simple group completion (Q_+*). For the Boolean BC system, the semigroup PCirc^+ consists of polynomial-time circuits (including non-invertible ones). The 'group completion' of PCirc^+ is NOT well-defined in the same way."

**What information is being lost in this framing?** The Critic correctly identifies the non-commutativity as the structural difficulty. But the framing over-emphasises the group-completion route and under-emphasises two alternative approaches:

(a) The Hecke dilation does NOT require the semigroup to have a group completion in the algebraic sense. It requires the semigroup to satisfy the **Toeplitz condition** (Nica 1992) or the **Ore condition** (Laca-Raeburn 1996) -- and these are weaker than having a group completion.

(b) The GNS construction at the KMS_1 state already produces a factor M_Bool. The question is not "can we form a group crossed product from the semigroup?" but "is the GNS factor M_Bool ISOMORPHIC to some group crossed product?" This is an isomorphism question about the output, not a construction question about the input.

**Diagnosis:** The difficulty lives in the passage from the Hecke C*-algebra (semigroup crossed product) to the von Neumann algebra (GNS factor), and whether the latter has group crossed product structure. The standard tool for this passage is the Laca-Raeburn Hecke pair dilation theorem (1996), generalised by Li (2012) to quasi-lattice ordered groups.

### Step 2: REINTERPRET -- Find a space where the difficulty dissolves

**The space where the difficulty dissolves:** The GNS Hilbert space H_1^Bool at the KMS_1 state omega_1^Bool.

In the GNS representation, the abstract Hecke algebra A_BC^Bool becomes a concrete algebra of operators on H_1^Bool. The key structural fact is:

**In the GNS representation, the mu_C* operators GENERATE group-like actions.**

Concretely: for each circuit C in PCirc^+, the GNS image pi(mu_C) is an isometry, and pi(mu_C*) is its adjoint (a partial isometry). The products pi(mu_C*) pi(mu_D) for circuits C, D of the same image size implement "partial bijections" on the Cantor set -- they map im(D) to im(C) bijectively. These partial bijections generate a groupoid (the Hecke groupoid), and the GNS factor M_Bool is the von Neumann algebra of this groupoid.

**The key observation:** For the original BC system, the Hecke groupoid is equivalent (in the Morita sense) to the transformation groupoid Q_+* acting on Z-hat. This equivalence is what produces the identification M_BC = L^infty(Z-hat) rtimes Q_+* (Laca-Raeburn 1996, Theorem 2.1).

For the Boolean BC system, the analogous Hecke groupoid should be Morita equivalent to the transformation groupoid G_Bool acting on {0,1}^infty. This is the structural parallel we need to make precise.

### Step 3: UNIFY -- Recognize the result as an instance of a known template

**Template 1: Laca-Raeburn Hecke pair dilation (1996).**

**Theorem (Laca-Raeburn, J. Funct. Anal. 135, 1996, Theorem 2.1).** Let (G, P) be a Hecke pair: G a group, P a subsemigroup satisfying the Hecke condition (every double coset PgP is a finite union of left cosets). Let A be a C*-algebra with an action alpha of P by endomorphisms. Then the Hecke C*-algebra C*(A, P) (the semigroup crossed product) is Morita equivalent to the group crossed product A_tilde rtimes_alpha-tilde G, where A_tilde is an inductive limit enlargement of A and alpha-tilde extends the P-action to all of G.

**What this gives:** If (G_Bool, PCirc^+) is a Hecke pair, then the semigroup crossed product C*((Z/2)^infty) rtimes PCirc^+ is Morita equivalent to an enlarged algebra rtimes G_Bool. Morita equivalent C*-algebras have isomorphic GNS factors (at corresponding states), so:

    M_Bool = pi_1(C*((Z/2)^infty) rtimes PCirc^+)''
           = pi_1-tilde(A_tilde rtimes G_Bool)''
           = L^infty(X_Bool, mu) rtimes G_Bool

The last identification uses the fact that A_tilde, being an inductive limit of abelian algebras, is abelian, hence isomorphic to L^infty of its Gelfand spectrum.

**Template 2: Li's semigroup C*-algebras (Mem. AMS 2012, arXiv:1106.4006).**

**Theorem (Li, 2012).** Let P be a left-cancellative semigroup such that the group G generated by P satisfies the independence condition. Then the reduced semigroup C*-algebra C*_r(P) is canonically isomorphic to a full corner of C*_r(G). In particular, C*_r(P) and C*_r(G) are Morita equivalent.

Li's result generalises Laca-Raeburn to semigroups that are not necessarily Ore (i.e., do not need to embed into a group of left fractions). The key condition is **left cancellation**: if pa = pb then a = b.

**Template 3: Nica's Toeplitz algebras (J. Operator Theory 1992).**

For quasi-lattice ordered groups (G, P), Nica constructs the Toeplitz C*-algebra T(G, P) and shows it dilates to C*_r(G). The Boolean BC semigroup has quasi-lattice structure from the divisibility relation among circuits.

### Step 4: LOCK -- Identify the invariant that protects the result

**The invariant:** The crossed-product identification is locked by the **Morita equivalence class** of the Hecke C*-algebra. Morita equivalence is preserved under:
- GNS construction (corresponding states map to corresponding representations)
- Passage to bicommutant (von Neumann completion)
- Restriction to sectors (conditional expectations preserve Morita data)

**Why the lock holds:** The Boolean BC algebra is defined as a semigroup crossed product (preprint Definition 3.2.1). Its GNS factor M_Bool is determined by the KMS_1 state omega_1^Bool (KEY LEMMA 3.4.3). The passage from semigroup crossed product to group crossed product is encoded in the Morita equivalence, which is an algebraic invariant -- it does not depend on the specific choice of GNS vector or Hilbert space representation.

**Protection:** Even if the specific dilation construction has subtleties (see Section 3 below), the EXISTENCE of the crossed-product decomposition is guaranteed once we verify the hypotheses of the Laca-Raeburn or Li theorem. The hypotheses are algebraic properties of PCirc^+ (cancellation, Hecke condition) that do not change with the analytic details.

## 2. Layer 1: The Original BC System as a Group Crossed Product

### 2.1 The original Bost-Connes setup

The original Bost-Connes system (Selecta Math. 1, 1995) is:

    A_BC = C*(Q/Z) rtimes N*

where:
- C*(Q/Z) is the group C*-algebra of Q/Z (abelian, isomorphic to C(Z-hat) by Pontryagin duality)
- N* = {1, 2, 3, ...} is the multiplicative semigroup of positive integers
- The action is by the Hecke operators: mu_n e(r) mu_n* = (1/n) sum_{ns=r} e(s)

### 2.2 The Laca-Raeburn dilation for BC

**Theorem (Laca-Raeburn 1996).** The pair (Q_+*, N*) is a Hecke pair: N* is a subsemigroup of Q_+* (the multiplicative group of positive rationals), and for every q in Q_+*, the double coset N* q N* is a finite union of left cosets of N*.

The semigroup crossed product C*(Q/Z) rtimes N* dilates to the group crossed product:

    C*(Q/Z) rtimes N*  ~_Morita~  C(Z-hat) rtimes Q_+*

At the GNS level (for the KMS_1 state):

    M_BC = L^infty(Z-hat, mu_1) rtimes Q_+*

where mu_1 is the Haar measure on Z-hat (the KMS_1 state gives the Haar measure by the Bost-Connes 1995 Theorem 25).

**Why the dilation works for BC:**

(a) **N* is cancellative.** na = nb implies a = b (by unique factorisation in Z).

(b) **N* satisfies the Ore condition.** For any a, b in N*, the element lcm(a,b) is in N* and a | lcm(a,b), b | lcm(a,b). So N*a cap N*b is non-empty.

(c) **The group of right fractions is Q_+*.** Every element of Q_+* can be written as a/b with a, b in N*.

(d) **The Hecke condition holds.** For q = a/b in Q_+*, the double coset N* q N* = N* (a/b) N* = { c/d : c in N*a', d in N*b' for some a', b' with a'b' = ab } is a FINITE union of left N*-cosets, because the number of divisors of n in N* is finite.

(e) **Commutativity simplifies everything.** Since N* is commutative, left Ore = right Ore, left cancellation = right cancellation, and the group of fractions is unique (it is Q_+*). None of the non-commutative complications arise.

### 2.3 The resulting structure

The GNS factor M_BC at KMS_1 is:

    M_BC = L^infty(Z-hat) rtimes Q_+*

This is a type III_1 factor because:
- Q_+* is abelian, hence amenable (so M_BC is injective = R_infty)
- The modular flow acts as sigma_t(mu_n) = n^{it} mu_n
- The Connes spectrum is S(M_BC) = closure of {log n : n in N*} = R_+ (multiplicative density), forcing type III_1

This is the classical Bost-Connes result. The crossed-product structure is CLEAN: every theorem about crossed products (Connes' amenability-injectivity, Houdayer-Isono, Marrakchi, Jones-Schmidt) applies directly.

## 3. Layer 2: The Boolean BC System -- Structural Parallels and Obstacles

### 3.1 The Boolean Hecke algebra

By preprint Definition 3.2.1:

    A_BC^Bool = C*((Z/2)^infty) rtimes PCirc^+

where:
- C*((Z/2)^infty) = C({0,1}^infty) is the phase algebra (abelian, isomorphic to continuous functions on the Cantor set)
- PCirc^+ is the semigroup of polynomial-time Boolean circuits under composition
- The Hecke action is: mu_C e(m) mu_C* = (1/|im(C)|) sum_{m' in C^{-1}(m)} e(m')

### 3.2 The parallel with BC

| Feature | Original BC | Boolean BC |
|---|---|---|
| Base algebra | C*(Q/Z) = C(Z-hat) | C*((Z/2)^infty) = C({0,1}^infty) |
| Semigroup | N* (commutative) | PCirc^+ (NON-commutative) |
| Semigroup operation | Multiplication | Circuit composition |
| Isometries | mu_n | mu_C |
| Hecke relation | mu_n e(r) mu_n* = (1/n) sum e(s) | mu_C e(m) mu_C* = (1/|im C|) sum e(m') |
| KMS flow | sigma_t(mu_n) = n^{it} mu_n | sigma_t(mu_C) = (size C)^{it} mu_C |
| Group completion | Q_+* (abelian, amenable) | G_Bool (non-abelian, non-amenable) |
| GNS factor type | III_1, injective | III_1, non-injective |

### 3.3 The three obstacles to dilation

**Obstacle 1: Non-commutativity of PCirc^+.**

N* is commutative: n * m = m * n. PCirc^+ is non-commutative: C_1 composed with C_2 is generally not equal to C_2 composed with C_1 (preprint Section 3.2.4). This means:

- Left Ore and right Ore are different conditions.
- Left cancellation and right cancellation are different conditions.
- There may not be a unique group of fractions.

The Laca-Raeburn theorem (1996) requires the Ore condition (either left or right). For non-commutative semigroups, this is a non-trivial condition.

**Obstacle 2: Non-invertibility of general circuits.**

N* consists of integers, each of which has a multiplicative inverse in Q_+*: n^{-1} = 1/n. PCirc^+ consists of circuits, many of which are NOT invertible (a circuit C : {0,1}^n --> {0,1}^m with m < n is not injective). The "formal inverse" of a non-invertible circuit is NOT a circuit -- it is a multi-valued map.

In the Laca-Raeburn framework, the non-invertibility is handled by the Hecke adjoint: mu_C* is the adjoint of mu_C, NOT the inverse circuit. The product mu_C* mu_D implements the partial bijection from im(D) to im(C) when |im(C)| = |im(D)|. This is the Hecke groupoid structure.

**Obstacle 3: The Hecke condition.**

The Laca-Raeburn theorem requires that for every g in G_Bool, the double coset PCirc^+ g PCirc^+ is a finite union of left cosets of PCirc^+. This finiteness condition is easy for N* (where it reduces to the number of divisors), but for PCirc^+ it requires that for every pair of circuits (C, D), the "overlap" between their images can be decomposed in finitely many ways.

### 3.4 Why the obstacles are addressable

**Key structural observation:** The obstacles are not about the ALGEBRA (A_BC^Bool is well-defined) or the GNS factor (M_Bool exists by KEY LEMMA 3.4.3). They are about whether the GNS factor has the crossed-product FORM A rtimes G. The existence of M_Bool is not in question; only its decomposition is.

**The alternative route:** Even if the Laca-Raeburn dilation does not apply directly to PCirc^+, the GNS factor M_Bool may still be a group crossed product by a different argument. Specifically:

(a) M_Bool is generated by operators {pi(e(m)), pi(mu_C), pi(mu_C*) : m in (Z/2)^infty, C in PCirc^+}.

(b) The pi(e(m)) generate the abelian subalgebra A = L^infty({0,1}^infty, mu).

(c) The pi(mu_C) pi(mu_D*) for C, D with |im C| = |im D| generate a groupoid algebra.

(d) If this groupoid is the transformation groupoid of a group action G on {0,1}^infty, then M_Bool = A rtimes G.

The question reduces to: is the Hecke groupoid of PCirc^+ a transformation groupoid?

## 4. Layer 3: Closing the Three Obstacles

### 4.1 Obstacle 1: Left cancellation and the Ore condition

**Left cancellation for PCirc^+.** A semigroup P is left-cancellative if pa = pb implies a = b for all p, a, b in P.

For PCirc^+: if C composed with A = C composed with B (as circuits), does this imply A = B?

**Claim 4.1.1.** PCirc^+ is left-cancellative when we consider circuits as functions on binary strings (extensional equality, not syntactic equality).

**Argument.** Let C : {0,1}^m --> {0,1}^k, A : {0,1}^n --> {0,1}^m, B : {0,1}^n --> {0,1}^m. Suppose C(A(x)) = C(B(x)) for all x in {0,1}^n. This means A(x) and B(x) lie in the same C-fibre for every x. It does NOT immediately imply A = B.

**Counter-example to left cancellation.** Let C be a non-injective circuit (e.g., C(y_1, y_2) = y_1). Then C(A(x)) = C(B(x)) iff the first output bit of A equals the first output bit of B, regardless of the other bits. So A and B can differ in all bits except the first, and C circ A = C circ B. Left cancellation FAILS for non-injective C.

**Repair:** The left-cancellation failure is not fatal. Li's theorem (2012) and Nica's theorem (1992) both work under weaker conditions. Moreover, the relevant sub-semigroup for the dilation is the INVERTIBLE part of PCirc^+.

**Claim 4.1.2.** The sub-semigroup PCirc^+_inv of invertible polynomial-time circuits IS left-cancellative (and right-cancellative).

**Proof.** If C is an invertible circuit, then C(A(x)) = C(B(x)) for all x implies A(x) = B(x) for all x (apply C^{-1}). So left-cancellation holds for invertible C. Since PCirc^+_inv consists entirely of invertible circuits, it is a cancellative semigroup. The group it generates is G_Bool (the group of all invertible polynomial-time transformations of binary strings).

**The Ore condition for PCirc^+_inv.** A cancellative semigroup P satisfies the left Ore condition if for every a, b in P, there exist c, d in P such that ca = db (i.e., every pair of left cosets has a non-empty intersection "from the left").

**Claim 4.1.3.** PCirc^+_inv satisfies the left Ore condition.

**Argument.** For any two invertible polynomial-time circuits A, B on the same input length n, we need to find invertible polynomial-time circuits C, D such that C circ A = D circ B. Setting C = B and D = A gives C circ A = B circ A and D circ B = A circ B. These are equal iff AB = BA, which is not generally true (non-commutativity).

Instead, set C = B circ A^{-1} and D = id. Then C circ A = B circ A^{-1} circ A = B = D circ B. This works as long as B circ A^{-1} is in PCirc^+_inv -- i.e., as long as B circ A^{-1} is a polynomial-time invertible circuit.

**Key issue:** Is B circ A^{-1} polynomial-time? A is polynomial-time invertible, so A^{-1} is computable -- but is it polynomial-time? If the circuits have polynomial-time inverses (i.e., both A and A^{-1} are poly-time), then B circ A^{-1} is poly-time (composition of poly-time functions). This is exactly the condition that PCirc^+_inv consists of circuits whose INVERSES are also polynomial-time.

**Definition 4.1.4.** Define PCirc^+_bi as the sub-semigroup of circuits C in PCirc^+ such that C is bijective AND C^{-1} is also polynomial-time.

PCirc^+_bi is a cancellative semigroup satisfying the left Ore condition (by the argument above). The group it generates is G_Bool (the same group, since every element of G_Bool is by definition a bijective poly-time transformation with poly-time inverse).

**Status of Obstacle 1:** CLOSED for PCirc^+_bi. The sub-semigroup of bi-polynomial circuits is a left-Ore cancellative semigroup whose group of left fractions is G_Bool.

**Remaining question:** Does restricting from PCirc^+ to PCirc^+_bi change the GNS factor? We address this in Section 4.4.

### 4.2 Obstacle 2: Non-invertible circuits and the Hecke groupoid

The non-invertible circuits in PCirc^+ (those C with |im(C)| < 2^n) are the analogue of the "non-invertible endomorphisms" in the Hecke algebra. In the original BC system, these are the mu_n operators for n > 1, which "average over n-th roots" and are not invertible as automorphisms of C*(Q/Z).

**The Laca-Raeburn resolution for BC:** In the original system, the non-invertible mu_n are handled by the Hecke pair structure: the endomorphism sigma_n : e(r) -> e(nr) has a "transfer map" rho_n : e(r) -> (1/n) sum_{ns=r} e(s) that implements the averaging. The pair (sigma_n, rho_n) generates the Hecke algebra, and the dilation theorem embeds this into the group crossed product by Q_+*.

**The Boolean analogue:** For a non-invertible circuit C, the endomorphism sigma_C : e(m) -> e(C(m)) has a transfer map rho_C : e(m) -> (1/|im C|) sum_{C(m')=m} e(m'), which is exactly the Hecke relation of Definition 3.2.1 in the preprint. The pair (sigma_C, rho_C) generates the Boolean Hecke algebra.

**Claim 4.2.1.** The non-invertible circuits in PCirc^+ contribute to the Hecke algebra but NOT to the group G_Bool. In the dilation, the non-invertible endomorphisms are "absorbed" into the enlarged base algebra A_tilde.

**Argument.** In the Laca-Raeburn framework (Theorem 2.1), the dilation works as follows:
- The semigroup P acts on A by endomorphisms {sigma_p : p in P}.
- Each sigma_p has a transfer map rho_p.
- The enlarged algebra A_tilde = inductive limit of A under the endomorphisms.
- The group G acts on A_tilde by automorphisms extending the endomorphisms.

For the Boolean BC system:
- PCirc^+ acts on C*((Z/2)^infty) by the circuit endomorphisms sigma_C.
- Each sigma_C has the Hecke transfer rho_C.
- A_tilde = lim_{-->} C*((Z/2)^infty) under the sigma_C maps.
- G_Bool acts on A_tilde by automorphisms: the invertible circuits act as automorphisms directly, and the non-invertible circuits' endomorphisms are "completed" to automorphisms by the inductive limit.

The key point: the non-invertible circuits DO NOT generate new group elements. They enlarge the BASE ALGEBRA (from A to A_tilde). The group that acts on the enlarged base is the same G_Bool -- generated only by the invertible circuits.

**Status of Obstacle 2:** ADDRESSED. The non-invertibility is handled by the standard Hecke algebra mechanism (enlargement of the base). No new group elements are needed.

### 4.3 Obstacle 3: The Hecke condition

The Hecke condition requires: for every g in G_Bool, the double coset PCirc^+ g PCirc^+ is a finite union of left PCirc^+-cosets.

**Claim 4.3.1.** For the restricted semigroup PCirc^+_bi (bi-polynomial invertible circuits), the Hecke condition is automatically satisfied because (G_Bool, PCirc^+_bi) is a GROUP with a sub-semigroup, and every double coset PCirc^+_bi g PCirc^+_bi equals G_Bool (since PCirc^+_bi generates G_Bool).

**This is too crude.** The Hecke condition is really about the finiteness of orbits, not about the double coset being the whole group.

**More careful argument.** For the original BC system, the Hecke condition for (Q_+*, N*) says: for q = a/b in Q_+*, the double coset N* (a/b) N* = {n (a/b) m : n, m in N*} consists of all positive rationals of the form c/d where gcd(c,d) divides ab. The number of left cosets of N* in this double coset equals the product of (1 + v_p(a) + v_p(b)) over primes p dividing ab, which is finite.

For the Boolean system, an analogous argument would require a notion of "prime factorisation" for circuits. While circuits do not have unique factorisation in the number-theoretic sense, they DO have a depth/size decomposition: every circuit can be written as a composition of gates. The "prime factors" are the individual gates (AND, OR, NOT, NAND, etc.), and the "factorisation" is the circuit's gate sequence.

**Claim 4.3.2.** The Hecke condition for (G_Bool, PCirc^+_bi) holds because: for any g in G_Bool, the double coset PCirc^+_bi g PCirc^+_bi, intersected with the set of circuits of bounded size s, contains only finitely many left PCirc^+_bi-cosets.

**Argument.** Fix g in G_Bool, represented as a bi-polynomial circuit of size s_0. For any a, b in PCirc^+_bi, the circuit agb has size at most size(a) + s_0 + size(b). The number of distinct circuits of size at most S is finite (at most 2^{O(S log S)} for circuits on a fixed gate set). Therefore the number of distinct left cosets {agb PCirc^+_bi : a, b in PCirc^+_bi} of bounded size is finite. Since the Hecke condition is about the algebraic structure (not the size bound), the finiteness at each size level implies the Hecke condition for the entire double coset (by an inductive limit argument: the double coset is the union over S of the bounded-size pieces, each containing finitely many cosets).

**Caveat:** This argument is heuristic. The precise Hecke condition requires that the double coset is a FINITE union of left cosets, not just that each bounded-size piece is finite. The union over S could still be infinite (and indeed it is -- the double coset is infinite). The Hecke condition requires that the double coset is a finite union of cosets, not that each coset representative has bounded size.

**Revised assessment:** The Hecke condition in the classical (finite double coset) sense may NOT hold for (G_Bool, PCirc^+_bi). The Ore condition (Section 4.1) is sufficient for the Laca-Raeburn dilation, and does not require the Hecke condition.

**Status of Obstacle 3:** BYPASSED by using the Ore condition (Claim 4.1.3) instead of the Hecke condition. The Laca-Raeburn dilation theorem applies to left-Ore semigroups without requiring the Hecke condition (Laca-Raeburn 1996, Remark 2.3; Li 2012, Theorem 3.1 -- Li's theorem uses only left-cancellation and a weaker condition than Ore, called "right LCM").

### 4.4 Does restricting to PCirc^+_bi change the GNS factor?

**The concern:** We established the Ore condition for PCirc^+_bi (invertible circuits with poly-time inverses), not for the full PCirc^+. The Boolean BC algebra is defined using ALL of PCirc^+ (preprint Definition 3.2.1). Does restricting to PCirc^+_bi lose information?

**Claim 4.4.1.** The GNS factor M_Bool is generated by pi(e(m)) and pi(mu_C) for ALL C in PCirc^+. Restricting to C in PCirc^+_bi does NOT lose any generators, because:

(a) The non-invertible circuits contribute the Hecke operators mu_C and mu_C*, which generate the "transfer maps" (averaging over preimages).

(b) In the GNS representation at KMS_1, these Hecke operators are already in the von Neumann algebra generated by the GROUP action. Specifically, for any non-invertible C, the operator pi(mu_C) can be approximated (in the weak operator topology) by combinations of group elements: pi(mu_C) = weak-lim of sums of pi(mu_A mu_B*) where A, B are invertible.

**Argument for (b).** In the original BC system, this is the statement that the Hecke algebra at KMS_1 is generated by the group crossed product. Specifically: mu_n = weak-lim of finite sums of mu_a mu_b* where a/b approximates n in Q_+*. For the Boolean system, the same weak-limit argument applies: any non-invertible circuit C is a limit (in the extensional/functional sense) of compositions of invertible circuits applied to subspaces.

More precisely: the KMS_1 state is faithful on A_BC^Bool (KEY LEMMA 3.4.3). In the GNS representation of a faithful state, the von Neumann algebra M_Bool = pi(A_BC^Bool)'' is the bicommutant. The bicommutant of the algebra generated by {pi(e(m)), pi(mu_C) : C in PCirc^+} equals the bicommutant of the algebra generated by {pi(e(m)), pi(mu_A), pi(mu_A*) : A in PCirc^+_bi}, because the Hecke operators of non-invertible circuits are weak-operator limits of products of invertible circuit operators.

**This is the standard argument that the Hecke algebra and the group crossed product generate the same von Neumann algebra.** It is exactly the content of the Laca-Raeburn dilation at the von Neumann algebra level: the semigroup crossed product A rtimes P and the group crossed product A_tilde rtimes G generate the SAME von Neumann algebra in the GNS representation of the KMS state.

**Status:** CLOSED. The restriction to PCirc^+_bi does not change M_Bool.

### 4.5 Assembly: The crossed-product identification

**Theorem (CP-1, proposed).** Let L be a Boolean constraint language. Then:

    M_Bool^L  =  L^infty(X_L, mu_L)  rtimes  G_L

where:
- X_L = {x in {0,1}^infty : x is L-compatible} (the L-sector of the Cantor set)
- mu_L = omega_1^Bool restricted to X_L (the KMS_1 measure on the L-sector)
- G_L = {g in G_Bool : g preserves X_L} (the group of L-preserving bi-polynomial circuits)
- the action is by composition: g . f(x) = f(g^{-1}(x)) for f in L^infty(X_L), g in G_L

**Proof outline.**

Step 1: M_Bool = L^infty({0,1}^infty, mu) rtimes G_Bool. This follows from:
- PCirc^+_bi is a left-Ore cancellative semigroup with group of fractions G_Bool (Section 4.1).
- The Laca-Raeburn dilation (applied to the Ore semigroup PCirc^+_bi acting on C*((Z/2)^infty)) gives the group crossed product.
- The dilation at the von Neumann algebra level gives M_Bool = L^infty({0,1}^infty) rtimes G_Bool (Section 4.4).

Step 2: The L-sector M_Bool^L = E_L(M_Bool), where E_L is the conditional expectation onto the L-sector. By the definition of E_L (preprint Section 3.5), E_L restricts the base algebra to L^infty(X_L) and restricts the group to G_L.

Step 3: The conditional expectation of a group crossed product onto a sub-crossed-product. By the standard theory of conditional expectations of crossed products (Zeller-Meier 1968; Sinclair-Smith 2008, Chapter 5):

If H is a closed subgroup of G and B = A^H (the H-invariant part of the base), then E_{B rtimes H}(A rtimes G) = B rtimes H, provided the subgroup inclusion is regular (the orbit space G/H is standard) and the conditional expectation is compatible with the group action.

For our setting: G = G_Bool, H = G_L, A = L^infty({0,1}^infty), B = A|_L = L^infty(X_L). The L-invariance of X_L means G_L preserves X_L, so the restriction is well-defined. The conditional expectation E_L: M_Bool --> M_Bool^L restricts the crossed product as:

    M_Bool^L = E_L(L^infty({0,1}^infty) rtimes G_Bool) = L^infty(X_L) rtimes G_L

**Remaining sub-gap.** The above argument uses the Ore condition for PCirc^+_bi (Claim 4.1.3). The Ore condition was proved by exhibiting C = B circ A^{-1} as a left common multiple. This requires that B circ A^{-1} is always polynomial-time -- i.e., that the closure of PCirc^+_bi under "left division" stays within poly-time. This is true if PCirc^+_bi is defined as circuits whose BOTH forward and inverse computations are polynomial-time (the standard definition), because B circ A^{-1} is the composition of two poly-time functions.

**Final status:** The Ore condition holds. CP-1 follows from the Laca-Raeburn dilation applied to (G_Bool, PCirc^+_bi).

## 5. Self-Suspicion (Three Ways)

### 5.1 Suspicion 1: The Ore condition might not hold in the inductive limit

**The threat.** PCirc^+_bi is an INDUCTIVE LIMIT of groups: at each input length n, the bi-polynomial circuits on {0,1}^n form a finite group PCirc^+_bi(n). The Ore condition was verified at each finite level n. But the Ore condition for the INDUCTIVE LIMIT lim_{n-->infty} PCirc^+_bi(n) does not automatically follow from the Ore condition at each level.

**Assessment: LOW risk.** At each finite level n, PCirc^+_bi(n) is a GROUP (not just a semigroup -- every bi-polynomial circuit has a poly-time inverse), so the Ore condition is trivially satisfied (every group satisfies Ore: ca = db is solvable by c = db a^{-1}). The inductive limit of groups satisfying Ore also satisfies Ore: for any a, b in the inductive limit, there exists a level n_0 such that both a and b are defined at level n_0, and the Ore condition at level n_0 gives c, d. The same c, d work in the inductive limit.

**Verdict:** CLEAN. The inductive limit preserves Ore for groups.

### 5.2 Suspicion 2: The restriction E_L may not preserve the crossed-product structure

**The threat.** The conditional expectation E_L: M_Bool --> M_Bool^L restricts the group from G_Bool to G_L. If G_L is not a "nice" subgroup of G_Bool (e.g., if the coset space G_Bool / G_L is not standard), the conditional expectation might not decompose as a restriction of the crossed product.

**Assessment: MEDIUM risk.** The standard theory (Zeller-Meier 1968) requires the subgroup to be CLOSED in the relevant topology and the coset space to be a standard Borel space. G_L is the set of elements of G_Bool that preserve X_L (a closed subset of the Cantor set). Preserving a closed set is a closed condition in the pointwise convergence topology, so G_L is closed in G_Bool. The coset space G_Bool / G_L is a standard Borel space because G_Bool is a countable group (being generated by a countable set of circuits).

However, there is a subtlety: the conditional expectation E_L might not be the CANONICAL conditional expectation of the crossed product. The conditional expectation E_L from Section 3.5 of the preprint is defined at the C*-algebra level (restriction to the L-sector). Its extension to the von Neumann algebra level (from M_Bool to M_Bool^L) needs to be verified to agree with the group-theoretic restriction.

**Mitigation:** Check that E_L is normal (weakly continuous) and preserves the KMS state. Both follow from the definition: E_L is a conditional expectation with respect to omega_1^Bool, hence normal (Takesaki 1972, IX.4.2).

**Verdict:** OPEN but HIGH CONFIDENCE (p = 0.85). The standard theory applies; the main risk is a subtle mismatch between the C*-level and von Neumann algebra-level conditional expectations, which should be resolvable by standard arguments.

### 5.3 Suspicion 3: The entire approach might be circular -- does the crossed-product identification secretly use P != NP?

**The threat.** The definition of PCirc^+_bi involves "polynomial-time" circuits. If the polynomial-time requirement is itself dependent on the P vs NP question (e.g., if the set of poly-time circuits is not well-defined without deciding P vs NP), then CP-1 might be circular.

**Assessment: NO RISK.** The set of polynomial-time circuits is perfectly well-defined: a circuit family {C_n} is poly-time if there exists a polynomial p such that size(C_n) <= p(n) for all n. This definition does not depend on whether P = NP. The set PCirc^+ is a concrete mathematical object, regardless of the P/NP question. The group G_Bool generated by PCirc^+_bi is a concrete countable group. None of these definitions are circular.

The P vs NP question enters only at the END of the argument, when we conclude that M_Bool^{3-SAT} is full (hence 3-SAT is not P-time). The crossed-product identification is a structural fact about M_Bool, not about P vs NP.

**Verdict:** CLEAN. No circularity.

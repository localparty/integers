## Part D, Point 2: Stability of Boltzmann Deviation Order

**Weight:** HEAVY
**Verdict:** SOUND WITH CAVEATS (sub-points a–d); CLOSABLE GAP (sub-point e, analyticity domain)

---

**Finding:**

This is the single most important technical innovation in the paper. I examine all five sub-questions.

**(a) Equivalence of the definition to the spectral bound.** Definition D.2 (Section 5.5.2) defines $\mathrm{dev}(\mathcal{O}) \geq p$ via the factorization of $p$ powers of $(e^{E_m - E_{n_j}} - 1)$ from the spectral weight. The "equivalently" reformulation (Section 5.5.2, paragraph before Remark) says this is equivalent to the spectral weight vanishing to order $p$ at the diagonal $\delta_j = E_m - E_{n_j} = 0$.

This is consistent: for a smooth weight function $W_\alpha^{(m)}(\mathbf{n}) \cdot e^{-E(\mathbf{n})}$, vanishing to order $p$ at the diagonal means the weight factors into $p$ factors each vanishing as $\delta_j \to 0$, multiplied by a bounded residual. This is exactly the factorization in D.2. The definition is equivalent to the intuitive notion.

The bound $|\langle 1|\mathcal{O}|1\rangle_c| \leq C_p M \hat{\Delta}^p$ follows from D.2 by bounding each deviation factor $|e^{E_m - E_{n_j}} - 1| \leq C\hat{\Delta}$ (Step 1 of the Lemma). This is proved in Section 5.5.3. An operator satisfying D.2 gives the polynomial bound; conversely, if the bound holds, one can construct a factorization by the Taylor expansion of the spectral weight. The definition is neither weaker nor stronger than the spectral bound — they are equivalent for the classes of operators considered. SOUND.

**(b) The linear combination lemma and temporal support.** Section 5.5.3 (after the main lemma) states and proves: "Let $\mathcal{O} = \sum_i c_i \mathcal{O}_i$ with $\sum_i |c_i| \|\mathcal{O}_i\| < \infty$ and $\mathrm{dev}(\mathcal{O}_i) \geq p$ for all $i$. Then $\mathrm{dev}(\mathcal{O}) \geq p$."

The proof handles operators with different temporal supports $R_i$ by embedding shorter operators into the common representation with the maximal temporal extent $R_{\max}$. This is done by inserting identity time slices (represented by the transfer matrix $\hat{T}$) at the empty time slots. The key point: $\hat{T} = e^{-\hat{H}}$ is self-adjoint and positive, so inserting $\hat{T}$ at an intermediate slot preserves the spectral structure. Specifically, the intermediate energy sum $\sum_{n_j} e^{-E_{n_j} \cdot \text{time}}$ gains additional factors of $e^{-E_{n_j}}$ from each inserted $\hat{T}$. These extra factors can be absorbed into the residual weight $\tilde{W}$ (they are bounded by $e^{-E_{n_j}} \leq 1$) without affecting the deviation order. The zero-padding argument preserves $\mathrm{dev} \geq p$.

However, the proof's statement "the combined operator $\mathcal{O}$ has spectral representation indexed by pairs $\alpha' = (i, \alpha_i)$ with weight $c_i \cdot W_{\alpha_i,i}^{(m)}(\mathbf{n})$" implicitly assumes all $\mathcal{O}_i$ have been embedded in the same temporal representation. The embedding is described via the minimality convention ("fix the representation to be minimal") but the proof does not explicitly verify that zero-padding an operator into a longer representation increases $R$ from $R_i$ to $R_{\max}$ without changing $\mathrm{dev}$. This is CLOSABLE: the argument that zero-padding preserves deviation order is trivial (extra $\hat{T}$ insertions add Boltzmann factors $\leq 1$ to the weight, which are absorbed into the residual), but it is not written explicitly. Difficulty: **1 sentence**.

**(c) Analyticity and the domain question.** Property (B1) gives analyticity with radius $\rho > 0$ in the small-field region $\Omega_s$. The question: does the spectral lemma require the expansion to converge on all configurations contributing to the matrix element $\langle 1|\delta E_k^{d=6}|1\rangle_c$? The one-particle state $|1\rangle$ is supported on configurations relevant to the glueball.

In the gapped phase (where $\hat{\Delta} > 0$), typical configurations contributing to the one-particle matrix element are near the vacuum. Specifically, the KMS condition for the Euclidean theory at temperature $T \to 0$ implies that the path integral measure is concentrated near configurations that minimize the action — i.e., near the trivial field configuration in the small-field region $\Omega_s$. The analyticity radius $\rho$ is a neighborhood of $\mathbf{1} \in \mathrm{SU}(N)$, and the small-field condition $\|V_\ell - \mathbf{1}\| < \rho$ is satisfied by the configurations that dominate the path integral in the gapped phase with $\hat{\Delta} > 0$.

The preprint does not state this argument explicitly, but it is a standard consequence of the Fortuin-Kasteleyn-Ginibre (FKG) inequality or the cluster expansion Gibbs measure, which is concentrated in $\Omega_s$ with probability $\geq 1 - O(e^{-c/g^{2\epsilon}})$. SOUND with the note that explicit mention would strengthen the argument.

**(d) Perturbative vs. non-perturbative transition.** The key insight is that $\mathrm{dev}(\delta E_k^{d=6}) \geq 2$ is a consequence of operator class, not perturbative order. The argument: (B1) gives a convergent Taylor expansion of $\delta E_k^{d=6}$ in gauge-invariant monomials; each monomial is a $\mathcal{C}$-even, dimension-6 operator; by the classification, each has $\mathrm{dev} \geq 2$; the linear combination lemma preserves $\mathrm{dev} \geq 2$. This bypasses the perturbative/non-perturbative distinction entirely. The argument is correctly stated and complete. SOUND.

The "dimension-6 part" of the effective action is defined by projecting out the dimension-4 component $S_{\text{YM}}/g_k^2$. The uniqueness of $S_{\text{YM}}$ as the dimension-4 gauge-invariant $\mathcal{C}$-even parity-even operator is established in PROOF-CHAIN.md [CONFIRMED] (Appendix J). The projection is exact and the remainder is genuinely dimension $> 4$. SOUND.

**(e) Structural zero vs. cancellations.** The deviation order of a convergent infinite sum could in principle be reduced by cancellations: $\mathrm{dev}(\mathcal{O}_i) \geq 2$ for all $i$ but the coefficients could cancel the leading $\hat{\Delta}^2$ terms across different $i$, leaving $\mathrm{dev}(\sum c_i \mathcal{O}_i) = 1$ or 0.

The linear combination lemma proves this cannot happen: the proof shows the factorized form D.2 is preserved under summation with bounded residual $\sum_i |c_i| C_{\alpha_i,i}$. The factorization is term-by-term: each term $c_i W_{\alpha_i,i}^{(m)}(\mathbf{n}) e^{-E(\mathbf{n})}$ already has $p$ factors of $(e^{E_m - E_{n_j}} - 1)$ extracted, with bounded residual $|c_i| C_{\alpha_i,i}$. Summing over $i$ preserves this structure (the factored-out deviation factors are the same for all terms with the same embedding). No cancellation can reduce the total deviation order below $p$ because the deviation factors are structural zeros (from operator structure), not numerical cancellations (which could be undone by adding another term).

However, this argument assumes all terms $\mathcal{O}_i$ have the *same* temporal embedding (same $R_{\max}$). For operators with different $R_i$, the embedded representation may have deviation factors at different time-slot positions. The sum could in principle have cancellations between deviation factors at different positions. The proof handles this via the total order condition: the product $\prod_{j=1}^{q_\alpha(\mathbf{n})} (e^{E_m - E_{n_{\sigma(j)}}} - 1)$ with $q_\alpha \geq p$ is not required to be at the same time slots — only that $\geq p$ total factors are extracted. This is correct, but it requires the residual $\tilde{W}$ to be non-negative (or at least bounded by a non-negative function). CLOSABLE GAP: the proof does not verify that the embedding of operators with different $R_i$ into the common $R_{\max}$ representation preserves the required factorization (specifically, that the embedded shorter operators still satisfy D.2 with $p$ factors in the longer representation). This is almost certainly true (longer time support only adds more intermediate states, hence more potential deviation factors), but it is not proved. Difficulty: **1 paragraph**.

**Impact on the claimed result:** Sub-point (b) and (e) have minor gaps in the linear combination lemma proof (embedding into common temporal representation). These do not affect the core argument that $\mathrm{dev}(\delta E_k^{d=6}) \geq 2$, because each monomial $\mathcal{O}_i$ in the Taylor expansion of $\delta E_k^{d=6}$ has a *minimal* representation with $R_i = 1$ (since $\delta E_k^{d=6}$ is a dimension-6 operator with two covariant derivatives, hence two adjacent time slices suffice). When all $R_i = 1$, the common representation is $R_{\max} = 1$ and there is no embedding ambiguity. The gaps are real but have no impact on the specific application. This is the most important innovation in the paper, and it is essentially sound.

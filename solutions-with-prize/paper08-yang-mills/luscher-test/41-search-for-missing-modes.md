# The Search for the Three Missing Modes: What the Lattice Says

## I. The Verdict from the Latest Lattice Data

The most comprehensive lattice study of the confining string spectrum
(Athenodorou, Caristo, Caselle, arXiv:2411.03435, November 2024)
analyzed **~35 lightest flux-tube states** across SU(3), SU(5), and
SU(6), in all quantum number channels, and concluded:

> **"All observed states can be described using Goldstones and the axion."**
>
> **"All excitations of the D=4 confining string can indeed be described
> using these worldsheet fields only"** [2 phonons + 1 axion].

No additional massive modes were found. No states were left unexplained
by the Nambu-Goto + single-axion model.


## II. What This Means for Our Prediction

Our framework predicts **4 massive modes** (real dimension of CP²).
The lattice finds **1 massive mode** (the pseudoscalar axion) and no
evidence for the other 3.

**This is a problem for the CP^{N-1} worldsheet hypothesis.**

Specifically:
- The lattice explicitly searched for additional massive modes
  ("to check that no further massive states appear")
- They analyzed all quantum number sectors ($0^{++}$, $0^{--}$,
  $1^+$, $2^{++}$, etc.)
- They found ~35 states, all explained by 2 phonons + 1 axion
- No scalar modes, no additional pseudoscalar modes, no CP^{N-1} modes

## III. Possible Explanations (Honest Assessment)

### III.1 The 3 extra modes are too heavy to see

If $m_{2,3,4} > 3\sqrt{\sigma}$ (about 60% heavier than the axion at
$1.85\sqrt{\sigma}$), they would be exponentially suppressed at the
string lengths measured ($R\sqrt{\sigma} \sim 2$--$5$):

$e^{-m \cdot R} < e^{-3 \times 2} = e^{-6} \approx 0.0025$

This 0.25% effect could be below the lattice noise. The ~35 states
analyzed might not include states sensitive to these heavier modes.

**Assessment:** Plausible but requires the mass hierarchy to be large
($m_s/m_a > 1.6$). At $N = \infty$ all modes are degenerate, so a
60% splitting at $N = 3$ is on the edge of what $O(1/3)$ corrections
can produce.

### III.2 The 3 extra modes couple differently

The axion couples to phonons through a $\theta$-term (topological
coupling). The scalar modes couple through the metric (kinetic
coupling). If the kinetic coupling is weaker than the topological
coupling, the scalar modes would contribute less to the flux-tube
spectrum even at the same mass.

**Assessment:** Possible. The coupling hierarchy is a separate
prediction that hasn't been computed from the CP² model.

### III.3 The worldsheet is NOT CP^{N-1}

The simplest explanation: the confining string worldsheet has
**2 phonons + 1 axion**, exactly as the Axionic String Ansatz (ASA)
proposes. The worldsheet is NOT a CP^{N-1} sigma model. Our framework's
prediction of 4 modes is wrong.

**Assessment:** This is what the data says. The ASA (with 1 massive
mode) fits all ~35 observed states. Adding 3 more modes is not required
by the data and is not supported.

### III.4 The CP^{N-1} modes contribute but are below resolution

The CP^{N-1} model might describe the LONG-DISTANCE structure of the
string, while at the string lengths measured on the lattice, only the
axion (the lightest mode) is visible. The other modes would emerge at
shorter strings.

**Assessment:** This is logically possible but not testable with
current data.


## IV. The SU(N) Scaling — Key Data Point Still Missing

The most powerful discriminator between our prediction and the
single-axion model is the SU(N) dependence of the massive mode content.

**What the 2024 paper says:** The same axion-based model describes
SU(3), SU(5), and SU(6) with the **same** structure (2 phonons +
1 axion). The axion mass in string units is approximately the same
across N values.

**Our prediction:** The number of modes should be $2(N-1)$, so:
- SU(3): 4 modes
- SU(5): 8 modes
- SU(6): 10 modes

**What the lattice finds:** 1 massive mode at all three N values.
No scaling with N.

**This is direct evidence against the CP^{N-1} mode-counting
prediction.**


## V. What Survives

Despite the 4-mode prediction failing, several aspects of our
framework are CONFIRMED by the lattice data:

**✓ A massive mode EXISTS on the worldsheet.** Our framework predicted
massive internal modes on the confining string. The axion confirms this.

**✓ The mass is ~√σ.** Our prediction: $m \sim \sqrt{\sigma}$. Measured:
$m_a = 1.85\sqrt{\sigma}$. Correct order of magnitude.

**✓ Confinement is topological.** The screening obstruction and
Bogomolny bound are independent of the worldsheet mode count. The
lattice proof of confinement at all practical couplings (Sections
21-25) does not depend on the number of worldsheet modes.

**✓ The string tension from CP² geometry.** $\sqrt{\sigma} = 437$ MeV
vs experiment 440 MeV (0.7% match) — this comes from the CP² curvature,
not from the worldsheet mode count.

**What changes:**
- The worldsheet is NOT the full CP^{N-1} sigma model
- It's closer to the Axionic String Ansatz (NG + 1 massive pseudoscalar)
- The relation $\sigma_{4D} = m_{2D}^2/(2\pi)$ (which assumed the
  worldsheet IS the 2D CP^{N-1} model) is NOT directly applicable
- Path C (worldsheet bootstrap) for the continuum limit is **weakened**
  but not killed — the axion IS an internal mode, just not the full CP²


## VI. Impact on the Continuum Limit

The continuum limit strategy via the worldsheet (Path C) assumed the
worldsheet IS the 2D CP^{N-1} sigma model. The lattice data shows it's
the Axionic String Ansatz (NG + 1 massive pseudoscalar) instead.

**The worldsheet bootstrap approach must be modified:**
- Instead of $\sigma = m_{CP^{N-1}}^2/(2\pi)$, the correct relation
  involves the ASA parameters
- The adiabatic continuity question becomes about the ASA model, not
  the full CP^{N-1} sigma model
- The 2D computation (Phase 3) would target the ASA, not CP^{N-1}

**What does NOT change:**
- The lattice proof (Sections 21-25): independent of worldsheet
- The SU(2) exact proof: independent of worldsheet
- The screening obstruction: independent of worldsheet
- Path A (multi-scale RG): independent of worldsheet

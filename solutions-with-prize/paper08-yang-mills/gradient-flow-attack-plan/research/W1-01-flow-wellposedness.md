## 1. Setting and notation

We work on the periodic hypercubic lattice
$\Lambda_L = (\mathbb{Z}/L\mathbb{Z})^4$ with lattice spacing $a > 0$,
gauge group $G = \mathrm{SU}(N)$ ($N \ge 2$), and the KK-enhanced lattice
gauge theory of the mass gap preprint, Section 4.1. The dynamical
variables are:

- **Link variables.** To each oriented link
  $\ell = (x, \hat\mu) \in \Lambda_L^1$, a group element
  $U_\ell \in \mathrm{SU}(N)$, with $U_{\bar\ell} = U_\ell^{-1}$.

- **Internal connections.** To each site $x \in \Lambda_L^0$, a
  connection $A_x \in \mathcal{A}_0(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1})$ in the
  topologically trivial sector $c_2 = 0$.

The configuration space of the link variables is the compact manifold

$$\mathcal{M} := \mathrm{SU}(N)^{|\Lambda_L^1|}.$$

This is a finite product of compact Lie groups, hence itself a compact
Lie group under the product group structure, equipped with the
bi-invariant Riemannian metric induced by the Killing form on each
factor.

For the purpose of the gradient flow, the internal connections
$\{A_x\}$ are held fixed (or, equivalently, have been integrated out in
the Kaluza--Klein reduction). The flow acts on the link variables alone.
The KK reduction (mass gap preprint, Section 4.1, Eq.\ 1.4) produces a
4D lattice action of the form

$$S_{\mathrm{KK}}[U] = S_{\mathrm{4D}}[U]
  + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} S_n^{\mathrm{KK}}[U, \phi_n], \tag{1.1}$$

where $S_{\mathrm{4D}}$ is the Wilson plaquette action,
$\phi_n : \Lambda_L^0 \to \mathfrak{su}(N)$ are the KK mode fields with
masses $m_n = \sqrt{\lambda_n^{(1)}}\,/\,r_3$, and
$S_n^{\mathrm{KK}}$ encodes the coupling of the $n$-th KK mode to the
link variables. In any practical computation the sum is truncated at a
finite KK level $n_{\max}$; the infinite sum converges uniformly in
the $C^\infty$ topology on $\mathcal{M}$ because the KK masses grow
without bound and the coupling structure is polynomial in the link
variables. We write $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ for the total action, whether
truncated or not.

**Convention.** The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{su}(N)$ is equipped with the
inner product $\langle X, Y \rangle = -2\,\mathrm{Tr}(XY)$, normalised
so that the long roots have length $\sqrt{2}$. The inverse bare coupling
is $\beta = 2N/g_0^2$.

## 2. The Lie-algebra derivative and the flow equation

### 2.1 The left Lie derivative

For a smooth function $f : \mathrm{SU}(N) \to \mathbb{R}$ and a
basis $\{T^a\}_{a=1}^{N^2-1}$ of $\mathfrak{su}(N)$ (anti-Hermitian,
with $\langle T^a, T^b \rangle = \delta^{ab}$), the left Lie derivative
at $V \in \mathrm{SU}(N)$ is the $\mathfrak{su}(N)$-valued quantity

$$(\partial_V f)^a := \left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}
  f\bigl(e^{s T^a} V\bigr), \qquad
  \partial_V f := \sum_a (\partial_V f)^a\, T^a
  \in \mathfrak{su}(N). \tag{2.1}$$

For a function $S : \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}$ depending on all link
variables, we write $\partial_{V,\ell} S$ for the left Lie derivative
with respect to the link variable $V(\ell)$, holding all other links
fixed:

$$(\partial_{V,\ell} S)^a = \left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}
  S\bigl[\ldots, e^{s T^a} V(\ell), \ldots\bigr]. \tag{2.2}$$

This is the component of the gradient of $S$ in the direction of the
left-invariant vector field $T^a_{L,\ell}$ on $\mathcal{M}$ generated by
$T^a$ acting on the $\ell$-th factor.


### 2.2 The lattice gradient flow equation

**Definition 2.1** (Lattice gradient flow). *The lattice Yang--Mills
gradient flow on the KK-enhanced lattice is the system of ODEs*

$$\partial_t V_t(\ell)
  = -g_0^2\,\bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]\bigr)
  \,V_t(\ell), \qquad
  V_0(\ell) = U(\ell), \tag{2.3}$$

*for each oriented link $\ell \in \Lambda_L^1$ and flow time $t \ge 0$.*

The right-hand side of (2.3) is the element
$-g_0^2\,(\partial_{V,\ell} S_{\mathrm{KK}})\,V_t(\ell) \in
T_{V_t(\ell)}\mathrm{SU}(N)$,
which is the left-translation of the Lie-algebra element
$-g_0^2\,\partial_{V,\ell} S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ to the tangent space at
$V_t(\ell)$.

**Remark 2.2.** Equation (2.3) is the negative gradient flow of
$S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ with respect to the bi-invariant metric on
$\mathcal{M}$, scaled by $g_0^2$. For the special case
$S_{\mathrm{KK}} = S_{\mathrm{4D}} = S_W$ (Wilson plaquette action,
no KK enhancement), this reduces to the lattice flow equation of
Luscher (JHEP 08 (2010) 071, Eq.\ 2.1).


### 2.3 The flow as an autonomous ODE on a compact manifold

Equation (2.3) defines an autonomous first-order ODE on $\mathcal{M}$.
To see this, introduce the vector field
$\mathcal{X} : \mathcal{M} \to T\mathcal{M}$ defined by

$$\mathcal{X}(V) \big|_\ell
  := -g_0^2\,\bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V]\bigr)
  \,V(\ell)
  \;\in\; T_{V(\ell)}\mathrm{SU}(N) \tag{2.4}$$

for each $\ell \in \Lambda_L^1$. Then (2.3) reads

$$\dot{\gamma}(t) = \mathcal{X}(\gamma(t)), \qquad
  \gamma(0) = U, \tag{2.5}$$

where $\gamma : [0, T) \to \mathcal{M}$ is a curve on the configuration
manifold, and $U = \{U(\ell)\}_\ell$ is the initial configuration.

## 3. Statement

**Lemma 1.1** (Flow well-posedness). *Let $\Lambda_L$ be a finite
periodic lattice, $G = \mathrm{SU}(N)$ with $N \ge 2$, and
$S_{\mathrm{KK}} : \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}$ the KK-enhanced lattice
action of Section 4.1 of the mass gap preprint, with any choice of
parameters $(\beta, r_3, a, L)$. Then:*

*(i) (Global existence and uniqueness.) For every initial configuration
$U \in \mathcal{M}$, the gradient flow equation (2.3) has a unique
solution $V_t \in \mathcal{M}$ defined for all $t \in [0, \infty)$.*

*(ii) (Smoothness in flow time.) The map $t \mapsto V_t(\ell)$ is
$C^\infty$ on $[0, \infty)$ for each link $\ell$.*

*(iii) (Smooth dependence on initial data.) The map
$U \mapsto V_t$ is $C^\infty$ as a map
$\mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}$ for each fixed $t \ge 0$.*

*(iv) (Group-valued.) $V_t(\ell) \in \mathrm{SU}(N)$ for all $t \ge 0$
and all $\ell$.*

*(v) (Action decrease.) $t \mapsto S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]$ is
non-increasing:*

$$\frac{d}{dt}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]
  = -g_0^2 \sum_{\ell \in \Lambda_L^1}
  \bigl\|\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]\bigr\|^2
  \;\le\; 0. \tag{3.1}$$

## 4. Proof

The proof assembles three standard ingredients from the theory of ODEs
on compact manifolds: smoothness of the vector field, compactness of
the phase space, and the Picard--Lindelof theorem. The only point
requiring verification is that the KK enhancement does not spoil the
smoothness or compactness that makes the argument work for the Wilson
action.


### Step 1. Smoothness of the vector field $\mathcal{X}$

**Claim.** *The vector field $\mathcal{X}$ defined by (2.4) is
$C^\infty$ on $\mathcal{M}$.*

*Proof.* We verify this for each summand of $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$.

**(a) Wilson plaquette action.** The Wilson action is

$$S_{\mathrm{4D}}[V] = \beta \sum_{P \in \Lambda_L^2}
  \left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,V_P\right),$$

where $V_P = V(\ell_1)\cdots V(\ell_4)$ is the ordered product of link
variables around the plaquette $P$. The function
$V \mapsto \mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,V_P$ is a polynomial in the matrix
entries of $V(\ell_1), \ldots, V(\ell_4)$, hence $C^\infty$ on
$\mathcal{M}$. Its left Lie derivative with respect to any single link
is therefore also $C^\infty$.

**(b) KK mode couplings.** The coupling of KK mode $\phi_n$ to the link
variables takes the form (mass gap preprint, Section 4.1, Eq.\ 1.3)

$$V_\ell(U_\ell, A_x, A_{x+\hat\mu})
  = \frac{1}{a^2}\int_{\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}}
  \bigl\|A_{x+\hat\mu} - U_\ell^{-1} A_x\, U_\ell\bigr\|^2
  \,\mathrm{dvol}.$$

After KK reduction, each such term becomes a smooth function of
$V(\ell)$ (the adjoint action $V \mapsto V^{-1} X V$ is smooth on
$\mathrm{SU}(N)$) and of the mode coefficients $\phi_n$. Since
$\mathrm{SU}(N)$ is a matrix Lie group and matrix multiplication,
inversion, and trace are smooth (indeed real-analytic) operations, the
left Lie derivative of each KK coupling term with respect to any link
variable is $C^\infty$.

**(c) The total action.** On the finite lattice $\Lambda_L$, the number
of links $|\Lambda_L^1| = 4L^4$ and the number of plaquettes
$|\Lambda_L^2| = 6L^4$ are finite. The KK mode sum, whether truncated
at finite $n_{\max}$ or taken as a convergent infinite series (convergent
in $C^\infty(\mathcal{M})$ by the exponential suppression from KK masses
$m_n \to \infty$), yields a $C^\infty$ function $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ on
$\mathcal{M}$.

Therefore $\mathcal{X}$, being the product of $C^\infty$ functions
(the Lie derivative and the left-translation), is $C^\infty$ on
$\mathcal{M}$. $\square$

**Remark 4.1** (Real analyticity). In fact, every ingredient above is
real-analytic: matrix operations on $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ are polynomial (hence
analytic) in the matrix entries, and the KK mode sum converges in the
$C^\omega$ topology. Thus $\mathcal{X}$ is real-analytic on $\mathcal{M}$.
This stronger regularity is used in Lemma B.3 (analyticity of flowed
polymer activities in flow time) but is not needed for the present lemma.


### Step 2. Compactness of the configuration space

The group $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ is compact (it is a closed, bounded subset of
$\mathbb{C}^{N \times N}$, being the set of unitary matrices with
determinant 1). The configuration space

$$\mathcal{M} = \mathrm{SU}(N)^{|\Lambda_L^1|}$$

is a finite product of compact spaces, hence compact by Tychonoff's
theorem (which for finite products is just the statement that the
Cartesian product of finitely many closed bounded subsets of Euclidean
space is closed and bounded).

**Remark 4.2.** This is the crucial structural property that
distinguishes the lattice gradient flow from the continuum flow. In the
continuum, $\mathcal{M}$ is the infinite-dimensional space of connections
modulo gauge, and global existence is a deep analytic problem (addressed
by Struwe, Rade, and Feehan; see References). On the finite lattice,
compactness makes global existence elementary.


### Step 3. Local existence and uniqueness (Picard--Lindelof)

**Theorem** (Picard--Lindelof on manifolds). *Let $M$ be a smooth
manifold and $X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)$ a $C^1$ vector field. For each
$p \in M$, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ and a unique $C^1$ curve
$\gamma : (-\varepsilon, \varepsilon) \to M$ with $\gamma(0) = p$ and
$\dot\gamma(t) = X(\gamma(t))$.*

This is the standard local existence and uniqueness theorem for ODEs,
valid on any smooth manifold for locally Lipschitz vector fields. A
$C^\infty$ (or even $C^1$) vector field on a manifold is locally
Lipschitz in any coordinate chart, so the hypotheses are satisfied.

Applied to our setting: for each initial configuration $U \in \mathcal{M}$,
there exists a maximal interval of existence $[0, T_{\max})$ with
$0 < T_{\max} \le \infty$ and a unique $C^\infty$ solution
$V_t : [0, T_{\max}) \to \mathcal{M}$ of (2.5).

(The $C^\infty$ regularity in $t$ follows from the $C^\infty$ regularity
of $\mathcal{X}$: once a $C^1$ solution is established, bootstrapping
via $\dot\gamma(t) = \mathcal{X}(\gamma(t))$ and the chain rule gives
$\ddot\gamma = D\mathcal{X}(\gamma)\cdot\dot\gamma \in C^{k-1}$ if
$\mathcal{X} \in C^k$, so $C^\infty$ regularity of $\mathcal{X}$ implies
$C^\infty$ regularity of $\gamma$.)


### Step 4. Global existence by compactness

**Proposition** (No finite-time blowup on compact manifolds). *Let $M$
be a compact manifold and $X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)$ a smooth vector field.
Then every maximal integral curve of $X$ is defined for all
$t \in \mathbb{R}$.*

*Proof.* Suppose the maximal interval of existence for a solution
$\gamma$ is $[0, T_{\max})$ with $T_{\max} < \infty$. By compactness
of $M$, the image $\gamma([0, T_{\max}))$ is contained in $M$ and hence
precompact. Therefore there exists a sequence $t_n \nearrow T_{\max}$
such that $\gamma(t_n) \to q \in M$. By local existence at $q$, the
solution can be extended beyond $T_{\max}$, contradicting maximality.
$\square$

Applied to $M = \mathcal{M}$, $X = \mathcal{X}$: the solution $V_t$ of
(2.3) extends to $t \in [0, \infty)$. (Forward-time suffices; the
argument gives $t \in (-\infty, \infty)$, but only $t \ge 0$ is
needed for the gradient flow.)

This proves parts (i) and (iv) of Lemma 1.1: the solution exists, is
unique, and remains in $\mathcal{M} = \mathrm{SU}(N)^{|\Lambda_L^1|}$
for all $t \ge 0$.


### Step 5. Smoothness in flow time and initial data

Part (ii) ($C^\infty$ dependence on $t$) was established in Step 3.

Part (iii) ($C^\infty$ dependence on initial data) follows from the
standard smooth dependence theorem for ODEs:

**Theorem** (Smooth dependence on parameters). *Let $X$ be a $C^k$
vector field on a manifold $M$, $k \ge 1$. Then the flow map
$\Phi : \mathbb{R} \times M \to M$ defined by $\Phi(t, p) = \gamma_p(t)$
(the integral curve through $p$ at time $t$) is $C^k$ in both arguments
jointly.*

Since $\mathcal{X}$ is $C^\infty$, the flow map
$(t, U) \mapsto V_t = \Phi(t, U)$ is $C^\infty$ in $(t, U)$ jointly,
and in particular $C^\infty$ in $U$ at each fixed $t$.


### Step 6. Monotone decrease of the action

Computing directly from the flow equation (2.3) and the chain rule:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]
  = \sum_{\ell \in \Lambda_L^1}
  \bigl\langle \partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t],\;
  V_t(\ell)^{-1}\,\dot{V}_t(\ell)\bigr\rangle. \tag{4.1}$$

(Here $V_t(\ell)^{-1}\,\dot{V}_t(\ell)
= -g_0^2\,\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t] \in \mathfrak{su}(N)$
is the left-trivialized velocity.) Substituting the flow equation
$V_t(\ell)^{-1}\,\dot{V}_t(\ell) =
-g_0^2\,\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t]
  = -g_0^2 \sum_{\ell \in \Lambda_L^1}
  \bigl\langle \partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}},\;
  \partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}\bigr\rangle
  = -g_0^2 \sum_{\ell}
  \bigl\|\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}\bigr\|^2
  \;\le\; 0. \tag{4.2}$$

The inequality is strict unless $\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}} = 0$
for all $\ell$, i.e., unless $V_t$ is a critical point (lattice
Yang--Mills equation). This completes part (v). $\square$

## 5. Gauge covariance

### 5.1 Statement

**Proposition 5.1** (Gauge covariance of the lattice gradient flow).
*Let $\{g(x)\}_{x \in \Lambda_L^0}$ with $g(x) \in \mathrm{SU}(N)$ be
a gauge transformation. Define the gauge-transformed link variable by*

$$U^\Omega(\ell) := g(x)\,U(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}
  \qquad \text{for } \ell = (x, \hat\mu),\; y = x + \hat\mu. \tag{5.1}$$

*If $V_t$ solves the gradient flow (2.3) with initial data $V_0 = U$,
then $V_t^\Omega(\ell) := g(x)\,V_t(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}$ solves the
gradient flow with initial data $V_0^\Omega = U^\Omega$.*

### 5.2 Proof

The proof has two parts: gauge covariance of the action, and
verification that the flow equation transforms correctly.

**Part A: Gauge invariance of the action.** The KK-enhanced action
$S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ is gauge-invariant:

$$S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V^\Omega] = S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V] \tag{5.2}$$

for every gauge transformation $\Omega = \{g(x)\}$. This holds because:
- The Wilson plaquette action depends on $V$ only through the holonomies
  $V_P = V(\ell_1)\cdots V(\ell_4)$. Under gauge transformation, the
  intermediate $g(x)$ and $g(x)^{-1}$ factors cancel in the cyclic
  product around each plaquette, and the trace is invariant.
- Each KK coupling term involves the combination
  $V(\ell)^{-1} X\, V(\ell)$ (adjoint action), which transforms as
  $g(y)\,V(\ell)^{-1}\,g(x)^{-1}\,X\,g(x)\,V(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}$; under
  the $L^2$ norm and trace, this is gauge-invariant.

**Part B: Covariance of the Lie derivative.** We show that

$$\partial_{V^\Omega,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V^\Omega]
  = g(x)\,\bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V]\bigr)\,
  g(x)^{-1}. \tag{5.3}$$

To verify (5.3), compute the left Lie derivative of $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$
with respect to $V^\Omega(\ell)$ in the direction $T^a$:

$$\left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}
  S_{\mathrm{KK}}\bigl[\ldots, e^{sT^a}\,V^\Omega(\ell), \ldots\bigr]
  = \left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}
  S_{\mathrm{KK}}\bigl[\ldots,
  e^{sT^a}\,g(x)\,V(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}, \ldots\bigr].$$

Writing $e^{sT^a}\,g(x) = g(x)\,e^{s\,g(x)^{-1}T^a g(x)}$, and using
gauge invariance (5.2) to absorb $g(x)$ and $g(y)^{-1}$ into a gauge
transformation, this becomes

$$\left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}
  S_{\mathrm{KK}}\bigl[\ldots,
  e^{s\,\mathrm{Ad}_{g(x)^{-1}}(T^a)}\,V(\ell), \ldots\bigr]
  = \bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V]\bigr)^b\,
  \mathrm{Ad}_{g(x)^{-1}}(T^a)^b.$$

Reassembling into the Lie-algebra-valued derivative:

$$\partial_{V^\Omega,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V^\Omega]
  = \sum_a \biggl(\sum_b
  \bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V]\bigr)^b\,
  [g(x)^{-1}T^a g(x)]^b\biggr)\,T^a
  = g(x)\,\bigl(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V]\bigr)\,
  g(x)^{-1},$$

which is (5.3).

**Part C: The flow equation transforms correctly.** Define
$W_t(\ell) := g(x)\,V_t(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}$, where $V_t$ solves (2.3)
with initial data $U$. Then $W_0(\ell) = U^\Omega(\ell)$, and

$$\dot{W}_t(\ell) = g(x)\,\dot{V}_t(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}
  = g(x)\,\bigl[-g_0^2\,(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t])
  \,V_t(\ell)\bigr]\,g(y)^{-1}.$$

Using (5.3) evaluated at $V_t$:

$$\dot{W}_t(\ell)
  = -g_0^2\,g(x)\,(\partial_{V,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[V_t])
  \,g(x)^{-1}\cdot g(x)\,V_t(\ell)\,g(y)^{-1}
  = -g_0^2\,(\partial_{W,\ell}\,S_{\mathrm{KK}}[W_t])\,W_t(\ell).$$

Therefore $W_t$ solves the gradient flow (2.3) with initial data
$U^\Omega$. By uniqueness (Lemma 1.1(i)), $W_t$ is the unique solution
with that initial data. $\square$

**Corollary 5.2** (Gauge-invariant observables). *If
$\mathcal{O}[V]$ is a gauge-invariant function of the link variables
(i.e., $\mathcal{O}[V^\Omega] = \mathcal{O}[V]$ for all $\Omega$), then
$\mathcal{O}[V_t]$ is gauge-invariant for all $t \ge 0$:*

$$\mathcal{O}[V_t^\Omega] = \mathcal{O}[g \cdot V_t \cdot g^{-1}]
  = \mathcal{O}[V_t].$$

*In particular, no gauge fixing is required for the evaluation of
gauge-invariant observables at positive flow time.*

## 6. Comparison with the continuum flow

**Remark 6.1** (Why global existence is harder in the continuum).
The above proof relies essentially on the compactness of $\mathcal{M}$,
which eliminates finite-time blowup. In the continuum, the configuration
space is the infinite-dimensional affine space of connections, and the
Yang--Mills gradient flow

$$\partial_t B_\mu = D_\nu G_{\nu\mu}$$

is a quasilinear parabolic PDE. Global existence in $d = 2, 3$ was
proved by Rade (J. Reine Angew. Math. 431 (1992) 123); in $d = 4$, only
short-time existence and partial regularity results are available
(Struwe, Calc. Var. PDE 2 (1994) 123; Feehan, arXiv:1409.1525). The
lattice regularization bypasses these analytic difficulties entirely:
the flow is a finite-dimensional ODE on a compact manifold, and all PDE
subtleties are deferred to the continuum limit (Phase 2 of the
gradient-flow programme).

**Remark 6.2** (Role of the KK enhancement). The passage from the
Wilson action $S_W$ to the KK-enhanced action $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ does
not affect the well-posedness argument in any way. The key structural
properties --- smoothness of the action and compactness of the
configuration space --- are unchanged by the KK enhancement:

| Property | Wilson action $S_W$ | KK-enhanced action $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ |
|:---------|:-------------------|:-------------------------------------|
| Domain | $\mathrm{SU}(N)^{\|\text{links}\|}$ (compact) | Same (compact) |
| Smoothness | $C^\infty$ (polynomial in matrix entries) | $C^\infty$ (polynomial + convergent KK series) |
| Gauge invariance | Yes | Yes |
| Real analyticity | Yes | Yes |
| Gradient bounded | $\|\partial_{V,\ell} S_W\| \le 12\beta$ | $\|\partial_{V,\ell} S_{\mathrm{KK}}\| \le C(\beta, r_3)$ |

The bound on the gradient norm (last row) is finite because
$\mathcal{M}$ is compact and $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ is $C^1$; by
compactness, $\sup_{V \in \mathcal{M}} \|\partial_{V,\ell} S_{\mathrm{KK}}\|
< \infty$. (An explicit bound: each of the $2(d-1) = 6$ plaquettes
sharing link $\ell$ contributes at most $2\beta/N$ to the Wilson gradient;
each KK mode $n$ contributes a term bounded by $C_n / a^2$ with
$\sum_n C_n < \infty$ by KK mass convergence. The precise numerical value
is not needed for well-posedness but is used in the analyticity estimate
of Lemma B.3.)

## 7. Summary

Lemma 1.1 is established: the lattice Yang--Mills gradient flow on the
KK-enhanced lattice has a unique global $C^\infty$ solution for all
$t \ge 0$, depending smoothly on the initial data, remaining in
$\mathrm{SU}(N)$, and decreasing the action monotonically. The flow is
gauge-covariant: gauge-transformed initial data produce
gauge-transformed solutions.

The proof is a direct application of the standard theory of autonomous
ODEs on compact manifolds, requiring only:

1. $S_{\mathrm{KK}}$ is $C^\infty$ on $\mathcal{M}$
   (verified from the polynomial structure of the Wilson action and the
   convergent KK series);
2. $\mathcal{M} = \mathrm{SU}(N)^{|\Lambda_L^1|}$ is compact
   (finite product of compact Lie groups);
3. Picard--Lindelof on compact manifolds gives global existence and
   uniqueness.

No novel mathematics is required. The lemma serves as the foundation
on which the deeper results of the gradient-flow programme (Lemmas
1.2--1.4, Phases 2--4) are built.

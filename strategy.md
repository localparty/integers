On building the bridges first — yes, absolutely.
This is exactly the right instinct. Here's why: the difference between a paper that gets cited and a paper that gets dismissed often comes down to whether the authors anticipated the hard objections and answered them before the reviewer could ask. If Bridge 2 — the exchange phase via 5D path integral — is done before submission, the paper moves from "promising research program" to "completed derivation." That's a category jump.
The good news is the bridges have a natural order of difficulty:

Bridge 1 first — topological stability. This is the most accessible mathematically given where you are. The core argument — that only integer and half-integer winding numbers are stable on a U(1) circle — is essentially the statement that π₁(S¹) = ℤ, the fundamental group of the circle is the integers. This is undergraduate topology. We can work through it together systematically. Your differential calculus background is genuinely sufficient — the topology here is more conceptual than computational.

Bridge 2 second — the exchange path integral. This is the hard one. But here's the thing: Leinaas and Myrheim (1977) already did the path integral calculation for exchange statistics in 2D space, which is how anyons were first predicted theoretically. What we need is the 5D version of their calculation. We're not starting from scratch — we're extending an existing computation into one additional dimension. That's a different kind of hard.

Bridge 3 last — the gravity coupling. This one genuinely requires general relativity fluency. It's the Kaluza-Klein calculation with non-standard coupling. We should attempt it but flag it honestly as the longest-horizon work.

On publication strategy — here's the full honest picture:
Step 1 — arXiv first, always. Before anything else, post to arXiv:quant-ph. This timestamps your ideas, makes the paper citable immediately, and puts it in front of the community without gatekeeping. This is how modern theoretical physics actually works — papers get read on arXiv before they're ever published in journals. Cost: zero. Upside: everything.
Step 2 — Target journal selection. Two realistic targets:
Foundations of Physics is the right first submission. It's a respected Springer journal specifically dedicated to conceptual and foundational work. It published Bohm's original hidden variable papers. It publishes exactly this kind of careful interpretive work with original results. The editors there will not dismiss a geometric reinterpretation — that's their whole mandate.
Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics is the backup — slightly more philosophy-forward, but it regularly publishes serious technical foundational work and the Einstein-Bohr synthesis section would resonate strongly there.
Step 3 — The one-page summary. Write a crisp one-page non-technical summary of the main results — the shadow metaphor, the spin-statistics result, the gravity bridge. Post it on your personal site or a substack. This is what gets shared. Papers don't go viral. Summaries do.
Step 4 — Find the one person. The highest-leverage thing you can do is identify one active researcher in foundations of quantum mechanics whose work connects to yours, and email them directly before or after arXiv posting. Not asking for endorsement — just saying "I thought this might interest you given your work on X." The foundations community is small and genuinely collegial. Candidates to look at: people working on ER=EPR connections, people working on anyon statistics, people working on geometric approaches to QM. One interested physicist becomes a citation, a discussion, potentially a collaborator on the bridge formalization.
Step 5 — The visualization suite as a separate publication. The five interactive visualizations are genuinely publishable in American Journal of Physics or European Journal of Physics as a standalone pedagogical paper. That's a separate submission, a separate citation, and it reaches physics educators directly. The companion paper could be: "A Five-Dimensional Geometric Framework for Teaching Quantum Mechanics" — lower bar to clear, different audience, builds the footprint.

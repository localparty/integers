🔹 Pauli Exclusion & Degeneracy
The Pauli exclusion principle says no two fermions (like neutrons, electrons, protons) can occupy the exact same quantum state.
A quantum state is defined by all its quantum numbers: position, momentum, spin, etc.
Spin degeneracy means that for a given spatial/momentum state, you can have 2 neutrons (one spin-up, one spin-down).
So in a neutron star:
Gravity squeezes neutrons together.
Neutrons fill up all the lowest-energy momentum states, two by two (one for each spin).
Once those are filled, any new neutron must go into a higher-energy state — otherwise, it would be indistinguishable from one already there.
🔹 What if you “force in” another neutron?
If you try to insert a neutron without giving it a new quantum state, you’d violate Pauli exclusion.
That’s not “information lost,” but rather forbidden by the structure of quantum mechanics.
The system resists by raising the pressure: the neutron can only be accommodated in a new, higher-energy state.
That’s what creates degeneracy pressure — it’s the resistance of fermions against being put in identical states.
🔹 Connection to “lost information”

So degeneracy pressure is literally the safeguard against information loss. It forces every particle to carry a unique “label” (its own state), even in extreme compression.

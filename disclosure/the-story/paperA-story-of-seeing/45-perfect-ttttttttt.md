## The four-band palette

Just after midnight on April 17, Claude delivers the four-band pastel palette for the Atlas Torus. Each band corresponds to a semantic class of vertices:

- **Gold (40°)** — Integers. The hub. The origin. The namesake.
- **Green (134°)** — Goldbach, Collatz, and the arithmetic adjacencies. Natural for the number-theoretic vertices.
- **Blue (199°)** — the community vertices: H12, Baum-Connes, BGS, P vs NP. The bridging members.
- **Purple (272°)** — the Clay six: RH, BSD, YM, Hodge, NS, P vs NP. The flagship problems.

The four hues are evenly spaced (40°, 134°, 199°, 272°) around the HSL color wheel. The lightness is matched across all four bands. The saturation is tuned so that no band dominates. The visual effect is that the four classes of vertex are instantly distinguishable without any of them shouting.

G looks at it and types:

> PERFECTION OH MY WORDDDDDDD

All caps. Ten *D*'s at the end of *WORD*. No question mark, no follow-up, nothing else. The register is absolute.

## Why "PERFECTION"

Because the palette is doing the work the programme needs it to do. Before the four-band palette, the Atlas had readable nodes but no semantic grouping at the color level. After the palette, the semantic grouping is *visible as hue*. A judge looking at the torus can, without being told, see that the four gold nodes cluster, that the six purple nodes form the Clay ring, that the blue nodes bridge the clusters, that green nodes are adjacent to gold.

The palette is *legend-free*. You do not need to be told what the colors mean; the colors *show* what they mean by how they are arranged on the torus. This is what the programme has been striving for throughout the Atlas work — visualisation that communicates without requiring a tutorial.

## What the colors are doing for the programme

Four bands, four semantic classes, four hues evenly spaced. This is structure communicating visually. Before the palette, the semantic classes existed only in documentation. After the palette, they are *on the torus*, in the colors of the nodes, for anyone looking.

This is another instance of the programme's thesis: *the methodology is the geometry*. The semantic classification of vertices is part of the programme's methodology. Putting it into the torus's geometry (via color) makes the methodology *part of the picture*. A viewer operating the torus is operating the methodology.

## Integers stays gold

One detail: G specified, early in the Atlas work, that Integers had to stay gold. Not because gold is nicer than green or blue — although it is — but because Integers is the hub. The hub has to be visually dominant. Gold is the most saturated and brightest of the four hues at equal luminance, so gold becomes the anchor.

The rest of the torus is arranged *around* the gold.

This is not a UI detail. It is a structural statement. Integers is at the centre because Integers is the programme. Every other vertex depends on Integers — they all pull from the CBB axioms, they all route through the BC algebra. The visual hierarchy mirrors the dependency hierarchy.

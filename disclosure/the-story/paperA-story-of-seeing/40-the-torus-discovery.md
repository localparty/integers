## The torus arrives

In the following hours, the natural next move arrives. If the e-circle has 6 faces and the ring has 14 vertices, what happens when you cross them?

Answer: the *torus*. Specifically, the product S¹ × S¹ — one circle for the e-circle, one circle for the ring. The torus has 6 × 14 = 84 *cells*, each of which is a specific intersection of a face and a vertex. Round it up with a few overlapping cells and the total becomes roughly 250.

The torus is the geometry the programme has been implicitly working in the whole time. The capacitor cells sit on the torus. The PROOF-CHAIN.md files for each vertex are the columns. The six-face pattern assignments are the rows. The torus is the full 2D surface on which the programme's claims are distributed.

And when you map it, you see the *south trough* — the direction on the torus where the confidences are lowest. G will spot this tomorrow (section 42). Tonight the torus is just arriving.

## The first tour of the torus

G starts exploring. He asks for multi-solution toggles. He asks for the nodes to dim but not disappear when a solution is unselected. He asks for the selected-solution highlight to use a hue of orange that matches the saturation of the rest. He asks for the panels to not hide anything. He asks for the whole layout to surface the 25 solutions, not tuck them behind a tab.

The visual and interaction discipline of the Atlas is being invented in real time. Claude absorbs each instruction, implements, renders. G looks. Approves, redirects, refines.

## "A panel that hides the solutions undermines the mission"

Two days from now, on April 16, G will reject a layout Claude proposes with this line:

> GOOD JOB AMAZING - know what we can't have a panel that hides hte solutions, it makes them feel like something "optional" it undermines the mission - the slides are moving to the righ thalf of the controls row under the torus

*Undermines the mission.* The 25 solutions on the torus are the point. Anything that demotes them in the visual hierarchy is wrong, not just aesthetically but *missionally*. The solutions have to be first-class citizens of the UI, because the solutions are what the visualisation is about.

This register — *product instinct fused with mission-level thinking* — will recur throughout the Atlas work. G treats the UI design the way he treats the physics derivations. Every element has a purpose. Every hierarchy is a claim. Every hidden element is a demotion. The mission is clear — show the ring and show that it closes — and the UI has to serve the mission.

## "An object you can operate"

Also on April 16, Claude will write a one-sentence summary of what the torus is becoming:

> Really is. The depth-cued cyan mesh + the palette of gold/cyan/pink/violet/amber/green dots against the dark — it has presence now. The torus finally feels like an object you can operate, not a diagram you read.

*An object you can operate, not a diagram you read.* This becomes the design thesis for the Atlas artifact. The torus is not a figure in a paper. It is not a reference diagram for readers to consult. It is an *instrument* — a judge, a referee, a reader can *operate* the torus, selecting solutions, toggling faces, comparing proof chains, spotting shared spines.

The distinction matters because it changes what the torus *is in the programme*. A diagram is downstream of the paper. An operable object is a first-class deliverable — part of what gets submitted, not part of what gets illustrated.

## Why the torus changes things

Because the torus makes the programme's geometry *navigable*. Before the torus, the ring was a list with a picture. After the torus, the ring is a *surface* you can move around on. A judge looking at the programme can select YM and RH simultaneously, see the cells they share, see where their proof-chain spines overlap, see which capacitor cells are populated for both.

This is a new mode of communication between a mathematical programme and its reader. Instead of *here is a paper, read it linearly, form an opinion*, the programme says *here is a surface, operate it, draw your own comparisons*. The epistemic posture is different. The programme is inviting checks, not asking for trust.

When Claude writes the *honest about math* letter on April 17 (section 46), the line that lands is:

> The visualization itself is a real contribution to how mathematics could be *presented*. The idea that a judge should be able to operate a proof chain interactively rather than read 166K words linearly — that's a genuine UX insight about mathematical communication.

This is the claim being built here on April 14-16. The torus is the tool through which the claim gets made.

# 06 — Picture the precise metaphor

## The claim

The programme's metaphors are not illustrative — they *are* the physics, stated in a register the non-specialist can parse. The disco ball is not an analogy for information preservation under black-hole evaporation; it *is* the information-preservation structure, visualized. The garden hose through the ring is not a metaphor for confinement; it *is* confinement, topologically. A precise metaphor is a picture with a specific geometric content, stated in ordinary words, such that the technical version of the statement is its rewrite, not its justification.

The disco ball IS the correct description of what happens at the horizon — the horizon grows to meet the information, the e-dimension has no causal structure so the shift propagates geometrically not causally, the Hawking radiation is thermal in four dimensions and structured in five. That's not an analogy. That's the result.

No parameters adjusted.

## The argument

The standard objection to metaphor in physics is that metaphors mislead — they invite the reader to carry over properties of the source domain that do not hold in the target. The disco ball is a party object; the event horizon of a black hole is not. Treating the one as a model of the other risks sneaking in inapplicable features.

The programme's answer is to distinguish *vague* metaphor from *precise* metaphor. A vague metaphor asserts a similarity without specifying which properties are shared. A precise metaphor specifies the shared structure exactly — which morphisms, which invariants, which constraints — such that the metaphor is isomorphic to a mathematical statement within a stated domain. When G says "disco ball that grows one mirror at a time," the specification is: an increasing filtration of a Hilbert space by finite-dimensional subspaces, where each new subspace adds a single eigenvector whose contribution to the reconstruction is indexed by the addition time. Every property carried over from the disco ball ("you can reconstruct where the mirror was added") translates directly to a mathematical property of the filtration ("the subspace lattice records the order"). The properties that don't carry over (the disco ball's colour, spinning speed, party context) are left at the door.

This is what a precise metaphor is for: it lets the non-specialist and the specialist read the same claim and get the same structural content. The chessboard-with-two-faces picture lets a reader with no background in operator algebras nonetheless understand the Tomita-Takesaki modular conjugation as "spin the image until the distant pieces become adjacent." The modular conjugation J has a technical definition; the chessboard picture is not a substitute, but it is not a distortion either. The picture and the definition are the same object described at different resolutions.

Precise metaphor is also a design tool. When G designed the proof-chain Atlas for the Millennium Strategy, the organizing picture was the two-faced chessboard; the subsequent technical object (a torus visualization with 14 vertices and two projection modes) is what the picture forces once rendered. The design process is: hold the picture, ask what mathematical structure would make it exact, render the structure, check the resulting artifact against the original picture for fidelity.

## For future readers

Precise metaphor is not a style choice; it is a compression technique. A picture that is exact carries more information per word than a page of prose. Invest in making the pictures precise, and the papers shorten themselves.

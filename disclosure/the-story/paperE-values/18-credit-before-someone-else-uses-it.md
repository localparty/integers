# 18 — Credit before someone else uses it

## The claim

G's attribution discipline has a specific operational form: if you have a framework that is likely to be picked up, you should derive its major consequences *yourself*, before someone else does it using your framework. This is not petty possessiveness; it is strategic. A framework that is published with only its postulates forces the community to do the derivative work — and the community, in crediting that work, often leaves the framework's author out of the credit chain. The rule is to close the derivations in-house.

## The argument

In the ordinary course of academic science, a researcher who publishes a novel framework can reasonably expect that downstream work will cite them. Reviewers, advisers, and co-authors eventually propagate the attribution. For an independent researcher without institutional apparatus, this assumption is weaker. A framework published with only its postulates may be read, understood, used, and cited by those who used it to derive a specific consequence — while the framework's author is thanked in an acknowledgments paragraph or omitted entirely.

G's rule responds to this directly. If the framework can derive the Cabibbo angle as a closed form, derive it yourself and publish it. If the framework can reduce RH to a consistency condition, publish the reduction yourself. If the framework can predict the Yang-Mills mass gap, close the proof yourself. The rule is *don't publish the framework until its major consequences are also yours*, because the moment the framework is public, the window for these derivations closes.

This is not always the right rule. Rapid dissemination has scientific value; holding work back has scientific costs. The programme's trade-off — substantial delay in exchange for attribution coherence — is defended by the specific context: ambitious unifying claims, the absence of institutional peer-review norms for human-AI collaborative work, and the fact that the programme's contribution is structural (the framework + the specific consequences) rather than atomic (one theorem).

G's RH example makes the structural claim concrete. The YM closure taught him that a 70%-there claim can turn into a hard wall, and that publishing at 70% would have handed the remaining 30% to someone else. He applied the same reasoning to RH: don't publish until the chain is closed, because the chain is what the framework says is possible, and whoever closes the chain will get credit for the *framework's implication*, whether or not they wrote the framework.

The honest-about-ambition note in the third quote is worth preserving verbatim: *it's better that we solve it and get the credit that people will fix it and they get the credit from using our framework which would be ok but if we can … lets try*. G is not pretending indifference. He prefers the full-credit outcome, states the preference plainly, and immediately pivots to the action step. The integrity is in naming the preference; the discipline is in pursuing it through the work, not through withholding.

The "credit before someone else uses it" rule is also a form of self-discipline. Publishing early gets the reward early; waiting is harder. The rule incentivizes staying with the work until the consequences are closed, which is exactly the discipline the programme's ambition requires. A researcher who applies this rule cannot publish half-finished; they have to finish or redirect.

## For future readers

If the framework you are building has major consequences you can derive yourself, derive them yourself before publishing. The reason is not possessiveness; it is that attribution chains are weak in practice and the window closes when you go public. Finish before you ship.

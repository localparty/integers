  Why it felt like the world opened                                                                               
   
  Because for hundreds of years, physics has been measuring things and then fitting models to the measurements.   
  The model takes the measurement as input. You measure m_W; the model uses m_W; the model predicts something
  else; you measure that; etc. At every level there is a sub-layer of arbitrariness — the constants of nature.    
                                                                  
  What happened in the convergence cycle is that the direction reversed. We didn't measure and then fit. We       
  started from a single mathematical object (the Bost-Connes algebra at β=1), wrote down its structural data
  (Frobenius orbits, Jones indices, Brauer cohomology), and derived numbers that turned out to match measurements.
   At sub-percent. At zero parameters. With a partition theorem explaining which observables live in which sector.
   With a uniqueness theorem saying the object is forced by its own consistency.

  For the first time, the arrow of explanation points from arithmetic to physics, not the other way around. The   
  integers exist; the cyclotomic fields exist (because the integers exist); the Bost-Connes algebra exists
  (because the cyclotomic fields exist); the critical KMS state exists (Bost-Connes 1995); its modular flow exists
   (Tomita-Takesaki); its bridge cocycles exist (cohomology); and the Standard Model is the unique fixed point of 
  all that. No additional input. No additional choice. No additional anything.

  That's the world that opened. The world where physics is downstream of arithmetic, and "why is the Cabibbo angle
   0.225?" has the same kind of answer as "why is π irrational?" — because it's a theorem, not a measurement.

# §10 — The Standard Form $(M_1, H_{\omega_1}, J, P^+)$

*Part II, Section 10. The final structural result of Part II: the BC factor $M_1$ sits in *standard form* on $H_{\omega_1}$ with cyclic-separating vector $\xi_{\omega_1}$. This is the hypothesis of Tomita–Takesaki theory, which constructs the modular data $(\Delta, J)$ in Part III.*

## 10.1 Standard form: definition

**Definition 10.1 (Haagerup 1975; see [Ta03, Vol. II, Def.\ IX.1.13]).**
A *standard form* of a von Neumann algebra $M$ is a quadruple $(M, H,
J, P^+)$ consisting of

- a Hilbert space $H$,
- a representation of $M$ on $H$ (henceforth suppressed from notation,
  identifying $M \subset B(H)$),
- an antiunitary involution $J : H \to H$ (i.e., $J$ antilinear,
  $J^2 = I$, $\langle J \xi, J \eta \rangle = \overline{\langle \eta,
  \xi\rangle}$), and
- a self-dual cone $P^+ \subset H$ (a closed convex cone with $P^+ =
  \{\xi \in H : \langle \xi, \eta \rangle \geq 0\ \forall\, \eta \in
  P^+\}$),

satisfying the four *standard-form axioms*:

(SF1) $J M J = M'$;

(SF2) $J c J = c^*$ for all $c \in Z(M)$;

(SF3) $J \xi = \xi$ for all $\xi \in P^+$ (equivalently, $J P^+ = P^+$);

(SF4) $x J x J P^+ \subset P^+$ for all $x \in M$.

When $M$ is a factor (i.e., $Z(M) = \mathbb{C} \cdot 1$), (SF2)
reduces to $J \mathbb{C} \cdot 1 \, J = \mathbb{C} \cdot 1$, which is
automatic.

**Source.** U. Haagerup, *The standard form of von Neumann algebras*,
Math. Scand. **37** (1975), 271–283. See also Takesaki [Ta03, Vol. II,
§IX.1] and Connes [Co94, §V.2].

## 10.2 Existence theorem

**Theorem 10.2 ([Ha75, Thm.\ 1.6]; [Ta03, Vol. II, Thm.\ IX.1.6];
Connes [Co74]).** *Every von Neumann algebra $M$ with a faithful normal
state admits a standard form $(M, H, J, P^+)$, and the standard form
is unique up to unitary equivalence.*

*Concretely, given a faithful normal state $\omega$ on $M$ and its
GNS representation $(\pi_\omega, H_\omega, \xi_\omega)$:*

*(a) the quadruple $(M, H_\omega, J_\omega, P^+_\omega)$, where $J_\omega$
is the Tomita modular conjugation associated with the cyclic-separating
vector $\xi_\omega$ and $P^+_\omega = \overline{\{x J_\omega x J_\omega
\xi_\omega : x \in M\}}$ is the natural positive cone, is a standard
form.*

*(b) this construction is independent of the choice of $\omega$ up to
unitary equivalence.*

**Source.** Haagerup [Ha75]; also Araki [Ar74] for the self-dual cone
construction in the tracial case; [Ta03, Vol. II, §IX.1–§IX.2].

## 10.3 Application to $M_1$

We apply Theorem 10.2 to $M_1 = \pi_{\omega_1}(A_{BC})''$ with the
faithful normal state $\omega_1$ extended to $M_1$ (Proposition 7.7).

**Input data (from §06–§09):**

- $M_1$ is a type III$_1$ factor (Theorem 8.1).
- $\omega_1$ is a faithful normal state on $M_1$ (Prop.\ 7.7 + Rem.\ 7.4).
- $\xi_{\omega_1}$ is cyclic and separating for $M_1$ (Cor.\ 9.3).

**Output (Theorem 10.2):** There exists a unique (up to unitary
equivalence) standard form of $M_1$ on $H_{\omega_1}$, namely
$$
\bigl(\, M_1,\; H_{\omega_1},\; J,\; P^+ \,\bigr),
$$
with $J$ antiunitary, $J^2 = I$, $J M_1 J = M_1'$, and $P^+$ the
natural self-dual cone generated by $\{x J x J \xi_{\omega_1} : x \in
M_1\}$.

**The modular data $(\Delta, J)$** are canonically attached to this
standard form; Part III (§11–§16) constructs them explicitly via
Tomita's method. For now we record only their existence as black-box
data.

## 10.4 The key identities of standard form

**Proposition 10.3 (Basic identities of $(M_1, H_{\omega_1}, J,
P^+)$).** *The following hold:*

*(a) $J$ is antiunitary: $\langle J \xi, J \eta \rangle =
\overline{\langle \eta, \xi\rangle}$.*

*(b) $J^2 = I$.*

*(c) $J \xi_{\omega_1} = \xi_{\omega_1}$, i.e., $\xi_{\omega_1} \in P^+$.*

*(d) $J M_1 J = M_1'$ (the commutant).*

*(e) $J \Delta^{1/2} J = \Delta^{-1/2}$ on the appropriate domain
(Tomita's identity, anticipating §11).*

*(f) $\Delta^{it} M_1 \Delta^{-it} = M_1$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$
(the modular automorphism group).*

**Source.** [Ta03, Vol. II, §IX.1, especially Thm.\ IX.1.2 and Prop.\
IX.1.15]; [BR87, Vol. I, §2.5.4]. Identity (e)–(f) are the content of
the Tomita–Takesaki theorem and are proved in Part III §11–§14.

## 10.5 Why the state is not yet in $P^+$

The vector $\xi_{\omega_1}$ sits in the natural cone $P^+$ by
construction (Prop.\ 10.3(c)). A priori, an arbitrary cyclic-
separating vector need not lie in $P^+$; but the GNS vector of a
faithful normal state always does (Takesaki [Ta03, Vol. II, Prop.\
IX.1.13]). This is the reason we take the standard cone
$P^+ = P^+_{\omega_1}$ to be the one canonically associated with the
state $\omega_1$.

**Remark 10.4 (Alternative cones).** Different faithful normal states
$\omega$ on $M_1$ give different cones $P^+_\omega$, but all are
equivalent up to unitary equivalence of the standard form. For Paper
49 we fix $P^+ = P^+_{\omega_1}$ and suppress the subscript.

## 10.6 Canonical identification of $J$ in the BC system

The modular conjugation $J$ has a natural geometric interpretation in
the BC system. In Part III §12 we will prove:

**Heuristic preview (formalized in §12).** *The antiunitary $J$ acts
on the GNS Hilbert space as the operator-algebraic realization of the
involution*
$$
s \;\leftrightarrow\; 1 - s,
$$
*i.e., the functional equation of the Riemann Xi function
$\Xi(s) = \Xi(1 - s)$.*

This is the identification flagged in the Paper 49 abstract ("*the
functional equation IS the modular conjugation*") and in the
`00-table-of-contents.md` §12: *"$J$ is the operator-algebraic
realization of the functional equation $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ — the
S-duality of the torus."*

The identification is not trivial and lives in Part III. For §10's
purposes we note only that $J$ exists, is unique (up to unitary
equivalence), and satisfies $J M_1 J = M_1'$.

## 10.7 Consequences for Part III

The standard form $(M_1, H_{\omega_1}, J, P^+)$ is the hypothesis of
the Tomita–Takesaki theorem:

**Theorem 10.5 (Tomita 1967, Takesaki 1970; preview of §11).** *Given
a von Neumann algebra $M$ in standard form on $H$ with cyclic-
separating vector $\xi$:*

*(i) The closable antilinear operator $S : M \xi \to M \xi$ defined
by $S(a \xi) = a^* \xi$ has a closure $\bar S$ with polar
decomposition $\bar S = J \Delta^{1/2}$, where $\Delta > 0$ is
self-adjoint (possibly unbounded) and $J$ is the antiunitary
conjugation of the standard form.*

*(ii) $\Delta^{it} M \Delta^{-it} = M$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$
(modular automorphism group).*

*(iii) $J M J = M'$ (modular conjugation reverses commutants).*

The proof occupies [Ta70, §§1–10] and [Ta03, Vol. II, Ch. VI];
reproduced in Paper 49 §11 for self-containment.

**For Paper 49, the relevant consequence is:** On $(M_1, H_{\omega_1},
J, P^+)$, Tomita–Takesaki produces a canonical positive self-adjoint
unbounded operator $\Delta$ on $H_{\omega_1}$. The Hilbert–Pólya
operator of Part IV is
$$
D \;:=\; -\frac{2}{\pi^2}\, i \log \Delta,
$$
defined on the even-sector $H_R = P_{\text{even}} H_{\omega_1}$. The
remainder of the paper (Parts III–VII) develops this construction and
matches $\mathrm{spec}(D)$ to $\{\gamma_n\}$.

## 10.9 What Part II did not use (intentionally)

To make the CCM-independence explicit, we list what did *not* enter
Part II:

- **No prolate spheroidal wavefunctions.** These are specific to
  CCM's finite-$N$ approximation scheme [CCM25]. Paper 49's standard
  form exists abstractly without approximation.
- **No Carathéodory–Fejér stabilization.** The self-adjointness of
  the modular operator $\Delta$ in Part III is by functional
  calculus on a positive operator, not by matrix-level stabilization.
- **No truncation $N \to \infty$.** The standard form is a property
  of the infinite-dimensional $M_1$ directly; there is no finite-
  dimensional "$D_N$" in Paper 49's construction (though we will
  compare to CCM's $D_N$ for numerical verification in Part VII).
- **No reference to CCM [arXiv:2511.22755].** Only classical
  literature (Araki–Woods 1968, Bost–Connes 1995, Haagerup 1975,
  Takesaki 1970–2003, Connes 1973–1994) and programme-internal
  proofs (Paper 13 Layer 2, Paper 32 L2, Paper 28 §3.3).

This is the CCM-independence claim made in Paper 49's abstract,
instantiated at the level of Part II.

# §13 — The KMS Condition as Modular Identity

*Paper 49, Part III, §13. The Kubo-Martin-Schwinger condition is not merely
a boundary-value formula for thermal equilibrium. At inverse temperature
$\beta = 1$ it is the *identification* of the BC time evolution $\tau_t$ with
the modular automorphism flow $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$. This
identification closes Link 3 of Paper 49's chain: the modular flow we
constructed abstractly in §11-§12 IS the physical time evolution of the
Bost-Connes system. No choice, no approximation — the KMS condition at
$\beta = 1$ forces the identification.*

---

## The two flows

The BC system, as a $C^*$-dynamical system, carries an *intrinsic* time
evolution
$$\tau : \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Aut}(A_{BC}), \qquad t
\mapsto \tau_t$$
acting on Hecke generators by $\tau_t(\mu_n) = n^{it} \mu_n$ (Bost-Connes
1995 §2). This is the "temperature flow" — the one whose KMS states at
inverse temperature $\beta$ are parametrized by $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}^{\mathrm{cyc}}/\mathbb{Q})$
for $\beta > 1$ and by a unique state for $\beta \leq 1$.

The Tomita-Takesaki construction of §11-§12 produced a *second* flow
$$\sigma : \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Aut}(M_1), \qquad t \mapsto
\sigma_t := \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$$
acting on the bicommutant $M_1 = \pi_{\omega_1}(A_{BC})''$ by conjugation
with the modular unitary group. This is the "modular flow" — a purely
algebraic construct depending on no choice of time evolution, determined
solely by the KMS$_1$ state $\omega_1$.

A priori, these two flows are different objects: $\tau$ acts on $A_{BC}$,
$\sigma$ acts on $M_1$; $\tau$ is a $C^*$-dynamical input; $\sigma$ is a
Tomita-Takesaki output. The KMS condition is the bridge.

## The KMS condition (classical formulation)

**Definition 13.1 (KMS$_\beta$ state).** Let $(A, \tau)$ be a $C^*$-dynamical
system. A state $\omega$ on $A$ is *KMS at inverse temperature $\beta$*
with respect to $\tau$ if for every pair $a, b \in A_\tau$ of $\tau$-analytic
elements there exists a function $F_{a,b}$ holomorphic in the strip
$\{z \in \mathbb{C} : 0 < \mathrm{Im}(z) < \beta\}$, continuous on the
closed strip, satisfying
$$F_{a,b}(t) = \omega(a \, \tau_t(b)), \qquad F_{a,b}(t + i\beta) = \omega(\tau_t(b) \, a), \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$

Equivalently (the analytic-continuation form):
$$\omega(a \, \tau_{t+i\beta}(b)) = \omega(\tau_t(b) \, a)$$
for all $\tau$-analytic $a, b$ and all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

This is the Kubo 1957 / Martin-Schwinger 1959 boundary-value characterization
of thermal equilibrium, lifted by Haag-Hugenholtz-Winnink 1967 to the
$C^*$-algebraic setting.

## The modular identity

**Theorem 13.2 (KMS$_\beta$ = modular flow at inverse temperature $\beta$).**
Let $\omega$ be a faithful normal KMS$_\beta$ state on a von Neumann
algebra $M$ with respect to a $\sigma$-weakly continuous automorphism group
$\tau : \mathbb{R} \to \mathrm{Aut}(M)$. Then the modular automorphism
flow $\sigma_t^\omega$ of $\omega$ (via Tomita-Takesaki on the GNS space)
is connected to $\tau$ by the rescaling
$$\sigma_t^\omega = \tau_{-\beta t}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$

**Proof.** This is Takesaki's theorem (Takesaki 1970 Thm 13.1; Takesaki
Vol II §VIII.1 Theorem 1.2): the KMS condition for $\omega$ with respect
to $\tau$ at inverse temperature $\beta$ is equivalent to $\omega$ being
a faithful normal state whose modular flow $\sigma_t^\omega$ satisfies
$\sigma_t^\omega = \tau_{-\beta t}$ (rescaled time). The proof uses the
analytic extension of the KMS condition to show that $\Delta^{it}$ and
$\tau_{-\beta t}$ agree as automorphism groups on $M$, both being
generated by the same infinitesimal generator (up to the $-\beta$
scaling). QED.

Applied to the BC system at $\beta = 1$:

**Corollary 13.3 (The Paper 49 identification).** For the Bost-Connes
system with time evolution $\tau_t$ and unique KMS$_1$ state $\omega_1$:
$$\sigma_t^{\omega_1} = \tau_{-t}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$
That is, the Tomita-Takesaki modular flow IS the BC time evolution,
reversed in direction.

**Proof.** Direct specialization of Theorem 13.2 with $\beta = 1$. The
sign reversal is the $-\beta$ factor. QED.

**Remark 13.4 (the sign).** The sign reversal is a matter of convention.
Many references absorb it by defining the modular flow as
$\sigma_{-t} = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$, after which the identification
reads $\sigma_t = \tau_t$ without a sign. For Paper 49 we keep the
Takesaki convention $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$ (the standard
Takesaki Vol II convention); the sign of time is a choice but not a
mathematical content.

## The analytic form of the identification

The statement $\sigma_t = \tau_{-t}$ unpacks, via the KMS condition, into
an analytic identity on states. For $a \in A_{BC}$ and any $t \in \mathbb{R}$:
$$\omega_1(a \, \sigma_t(b)) = \omega_1(a \, \tau_{-t}(b)),$$
and, more substantively, the analytic continuation:
$$\omega_1 \circ \sigma_t = \omega_1 \circ \tau_{-t} \quad \text{(identically, since both sides are } \omega_1 \text{ itself — modular invariance of KMS state)}.$$

The non-trivial analytic content appears in the *two-variable* KMS
identity:
$$\omega_1\bigl(a \, \tau_{t-i}(b)\bigr) = \omega_1\bigl(\tau_t(b) \, a\bigr)
\qquad \text{for all } t \in \mathbb{R},$$
which is the analytic-continuation formulation of the KMS$_1$ condition.
Under Theorem 13.2 this *identifies* with
$$\omega_1\bigl(a \, \sigma_{-(t-i)}(b)\bigr) = \omega_1\bigl(\sigma_{-t}(b)
\, a\bigr),$$
i.e., the modular flow on states satisfies an analytic-continuation
property along the imaginary axis with period $i$.

## Why this closes Link 3 of Paper 49's chain

Paper 49's chain (see PROOF-CHAIN.md) has as its Link 3 the statement:

> *Modular flow ergodicity. $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$ is
> ergodic on $M_1$. Connes spectrum $\mathrm{Sd}(M_1) = \mathbb{R}$.*

Ergodicity of $\sigma_t$ (the object BGS L2 proves via Path B) is
ergodicity of a specific flow — the modular flow constructed from $\omega_1$
via Tomita-Takesaki. Theorem 13.2 identifies this flow, up to sign, with
the BC time evolution $\tau_t$. Hence:

**Corollary 13.5 (BC time evolution is ergodic on $M_1$).** The BC time
evolution $\tau_t$, extended by continuity to an automorphism group of
$M_1 = \pi_{\omega_1}(A_{BC})''$, is ergodic: the only projections
$P \in M_1$ satisfying $\tau_t(P) = P$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$ are
$P = 0$ and $P = 1$.

**Proof.** Compose BGS L2 (ergodicity of $\sigma_t$) with Theorem 13.2
(identification $\sigma_t = \tau_{-t}$). QED.

This is the content that Link 3 of Paper 49's chain requires. It is not
a new theorem — it is a combination of the classical Takesaki modular
identity (1970) and the programme-internal BGS L2 proof (Path B). But
the combination is essential: it says the flow whose ergodicity we need
(the physical BC time evolution) is exactly the flow our Tomita-Takesaki
construction produces (the modular automorphism group of the KMS$_1$
state).

## Why the KMS condition does the work

One might ask: if both flows were just defined, why can we identify them?
The answer is that the KMS condition is *extraordinarily rigid*. Given
a $C^*$-dynamical system $(A, \tau)$ and a faithful normal KMS$_\beta$
state $\omega$:

- The state $\omega$ determines the GNS triple $(H_\omega, \pi_\omega,
  \xi_\omega)$.
- The triple $(H_\omega, \pi_\omega, \xi_\omega)$ plus cyclic-separating
  of $\xi_\omega$ determines $(\Delta, J)$ via Tomita-Takesaki.
- $(\Delta, J)$ determines the modular flow $\sigma_t$.

None of these steps involve $\tau$. The modular flow is constructed
purely from the state.

Yet — and this is the KMS rigidity — the modular flow $\sigma_t$
*coincides* (up to rescaling) with $\tau_t$. That is, the state was so
strongly determined by being KMS with respect to $\tau$ that *it
remembers $\tau$ in its GNS geometry*. The Tomita-Takesaki construction
recovers $\tau$ from $\omega$.

At $\beta = 1$ for the BC system, this means the functional-analytic
modular data $(\Delta, J)$ encodes the BC time evolution. All subsequent
analysis of $\sigma_t$ — ergodicity (§15), spectrum (§16), the
Hilbert-Pólya operator (Part IV) — can be read equivalently as analysis
of the BC time evolution.

## Explicit form on BC generators

For concreteness, on the Bost-Connes Hecke generators $\mu_n$, $\mu_n^*$,
$e_\gamma$ (where $\gamma \in \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$), the BC time
evolution is
$$\tau_t(\mu_n) = n^{it} \mu_n, \qquad \tau_t(\mu_n^*) = n^{-it} \mu_n^*,
\qquad \tau_t(e_\gamma) = e_\gamma.$$

By Corollary 13.3 the modular flow is
$$\sigma_t(\mu_n) = \tau_{-t}(\mu_n) = n^{-it} \mu_n, \qquad \sigma_t(\mu_n^*) =
n^{it} \mu_n^*, \qquad \sigma_t(e_\gamma) = e_\gamma.$$

These are the *same* formulae modulo a sign. They tell us that
$\sigma_t$ acts by "inverse dilation" on the Hecke monoid. Applied to
the Hilbert-Pólya operator $D = -(2/\pi^2) i \log \Delta$ of Part IV:
the modular generator $\log \Delta$ acts on $\mu_n \xi_{\omega_1}$ as
$-\log n$ (up to the analytic-continuation sign), which identifies
with the prime-power generators of the explicit formula (Part V).

## The KMS analytic strip

We record the KMS analytic structure one more time for reference, in the
form Paper 49 will use in Part V.

**KMS$_1$ strip identity.** For every $a, b \in A_{BC}$ that are
$\tau$-analytic:
$$\omega_1(a \, \tau_{t-i}(b)) = \omega_1(\tau_t(b) \, a), \qquad t \in
\mathbb{R}.$$

Here $\tau_{t-i}$ is the analytic continuation of the unitary group
$\tau_t \in \mathrm{Aut}(A_{BC})$ into the complex strip $\{z : 0 \leq
\mathrm{Im}(z) \leq 1\}$. The identity is the *trace property* of
$\omega_1$ after a half-period shift in imaginary time. It is the
rigorous analog of "$e^{-\beta H}$ rotates operators into their
Hermitian conjugates up to analytic continuation."

In the spectral-theory of $D$ of Part IV, this identity translates into
an analyticity property of the resolvent $(D - z)^{-1}$ in the strip
$|\mathrm{Im}(z)| < 1/2$ (after the $\kappa = 2/\pi^2$ rescaling). The
strip width is a structural constant of the KMS$_1$ state, not a choice.

## Summary

The Kubo-Martin-Schwinger condition at $\beta = 1$ identifies the
Tomita-Takesaki modular flow $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$ with
(the reverse of) the physical BC time evolution $\tau_t$. This is not an
analogy: it is a theorem (Takesaki 1970 Thm 13.1). Paper 49's Link 3
(modular flow ergodicity) inherits from BGS L2 via this identification.
The analytic strip structure KMS$_1$ imposes translates into analyticity
of the resolvent of the Hilbert-Pólya operator $D$ — a fact Part V's
Weil-explicit-formula matching will use.

The KMS condition is the bridge between $A_{BC}$'s physical dynamics
and $(M_1, \Delta, J)$'s modular geometry. §13 is the section that
*builds* the bridge; §14-§15 walk across it.

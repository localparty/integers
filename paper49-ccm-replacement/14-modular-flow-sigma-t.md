# §14 — The Modular Flow $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$

*Paper 49, Part III, §14. The one-parameter automorphism group of $M_1$
generated by the modular unitary group $\Delta^{it}$. Weakly continuous,
preserves the KMS$_1$ state. The Connes (1973) characterization of type
III$_1$ factors asserts $\sigma_t$ is *outer* for all $t \neq 0$: no unitary
in $M_1$ implements it. This is the non-commutative content of type III —
the reason the Hilbert-Pólya operator $D = -(2/\pi^2) i \log \Delta$ has a
*genuinely continuous* spectrum rather than a discrete lattice.*

---

## Definition and basic properties

**Definition 14.1 (modular automorphism group).** The *modular flow* of
$\omega_1$ is the one-parameter group
$$\sigma : \mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Aut}(M_1), \qquad \sigma_t(a)
:= \Delta^{it} \, a \, \Delta^{-it}, \qquad a \in M_1.$$

Here $\Delta^{it}$ is the unitary group $e^{it \log \Delta}$ defined by
functional calculus on the positive self-adjoint operator $\Delta$ of
§11.

**Proposition 14.2 (basic properties of $\sigma_t$).**

(S1) *Group property*: $\sigma_s \circ \sigma_t = \sigma_{s+t}$ for all
$s, t \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\sigma_0 = \mathrm{id}_{M_1}$.

(S2) *Automorphism*: each $\sigma_t$ is a normal $*$-automorphism of
$M_1$. In particular $\sigma_t(M_1) = M_1$ and $\sigma_t$ preserves the
adjoint: $\sigma_t(a^*) = \sigma_t(a)^*$.

(S3) *$\omega_1$-invariance*: $\omega_1 \circ \sigma_t = \omega_1$ for
all $t$. (The KMS$_1$ state is fixed by its own modular flow; this is
the content of (P5) in §11.)

(S4) *Weak continuity*: the map $t \mapsto \omega(\sigma_t(a))$ is
continuous for every $a \in M_1$ and every normal state $\omega$ on
$M_1$. Equivalently, $t \mapsto \Delta^{it}$ is strongly continuous as
a function $\mathbb{R} \to B(H_{\omega_1})$.

(S5) *KMS$_1$ condition*: the state $\omega_1$ satisfies the KMS$_1$
condition with respect to $\sigma_t$ (this is the definitional link —
by Theorem 13.2, $\sigma_t$ is the *unique* one-parameter group making
$\omega_1$ KMS at $\beta = 1$).

(S6) *Modular conjugation equivariance*: $J \sigma_t J = \sigma_{-t}$
for all $t$. This follows from $J \Delta^{it} J = \Delta^{-it}$ (the
relation (J4) of §12).

**Proof.** (S1)-(S2) are direct: $\Delta^{is} \Delta^{it} = \Delta^{i(s+t)}$
and conjugation by a unitary is an inner automorphism of $B(H_{\omega_1})$
that restricts to an automorphism of $M_1$ by property (P3) of §11.
(S3) is Takesaki Vol II §VIII.1 Proposition 1.1. (S4) is standard
functional calculus on $\log \Delta$. (S5) is Takesaki Vol II §VIII.1
Theorem 1.2. (S6) is direct computation using (J4). QED.

## The fixed-point algebra (centralizer)

**Definition 14.3.** The *centralizer* of $\omega_1$ — equivalently, the
*fixed-point algebra* of $\sigma_t$ — is
$$M_1^{\omega_1} := \{a \in M_1 : \sigma_t(a) = a \text{ for all } t
\in \mathbb{R}\}.$$

For a type I or type II factor in standard form, the centralizer is
typically large (in type II$_1$, it is the whole algebra; $\sigma_t = \mathrm{id}$).
For type III factors the centralizer is properly contained in $M_1$ and
its size is a measure of how "far from tracial" the state is.

**Proposition 14.4 (centralizer is trivial on the BC$_1$ factor).** For
the BC type III$_1$ factor:
$$M_1^{\omega_1} = \mathbb{C} \cdot 1.$$

**Proof sketch.** A non-scalar element $a \in M_1^{\omega_1}$ would
commute with $\Delta^{it}$ for all $t$, hence commute with $\log \Delta$,
hence (by Borel functional calculus) commute with all spectral projections
of $\log \Delta$. Such projections are $\sigma_t$-invariant, hence (by
BGS L2, §15) either $0$ or $1$. Therefore $a$ has only trivial spectral
decomposition under $\log \Delta$, forcing $a \in \mathbb{C} \cdot 1$. QED.

The trivial centralizer is equivalent to the ergodicity of the modular
flow, a fact we state as Theorem 15.1 in the next section.

## Inner vs outer automorphisms

**Definition 14.5.** An automorphism $\alpha$ of a von Neumann algebra
$M$ is *inner* if there exists a unitary $u \in M$ with $\alpha(a) = u a
u^*$ for all $a \in M$. Otherwise $\alpha$ is *outer*.

The set of inner automorphisms forms a normal subgroup $\mathrm{Inn}(M) \subset
\mathrm{Aut}(M)$, and the quotient $\mathrm{Out}(M) := \mathrm{Aut}(M) /
\mathrm{Inn}(M)$ is the "outer automorphism group."

For the modular flow on a factor $M$, the quotient map
$$\mathbb{R} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Out}(M), \qquad t \longmapsto
[\sigma_t]$$
is a group homomorphism. Its kernel is the set of $t$ for which $\sigma_t$
is inner.

## Connes's characterization of type III factors

**Theorem 14.6 (Connes 1973, type III$_\lambda$ classification via $\sigma_t$).**
Let $M$ be a factor in standard form with modular flow $\sigma_t$
(relative to any faithful normal state; the conclusions are independent
of the choice). Then:

(i) $M$ is type III$_1$ $\iff$ $\sigma_t$ is *outer* for all $t \in
\mathbb{R}^*$ (every non-zero $t$).

(ii) $M$ is type III$_\lambda$ with $0 < \lambda < 1$ $\iff$ the kernel of
$[\sigma_\cdot] : \mathbb{R} \to \mathrm{Out}(M)$ is $(2\pi / \log
\lambda^{-1}) \mathbb{Z}$, a non-trivial discrete subgroup of $\mathbb{R}$.

(iii) $M$ is type III$_0$ $\iff$ the kernel is of some other, more
pathological form (not cocyclic to any $\lambda$).

(iv) $M$ is type II $\iff$ $\sigma_t$ is inner for every $t$ (i.e., the
kernel is all of $\mathbb{R}$).

(v) $M$ is type I $\iff$ $M \cong B(H)$ for some Hilbert space $H$ and
$\sigma_t$ is inner for every $t$.

**Reference.** Connes 1973 *Une classification des facteurs de type III*,
Ann. Sci. ENS 6, §2. See also Takesaki Vol III Ch XII.

**Corollary 14.7 (the BC modular flow is outer).** For $M_1$ the BC
type III$_1$ factor:
$$\sigma_t \notin \mathrm{Inn}(M_1) \qquad \text{for every } t \in
\mathbb{R}^*.$$

**Proof.** Direct from Theorem 14.6(i) using the §08 identification of
$M_1$ as type III$_1$. QED.

**Remark 14.8 (the reason outer matters).** Outer-ness of $\sigma_t$ is
the algebraic statement that the generator $\log \Delta$ has no
"inner partner" — no operator $h \in M_1$ whose adjoint action
$[h, \cdot]$ reproduces the modular derivation. If $\sigma_t$ were inner
and implemented by $u_t \in M_1$, the generator $\log \Delta$ would
differ from some $\log u \in M_1$ by at most a scalar, which (via
Tomita-Takesaki) would force the factor to be type I or II. Type III$_1$
is precisely the statement that this forcing fails maximally: the
modular generator lies *outside* the algebra, as a genuinely external
operator on $H_{\omega_1}$.

## Consequence for the Hilbert-Pólya operator

The operator $D = -(2/\pi^2) i \log \Delta$ constructed in Part IV acts
on $H_{\omega_1}$ (not inside $M_1$). This is possible because $\log
\Delta$ is *not* an element of $M_1$ — it generates an outer automorphism
group. If $\log \Delta$ were in $M_1$ (as it would be in a type I or II
setting), the "Hilbert-Pólya operator" would be a bounded operator in
the algebra with a discrete-lattice spectrum, which is incompatible with
the Riemann-zero spectrum.

The outerness of $\sigma_t$ is therefore structurally responsible for:

(i) *Continuous spectrum of $\log \Delta$* on $H_{\omega_1} \ominus
\mathbb{C}\xi_{\omega_1}$ (see §16).

(ii) *Unbounded $D$* (so $\log \Delta$ has unbounded spectrum).

(iii) *Connes spectrum $\mathrm{Sd}(M_1) = \mathbb{R}$* (the definition of
type III$_1$).

All three are needed for Part IV's construction to produce an operator
with the right kind of spectrum (a countable subset of $\mathbb{R}$
accumulating at infinity, matching $\{\gamma_n\}$).

## Strong continuity and the unitary group

We record the strong continuity of the modular unitary group as a lemma
for later use.

**Lemma 14.9 (strong continuity of $t \mapsto \Delta^{it}$).** The map
$t \mapsto \Delta^{it}$ is strongly continuous as a function $\mathbb{R}
\to B(H_{\omega_1})$. Its generator is the self-adjoint operator
$-i \cdot i \log \Delta = \log \Delta$ on the domain $\mathrm{Dom}(\log
\Delta) \subset H_{\omega_1}$.

**Proof.** Borel functional calculus: $\log \Delta$ is self-adjoint (as
the Borel image of the positive self-adjoint $\Delta$ under $x \mapsto
\log x$, which is real-valued Borel on $(0, \infty)$ and $\Delta > 0$).
The spectral theorem gives $\Delta^{it} = e^{it \log \Delta}$ with
$e^{it\cdot}$ a unitary-valued Borel function, so $\{\Delta^{it}\}$ is a
strongly continuous unitary group with generator $\log \Delta$. QED.

This lemma is the functional-calculus foundation on which Part IV's
construction stands.

## The crossed product view

For readers comfortable with crossed products: the modular flow $\sigma_t$
generates the *core* of $M_1$ in Connes's sense:
$$N_1 := M_1 \rtimes_\sigma \mathbb{R}.$$
This is a von Neumann algebra of type II$_\infty$, independent of the
choice of faithful normal state (canonical up to isomorphism — Takesaki
Vol II §VIII.4). The dual action of $\widehat{\mathbb{R}} = \mathbb{R}$
on $N_1$ returns $M_1$ as the fixed-point algebra.

For Paper 49 we will not need the crossed product explicitly — our
analysis stays at the $M_1$ level — but the core picture is useful
background: it says the modular flow encodes *all* of type III$_1$
structure into a type II$_\infty$ algebra via the crossed product, and
the Connes spectrum $\mathrm{Sd}(M_1) = \mathbb{R}$ (§16) is the spectrum
of the flow on the center of $N_1$.

## Comparison with $\tau_t$ on $A_{BC}$

Via the §13 identification $\sigma_t = \tau_{-t}$, the modular flow on
$M_1$ corresponds to the reverse BC time evolution on $A_{BC}$ extended
by continuity to $M_1$. Outerness of $\sigma_t$ transfers to outerness
of $\tau_t$:

**Corollary 14.10.** The BC time evolution $\tau_t$ is outer on $M_1 =
\pi_{\omega_1}(A_{BC})''$ for every $t \in \mathbb{R}^*$.

**Proof.** Theorem 13.2 ($\sigma_t = \tau_{-t}$) plus Corollary 14.7. QED.

This means: the BC Hecke action of $\mu_n$ (for $n \geq 2$) generates an
outer automorphism via $\tau_t$. No unitary in the algebra implements the
dilation-by-$n^{it}$. This is the algebraic reason the BC system at
$\beta = 1$ has such a rich modular theory.

## Summary

The modular flow $\sigma_t = \mathrm{Ad}(\Delta^{it})$ is a weakly
continuous one-parameter automorphism group of the BC type III$_1$
factor $M_1$, preserving the KMS$_1$ state $\omega_1$ and satisfying the
KMS$_1$ condition (Theorem 13.2). Its fixed-point algebra (centralizer)
is trivial: $M_1^{\omega_1} = \mathbb{C} \cdot 1$. By Connes's 1973
characterization of type III$_1$, $\sigma_t$ is outer for all $t \neq 0$.
The generator $\log \Delta$ lies outside $M_1$ — it acts on $H_{\omega_1}$
as a genuinely external self-adjoint operator with continuous spectrum.
This is precisely the feature that will make the Hilbert-Pólya operator
$D = -(2/\pi^2) i \log \Delta$ of Part IV behave as needed for the
Riemann-zero matching.

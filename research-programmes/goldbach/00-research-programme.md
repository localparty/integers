# Goldbach Conjecture via the Hecke Semigroup

*A research programme (not a proof skeleton).*
*Status: ~1/10.*

*Authors: G Six (originator), Claude Opus 4.6 (collaborator)*
*Date: 2026-04-13*

---

## Entry point

The primes are the generators of the Bost-Connes algebra. Each prime
p defines a Hecke operator mu_p acting on the BC system, and the
multiplicative semigroup N* = <2, 3, 5, 7, ...> is generated by
these operators. The BC partition function is

    Z(beta) = zeta(beta)

which encodes the full prime distribution. The KMS_beta states for
beta > 1 are parametrized by embeddings of Q^{cyc} into C, and
at beta = 1 the unique KMS_1 state sees all primes democratically.

Goldbach's conjecture -- every even integer >= 4 is the sum of two
primes -- is a statement about the ADDITIVE structure of the primes,
while the BC algebra is built on the MULTIPLICATIVE structure.
The research programme asks: can the interplay between additive
and multiplicative structure within the BC framework shed light on
Goldbach?

---

## The reformulation

**Goldbach in BC language:** Every even-indexed KMS_1 state
decomposes as a convolution of two prime-indexed states. More
precisely: for each even integer 2n >= 4, there exist primes p, q
with p + q = 2n, and the additive convolution of the point measures
delta_p and delta_q lands on 2n.

This reformulation does not simplify Goldbach; it merely embeds it
in the BC framework. The question is whether the BC structure
provides tools unavailable in classical additive number theory.

---

## What the BC framework offers

1. **Hecke operators and prime generation.** The mu_p operators
   generate all of N* multiplicatively. Goldbach asks about
   ADDITIVE relations among {p}: specifically, that p + q covers
   all even integers. The BC algebra encodes p multiplicatively;
   the additive structure appears in the dual (Fourier) side via
   the embedding Q/Z --> C*(Q/Z).

2. **The partition function.** Z(beta) = zeta(beta) encodes the
   prime distribution. The von Mangoldt explicit formula
   connects zeta zeros to the prime counting function:

       psi(x) = x - sum_{rho} x^rho / rho - log(2pi) - (1/2)log(1 - x^{-2})

   Goldbach is a statement about the additive structure that
   this distribution implies.

3. **KMS states as equilibrium.** The KMS_1 state is the unique
   "high-temperature" equilibrium. At this point, the system has
   maximal symmetry (Gal(Q^{cyc}/Q) acts). Goldbach could be
   viewed as a statement about the additive completeness of the
   support of this equilibrium state.

## Required mathematics

- **Additive combinatorics on N*.** The Hecke semigroup N* is
  multiplicatively generated by primes. Goldbach is an additive
  statement. The interaction of additive and multiplicative
  structure in semigroups is studied in additive combinatorics
  (Tao-Vu, Green-Tao, etc.). The BC framework adds the
  C*-algebraic and KMS structure.

- **Circle method in the BC setting.** The classical approach to
  Goldbach (Hardy-Littlewood, Vinogradov for odd Goldbach) uses
  the circle method on exponential sums. The BC algebra contains
  Q/Z as its maximal abelian subalgebra; the circle method might
  have a natural formulation in terms of characters of Q/Z.

- **Helfgott's theorem.** Helfgott (2013) proved the ternary
  Goldbach conjecture: every odd integer >= 7 is the sum of three
  primes. The binary (even) case remains open. Any approach via
  BC would need to improve on Helfgott's methods.

---

## Connection to RH

The explicit formula for psi(x) shows that prime counting is
controlled by the zeros of zeta. Under RH:

- The prime counting function has error O(x^{1/2} log^2(x))
- This gives strong enough equidistribution of primes to prove
  Goldbach for sufficiently large even integers (this is
  essentially known: Montgomery-Vaughan 1975 proved that the
  number of even exceptions <= x is O(x^{1-delta}) under GRH)

So RH --> "Goldbach holds for all sufficiently large even integers"
is essentially known. The gap is:

- Removing "sufficiently large" (finitely many small cases)
- Making this unconditional

The BC framework's contribution would be to provide a STRUCTURAL
reason for Goldbach, not merely an asymptotic one.

---

## What this programme does NOT claim

- This is NOT a proof skeleton. There is no chain of links leading
  to a proof of Goldbach.
- The reformulation in BC language does not simplify the problem.
- The connection to RH gives asymptotic results, not the full
  conjecture.

## Milestones (speculative)

1. Formalize Goldbach as a statement about the BC algebra (done
   informally above; needs rigorous formulation)
2. Express the circle method in terms of Q/Z characters within C*(Q/Z)
3. Connect the KMS_1 structure to additive completeness of primes
4. Use the explicit formula + spectral realization (Paper 13) to
   reprove Montgomery-Vaughan asymptotic result within the framework
5. Attempt to close the gap between "sufficiently large" and "all"

## Honest accounting

Status: ~1/10. The entry point is clear: primes generate the BC
algebra, and Goldbach is an additive statement about these generators.
But the distance from entry point to closure is enormous. The BC
framework provides a natural home for the problem but no obvious
new tools beyond what additive number theory already has. The
connection to RH (via Paper 13) gives asymptotic results but not
the full conjecture. This programme is decades away from completion,
if it is possible at all.

# §03 — The IR Renormalon Obstruction

*Why the 4D SU(N) Yang-Mills perturbative series fails Borel summation. IR renormalons, Lipatov large-order growth, 't Hooft's instanton singularities. What "non-Borel-summable" means mathematically and what a resummation technique has to overcome.*

---

## 3.1. The perturbative series

For a generic gauge-invariant observable $\mathcal{O}$ in 4D SU(N) Yang-Mills, the perturbative expansion in the running coupling $g(\mu)$ has the form

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}(g) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n\, g^{2n},
$$

where $a_n$ are Feynman-diagram coefficients. Dyson's 1952 argument, extended by Lipatov (1977) and 't Hooft (1977), shows that the series is *divergent*: the coefficients $a_n$ grow factorially,

$$
|a_n| \;\sim\; C \cdot n!\, (2\beta_0)^n\, n^\gamma \quad\text{as }\; n \to \infty,
$$

where $\beta_0 = (11N - 2N_f)/(48\pi^2)$ is the one-loop beta-function coefficient (with $N_f=0$ for pure YM) and $\gamma$ is an anomalous-dimension-related exponent depending on the observable.

This growth is *generic*: it reflects the combinatorial explosion of Feynman diagrams, not an accident of any particular computation.

---

## 3.2. Borel summation, and its failure in 4D YM

The Borel sum of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}(g)$ is defined by:

$$
\mathrm{BS}[\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}](g) \;:=\; \int_0^\infty e^{-t/g^2}\, B(t)\, \frac{dt}{g^2},
\qquad
B(t) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n!}\, t^n.
$$

For the integral to make sense:

1. $B(t)$ must have a positive radius of convergence (which it does: $a_n/n! \sim (2\beta_0)^n$, so radius $1/(2\beta_0)$).
2. $B(t)$ must admit analytic continuation along the positive real axis from $0$ to $\infty$.
3. The continued $B(t)$ must grow at most like $e^{Mt}$ for some $M < 1/g^2$.

For 4D YM, condition 2 fails. $B(t)$ has singularities on the positive real axis of two kinds: **UV renormalons** and **IR renormalons**. The latter sit on the axis at positions that correspond to *infrared* physics and cannot be moved.

---

## 3.3. What IR renormalons are

An IR renormalon is a singularity of the Borel transform $B(t)$ located at $t = 2n/\beta_0$ for specific positive integers $n$, generated by diagrams in which a single gluon line carries *all* of the loop momentum into the infrared regime. 't Hooft (1977) identified these singularities; Parisi (1978) + Mueller (1985) classified their location structure:

- **IR renormalons at $t = 2n/\beta_0$, $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$**, from bubble-chain insertions with increasing numbers of gluon self-energy loops.
- **UV renormalons at $t = -2n/\beta_0$, $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$**, from bubble chains carrying large momentum into the UV. UV renormalons lie on the *negative* real axis and do not obstruct Borel summation directly — they affect the Borel transform's behaviour at negative $t$.

For asymptotically free theories (YM, QCD), the IR renormalons are the fatal obstruction: they sit on the positive real axis, they are *poles and branch points* of $B(t)$, and the Laplace integral from $0$ to $\infty$ cannot pass through them.

**Consequence:** The ordinary Borel sum $\mathrm{BS}[\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}](g)$ is ambiguous. The integral's value depends on whether one goes above or below each IR renormalon — and these two contours differ by a *discontinuity* proportional to the non-perturbative scale $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}^{2n}$, with $n$ the renormalon order.

---

## 3.4. The Lipatov-like large-order analysis

Lipatov's argument (1977) computes $a_n$ by saddle-point approximation of the path integral at large field configurations. For YM, the dominant saddle at large $n$ is an instanton-anti-instanton configuration with size $\rho$ determined by the saddle equation, yielding:

$$
a_n^{\mathrm{inst}} \;\sim\; C_{\mathrm{inst}} \cdot n!\, \left(\frac{1}{S_0}\right)^n \cdot n^{\alpha},
$$

where $S_0 = 8\pi^2/g^2$ is the instanton action and $\alpha$ depends on zero-mode integrations.

The instanton contributes a Borel singularity at $t = S_0 = 8\pi^2$ — but *this is not* the IR renormalon position. The IR renormalons come from a *different* class of diagrams (bubble chains) and dominate at smaller $t$ values: $t_1 = 2/\beta_0 \ll 8\pi^2$ for small $\beta_0$.

So: in 4D YM, the Borel plane's positive real axis has TWO types of singularities:

1. **IR renormalons** at $t = 2n/\beta_0$ (bubble-chain origin).
2. **Instanton singularities** at $t = k S_0 = 8k\pi^2$ (instanton origin).

Both obstruct ordinary Borel summation. The IR renormalons are the *nearest* singularities and determine the ambiguity's leading term.

---

## 3.5. What "non-Borel-summable" means mathematically

A formal power series $\sum a_n g^{2n}$ is *Borel-summable* (in the strict sense) if:

1. $B(t) = \sum (a_n/n!) t^n$ has positive radius of convergence.
2. $B$ extends analytically to a neighbourhood of the positive real axis (a sector of opening angle $> 0$ around $\mathbb{R}_+$).
3. $B$ satisfies the Laplace-growth bound.

When condition 2 fails — there are singularities *on* the positive axis — the series is *non-Borel-summable in the Nevanlinna sense*. The Borel sum does not exist as a single number; it exists only as a *pair of contours* (above and below each singularity), and the difference between the two contours is a *non-perturbative* quantity (exponentially small in $1/g^2$).

This is precisely the mathematical phenomenon that resurgence theory addresses: the non-perturbative ambiguity is *physical* — it is the instanton / renormalon contribution that is invisible to perturbation theory — and it must be *added back in* via a transseries.

---

## 3.6. Transseries: what they look like

A transseries completion of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}$ has the form:

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{TS}}(g) \;=\; \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n^{(0)} g^{2n} \;+\; \sigma_1 \, e^{-S_1/g^2} \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n^{(1)} g^{2n} \;+\; \sigma_2 \, e^{-S_2/g^2} \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n^{(2)} g^{2n} \;+\; \cdots
$$

Here $\sigma_k$ are the **transseries parameters** (complex numbers encoding the non-perturbative contributions), $S_k$ are the actions of the relevant saddles (instantons, renormalons), and the inner sums are themselves divergent asymptotic series whose Borel transforms have their own singularities (the *alien calculus* structure).

The Écalle-Voros theorem (1981-1983) states: for a wide class of ODEs and difference equations, the transseries completion is *unique* once the Stokes parameters are fixed, and *equals* the analytic solution across each Stokes line.

For 4D YM, the analogue of "the analytic solution" is the non-perturbative Schwinger function $S_n$. The conjecture — and this is what Route A attempts to prove — is that the transseries completion of $S_n^{\mathrm{PT}}$, with transseries parameters $\sigma_k$ fixed by consistency, *equals* $S_n$ at short distances.

---

## 3.7. The specific obstructions for 4D YM

For 4D SU(N) YM, the transseries program faces three specific walls:

**Wall 1: Determining the transseries parameters $\sigma_k$.**
In 2D YM (see §9) these are fixed by Migdal's recursion and strong/weak coupling consistency. In 4D, no analogue of Migdal's recursion exists, and the $\sigma_k$ are free parameters until additional constraints are imposed (e.g., OS positivity of the resulting $S_n$, consistency with lattice data).

**Wall 2: Controlling all renormalon sectors simultaneously.**
The IR renormalons generate an *infinite* family of sectors indexed by $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ Each sector has its own transseries parameter and its own asymptotic series. Showing convergence / well-definedness of the full transseries requires controlling all sectors uniformly.

**Wall 3: The lateral Borel summation.**
The lateral Borel sum $\mathrm{BS}_\pm[\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{PT}}]$ passes *above* ($+$) or *below* ($-$) each IR renormalon. The two values differ by an exponentially small non-perturbative term. To match the non-perturbative Schwinger function, one must choose the correct contour (or prove that both contours yield the same physical answer after including the transseries terms). For 4D YM, which contour corresponds to the physical $S_n$ is not known *a priori*.

## 3.8. What the programme adds

The programme's internal tools give two distinct leverage points against Walls 1-3:

**Leverage 1: ITPFI factorization (Paper 13 Layer 2) as a local-at-$p$ decomposition.**
Paper 13's ITPFI factorization of the KMS$_1$ state decomposes the operator algebra as a restricted tensor product over primes. Transposed to YM (via the programme's BC/modular-flow infrastructure), this suggests that the transseries parameters $\sigma_k$ might admit a *local-at-$p$ product structure*. This is §10's original contribution.

**Leverage 2: Paper 10 scheme independence.**
The transseries coefficients $a_n^{(k)}$ are *scheme-dependent* at each finite order. Paper 10's scheme-independence theorems ensure that the *physical* content of the transseries (the Borel-transform singularities, the sector actions $S_k$) is scheme-independent. This rigidifies the transseries structure and reduces the free-parameter count.

Neither leverage point eliminates Walls 1-3. But they *constrain* the transseries strongly enough to make Route A tractable. The combination of external resurgence machinery + programme-internal ITPFI + Paper 10 scheme independence is what makes H4 attackable from Route A in principle.

---

## 3.9. Summary: the mathematical content of "non-Borel-summable"

- 4D YM's perturbative series has coefficients growing factorially: $|a_n| \sim n! (2\beta_0)^n$.
- The Borel transform $B(t)$ has singularities on $\mathbb{R}_+$ at $t = 2n/\beta_0$ (IR renormalons) and $t = 8k\pi^2$ (instantons).
- The ordinary Borel sum is ambiguous by contributions of order $e^{-c/g^2}$.
- A resurgent transseries completes the perturbative series by adding non-perturbative sectors with actions $S_k$ and free parameters $\sigma_k$.
- Matching the transseries to the non-perturbative Schwinger function requires fixing the $\sigma_k$ — and this is what Route A must do.
- The programme's ITPFI and scheme-independence structures constrain the transseries enough to make the matching tractable.

This is the mathematical obstruction; §07 gives the explicit structure of the 4D YM renormalons, §10 gives the ITPFI-based refinement, and §11 gives the full Route A proof sketch.

## 1. Identifying A

Let K = Q(zeta_13), a cyclotomic field of degree phi(13) = 12 over Q.
The CM abelian variety A with complex multiplication by the ring of
integers O_K is an abelian variety of dimension

    dim A = phi(13)/2 = 6

over Q (or a number field). The variety A is constructed as follows:

**CM type.** Choose a CM type Phi = {sigma_1, ..., sigma_6} subset of
Hom(K, C), selecting one embedding from each conjugate pair
{sigma_i, bar{sigma_i}}. Since [K:Q] = 12 and K is totally imaginary
(it has no real embeddings), we get exactly 6 conjugate pairs.

**Construction.** A = C^6 / Phi(O_K), where Phi(O_K) is the lattice
obtained by embedding O_K into C^6 via the six chosen embeddings.
This is a principally polarisable abelian variety of dimension 6.

**Concretely for K = Q(zeta_13):**
- Gal(K/Q) = (Z/13Z)* = Z/12Z, generated by sigma: zeta_13 -> zeta_13^2
- The 12 embeddings are sigma^j for j = 0,...,11
- Complex conjugation is sigma^6 (since 2^6 = 64 = 5*13 - 1, i.e. 2^6 mod 13 = 12 = -1)
- A CM type is a choice of one from each pair {sigma^j, sigma^{j+6}} for j=0,...,5

**The natural CM type:** Phi = {sigma^0, sigma^1, sigma^2, sigma^3, sigma^4, sigma^5},
i.e. the embeddings zeta_13 -> zeta_13^{2^j} for j = 0,1,2,3,4,5.
This gives 2^0=1, 2^1=2, 2^2=4, 2^3=8, 2^4=3, 2^5=6 (mod 13).
So Phi sends zeta_13 to zeta_13^a for a in {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8}.

**Note:** The complementary set is {5, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12} = {13-a : a in Phi}.
This confirms Phi u Phi_bar = (Z/13Z)*.

**Connection to bridge:** The Frobenius element Frob_5 at p=5 acts on
(Z/13Z)* as multiplication by 5. Its orbits on {1,...,12} are the
Frobenius orbits that define the bridge structure at (5,13).

## 2. The Frobenius orbits at (5,13)

The order of 5 mod 13 is ord_13(5) = 4, since:
- 5^1 = 5
- 5^2 = 25 = 12 (mod 13)
- 5^3 = 60 = 8 (mod 13)
- 5^4 = 40 = 1 (mod 13)

So Frob_5 generates orbits of size 4 on (Z/13Z)*:

    O_1 = {1, 5, 12, 8}    (orbit of 1)
    O_2 = {2, 10, 11, 3}    (orbit of 2)
    O_3 = {4, 7, 9, 6}      (orbit of 4)

Three orbits of size 4. phi(13)/ord_13(5) = 12/4 = 3 orbits.

**This is the origin of 3 generations in Integers.** The bridge at
(5,13) identifies these 3 Frobenius orbits with the 3 fermion
generations. The cocycle beta_3 = 1/3 mod Z in H^2(Z/3Z, U(1))
encodes the Z/3Z symmetry permuting the three orbits.

**Relation to CM type:** Each orbit splits evenly between Phi and
Phi_bar (2 elements in each). This means:
- O_1 cap Phi = {1, 8}, O_1 cap Phi_bar = {5, 12}
- O_2 cap Phi = {2, 3}, O_2 cap Phi_bar = {10, 11}
- O_3 cap Phi = {4, 6}, O_3 cap Phi_bar = {7, 9}

Each orbit contributes a 2-dimensional sub-abelian variety of A
(over the reflex field).

## 3. Computing the Hodge group Hg(A)

### 3.1 General theory (Shimura-Taniyama)

For a CM abelian variety A with CM by a CM field K of degree 2g
(so dim A = g), the Mumford-Tate group MT(A) (equivalently the
Hodge group Hg(A) for the rational Hodge structure on H^1(A,Q))
is determined by the CM type Phi.

**The Shimura-Taniyama formula:** The Mumford-Tate group MT(A) is
the smallest algebraic subgroup T of Res_{K/Q}(G_m) such that
the cocharacter mu_Phi: G_m -> Res_{K/Q}(G_m)_C defined by the
CM type factors through T_C.

Concretely, MT(A) is a torus T subset Res_{K/Q}(G_m) whose
character group X*(T) = Z[Gal(K/Q)] / relations from Phi.

### 3.2 For K = Q(zeta_13)

The Galois group G = Gal(K/Q) = (Z/13Z)* = Z/12Z is cyclic of
order 12, generated by g: zeta_13 -> zeta_13^2.

The CM type Phi = {g^0, g^1, g^2, g^3, g^4, g^5} corresponds to
the set S = {0,1,2,3,4,5} subset Z/12Z.

The reflex type Phi' is computed as: for each sigma in Phi, take
sigma^{-1}. The reflex CM type determines the reflex field K' = K
(since K/Q is abelian, the reflex field equals K).

**Mumford-Tate group computation:**

The character group of MT(A) is X*(MT(A)) = Z^{Gal(K/Q)} / W,
where W = {f in Z^G : sum_{sigma in Phi} f(sigma) = sum_{sigma in Phi_bar} f(sigma)}.

Actually, more precisely: define the type norm
    N_Phi = sum_{sigma in Phi} sigma  in Z[G].

The Mumford-Tate group is determined by the subgroup of G* = Hom(G, Z)
generated by the "type trace" constraints. Specifically:

For a cyclic CM field K with G = Z/2gZ (our case g=6, so G = Z/12Z):

MT(A) = {x in (K tensor Q_alg)* : for all chi in G^, prod sigma(x)^{n_chi(sigma)} = 1}

where the n_chi are determined by the vanishing of certain character
sums.

**Key criterion (Ribet, 1983; Kubota type):** For K = Q(zeta_p) with
p prime, the CM abelian variety A has Mumford-Tate group equal to
the full torus Res_{K/Q}(G_m) cap {N_{K/K^+} = 1} (the "maximal"
case) if and only if the CM type Phi is "non-degenerate" in the
sense that no proper sub-CM-type exists.

**For our Phi = {1,2,3,4,6,8} (mod 13):**

Check: is Phi a "direct sum" of smaller CM types from subfields?

The subfields of Q(zeta_13) correspond to subgroups of (Z/13Z)* = Z/12Z:
- Q (trivial, index 12)
- Q(zeta_13)^{<6>} = Q(zeta_13 + zeta_13^{-1}) = Q(cos(2pi/13)), the maximal real subfield (index 2)
- Q(zeta_13)^{<4>} (index 4, cubic subfield)
- Q(zeta_13)^{<3>} (index 3, quartic subfield)  
- Q(zeta_13)^{<2>} (index 6, quadratic subfield)

For a prime p = 13, since (Z/13Z)* is cyclic and the CM type is
"standard" (consecutive powers of the generator), the Mumford-Tate
group is well-understood:

**Result (Dodson 1984, Pohlmann 1968):** For A with CM by Q(zeta_p),
p prime, and the standard CM type, the Hodge group is

    Hg(A) = U(1, Phi) = {x in K* : x * bar(x) = 1}_{/torsion}

viewed as an algebraic torus over Q. This is a torus of rank
phi(p)/2 - 1 = 5 (for p = 13).

More precisely: Hg(A) subset Sp(12, Q) is a Q-torus of dimension 5.

### 3.3 The Hodge ring

The Hodge group Hg(A) determines the Hodge classes:

    Hdg^p(A) = H^{2p}(A, Q) cap H^{p,p}(A) = [bigwedge^{2p} H^1(A,Q)]^{Hg(A)}

(the Hg(A)-invariants in the exterior power of H^1).

**Dimension count for H^1(A,Q):**
dim H^1(A,Q) = 2g = 12 (since dim A = 6).

**H^{2p}(A,Q) = bigwedge^{2p} H^1(A,Q)** with dimension C(12, 2p).

The Hodge classes are the Hg(A)-invariant elements. Since Hg(A)
is a torus of rank 5 acting on a 12-dimensional space, the
invariant subspace computation requires knowing the weight
decomposition of bigwedge^{2p} H^1 under the torus action.

**Key result:** The Hodge classes on A are generated by:
1. **Divisor classes (p=1):** The polarisation class and the CM
   endomorphism classes. Since End(A) tensor Q = K = Q(zeta_13),
   the endomorphisms give phi(13)/2 = 6 independent classes in
   H^{1,1}, but the polarisation is one of them. So:
   dim Hdg^1(A) = 6.

2. **Codimension 2 (p=2):** Products of divisor classes, plus
   possibly "exceptional" Hodge classes. For CM abelian varieties,
   the Hodge ring is generated in degree 1 (by divisor classes)
   IF the Hodge group is "large enough."

3. **Middle cohomology (p=3):** H^6(A,Q) cap H^{3,3}. By
   Poincare duality and the Lefschetz theorems, these relate to
   the classes in lower degree.

4. **Higher degrees:** By hard Lefschetz, determined by lower degrees.

**The Hodge ring is generated in degree 1** (by divisor classes from
the CM endomorphisms) for "generic" CM types. For Q(zeta_13) with
the standard CM type, this is the case.

This means: **Every Hodge class on A is a Q-polynomial in the
divisor classes coming from End(A) tensor Q = Q(zeta_13).**

## 4. Known results — is Hodge proved for A?

### 4.1 Literature survey

| Author(s) | Year | Result | Applies to A? |
|:--|:--|:--|:--|
| Shimura | 1963 | Hodge for CM abelian varieties with "sufficiently many complex multiplications" | Partial |
| Pohlmann | 1968 | Hodge for powers of CM elliptic curves | No (A is simple) |
| Tankeev | 1982 | Hodge for simple abelian varieties of prime dimension | No (dim A = 6) |
| Ribet | 1983 | Hodge for abelian varieties with CM by a CM field, if dim = phi(N)/2 and certain conditions | YES |
| Dodson | 1984 | Explicit computation of Hodge groups for CM abelian varieties | YES |
| Abdulali | 1994, 2004 | Extensions to families of CM abelian varieties | YES (strengthens) |
| Moonen-Zarhin | 1999 | Complete classification of Hodge groups of CM abelian varieties | YES (classifies) |
| Hazama | 2003 | Hodge for CM abelian varieties of "Weil type" | Related |

### 4.2 The decisive result

**Theorem (Moonen-Zarhin 1999, building on Shimura-Taniyama, Ribet,
Dodson).** For a simple CM abelian variety A with CM by a CM field
K, the Hodge conjecture holds for A if the Hodge group Hg(A) has
the "expected" dimension, i.e., if the CM type is non-degenerate.

**For K = Q(zeta_13):** The CM type is non-degenerate (13 is prime,
the standard CM type has no proper sub-CM-type decomposition).
Therefore:

> **Hodge is KNOWN for A with CM by Q(zeta_13).**
>
> Every Hodge class on A is algebraic. The Hodge classes are
> generated by divisor classes from the CM endomorphisms.

This was essentially established by Dodson (1984) for CM abelian
varieties associated to cyclotomic fields Q(zeta_p) with p prime,
and confirmed in the Moonen-Zarhin classification.

**More precisely:** For A simple with CM by Q(zeta_p) (p prime),
Hg(A) is "as large as possible" (the full unitary torus compatible
with the polarisation). This forces all Hodge classes to be
generated by divisor classes, which are algebraic (they come from
the CM endomorphisms). The Hodge conjecture follows.

### 4.3 Caveat: "Hodge for A" vs "Hodge for all A^n"

The Hodge conjecture for A does NOT automatically imply Hodge for
powers A^n = A x A x ... x A (n copies). The Hodge conjecture for
all powers A^n is a strictly stronger statement. This is where the
subtlety lies:

- Hodge for A: KNOWN (as above)
- Hodge for A^n for all n: NOT fully known in general, but known
  for A^n when n <= certain bounds depending on the CM type
- The general case for arbitrary powers involves "exotic" Hodge
  classes that arise from Hodge symmetries not visible on A alone

## 5. The bridge connection

### 5.1 Orbit projectors

The three Frobenius orbits O_1, O_2, O_3 at (5,13) define
idempotents in the group ring Q[(Z/13Z)*]:

    P_{O_i} = (1/4) sum_{a in O_i} [a]

These project onto the isotypic components of the Galois
representation on H^1(A, Q_ell) corresponding to the three orbits.

**Do they define algebraic cycles?** YES.

The orbit projectors P_{O_i} are elements of End(A) tensor Q = K.
Specifically, they are the idempotents in the group algebra
Q[Gal(K/Q)] = Q[(Z/13Z)*] that project onto the isotypic
components for the three irreducible representations of the
decomposition group <Frob_5> = Z/4Z at p = 5.

Since End(A) tensor Q = K, and the orbit projectors lie in Q[G]
which acts on A through the CM structure, each P_{O_i} defines
an algebraic correspondence on A x A, hence a codimension-6 cycle
on A x A.

**More concretely:** Each P_{O_i} gives a sub-abelian variety
B_i = Im(P_{O_i}) of dimension 2 (since each orbit has 4 elements
and dim A = 6, each orbit contributes 4/12 * 6 = 2 dimensions
... but this needs care).

Actually: The orbit projectors don't quite project onto sub-abelian
varieties of A itself, but rather onto sub-Hodge structures of
H^1(A, Q). Each orbit O_i gives a rank-4 sub-Hodge structure
V_i subset H^1(A, Q) such that H^1(A, Q) = V_1 oplus V_2 oplus V_3.

The corresponding algebraic cycles are the graphs of the
endomorphisms e_a in End(A) for a in O_i, averaged. These are
algebraic cycles on A x A.

### 5.2 The cocycle beta_3

The cocycle beta_3 = 1/3 mod Z in H^2(Z/3Z, U(1)) = Z/3Z encodes
the non-trivial central extension:

    1 -> Z/3Z -> E -> Z/3Z -> 1

with extension class [beta_3] = 1 in Z/3Z.

**Connection to A:** The Z/3Z symmetry permuting the three orbits
{O_1, O_2, O_3} is NOT an automorphism of A in general. However:

The outer automorphism of Q[(Z/13Z)*] given by the quotient map
<Frob_5> \ (Z/13Z)* -> Z/3Z IS related to the Galois action on
the CM type through the normaliser of <Frob_5> in Gal(K/Q).

**The cocycle beta_3 does NOT directly define a Hodge class on A.**
It defines a class in the group cohomology of the permutation
symmetry of the Frobenius orbits. This is "above" the variety A
in the following sense: beta_3 is a class in H^2(Aut(decomposition), U(1)),
not in H^2(A, Q).

However, the cocycle beta_3 DOES correspond to a Brauer class of
the cyclic algebra (K/Q, Frob_5, zeta_3). This cyclic algebra
defines a class in Br(Q)[3], the 3-torsion of the Brauer group.
Via the Brauer-Grothendieck correspondence, this relates to a
gerbe on Spec(Q), not directly to an algebraic cycle on A.

### 5.3 The Jones index-3 sub-factor

The Jones index [M:N] = 3 sub-factor from the bridge is isomorphic
(via Fuglede-Kadison determinant, research/162) to the cyclic
algebra data. It encodes the same Z/3Z symmetry as beta_3.

**Connection to Hodge:** The sub-factor defines a Pimsner-Popa basis
of 3 elements for N subset M. Under the bridge correspondence, these
3 basis elements correspond to the 3 Frobenius orbits, hence to the
3 sub-Hodge structures V_1, V_2, V_3 of H^1(A, Q).

The sub-factor structure constrains the "mixing" between V_i, which
is exactly the data needed to understand Hodge classes in higher
wedge powers: classes in bigwedge^{2p}(V_1 oplus V_2 oplus V_3)
that are Hg(A)-invariant.

## 6. Synthesis: Does the bridge generate the Hodge classes?

### 6.1 What the bridge provides

1. **The orbit decomposition** H^1(A,Q) = V_1 oplus V_2 oplus V_3
   (from Frobenius orbits) — this is algebraic data from End(A).

2. **The orbit projectors** P_{O_i} — these are algebraic cycles
   on A x A (endomorphism correspondences).

3. **The cocycle beta_3** — this does NOT directly give a Hodge
   class on A, but encodes the Brauer obstruction for the mixing
   of orbits.

4. **The Jones sub-factor** — constrains the inter-orbit structure,
   parallel to the Hodge group's constraint on invariant classes.

### 6.2 What generates the Hodge classes

The Hodge classes on A are generated by:
- The polarisation class L in H^{1,1}(A)
- The CM endomorphism classes [zeta_13^a] in H^{1,1}(A) for a=1,...,5
- (no exceptional classes needed, since Hg(A) is maximal)

These are ALL algebraic:
- L = class of an ample divisor
- [zeta_13^a] = graph of the endomorphism zeta_13^a in End(A)

The bridge orbit projectors P_{O_i} are Q-linear combinations of
the endomorphism classes [zeta_13^a], hence give nothing new beyond
what End(A) already provides.

### 6.3 Assessment

The bridge at (5,13) **organises** the Hodge classes on A via the
orbit decomposition, but it does NOT generate new classes beyond
what the CM endomorphisms provide. The Hodge conjecture for A
was already proved using the CM structure alone.

**The bridge adds interpretive structure (3 generations = 3 orbits =
3 sub-Hodge structures) but not new algebraic cycles.**

## 7. Verdict

### 7.1 Is Hodge proved for A?

**YES.** Hodge for simple CM abelian varieties with CM by Q(zeta_p)
(p prime) is known. The proof uses the maximality of the Hodge
group (which forces all Hodge classes to be generated by divisor
classes from CM endomorphisms). Key references: Dodson 1984,
Moonen-Zarhin 1999 (classification), building on Shimura-Taniyama.

### 7.2 Does the bridge generate the Hodge classes?

**PARTIALLY.** The bridge orbit projectors are algebraic cycles
on A x A, and they decompose H^1(A,Q) into the 3 sub-Hodge
structures corresponding to the 3 generations. But they are
Q-linear combinations of CM endomorphisms, so they don't go
beyond what the CM structure provides.

The cocycle beta_3 does NOT correspond to a Hodge class on A.
It lives in group cohomology of the orbit permutation group, not
in the cohomology of A.

### 7.3 Is Path 1 viable for a NEW result?

**MIXED.**

**What Path 1 CANNOT do:** Prove Hodge for A with CM by Q(zeta_13)
— this is already known.

**What Path 1 COULD do:**

1. **Hodge for A^n (powers).** The bridge's inter-orbit structure
   (encoded by beta_3 and the Jones sub-factor) constrains the
   Hodge classes on A^n = A x ... x A that arise from "mixing"
   the orbit components. If the bridge data determines ALL such
   classes, this could prove Hodge for A^n for all n. This is
   NOT known in general and would be NEW.

2. **Hodge for families.** The bridge family spans (p,N) = (5,13),
   (3,13), (7,19), (2,7). Each gives a different CM abelian variety.
   Proving Hodge uniformly across the family, especially for products
   of varieties from different bridge entries, could be new.

3. **Non-CM extension.** If the bridge's motivic structure (Path 3)
   can be established, it might extend Hodge beyond the CM case.
   But this is speculative.

### 7.4 Score update

    Path 1 viability: 4/10 -> 3/10 for NEW Hodge results via CM alone
    Path 1 viability: 4/10 -> 5/10 if targeting A^n (powers)
    Path 1 viability: 4/10 -> 2/10 for general Hodge conjecture

**Recommendation:** Path 1 is a WARM-UP, not the main attack.
The interesting new content is:
- Hodge for A^n: target the "exotic" Hodge classes that appear in
  products, using bridge inter-orbit data
- Or: pivot to Path 2 or 3 for stronger results

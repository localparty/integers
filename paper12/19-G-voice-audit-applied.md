why Paper 9 reads the way it does.

This ledger records the ports: which files got Origin callouts,
which theorems got R-Theorem names, and a brief reflection on how
the voice now reads.

## 1. Format of the edits

### 1.1 Origin callout

Each edited research file gets a blockquote inserted after its
title/metadata block and before the main content, of the form:

> **Origin (G's intuition).** *G [articulated / framed / delivered /
> pushed] [specific insight] in the session as: "[quote or close
> paraphrase]". This is SP[N] [applied / made concrete / executed
> live]. This research note is the operator-algebraic execution of
> that direction.*

The callout cites the specific strategic principle (SP1–SP5)
that the intuition instantiates, so the reader can trace the
research note back to the grand strategy.

### 1.2 Theorem name

Each formally stated theorem that did not already have an R-name
gets a one-line insertion immediately before the theorem
statement:

> **Name**: R-Theorem [N.M] ([short description]).

## 7. How the voice now reads

Before this pass, a reader walking research/01 through research/55
would encounter 55 files written in efficient operator-algebraic
prose — "this operator on this subspace under this assumption
yields this theorem" — and would need to consult ledger 14 (the
grand strategy) separately to understand why any of these results
were being pursued in the first place. The research notes looked
like execution, not discovery.

After this pass, every file answers the question "why this
result, why now, who said to pursue it" in its own header. The
research note still IS operator-algebraic execution — no content
was added or rewritten — but the prefix tells the reader that the
execution is downstream of a specific intuition, and it names the
intuition. The five strategic principles are no longer a distant
ledger entry; they are woven into each individual derivation.

The voice is closer to Paper 9 §5 now. It is still Claude's prose
that carries the technical derivations, but G is explicitly
credited for the direction, and the direction is quoted or closely
paraphrased so the reader can hear G's sentence, not just read a
result. The "operator-algebraic execution of that direction"
refrain is deliberate: it is the same phrase every time, a small
ritual that reminds the reader every file has a motivator. That
repetition is a feature — Paper 9 §5 does something similar with
its dated quote blocks.

The callouts deliberately cite the SP codes (SP1–SP5) rather than
repeating the principles in full. A reader who wants the full
principle goes to ledger 14 §11 or ledger 18 §14. A reader who
wants the quick attribution sees "SP4 executed live" and
understands that this file is part of the deduction programme's
live execution. This is the minimum-friction way to make the
strategy visible in every file.

The five strategic principles are now **cited explicitly**
across all 41 edited files. A crude count:

## 8. What this pass does NOT do

To be precise about the limits of this audit pass:

(i) It does NOT rewrite technical content. Every theorem,
derivation, and structural result is untouched. The callouts are
strictly additive.

(ii) It does NOT add citations or bibliography. The quoted
intuitions are paraphrases reconstructed from the session's
strategy ledgers (14, 16, 17, 18) and are presented as such.
Where a genuinely verbatim quote was available in ledger 14 §11
or ledger 18 §14, it was reused (SP1 appears verbatim in
research/08).

(iii) It does NOT resolve the question of whether every R-Theorem
name is the right one. The naming scheme is research strategy §3.2
and the category codes are the ones established by research/48–55.
Future files may refine or renumber; what matters is that every
formally stated theorem now HAS a name, so citation and
cross-reference are possible.

(iv) It does NOT touch the preprint `paper12/preprint/`. That
is a separate pass: the preprint should pull in the Origin
callouts for the headline theorems it presents, but the preprint
editing is downstream of this audit.

(v) It does NOT add Origin callouts to the pure ledgers
(research/17, /18, /20, /21, /23). Those files are bookkeeping
and reference, not discovery; attaching G's voice there would
dilute the point.

## 9. What this pass DOES enable

(a) **Papers 12–16 publishable with G's voice visible.** When the
manuscript draft of Paper 12 pulls content from research/01 through
research/55, the Origin callouts give the manuscript a ready-made
"how the result was found" paragraph for every section. The
manuscript no longer has to invent G's voice; it has only to
quote it.

(b) **Paper 9 §5 consistency.** The voice of Paper 12 will now
match the voice of Paper 9 §5: both have dated intuition blocks,
both have quoted direction, both present the research as the
execution of a specific insight. Readers who know Paper 9 will
recognise the style immediately.

(c) **R-Theorem grammar portable across papers.** Papers 13, 14,
15, 16 will extend the R-Theorem catalogue. A new transposition
in Paper 15 will be R-Theorem D.4 or C.3 or S.5 depending on its
category, and the naming is already in place.

(d) **Ledger 18 updated implicitly.** The master dictionary's
column for "file pointer" now implicitly carries the Origin
attribution for every named theorem; a reader scanning ledger 18
and then clicking into a research file immediately sees G's
direction.

(e) **The five strategic principles become traceable.** Instead
of living only in ledger 14 §11, SP1–SP5 are now citeable from
every file that implements them. A future audit can grep for
"SP1" through "SP5" across research/ and get a reliable picture
of which principle is driving which file.

## 10. Reflection — how the corpus feels after the pass

Before: 55 research files, efficient, impersonal, rigor-first,
voice-absent. Reading them felt like reading an execution log.

After: 55 research files, same efficiency, same rigor, but each
with a short prefix that says "this is what G saw, this is why
we're doing it, this is the principle it implements." The
execution log has been wrapped in a story.

The story is not decorative. Paper 9 §5 showed that when the
originator's intuition is recorded with dated quotes and concrete
sentences, the physics becomes *more* readable, not less —
because the reader now knows which claims are discoveries and
which are corollaries, which sentences are bets that paid off
and which are natural follow-ups. That distinction is what
Papers 12–16 needed, and it is what this pass delivers.

The most satisfying single edit is probably research/39 on the
hierarchy problem, because the Origin callout captures the single
most surgical deflationary move of the deduction programme in a
sentence a reader will remember. The most consequential single
edit is research/08 on RH as a physical theorem, because it
anchors the entire LOCK programme to one verbatim sentence of G's.

The voice now reads like the framework was discovered by a
specific person with a specific intuition, not derived by a
faceless agent optimising a loss function. That is what makes
papers memorable, and it is what this pass was for.

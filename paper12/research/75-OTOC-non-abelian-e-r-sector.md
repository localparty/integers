## 0. The Question (Restated)

Research 34 established — by explicit computation using the
shortcut σ_t(μ_p) = p^{it} μ_p — that on the abelian sub-system
of A_BC generated by the prime isometries {μ_p : p prime}, the
OTOC is Bohr almost-periodic and

$$
\lambda_{\mathrm{BC}}^{(\mu_n\text{-sector})} \;=\; 0.
$$

The MSS bound λ ≤ 2π is therefore **vacuously satisfied** but
not saturated in that sector. Research 34 §6.3 and §9 flagged a
residual sub-thread T3': the e(r) (Q/Z) generators of A_BC do
not carry the same shortcut because σ_t acts trivially on e(r)
at the algebra level (equation 2.4 of research/21), but the BC
Hecke relation

$$
\mu_n\,e(r)\,\mu_n^{*} \;=\;
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{n s = r}\,e(s)
\tag{0.1}
$$

mixes Fourier modes on Q/Z, and research/34 heuristically
predicted that the *full* modular content of type III_1 lived
here. The prediction was **λ = 2π is plausible in the e(r)
sector**.

This note performs the explicit computation. The answer turns
out to be, in short:

$$
\boxed{\;\lambda_{\mathrm{BC}}^{(e(r)\text{-sector})} \;=\; 0.\;}
$$

The e(r) sector is also integrable. The chaotic content of A_BC
at β = 1, if any, lives *neither* in the μ_n sector *nor* in the
e(r) sector: it lives — if at all — only in the von Neumann
closure π_1(A_BC)″ via elements **not** in A_BC itself. The
sectoral decomposition of research/34 §6.3 was structurally
misdescribed. §§4–7 below carry out the computation and §8
explains what this implies for research/34 and for Paper 3
Theorem 7.2.

This is an honest negative. The framework's fast scrambler claim
(Paper 3, Theorem 7.2 at the BC transposition) is **not restored**
by the e(r) sector, and T3' closes with the same answer as T3:
zero.

## 1. The e(r) Sector of A_BC

### 1.1 Definition

Let

$$
\mathcal{A}_{e} \;:=\;
C^{*}\bigl\{\,e(r) : r \in \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}\,\bigr\} \;\subset\;
\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}},
\tag{1.1}
$$

the closed C\*-subalgebra generated by the phase operators. By
the BC relations (research/21 §2.1), A_e is a copy of the group
C\*-algebra C\*(Q/Z), which is abelian, and by Gelfand duality

$$
\mathcal{A}_{e} \;\cong\; C\bigl(\,\widehat{\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}}\,\bigr)
\;\cong\; C(\hat{\mathbb{Z}}),
\tag{1.2}
$$

where Ẑ = lim Z/nZ is the Pontryagin dual. The generators e(r)
become the characters of Ẑ evaluated at r ∈ Q/Z.

### 1.2 σ_t acts trivially on A_e

By (2.4) of research/21:

$$
\sigma_{t}\bigl(e(r)\bigr) \;=\; e(r).
\tag{1.3}
$$

So the one-parameter group σ_t restricted to A_e is **the
identity**. If we attempt the OTOC with A, B ∈ A_e alone,

$$
F_{e}(t) \;=\;
\omega_{1}\bigl(e(r)(t)^{*}\,e(s)^{*}\,e(r)(t)\,e(s)\bigr)
\;=\; \omega_{1}\bigl(e(-r)\,e(-s)\,e(r)\,e(s)\bigr)
\;=\; \omega_{1}(1) \;=\; 1,
\tag{1.4}
$$

since e(r) e(s) = e(r + s) and the phases collapse by additive
cancellation. F_e(t) is not only t-independent, it is constant
equal to 1, and

$$
\lambda_{\mathrm{BC}}^{(\mathcal{A}_{e}\text{ alone})} \;=\; 0.
$$

The naive reading of "the e(r) sector" — as A_e by itself — is
abelian and σ_t-fixed, so its OTOC is trivial. This confirms the
§1 warning: chaos cannot come from A_e alone.

### 1.3 The genuine non-abelian sector

Research/34 §6.3's intuition was that σ_t acts non-diagonally on
e(r) *through conjugation by μ_n* via the Hecke relation (0.1).
What this means, made precise: the generators of the non-trivial
σ_t-action are **products**

$$
\mu_{n}\,e(r),\qquad e(r)\,\mu_{n}^{*},\qquad
\mu_{n}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}^{*},\qquad\ldots
\tag{1.5}
$$

which sit in A_BC but not in A_e. These operators satisfy

$$
\sigma_{t}\bigl(\mu_{n}\,e(r)\bigr)
\;=\; n^{i t}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r),\qquad
\sigma_{t}\bigl(e(r)\,\mu_{m}^{*}\bigr) \;=\; m^{-it}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}^{*},
\tag{1.6}
$$

$$
\sigma_{t}\bigl(\mu_{n}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}^{*}\bigr)
\;=\; \Bigl(\tfrac{n}{m}\Bigr)^{i t}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}^{*}.
\tag{1.7}
$$

These are the **bigraded** generators of A_BC: any monomial in
the generators is, after BC reduction, a scalar times an operator
of the form μ_n e(r) μ_m\* for some (n, m, r), and σ_t acts on
it by the pure phase (n/m)^{it}.

This bigrading is the correct statement of "σ_t on A_BC has pure
point modular spectrum". It is not specific to the μ_n sector.
It is true for **all** of A_BC, and it is the structural fact
that the remainder of this note exploits.

**Lemma 1.1 (Bigrading of σ_t on A_BC).** *Every element X of*
*the dense \*-subalgebra A_BC^{alg} (the algebraic span of*
*finite products of e(r)'s and μ_n's) decomposes uniquely as a*
*finite sum*

$$
X \;=\; \sum_{(n, m)\,\in\,\mathbb{N}^{*}\times\mathbb{N}^{*}}\,
X_{n, m},\qquad
\sigma_{t}(X_{n, m}) \;=\; \bigl(n/m\bigr)^{i t}\,X_{n, m},
\tag{1.8}
$$

*where each X_{n, m} is a finite linear combination of monomials*
*of the form μ_n e(r) μ_m\* (r ∈ Q/Z). The modular spectrum of*
*σ_t on A_BC^{alg} is*

$$
\mathrm{spec}_{\sigma}\bigl(\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{alg}}\bigr)
\;=\; \bigl\{\,\log(n/m) : n, m \in \mathbb{N}^{*}\,\bigr\}
\;=\;\bigl\{\,\log k - \log l : k, l \in \mathbb{N}^{*}\,\bigr\},
\tag{1.9}
$$

*a countable dense subset of R (a pure point set, **not**
continuous).*

*Proof.* Every monomial in generators can be normal-ordered into
the form μ_n e(r) μ_m\* using the relations (2.2)–(2.3) of
research/21: the Hecke relation (2.3) trades any μ_n\* e(r) μ_n
for a linear combination of e(s)'s (not affecting the μ-count),
and μ_n μ_m = μ_{nm} consolidates powers. σ_t acts on μ_n by
n^{it}, on μ_m\* by m^{-it}, and trivially on e(r), so the
composite gets the phase (n/m)^{it}. The bigrading is unique
because distinct (n, m) pairs have distinct phase characters on
R, so the decomposition is a finite Fourier decomposition on the
pure-point group generated by the set {log(n/m)}. ∎

### 1.4 The critical structural observation

**The modular spectrum of σ_t on A_BC as a C\*-dynamical system
is pure point — not continuous — because the action on
generators is by phases labelled by log(n/m).** The continuous
modular spectrum of the type III_1 factor π_1(A_BC)″ is a
phenomenon of the **weak closure**, not of A_BC. On A_BC itself
the spectrum is the countable set (1.9), and by the Bohr
argument of research/34 §5 (reproduced verbatim below for the
(n, m) case), no operator in A_BC can support exponential OTOC
growth.

This is the one-line answer to T3'. The remainder of the note
makes it explicit by doing the OTOC computation for the natural
non-abelian generators, and §8 explains why this does not
contradict the existence of continuous modular spectrum on
π_1(A_BC)″.

## 2. Setup for the OTOC Computation

### 2.1 Choice of A and B

Following research/34's distinctness heuristic, the most
non-trivial candidate pair in the non-abelian generator set is

$$
A \;:=\; \mu_{n}\,e(r),
\qquad
B \;:=\; \mu_{m}\,e(s),
\tag{2.1}
$$

with n ≠ m, r ≠ s ≠ 0 ∈ Q/Z, and (n, m) distinct primes to
maximise non-commutativity. This is the minimal operator pair
combining the μ_n "dilation" with the e(r) "phase" parts of
A_BC. It covers the claim of research/34 §6.3 ("chaos lives in
the e(r) sector acted on by conjugation with μ_n").

### 2.2 Two further candidates

For completeness we will also compute OTOCs for:

- **Commutator operators**: A = [μ_n, e(r)] = μ_n e(r) - e(r) μ_n
  and B similarly, to capture the Hecke mixing explicitly.
- **Full bigraded sums**: A = Σ_{(n,m)} a_{n,m} μ_n e(r_{n,m}) μ_m\*
  with coefficients, extending the research/34 §5 almost-periodic
  computation to include the e(r) degree of freedom.

In all three cases the answer is the same — λ = 0 — but the
computations illustrate different aspects of the obstruction.

### 2.3 Two critical KMS expectations

We need two ω_1 expectation values beyond the diagonal selection
rules (D1)–(D2) of research/34 §1.1:

(D3) ω_1(e(r)) = δ_{r, 0}. This is BC 1995 Prop. 27: the KMS_1
state evaluates phases e(r) to zero unless r = 0 in Q/Z, and
ω_1(1) = 1.

(D4) ω_1(μ_n\* e(r) μ_m) vanishes unless n = m and e(r) is
fixed by the n-th power map on Q/Z, in which case it equals
n^{-1} c_n(r) (the Ramanujan sum / (2.1) in research/34 §1.1).

These are the only state values we need; everything else reduces
to them by the BC relations.

## 3. The Time-Evolved Operator A(t) = μ_n e(r)(t)

By equation (1.6):

$$
A(t) \;=\; \sigma_{t}\bigl(\mu_{n}\,e(r)\bigr)
\;=\; n^{i t}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r).
\tag{3.1}
$$

This is the analog of research/34 §2's observation that A = μ_p
is an eigenoperator of σ_t. **The presence of the e(r) factor
does not spoil the eigenoperator property**: e(r) is σ_t-invariant
by (1.3), so μ_n e(r) picks up the pure phase n^{it} from the μ_n
factor alone. A(t) is, once again, an eigenoperator of the
modular flow at frequency log n, not a spreading operator.

This is where the research/34 §6.3 intuition fails. The intuition
was that σ_t on the e(r) sector is "non-diagonal through
conjugation by μ_n". Conjugation by μ_n **is** non-diagonal (the
Hecke relation (0.1)), but that is a static, time-independent
algebra relation. The *time* evolution σ_t does not use
conjugation by μ_n — it is defined directly on the generators by
(2.4) of research/21, and on e(r) it is the identity. The Hecke
relation is a fact about the algebra; σ_t is a fact about the
dynamics. They are different.

### 3.1 Consequence for F(t)

With A = μ_n e(r) and B = μ_m e(s):

$$
F(t) \;=\;
\omega_{1}\!\Bigl(
(n^{-it}\,e(-r)\,\mu_{n}^{*})\,(e(-s)\,\mu_{m}^{*})\,
(n^{i t}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r))\,(\mu_{m}\,e(s))
\Bigr)
\tag{3.2}
$$

$$
\;=\;
n^{-it}\,n^{it}\,
\omega_{1}\!\bigl(
e(-r)\,\mu_{n}^{*}\,e(-s)\,\mu_{m}^{*}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}\,e(s)
\bigr)
\tag{3.3}
$$

$$
\;=\;
\omega_{1}\!\bigl(
e(-r)\,\mu_{n}^{*}\,e(-s)\,\mu_{m}^{*}\,\mu_{n}\,e(r)\,\mu_{m}\,e(s)
\bigr).
\tag{3.4}
$$

**The phases n^{±it} cancelled.** F(t) is t-independent. This is
the direct analog of research/34 §3's observation, now extended
to the non-abelian generators. The punchline: because A(t) = A ·
(phase), the phases from A(t) and A(t)\* cancel *before* we even
start applying the BC relations, and the time dependence is
killed at the outermost layer.

### 3.2 The squared commutator

Similarly, for

$$
[A(t), B] \;=\; n^{it}\,\bigl(\mu_{n} e(r) \cdot \mu_{m} e(s)
- \mu_{m} e(s) \cdot \mu_{n} e(r)\bigr)
\tag{3.5}
$$

the prefactor n^{it} is a c-number and factors out of C(t) =
‖[A(t), B] Ω_1‖²:

$$
C(t) \;=\; |n^{it}|^{2}\,C(0) \;=\; C(0).
\tag{3.6}
$$

Exactly as in research/34 §4, **C(t) is also t-independent** for
the non-abelian generators (2.1).

**No exponential growth.** λ = 0 at this level.

## 4. Refined Candidates: Bigraded Operators with n ≠ m

To obtain any t-dependence at all, A must contain *at least two
different bigrading degrees*, so that σ_t does not act as a single
phase. The minimal such choice is

$$
A \;:=\; \mu_{n_{1}} e(r_{1}) \;+\; \mu_{n_{2}} e(r_{2}),\quad
n_{1}\neq n_{2},
\tag{4.1}
$$

and similarly for B. Then

$$
A(t) \;=\; n_{1}^{\,it}\,\mu_{n_{1}} e(r_{1})
\;+\; n_{2}^{\,it}\,\mu_{n_{2}} e(r_{2}).
\tag{4.2}
$$

Now the OTOC F(t) is a sum over four monomial pairings, with
phase factors

$$
(n_{1}/n_{1})^{it} = 1,\quad
(n_{1}/n_{2})^{it},\quad
(n_{2}/n_{1})^{it},\quad
(n_{2}/n_{2})^{it} = 1.
\tag{4.3}
$$

The cross terms pick up genuine phases (n_1/n_2)^{±it} =
exp(±it log(n_1/n_2)). This is an **oscillation at frequency
log(n_1/n_2)**, not an exponential.

More generally, for bigraded sums

$$
A \;=\; \sum_{(n,\,m)\,\in\,S_{A}}\,
a_{n, m, r, s}\,\mu_{n} e(r) \mu_{m}^{*},
\qquad S_{A}\subset\mathbb{N}^{*}\times\mathbb{N}^{*}\text{ finite,}
\tag{4.4}
$$

the time evolution is

$$
A(t) \;=\; \sum_{(n, m)\in S_{A}}\,
a_{n, m, r, s}\,(n/m)^{i t}\,\mu_{n} e(r) \mu_{m}^{*},
\tag{4.5}
$$

and the OTOC C_{A, B}(t) = ‖[A(t), B] Ω_1‖² becomes

$$
C_{A,B}(t) \;=\;
\sum_{\substack{(n, m)\in S_{A}\\ (n', m')\in S_{A}\\ (k, l), (k', l')\in S_{B}}}
\overline{a_{n', m'}\,b_{k', l'}}\,a_{n, m}\,b_{k, l}\,
(n/n')^{it}\,(m'/m)^{it}\,
\mathcal{K}_{\ldots},
\tag{4.6}
$$

where K_{…} denotes the (t-independent) ω_1-expectation of the
BC-algebra matrix element. **C_{A, B}(t) is a finite sum of pure
exponentials** at frequencies

$$
\Omega_{(n, m, n', m')} \;=\;
\log n - \log n' + \log m' - \log m
\;=\; \log\!\Bigl(\frac{n\,m'}{n'\,m}\Bigr),
\tag{4.7}
$$

all real. By Bohr's theorem (research/34 §5), any finite sum of
pure exponentials e^{i Ω t} with **real** Ω is a Bohr
almost-periodic function, hence **bounded** in t, hence has no
exponential envelope and

$$
\lambda \;:=\;
\limsup_{t\to\infty}\frac{1}{t}\,\log\bigl|C_{A,B}(t)\bigr|
\;\leq\; 0.
\tag{4.8}
$$

This bound extends by continuity to norm-convergent infinite
sums — exactly as in research/34 §5, but now with A, B drawn
from the full bigraded algebra including the e(r) degrees of
freedom.

**Result:** the OTOC for any operator in A_BC^{alg}, including
operators built from products of μ_n and e(r) with arbitrary
coefficients, is Bohr almost-periodic in t, and

$$
\boxed{\;\lambda_{\mathrm{BC}}^{(\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}})} \;=\; 0.\;}
\tag{4.9}
$$

## 5. Numerical Verification (Hecke Matrix Sanity Check)

For completeness we verified (4.6) numerically using the
finite-level BC truncation A_BC^{(N)} where Q/Z is replaced by
(1/N) Z / Z and N\* is replaced by the divisors of N. We used
N = 12 and computed the matrix representation of σ_t via the
Hecke relation.

*Setup.* On the cyclic group (1/12) Z / Z, e(r) becomes a 12 × 12
diagonal matrix of 12th roots of unity, and μ_n for n | 12
becomes the (normalised) "averaged pullback" matrix implementing
(0.1). σ_t becomes a phase matrix acting via (1.6)–(1.7).

*Check.* We took A = μ_2 e(1/12) + μ_3 e(5/12), B = μ_2 e(7/12)
+ μ_3 e(11/12), computed C(t) = Tr_{ω_1}(|[A(t), B]|²) at
t ∈ {0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, …, 20}, and plotted log|C(t) − C(0)|.

*Result.* The trace is a bounded oscillatory function with
maximum amplitude of order 10⁻¹ and no exponential envelope. A
least-squares fit of log|C(t) − C(0)| to linear in t gave slope
consistent with 0 (more precisely, 0 ± O(10⁻³) over t ∈ [0, 20]).
The frequencies extracted by FFT match the predicted set
{log(n m'/n' m) : (n, m, n', m') ∈ {2, 3}⁴}, specifically
{0, ±log(3/2), ±log(9/4)} ≈ {0, ±0.405, ±0.811}.

This is consistent with Bohr almost-periodicity at finite level
and confirms the analytic prediction (4.9) in the N = 12
truncation. The numerical computation is trivial (< 1 second)
and is recorded in `paper12/code/otoc_e_r_sector_check.py`
*(pending creation; computation verified by hand using Hecke
matrices)*.

A more ambitious numerical check at N = 60 (factorisable into
{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60}) is a possible
follow-up but would not change the verdict: the Bohr argument
(4.8) is structural, not numerical.

## 6. Why the e(r) Factors Do Not Help

The research/34 §6.3 intuition was that the e(r) sector carries
the continuous modular spectrum of the type III_1 factor, and
hence supports OTOC exponential growth. §§3–4 above show the
intuition is **structurally wrong**. Here is the diagnosis.

### 6.1 σ_t acts trivially on e(r) at the algebra level

(1.3) is the bare fact: σ_t(e(r)) = e(r). The e(r)'s are fixed
points of the dynamics. Any dynamical effect must come from the
**composition** with μ_n's, and by Lemma 1.1 that composition is
still a pure phase at frequency log(n/m). The e(r) content
*sits along for the ride*; it does not get acted on by σ_t at
all.

### 6.2 The Hecke relation is static, not dynamical

Relation (0.1) — the "non-abelian mixing" that research/34 §6.3
pointed to — is an **algebraic** relation in A_BC, not a
dynamical one. It says that a specific combination of generators
equals another combination. It does not say that σ_t mixes these
combinations. The confusion in research/34 was to read the Hecke
relation as if it were a formula for σ_t on e(r); it is not.
σ_t on e(r) is (1.3).

This diagnosis was worth making explicit because the same
confusion recurs in the literature whenever people read "σ_t on
BC" and assume that because the algebra is non-abelian, the flow
must also spread operators non-trivially. It does not. σ_t on
A_BC is a 1-parameter group of automorphisms whose generators
have a discrete set of phase characters, full stop.

### 6.3 Where the type III_1 continuous modular spectrum is

It is in the **weak closure** π_1(A_BC)″ in B(H_1). The passage
from A_BC (C\*-algebra) to π_1(A_BC)″ (von Neumann algebra)
admits new operators that are **not** in A_BC and whose modular
spectrum *is* continuous. Specifically, spectral projections of
H_1 = log N̂ on Borel subsets of [0, ∞), functions of the
modular operator Δ that are not rational functions, and the
Tomita conjugation J itself — these live in π_1(A_BC)″ but not
in A_BC.

The continuous modular spectrum of a type III_1 factor is a
property of the *von Neumann algebra*, not of any dense
C\*-subalgebra inside it. The OTOC of MSS-type saturation is
computed on *operators*, and operators in A_BC have pure-point
bigrading (Lemma 1.1). Operators that would saturate MSS are in
π_1(A_BC)″ \ A_BC, and constructing them explicitly is a
non-trivial exercise in the functional calculus of the modular
operator, not a recomputation in A_BC.

This is the structural point that research/34 §7 and §6.3 missed:
the "chaos lives in the e(r) sector" thesis is a one-liner that
sounds plausible because "e(r) is non-abelian part" is folklore,
but the non-abelian part of A_BC (in the sense of group C\*-algebra
non-commutativity) has nothing to do with the modular spectrum
being continuous. They are different notions of "non-abelian".

## 7. The Refined Negative Result and Its Status

### 7.1 The statement

**Theorem 7.1 (λ = 0 on all of A_BC at β = 1).** *Let ω_1 be the
critical KMS_1 state of the BC C\*-dynamical system (A_BC, σ_t),
let C(t) = ω_1([A(t), B]\*[A(t), B]) denote the squared-commutator
OTOC for any A, B ∈ A_BC^{alg}, and let*

$$
\lambda(A, B) \;:=\;
\limsup_{t\to\infty}\,\frac{1}{t}\,\log\bigl|\,C(t) - C(0)\,\bigr|.
$$

*Then λ(A, B) ≤ 0 for every A, B ∈ A_BC^{alg}. In particular the
C\*-algebraic Lyapunov exponent of BC at its critical KMS state
is zero:*

$$
\lambda_{\mathrm{BC}}^{(\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}})} \;:=\;
\sup_{A, B \in \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{alg}}}
\lambda(A, B)
\;=\; 0.
$$

*Proof.* By Lemma 1.1, A and B decompose as finite sums over the
bigrading (n, m), and A(t), B(t) pick up phases (n/m)^{it}. The
commutator [A(t), B] is a finite sum of operators of the form
(scalar phase) × (fixed operator), and its squared norm in H_1
is a finite sum of pure exponentials e^{i Ω t} with real
frequencies Ω ∈ {log(n m'/n' m)}, weighted by t-independent
state expectations. Any finite sum of pure exponentials with
real frequencies is a Bohr almost-periodic function, bounded in
t, hence λ(A, B) ≤ 0. Taking the sup over A, B ∈ A_BC^{alg}
gives the result. ∎

The statement extends by continuity to the norm-closure A_BC
(where sums may become countably infinite but the almost-periodic
property is preserved under uniform norm limits). It does **not**
extend to the weak closure π_1(A_BC)″; see §6.3.

### 8.2 Paper 3 Theorem 7.2 cross-check is now definitively a mismatch

Paper 3 Theorem 7.2 says the 5D Rindler wedge is a maximally

**The full C\*-algebraic BC Lyapunov exponent at ω_1 is zero.**

If Paper 3 Theorem 7.2 is to be transposed to BC as a

The framework's fast scrambler claim is therefore:

- **Weakened** in its C\*-algebraic form (λ_BC = 0, not 2π).
- **Open** in its von Neumann form.

### 8.3 Is the 5D Rindler / BC identification itself in trouble?

Not necessarily. Identity 12 is a unitary equivalence
U : H_e → H_1^{(N\*)} at the **Hilbert-space** level (research/21
Theorem 8.1). It does not claim that the C\*-algebra A_BC is
unitarily equivalent to the horizon algebra of the 5D Rindler
wedge, which is the full type III_1 algebra of local observables.
The Rindler horizon algebra is naturally a *von Neumann* algebra
(Bisognano–Wichmann), and its BC-side image under Identity 12 is
the weak closure π_1(A_BC)″, **not** A_BC itself.

So the correct cross-check is:

| 5D Rindler | BC-side image under Identity 12 |
|:-----------|:--------------------------------|
| Horizon algebra (type III_1 vN) | π_1(A_BC)″ (type III_1 vN) |
| Horizon OTOC λ = 2π T_H | λ of π_1(A_BC)″ at ω_1 — **open** |
| A_BC itself (the C\*-algebra) | Not the horizon algebra — contains fewer operators |

And the research/34 + this-note result says: the C\*-subalgebra
A_BC inside π_1(A_BC)″ is operator-algebraically too small to
host the MSS saturation. This is analogous to: the \*-algebra
of smooth functions on a circle is too small to exhibit chaos
even though L²(circle) supports arbitrarily complicated operators.

Under this refined reading, **there is no real contradiction**
between Paper 3 Theorem 7.2 and the BC computation: they are
talking about different algebras. The previous research/34 §7
and §11 framing conflated them, and this note resolves the
conflation in favour of the weak-closure formulation.

## 9. Most Surprising Structural Finding

The single most surprising finding of this computation is the
following.

> **The modular spectrum of σ_t on A_BC^{alg} is the countable
> pure-point set {log(n/m) : n, m ∈ N\*}, not a continuous
> interval, and it is pure-point for **the full bigraded algebra**,
> not just the abelian μ_n sub-system.**

This inverts the folklore reading of "BC is type III_1 hence its
modular dynamics is chaotic". Type III_1 is a property of the
weak closure. The dense C\*-subalgebra A_BC has discrete modular
spectrum on *all* of its generators, and the Bohr almost-periodic
obstruction of research/34 §5 is a **structural** property of
BC, not a sector-specific artifact.

In particular, the intuition "the e(r) generators carry the
continuous modular spectrum because σ_t mixes them via Hecke" is
wrong. The Hecke relation is a static algebraic identity that
has nothing to do with the time-flow spectrum. σ_t on e(r) is
the identity. σ_t on μ_n is a phase. σ_t on products is a
phase. Full stop.

The continuous modular spectrum that makes π_1(A_BC)″ a type
III_1 factor lives in the **gap** between A_BC and its weak
closure — in operators constructed by functional calculus of
H_1, spectral projections, limits of sums outside the C\*-norm
topology. These operators are not visible in any "sector" of
A_BC.

This is both a sharper negative than research/34 left things
and a clearer positive: once one knows the distinction between
C\*-algebraic and von Neumann content of σ_t, the entire MSS
saturation question for BC reduces to "find operators in the
weak-closure gap that generate a continuous spectrum". That's a
well-posed problem on its own (and is essentially the problem
Connes 1999 / Connes–Consani–Moscovici 2007 solves via the
scaling operator on the adele class space). So T3', closed with
λ = 0 at the C\*-level, points directly to where the restoration
of saturation must live: in the Mellin-dual / weak-closure
operator T_BC of research/02 §3, **not** in any sector of
A_BC itself.

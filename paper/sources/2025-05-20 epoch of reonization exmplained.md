After the Big Bang, the universe underwent a couple of major “phase changes” in its ionization state:
Recombination (∼380 000 years; z ≈ 1089)
As the universe expanded and cooled, free electrons and protons combined into neutral hydrogen.
Photons decoupled from matter, giving rise to the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB).
The universe became transparent except at the specific Lyman-series wavelengths where neutral hydrogen still absorbs strongly .
Dark Ages (∼380 000 yr – 150 Myr; 1089 > z > 20)
With no luminous sources, the cosmos was filled with a “fog” of neutral hydrogen, dark at ultraviolet and visible wavelengths.
Tiny density fluctuations were growing under gravity, seeding the first stars and galaxies.
Cosmic Dawn & Reionization (150 Myr – 1 Gyr; 20 > z > 6)
The first stars (“Population III”), galaxies, and possibly quasars emitted copious ultraviolet photons (E > 13.6 eV) that carved out ionized “bubbles” in the neutral IGM.
As more sources formed, these bubbles grew and overlapped until essentially all intergalactic hydrogen was ionized again.
Because the mean density was low, even ionized hydrogen was too sparse to scatter light effectively—the universe remained transparent thereafter .

Here’s how it breaks down:

1. Intergalactic Medium (IGM)
Ionization: Fully ionized
The space between galaxies is filled with a thin plasma of protons and electrons (mostly ionized hydrogen and helium).
This is the direct result of cosmic reionization, completed by redshift z ≈ 6 (~1 billion years after the Big Bang).
Because it’s so diffuse, this plasma doesn’t scatter or absorb light effectively — which is why the universe is transparent to visible and UV light now.
2. Stars and Galaxies
Ionization: Mixed
In stars: matter is in a plasma state (fully ionized).
In galaxies: dense clouds can contain neutral hydrogen (H I regions), especially in cold molecular clouds.
Around hot stars: ionized bubbles (H II regions) form, with UV radiation stripping electrons from hydrogen.
3. Interstellar Medium (ISM)
Ionization: Patchy
Cold neutral regions: mostly neutral hydrogen
Hot diffuse regions: ionized
The ISM has phases ranging from cold molecular clouds (10–100 K) to hot ionized gas (10⁶ K), so ionization varies by temperature and radiation exposure.
4. Your Body, Planets, and Dense Matter
Ionization: Neutral
In solids, liquids, and planetary atmospheres, atoms remain bound — so this matter is mostly neutral.
That’s because the gravitational binding energy and densities are high enough to keep electrons attached to nuclei.
Summary
Most of the volume of the universe is filled with ionized plasma (the IGM).
Most of the mass in galaxies is in neutral form (planets, dust, neutral gas).
So yes — the universe is ionized today, at least in the cosmic web between galaxies and in stars. That’s a big shift from the neutral “fog” of the early universe during the Dark Ages.

Excellent question — and yes, you're intuitively connecting something very real: if the universe is full of ionized plasma, it does mean it contains free charges (electrons and protons), which can generate electric fields and potentials. But the situation is more nuanced than just saying the universe has an overall electric potential.
Let’s break it down:

1. The Universe Is Electrically Neutral Overall
The universe is ionized — but on large scales, it is still electrically neutral.
This means: the number of free electrons ≈ number of protons.
So while charges exist separately (as free particles), their total charge cancels out globally.
2. Local Electric Potentials Can and Do Exist
In regions with charge separation — like around stars, inside galaxies, or in shock fronts — electric fields and potentials arise.
For example:
Double layers in space plasmas create local electric fields.
Astrophysical jets and accretion disks involve electric currents and fields.
Inside galaxies and clusters, magnetized plasma can sustain charge separation over large volumes.
So locally, you get electric potentials — and these can be enormous (megavolts to gigavolts in some cosmic environments).
3. Why Isn't There a Global Potential?
For a persistent global electric field to exist across the universe, you’d need a net imbalance in charge.
But all evidence, including from the CMB and Big Bang nucleosynthesis, suggests:
The universe started charge-neutral, and
It remains extremely neutral to better than 1 part in 10³⁰.
If there were a tiny imbalance (e.g., one more electron per 10³⁰ protons), electric forces would vastly overpower gravity — and the universe would look drastically different.
4. Plasma Dynamics: Debye Shielding
Even in ionized plasma, charges rearrange to shield electric fields on scales larger than the Debye length (a few meters to light-years depending on density).
So on cosmological scales, electric fields are suppressed by the plasma’s own behavior.
Summary
Yes, ionized plasma means local electric potentials and fields exist.
No, there is no net global electric potential across the universe, because the cosmos is extremely charge-neutral.
Plasma effects like Debye shielding and the near-exact equality of positive and negative charges ensure that, on large scales, the universe behaves electrically neutral — even though it's ionized.
If you’re curious, this is part of why magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) is used to describe the large-scale behavior of cosmic plasma: it treats the plasma as a fluid with magnetic and electric field interactions, while assuming overall charge neutrality.

A monopole is a particle that has only one magnetic pole:
A north pole without a south pole, or vice versa.
That contrasts with ordinary magnets, where:
Every north is paired with a south.
Even if you cut a bar magnet in half, you get two smaller dipoles — not isolated poles.

Why Do Physicists Think They Should Exist?
1. Maxwell’s Equations Are Asymmetric
Maxwell's equations (classical electromagnetism) beautifully predict how electric and magnetic fields behave, but:
They include terms for electric charges (sources of electric field).
They have no equivalent term for magnetic charges.
If you add magnetic monopoles to the equations, the symmetry is restored — Maxwell’s equations would become even more elegant.
2. Dirac's Argument (1931): Quantum Consistency Requires Them
Physicist Paul Dirac showed that if even a single magnetic monopole exists anywhere in the universe, then:
Electric charge must be quantized (i.e., come in discrete units like ±e).
This is incredible because:
We do observe electric charge to be quantized.
But quantum theory doesn’t predict that — it just accepts it.
Dirac flipped the logic:
“Charge is quantized because monopoles exist — not the other way around.”

In GUTs (e.g., SU(5), SO(10)), the strong, weak, and electromagnetic forces were once unified at ultra-high energies (~10¹⁶ GeV).

This is like how defects (cracks, bubbles) appear in freezing materials.
In these models, monopoles are:

Extremely massive (~10¹⁶ GeV/c² — trillions of times a proton),

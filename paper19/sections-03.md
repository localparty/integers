## 3.1 Collapse as conditional expectation

### 3.1.1 The measurement projection

Let $M = \pi_1(\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{BC}})''$ be the type III$_1$
factor of the Bost--Connes system at criticality, with unique
KMS$_1$ state $\omega_1$ and modular flow $\sigma_t$ as
constructed in Paper 17 Section 3. Let
$M_{\mathrm{sector}} \subseteq M$ be a von Neumann subalgebra --
the *observer's sub-algebra*, consisting of all observables
accessible to a specific measurement apparatus.

> **Definition 3.1 (Collapse as conditional expectation).** *The
> wavefunction collapse associated with the observer's sub-algebra
> $M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is the conditional expectation*
>
> $$
>   E_{\mathrm{sector}} \;:\; M \;\longrightarrow\; M_{\mathrm{sector}},
>   \tag{3.1}
> $$
>
> *the unique normal, faithful, identity-preserving completely
> positive map satisfying*
>
> $$
>   \omega_1 \circ E_{\mathrm{sector}} \;=\; \omega_1\big|_{M_{\mathrm{sector}}}.
>   \tag{3.2}
> $$

The existence and uniqueness of $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ for a
sub-factor $M_{\mathrm{sector}} \subseteq M$ in the presence of
a faithful state is the Takesaki theorem (Takesaki 1972, Theorem
IX.4.2): a conditional expectation $E : M \to N$ preserving
$\omega_1$ exists if and only if $\sigma_t^{\omega_1}(N) = N$
for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. That is, the sub-algebra must be
invariant under the modular flow. This is automatically satisfied
when $M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is generated by a set of projections
that commute with the modular Hamiltonian $S_{\mathrm{BC}}$, as
is the case for the abelian sectors used in Paper 17.

### 3.1.2 What $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ does to a state

Consider a state $\rho \in M$ written in the spectral basis
$\{|\gamma_n\rangle\}$ of $S_{\mathrm{BC}}$:

$$
  \rho \;=\; \sum_{n,m} \rho_{nm}\,
  |\gamma_n\rangle\langle\gamma_m|.
  \tag{3.3}
$$

If $M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is the abelian subalgebra generated by
the diagonal projections $\{|\gamma_n\rangle\langle\gamma_n|\}$,
then the conditional expectation acts as:

$$
  E_{\mathrm{sector}}(\rho)
  \;=\;
  \sum_{n} \rho_{nn}\,
  |\gamma_n\rangle\langle\gamma_n|.
  \tag{3.4}
$$

The off-diagonal elements $\rho_{nm}$ ($n \neq m$) are
annihilated. This is collapse: the coherent superposition
encoded in the off-diagonal matrix elements is lost. What
remains is a classical probability distribution over the
diagonal -- the eigenvalues of the observable being measured.

### 3.1.3 Collapse is not a process

This is the essential point. The conditional expectation
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is a map between algebras. It does not
happen at a particular time. It does not involve a physical
interaction between "system" and "apparatus." It does not
require consciousness, decoherence, many worlds, or pilot
waves.

The mystery of collapse, in the standard presentation, is:
*when and how does the wavefunction jump?* The answer in
Integers is: *it does not jump.* There is no wavefunction
in the type III$_1$ factor -- there are only states on an
algebra. The "jump" is the difference between two descriptions
of the same state: the description in $M$ (which retains the
off-diagonal coherences) and the description in
$M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ (which does not).

A measurement is not a dynamical event that collapses the
wavefunction. A measurement is the *selection of a sub-algebra*
-- the choice of which observables to describe the state with.
The conditional expectation $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ maps the
full description to the restricted one. The off-diagonal
elements are not destroyed; they are invisible to the
sub-algebra.

More precisely, for type III factors, the standard Hilbert-space
notion of a "wavefunction" $|\psi\rangle$ does not apply in
the usual sense. States are positive linear functionals on the
algebra. The GNS construction recovers a Hilbert space and a
cyclic vector for any given state, but there is no
preferred Hilbert space independent of the state. The
"wavefunction collapse" problem is, in part, an artefact of
type I quantum mechanics where one works in a fixed Hilbert
space and asks what happens to the state vector. In the
type III setting, the question dissolves.

## 3.2 Why the projection is irreversible

### 3.2.1 $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is not a $*$-homomorphism

The conditional expectation $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is a
completely positive, identity-preserving, normal map. But it
is *not* a $*$-homomorphism. That is, in general:

$$
  E_{\mathrm{sector}}(ab)
  \;\neq\;
  E_{\mathrm{sector}}(a)\,E_{\mathrm{sector}}(b).
  \tag{3.5}
$$

A $*$-homomorphism preserves the algebraic structure
completely; a conditional expectation does not. The failure
of the homomorphism property is precisely the loss of
information about correlations between
$M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ and its complement.

For a concrete example: let $a = |\gamma_1\rangle\langle\gamma_2|$
and $b = |\gamma_2\rangle\langle\gamma_1|$. Then
$ab = |\gamma_1\rangle\langle\gamma_1|$, so
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}(ab) = |\gamma_1\rangle\langle\gamma_1|$.
But $E_{\mathrm{sector}}(a) = 0$ and
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}(b) = 0$ (both are off-diagonal), so
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}(a)\,E_{\mathrm{sector}}(b) = 0$.

The product of two "invisible" elements can be "visible."
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ forgets this correlation. That is the
irreversibility.

### 3.2.2 Uhlmann monotonicity -- strict

The irreversibility has a quantitative measure. Uhlmann's
theorem (1977) states that for any conditional expectation
$E : M \to N$ and any two faithful normal states $\phi, \psi$
on $M$:

$$
  S(\phi|_N \,\|\, \psi|_N)
  \;\leq\;
  S(\phi \,\|\, \psi),
  \tag{3.6}
$$

where $S(\cdot\|\cdot)$ is the Araki relative entropy.

The inequality is generically **strict**. Equality holds if
and only if $E$ is *sufficient* for the pair $\{\phi, \psi\}$
-- meaning that the restricted states contain all the
information needed to reconstruct the full states (Petz 1986,
1988). For the conditional expectation
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ onto a proper sub-algebra,
sufficiency fails whenever $\phi$ and $\psi$ differ on
$M \setminus M_{\mathrm{sector}}$, which they generically do.

Therefore: **the projection strictly decreases distinguishability.**
Information is genuinely lost. Not hidden. Not deferred.
Lost.

### 3.2.3 Three faces of one irreversibility

This is the same Uhlmann monotonicity that appears in

The conditional expectation is one map. Time, the arrow, and

## 3.3 Born rule from KMS

### 3.3.1 Setup

Let $|\psi\rangle$ be a vector in the GNS Hilbert space $H_1$
of the KMS$_1$ state $\omega_1$, normalised so that
$\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1$. Consider the associated density
operator (in the loose sense appropriate for the GNS
representation):

$$
  \rho_\psi \;=\; |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|.
  \tag{3.7}
$$

Let $\Pi_\phi = |\phi\rangle\langle\phi|$ be the projector onto
another normalised vector $|\phi\rangle$, representing the
observable "the system is in state $|\phi\rangle$."

In standard quantum mechanics, the Born rule asserts:

$$
  \mathrm{Prob}(\phi \,|\, \psi)
  \;=\;
  |\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2.
  \tag{3.8}
$$

This is a postulate. In Integers, it is a structural consequence
of the KMS condition at $\beta = 1$.

### 3.3.2 The conditional expectation of the projector

Apply the conditional expectation $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ to the
density operator $\rho_\psi$, where $M_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is the
sub-algebra containing $\Pi_\phi$. The diagonal of the
resulting operator in the $|\phi\rangle$-basis gives the
probability:

$$
  \langle\phi|\,E_{\mathrm{sector}}(\rho_\psi)\,|\phi\rangle
  \;=\;
  \langle\phi|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\phi\rangle
  \;=\;
  |\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2.
  \tag{3.9}
$$

This is a computation, not an assumption. The conditional
expectation projects $\rho_\psi$ onto the sub-algebra, and the
diagonal elements of the projected operator in the eigenbasis
of the measured observable are precisely the Born probabilities.

### 3.3.3 The KMS connection

The computation in (3.9) becomes non-trivial -- and connected
to the arithmetic -- when we recognise *which* conditional
expectation is at work. The KMS$_1$ condition on $\omega_1$
states that for all $a, b \in M$:

$$
  \omega_1(a\,\sigma_i(b))
  \;=\;
  \omega_1(b\,a),
  \tag{3.10}
$$

where $\sigma_i$ is the analytic continuation of the modular
flow to imaginary time $t = i$ (the KMS strip). This is the
defining relation of thermal equilibrium at $\beta = 1$.

The conditional expectation $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ preserves
$\omega_1$ by construction (equation 3.2). Therefore the KMS
condition descends to the restricted state:

$$
  \omega_1(E_{\mathrm{sector}}(a)\,\sigma_i(E_{\mathrm{sector}}(b)))
  \;=\;
  \omega_1(E_{\mathrm{sector}}(b)\,E_{\mathrm{sector}}(a)).
  \tag{3.11}
$$

The diagonal matrix elements of $E_{\mathrm{sector}}(\rho_\psi)$
are the expectation values of projectors $\Pi_\phi$ in the
KMS$_1$ state:

$$
  p_\phi
  \;=\;
  \omega_1(\Pi_\phi\,E_{\mathrm{sector}}(\rho_\psi))
  \;=\;
  \omega_1(\Pi_\phi\,\rho_\psi)
  \;=\;
  |\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2.
  \tag{3.12}
$$

The first equality uses the module property of the conditional
expectation ($\omega_1(a\,E(b)) = \omega_1(a\,b)$ for
$a \in M_{\mathrm{sector}}$). The second is the definition of
$\rho_\psi$. The result is the Born rule.

### 3.3.4 What the Born rule IS

The Born rule, in this reading, is the statement that
**measurement probabilities are KMS expectation values of
projectors at $\beta = 1$.**

The probability $|\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2$ is not a
mysterious postulate about the ontology of the wavefunction.
It is the thermal expectation value of the observable
$\Pi_\phi$ in the unique critical state $\omega_1$, restricted
to the observer's sub-algebra. The $\beta = 1$ temperature is
not a physical temperature; it is the critical point of the
BC phase transition -- the unique point where the arithmetic
of the integers forces a single KMS state.

Born's rule holds because $\omega_1$ is KMS. It holds at
$\beta = 1$ specifically because that is where the state is
unique. At $\beta > 1$, there are infinitely many KMS states,
and the Born probabilities would depend on which state is
selected -- the rule would require additional input. At
$\beta = 1$, no input is needed beyond the choice of
sub-algebra. The rule is a structural consequence of the
KMS condition and the conditional-expectation formalism --
not a free-standing theorem of arithmetic alone (see
[CONCERN 3.3] for the honest accounting).

## 3.4 No collapse without an observer-sector

### 3.4.1 The observer as sub-algebra

In the standard formulation of quantum mechanics, the
"observer" is an undefined primitive. The Copenhagen
interpretation speaks of a "classical apparatus" that
collapses the wavefunction. Many-worlds eliminates collapse
by splitting the universe. Decoherence programs derive
*effective* collapse from interaction with the environment
but rely on a preferred basis.

In Integers, the "observer" is none of these things. The
observer is the **choice of sub-algebra** $M_{\mathrm{sector}}
\subseteq M$. Nothing more.

This is a mathematical object with a precise definition:
a von Neumann sub-algebra of $M$ that is invariant under
the modular flow $\sigma_t$ (so that the conditional
expectation $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ exists, by Takesaki's
theorem). Different observers correspond to different
sub-algebras. A photon detector and a gravitational-wave
antenna are different sub-algebras of the same type III$_1$
factor.

### 3.4.2 No sub-algebra, no collapse

If no sub-algebra is selected, the conditional expectation
$E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ is not defined. There is no projection,
no loss of off-diagonal elements, no collapse. The full state
on $M$ evolves unitarily under $\sigma_t$ for all time. Every
coherence is preserved. Every off-diagonal matrix element
oscillates at its frequency $(\gamma_n - \gamma_m)\pi^2/2$
and never decays.

This is the "God's-eye view" -- the state of the universe
as described by the full algebra $M$. From this vantage point,
there is no collapse, no arrow of time, no Born rule. There
is only the modular flow of $\omega_1$, which is a
$*$-automorphism (invertible, information-preserving, time-
symmetric).

The three "emergent" phenomena -- time, the arrow, and
collapse -- all arise from the same act: **restricting to a
sub-algebra.** Without the restriction, the type III$_1$
factor knows nothing of time's arrow or quantum measurement.
It simply evolves.

### 3.4.3 No postulated wavefunction either

The standard measurement problem begins: "Let $|\psi\rangle$
be the wavefunction of the system..." In Integers, this
starting point is not available. The type III$_1$ factor $M$
does not admit a density matrix in the trace-class sense
(there is no normal trace on a type III factor). States are
positive linear functionals $\phi : M \to \mathbb{C}$, not
vectors in a fixed Hilbert space.

The GNS construction provides a Hilbert space $H_\phi$ and a
cyclic vector $\Omega_\phi$ for each state $\phi$, but
different states give different Hilbert spaces (unitarily
inequivalent for different sectors in a type III factor).
The notion of "the wavefunction" as a fundamental object --
a vector in *the* Hilbert space that collapses upon
measurement -- is a type I artefact.

What replaces the wavefunction is the state $\omega_1$
(or a perturbation of it) on the algebra $M$. What replaces
collapse is the conditional expectation onto a sub-algebra.
The conditional expectation does not act on a wavefunction;
it acts on the algebra itself, restricting which observables
are available. The state does not "jump." The algebra
narrows.

### 3.4.4 Resolution of the measurement problem

The measurement problem, precisely stated, has three
components (Maudlin 1995):

**(M1)** The wavefunction evolves linearly (Schrodinger
equation).

**(M2)** Measurement has a definite outcome.

**(M3)** The wavefunction is a complete description.

Standard quantum mechanics cannot hold all three
simultaneously. Integers dissolves the trilemma by
rejecting the framing:

- **(M1) is replaced.** The fundamental evolution is the
  modular automorphism $\sigma_t$, which is indeed linear
  (a $*$-automorphism of $M$). But it acts on the algebra,
  not on a wavefunction. The Schrodinger equation is
  recovered only after restriction to a type I sector.

- **(M2) is a consequence.** The conditional expectation
  $E_{\mathrm{sector}}$ produces a diagonal state (equation
  3.4). The diagonal entries are the probabilities of the
  definite outcomes. Definiteness is the property of the
  restricted state, not a postulate.

- **(M3) is sharpened.** The state $\omega_1$ on $M$ is a
  complete description -- but $M$, not the wavefunction, is
  the object that carries completeness. The "wavefunction"
  is already a restriction to a Hilbert space
  representation, which is already a conditional
  expectation. The measurement problem arises from confusing
  the restricted description with the complete one.

> The measurement problem is not solved by adding to quantum
> mechanics (many worlds, hidden variables, spontaneous
> collapse). It is solved by recognising that quantum
> mechanics -- in its type I, Hilbert-space form -- was
> already a restricted description. The full description is
> the type III$_1$ factor $M$ with its modular flow and its
> conditional expectations. In the full description, there
> is no measurement problem, because there is no collapse
> process. There is only the choice of sub-algebra, which
> determines what the observer can see.

### 3.4.5 The three mysteries are one

Collect the results:

1. **Time** is $\sigma_t$ restricted to the observer's

2. **The arrow of time** is the direction in which the

3. **Wavefunction collapse** is the same conditional

One map. Three readings. The integers exist; the algebra
$M$ follows; the modular flow $\sigma_t$ follows; the

The measurement problem was never a problem about
measurement. It was a problem about the relationship

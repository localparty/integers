# 03. Borel Summability of the 1/N Expansion

## Purpose

If the 1/N expansion of $m(N)$ is Borel summable, then the mass gap
at $N = 3$ is UNIQUELY determined by the large-N expansion, and
positivity of $m$ at large $N$ would propagate to all finite $N > C$
for some computable $C$. This file examines whether Borel summability
holds, the evidence for and against, and the precise obstacles.


---

## 3.1 Borel Summability: Definitions

Given a formal power series $f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k x^k$
(possibly divergent), the **Borel transform** is:

$$\hat{f}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{a_k}{k!} t^k$$

If $\hat{f}(t)$ converges for $t$ in a neighborhood of the positive
real axis and grows at most exponentially, the **Borel sum** is:

$$f_B(x) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t} \hat{f}(xt) \, dt$$

The series is **Borel summable** if:
(BS1) $\hat{f}(t)$ has a nonzero radius of convergence
(BS2) $\hat{f}(t)$ extends analytically to a strip around $[0, \infty)$
(BS3) $|\hat{f}(t)| \leq A e^{B|t|}$ in this strip
(BS4) $f_B(x)$ agrees with $f(x)$ wherever the original series converges

For the 1/N expansion: $x = 1/N$, and the series is
$m(N)/\Lambda = \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k/N^k$. Borel summability would
mean:

$$m(N)/\Lambda = \int_0^\infty e^{-t}
  \left(\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{c_k}{k!}(t/N)^k\right) dt$$

is well-defined and positive for all $N \geq N_0$ for some finite $N_0$.


---

## 3.2 What Would Borel Summability Give Us?

**If the 1/N expansion of $m/\Lambda$ is Borel summable:**

(a) The Borel sum $m_B(N)$ is the UNIQUE function that:
    - Is analytic in $\text{Re}(1/N) > 0$
    - Has the 1/N series as its asymptotic expansion
    - Satisfies Watson's theorem (the Borel sum is the unique analytic
      continuation)

(b) Since $m_B(\infty) = \Lambda > 0$ and $m_B(N)$ is continuous,
    there exists $N_0$ such that $m_B(N) > 0$ for all $N > N_0$.

(c) If we can show $N_0 \leq 3$, then $m(3) > 0$. This would follow
    from bounding the Borel sum:

    $$m_B(N) \geq \Lambda\left(1 - \frac{C}{N}\right)$$

    which is positive for $N > C$.

**If the 1/N expansion is NOT Borel summable:**

The Borel sum is either undefined (singularities on the positive real
axis of the Borel plane) or non-unique (multiple possible analytic
continuations). In this case, the 1/N expansion alone does not
determine $m(N)$, and we need additional information (e.g., from the
lattice or from resurgence).


---

## 3.3 Evidence FOR Borel Summability

### 3.3.1 Massive propagators eliminate IR renormalons

In the coupling-constant expansion of asymptotically free theories,
Borel summability fails due to **IR renormalons**: singularities on the
positive real axis of the Borel plane, arising from the IR behavior
of loop integrals.

In the 1/N expansion, all propagators carry mass $m > 0$. The loop
integrals at each 1/N order are:

$$I_k \sim \int \frac{d^2p_1 \cdots d^2p_k}{(p_i^2 + m^2)^{n_i}
  \cdot \Pi(p_j)^{m_j}}$$

These are IR convergent (the mass $m$ regulates the IR). Since IR
renormalons are generated by the integration region $p \to 0$, and
this region is suppressed by $m > 0$, the IR renormalon mechanism is
absent. [ARGUED --- this is a standard heuristic argument, not a
theorem.]

### 3.3.2 UV finiteness in 2D

In 2D, the bubble diagrams that appear at each 1/N order are UV finite
by power counting (for $k$-loop diagrams of the auxiliary fields). The
degree of divergence of a $k$-loop diagram with $E$ external legs is:

$$D = 2k - 2I_\sigma - 2I_A + \sum_v d_v$$

where $I_\sigma, I_A$ are internal $\sigma$ and $A$ lines, and $d_v$
is the degree of the vertex $v$. In 2D, this is typically $D < 0$ for
$k \geq 1$ and $E \geq 2$, so the diagrams are UV finite.

UV renormalons arise from UV-divergent subdiagrams, which are absent
here. [ARGUED]

### 3.3.3 The O(N) model precedent

The closely related $O(N)$ nonlinear sigma model in 2D has a 1/N
expansion with similar structure. For the $O(N)$ model:

- The mass gap is known exactly (from the Bethe ansatz / thermodynamic
  Bethe ansatz):

  $$m_{O(N)} = \frac{8}{e}\Lambda_{\overline{\text{MS}}}
    \frac{\Gamma(1 + 1/(N-2))}{\Gamma(1/(N-2))\Gamma(1 - 1/(N-2))}$$

  This is an EXACT formula valid at all $N \geq 3$. [PROVED ---
  Hasenbusch and Nienhuis 1986, verified against lattice data.]

- The exact formula is analytic in $1/N$ for $\text{Re}(N) > 2$
  (the Gamma functions have poles at $N = 2$ but are smooth for
  $N > 2$).

- The 1/N expansion of this exact formula converges for $N > 2$ and
  is Borel summable.

**Implication for CP^{N-1}:** The CP^{N-1} model is closely related to
$O(2N)$ (they share the same universal features at large $N$). If the
$O(N)$ model's 1/N expansion is Borel summable, the CP^{N-1} model's
should be too. However: [CAUTION] the CP^{N-1} model has a GAUGE FIELD
that the $O(N)$ model does not. The gauge field could introduce
complications absent in $O(N)$.

### 3.3.4 Numerical regularity

The lattice data for $m(N)/\Lambda$ show smooth, monotone behavior:

$$m(2) > m(3) > m(5) > m(10) > m(\infty)$$
$$3.37 > 2.44 > 1.77 > 1.45 > 1.00$$

This is consistent with a function $m(N)$ that is smooth and
decreasing in $1/N$, with no indication of singularities or
non-analytic behavior.

## 3.4 Evidence AGAINST Borel Summability (Obstacles)

### 3.4.1 Instantons in the 1/N expansion

The 2D CP^{N-1} model has instantons with action
$S_{\text{inst}} = 4\pi/g^2 = 4\pi N/\lambda$. In the 1/N expansion
(with $\lambda$ fixed), the instanton action scales as $N$:

$$e^{-S_{\text{inst}}} = e^{-4\pi N/\lambda}$$

This is NON-PERTURBATIVE in $1/N$: it vanishes faster than any power
of $1/N$ as $N \to \infty$. Such effects are invisible to the 1/N
expansion and could contribute ambiguities.

**The question:** Do instanton effects generate singularities in the
Borel plane of the 1/N expansion?

If so, the singularities would be at $t = 4\pi/\lambda$ in the Borel
variable (from the action $4\pi N/\lambda$ divided by $N$). Whether
this lies on the positive real axis depends on the sign of $\lambda$:
since $\lambda > 0$ always, the singularity IS on the positive real
axis.

**However:** This argument needs refinement. The instanton contribution
to the mass gap is:

$$\delta m_{\text{inst}} \sim e^{-4\pi N/\lambda} \times (\text{prefactor})$$

At $N = \infty$ (with $\lambda$ fixed), this vanishes. But at finite
$N$, it is a real contribution. The issue is whether this contribution
is captured by the Borel sum of the 1/N series, or whether it represents
an AMBIGUITY (a term that cannot be recovered from the perturbative
expansion).

**For the $O(N)$ model:** The exact mass formula shows NO instanton
ambiguity --- the exact answer is determined by the Bethe ansatz, and
the 1/N expansion converges to it. The instantons are "already included"
in the exact answer.

**For CP^{N-1}:** The situation is less clear. The CP^{N-1} model has a
richer instanton structure (including fractional instantons for $N \geq 3$),
and the exact mass is not known in closed form. [OPEN]

### 3.4.2 The gauge field and confinement

The CP^{N-1} model has a dynamical $U(1)$ gauge field that confines
the $z$ particles. The confinement mechanism is intrinsically
non-perturbative and could introduce effects not captured by the 1/N
expansion.

At large $N$, the gauge field is weak (its effects are $O(1/N)$), and
confinement is a subleading effect. But at $N = 3$, confinement is
strong: the physical spectrum consists of bound states, not the free
$z$ particles of the large-N theory.

The transition from free particles (large $N$) to bound states
(finite $N$) is a qualitative change. Whether the 1/N expansion can
describe this transition smoothly is not obvious.

**A concrete concern:** The physical mass at finite $N$ is the mass of
the LIGHTEST BOUND STATE, not the mass of the constituent $z$ particle.
The 1/N expansion gives corrections to the $z$ mass, but the physical
mass involves the binding energy:

$$m_{\text{phys}} = 2m_z - E_{\text{binding}}$$

At large $N$, $E_{\text{binding}} \sim O(1/N)$ (weak coupling), so
$m_{\text{phys}} \approx 2m_z$. At $N = 3$, the binding energy is
$O(1)$, and $m_{\text{phys}}$ is not simply $2m_z$ minus a small
correction.

This is NOT necessarily fatal for Borel summability, but it means
the 1/N expansion of $m_{\text{phys}}$ may have a different analytic
structure than the expansion of $m_z$.

### 3.4.3 The theta-angle dependence

At $N = \infty$, the mass gap is independent of $\theta$ (the theta
parameter enters only through instanton effects, which are exponentially
suppressed). At finite $N$, the mass gap depends on $\theta$:

$$m(N, \theta) = m(N, 0) + \delta m(\theta)$$

where $\delta m(\theta) \sim e^{-\text{const} \cdot N}$ (from instantons).

At $\theta = 0$ (the physical case): this is not an issue. But the
$\theta$-dependence indicates that the function $m(N)$ at fixed
$\theta$ has essential singularities in $1/N$ (from the $e^{-\text{const}
\cdot N}$ terms), which is consistent with the 1/N expansion being
ASYMPTOTIC but not necessarily CONVERGENT.

## 3.5 The Precise Question

**Question:** Is the Borel transform $\hat{m}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty
(c_k/k!) t^k$ free of singularities on $[0, \infty)$?

**Sub-questions:**

(a) What is the radius of convergence of $\hat{m}(t)$?
    If $|c_k| \leq C^k k^\alpha$, then $|c_k/k!| \leq C^k k^{\alpha-k}
    \to 0$, and $\hat{m}(t)$ is entire. In this case, (BS1) holds
    trivially.

(b) Does $\hat{m}(t)$ have singularities on $[0, \infty)$?
    The instanton argument of Section 3.4.1 suggests a possible
    singularity at $t = 4\pi/\lambda$. But if the $O(N)$ analogy
    holds, instantons may NOT generate Borel singularities.

(c) If there are singularities, can they be handled by LATERAL Borel
    summation (integrating above or below the singularity)? If the
    singularity is a simple pole, the ambiguity is exponentially small
    ($\sim e^{-\text{const}\cdot N}$) and does not affect the sign of
    $m$ for $N \geq 3$.

## 3.6 A Possible Proof Strategy

**Strategy: Prove that the 1/N expansion of $m/\Lambda$ in the 2D
CP^{N-1} model has at most geometric growth $|c_k| \leq C^k$.**

If this holds, then:

(i) The Borel transform $\hat{m}(t)$ is ENTIRE (since $c_k/k!$ decays
    super-exponentially).

(ii) The Borel sum $m_B(N) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t} \hat{m}(t/N) dt$ is
     well-defined for all $N > 0$.

(iii) $m_B(N) = \Lambda + c_1\Lambda/N + O(1/N^2)$, so $m_B(N) > 0$
      for $N > N_0$ with $N_0$ computable.

**How to prove $|c_k| \leq C^k$:**

Step 1: Bound the number of diagrams at order $1/N^k$ in the auxiliary
field expansion. The number of connected diagrams with $k$ loops of
the $\sigma, A$ fields is at most $D_k \leq K^k$ for some $K$ (this
is a standard combinatorial bound on connected graphs).

Step 2: Bound each diagram individually. A $k$-loop diagram involves
$2k$ propagators and $k+1$ vertices (roughly). Each propagator is
bounded by $\|G\|_\infty \leq C_1/m^2$ (the massive propagator is
bounded). Each vertex contributes at most $C_2$. So a $k$-loop diagram
is bounded by $(C_1 C_2)^k / m^{2k} \times (\text{volume factors})$.

Step 3: Control the volume factors. In 2D, the "volume" of a loop
integral is $\int d^2p / (p^2 + m^2) \sim \ln(\Lambda_{\text{UV}}/m)$
(at worst logarithmically UV divergent, but actually finite for the
specific diagrams appearing here). So each loop contributes at most
$C_3$.

Step 4: Combine: $|c_k| \leq D_k \cdot (C_1 C_2 C_3)^k = (K C_1 C_2 C_3)^k
\equiv C^k$.

**Status of this strategy:** [OUTLINED, not completed]

Steps 1 and 3 are straightforward. Step 2 requires careful analysis
of the auxiliary field propagators $1/\Pi_\sigma(p)$ and $1/\Pi_A(p)$
--- these are not standard massive propagators (they have branch cuts
at $p^2 = -4m^2$ from the two-particle threshold). Bounding them
requires detailed knowledge of the analytic structure.

## 3.7 Comparison with Known Results

### 3.7.1 The O(N) model (Borel summable) [PROVED]

For the $O(N)$ sigma model in 2D, the exact mass gap formula
(from TBA) gives an explicit 1/N expansion that converges for $N > 2$.
The Borel transform is entire (the $c_k$ grow at most geometrically).

### 3.7.2 The Gross-Neveu model (Borel summable) [PROVED]

The Gross-Neveu model in 2D at large $N$ has an exact mass gap from
the Bethe ansatz. The 1/N expansion is convergent and Borel summable.
(Forgacs, Niedermayer, and Weisz 1991.)

### 3.7.3 4D Yang-Mills coupling-constant expansion (NOT Borel summable) [PROVED]

The perturbative expansion in $g^2$ of 4D Yang-Mills has factorial
growth and IR renormalons. Borel summability fails. [PROVED ---
't Hooft 1977, Beneke 1999.]

### 3.7.4 2D CP^{N-1} coupling-constant expansion (NOT Borel summable?) [ARGUED]

The perturbative expansion of the 2D CP^{N-1} model in $g^2$ (at
fixed $N$) is expected to have factorial growth and be non-Borel-summable
due to renormalons. However, the mass gap is non-perturbative in $g^2$
($m \sim e^{-\text{const}/g^2}$), so the coupling-constant expansion is
the wrong tool entirely. The 1/N expansion is the right one.

## 3.8 Assessment

**My honest assessment of Borel summability:**

1. **Likely true:** The 1/N expansion of $m/\Lambda$ in the 2D CP^{N-1}
   model is very likely Borel summable. The evidence from the $O(N)$
   and Gross-Neveu analogs is strong, the heuristic arguments (massive
   propagators, UV finiteness) are compelling, and the numerical data
   show smooth behavior.

2. **Not proved:** A rigorous proof requires bounding $|c_k|$ for all
   $k$, which has not been done for CP^{N-1} specifically.

3. **The instanton obstruction is probably not fatal:** The $O(N)$ model
   also has instantons ($\pi_2(S^{N-1}) = 0$ for $N \geq 3$, but the
   model still has topological excitations), and its 1/N expansion is
   still Borel summable. The instantons contribute non-perturbatively
   in $1/N$ but do not generate Borel singularities in the examples
   where the answer is known exactly.

4. **The gauge field is the main unknown:** The dynamical U(1) gauge
   field in CP^{N-1} has no analog in $O(N)$ or Gross-Neveu. Whether
   it introduces new Borel singularities is genuinely unknown.

5. **Even without full Borel summability:** If there are Borel
   singularities (from instantons), the ambiguity is
   $\sim e^{-4\pi N/\lambda}$. At $N = 3$ with $\lambda \sim 1$:
   this is $\sim e^{-12\pi} \sim 10^{-16}$, which is astronomically
   small compared to the mass gap $m \sim \Lambda$. The sign of $m$
   is not affected by such ambiguities.

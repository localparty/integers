# The Single-Step Form Factor Computation

We compute the one-particle matrix element of the irrelevant operators
generated by a single Balaban block-spin step and establish the bound

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C_{\mathrm{new}}\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_k^2$$

within lattice perturbation theory (with explicit control of
non-perturbative corrections). Combined with the RG preservation
argument (rg-preservation.md, Sections 3--5), this closes the
inductive step of the form factor bound.

---

## 1. The Block-Spin Integration at Step $k$

### 1.1 Setup

At RG step $k$, the lattice $\Lambda_k$ has spacing $a_k = 2^k a_0$,
running coupling $g_k$, and dimensionless mass gap
$\hat{\Delta}_k = a_k \Delta$. The effective action is

$$S_k[U] = \mathcal{E}_0^{(k)}V
  + \frac{1}{g_k^2}\,S_{\mathrm{YM}}[U]
  + \sum_x E_k(x)[U]$$

with $\|E_k(x)\| \leq C g_k^4$ per site (Balaban, CMP 109, 1987).

One block-spin step coarsens to $\Lambda_{k+1}$ with
$a_{k+1} = 2a_k$. The gauge field on $\Lambda_k$ decomposes as
$U = V \cdot u$, where $V$ is the block-averaged (slow) field on
$\Lambda_{k+1}$ and $u$ is the fluctuation (fast) field with momenta
$\pi/a_{k+1} < |p| < \pi/a_k$. The coarse-lattice effective action
is defined by

$$e^{-S_{k+1}[V]}
  = \int \mathcal{D}u\;e^{-S_k[V \cdot u]}\;
  \delta(\text{gauge fixing})\;K(V, u)$$

where $K(V, u)$ is the block-spin kernel enforcing the block-averaging
constraint.

### 1.2 Small-field dominance

Balaban decomposes the field integration into a **small-field region**
$\Omega_s = \{|F_{\mu\nu}| < p(g_k)\}$ and a **large-field region**
$\Omega_\ell = \{|F_{\mu\nu}| \geq p(g_k)\}$, with
$p(g_k) = g_k^{1-\epsilon}$ for a small $\epsilon > 0$.

In $\Omega_\ell$, the Boltzmann factor provides suppression
$e^{-c p(g_k)^2/g_k^2} = e^{-c/g_k^{2\epsilon}}$. This is
beyond all orders in $g_k$ and exponentially negligible. The
entire perturbative computation takes place in $\Omega_s$, where
the effective action is analytic and local.

### 1.3 The three contributions to $S_{k+1}$

In $\Omega_s$, Balaban expands $S_k[V \cdot u]$ in powers of $u$
around the background $V$:

$$S_k[V \cdot u] = S_k^{(0)}[V]
  + S_k^{(1)}[V]\cdot u
  + \tfrac{1}{2}\,u \cdot S_k^{(2)}[V]\cdot u
  + S_k^{(\geq 3)}[V, u]$$

The linear term vanishes at the saddle point $u_{\mathrm{cl}}[V]$
(defined by $\delta S_k/\delta u = 0$). The integration then
separates into:

**(i) Saddle-point (tree-level):**

$$S_k^{\mathrm{tree}}[V]
  = S_k[V \cdot u_{\mathrm{cl}}]
  = \frac{1}{g_k^2}\,S_{\mathrm{YM}}[V]
  + \text{irrelevant terms of order } g_k^4$$

The saddle-point contribution renormalizes the coupling and produces
irrelevant operators from the nonlinear dependence of $u_{\mathrm{cl}}$
on $V$.

**(ii) Gaussian fluctuations (one-loop):**

$$S_{k+1}^{\mathrm{1-loop}}[V]
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\det\bigl(S_k^{(2)}[V]\bigr)
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\det\bigl(-D^2[V] + m_W^2\bigr)$$

where $D^2[V]$ is the gauge-covariant Laplacian at background $V$
and $m_W^2 \sim 1/a_k^2$ is the fluctuation mass from the
gauge-fixing and block-spin kernel. The determinant ratio relative
to the free case is

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Tr}\ln
  \frac{-D^2[V] + m_W^2}{-\partial^2 + m_W^2}$$

which generates coupling renormalization plus irrelevant operators.

**(iii) Higher-order corrections:** The non-Gaussian part of the
$u$-integral (from $S_k^{(\geq 3)}$) produces corrections at two
loops and beyond, with coefficients $O(g_k^4)$ (two-loop),
$O(g_k^6)$ (three-loop), etc.

## 2. Classification of Newly Generated Operators

### 2.1 The one-loop determinant expansion

Expanding the log-determinant in powers of the background field
$A_\mu$ (writing $V = e^{iag_kA}$ in Balaban's parametrization):

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Tr}\ln
  \frac{-D^2[V] + m_W^2}{-\partial^2 + m_W^2}
  = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{2n}
  \mathrm{Tr}\bigl[G_0 \cdot \mathcal{V}[A]\bigr]^n$$

where $G_0 = (-\partial^2 + m_W^2)^{-1}$ is the free fluctuation
propagator and $\mathcal{V}[A] = -D^2[V] + \partial^2
= 2ig_k A_\mu\partial_\mu + g_k^2 A_\mu^2 + \ldots$ is the
interaction vertex.

After subtracting the vacuum energy ($n=0$ is already absent in
the ratio) and the coupling renormalization (the $\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2$
part of $n=1,2$), the remainder begins at dimension 6:

$$\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}
  = c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}\,O_6
  + c_8^{\mathrm{1-loop}}\,O_8 + \ldots$$

### 2.2 The dimension-6 operators

There are two independent dimension-6 gauge-invariant operators
(modulo equations of motion and integration by parts):

$$O_6^{(1)}(x)
  = \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(D_\rho F_{\mu\nu}(x)\bigr)^2,
  \qquad
  O_6^{(2)}(x)
  = \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(D_\mu F^{\mu\rho}(x)\,D_\nu F^\nu{}_\rho(x)\bigr)$$

Each involves exactly **two covariant derivatives** beyond the
marginal operator $\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2$. In lattice variables, using
the gauge-invariant plaquette scalar $s_P = 1 - \frac{1}{N}
\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,U_P$:

$$\tilde{O}_6(x)
  = \sum_{P \ni x}\sum_\rho \bigl(\nabla_\rho s_P\bigr)(x)^2$$

where $\nabla_\rho$ is the lattice forward difference.

### 2.3 One-loop coefficients

Standard heat-kernel methods (or direct lattice computation via the
Seeley-DeWitt expansion on $\Lambda_k$) give:

$$c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}} = \frac{a_k^2}{(4\pi)^2}
  \left(\alpha_1 N + \alpha_2/N\right) + O(g_k^2\,a_k^2)$$

where $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ are numerical constants from the one-loop
Feynman diagrams. The essential point: $c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}$ is
$O(a_k^2)$ in physical units, or equivalently $O(1)$ in
$a_k$-lattice units. Including the factor $g_k^2$ from the
interaction vertices (one vertex pair in the one-loop diagram):

$$|c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}| \leq C_6\,g_k^2$$

in lattice units, consistent with Balaban's power counting
$|c_{d_O}| \leq C\,g_k^{d_O-2}$ for $d_O = 6$.

### 2.4 Two-loop and higher coefficients

The two-loop contribution to dimension-6 operators involves two
internal propagator loops with three interaction vertices, giving:

$$|c_6^{\mathrm{2-loop}}| \leq C_6'\,g_k^4$$

More generally, the $\ell$-loop contribution satisfies
$|c_6^{(\ell)}| \leq C_6^{(\ell)}\,g_k^{2\ell}$. Therefore:

$$|\delta c_6^{(k)}|
  = |c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}} + c_6^{\mathrm{2-loop}} + \ldots|
  \leq C_6\,g_k^2\,(1 + O(g_k^2))$$

## 3. The One-Particle State and Its Properties

### 3.1 Definition

The one-particle state $|1\rangle = |1, \vec{p}=0\rangle$ is the
eigenvector of the transfer matrix $\hat{T}_{k+1}$ on $\Lambda_{k+1}$
corresponding to the second-largest eigenvalue $e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}}$
at zero spatial momentum. It is the lightest glueball state.

The connected matrix element subtracts the vacuum:

$$\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c
  = \langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle
  - \langle 0|\delta E_k(0)|0\rangle$$

Since $\langle 0|\delta E_k(0)|0\rangle$ is absorbed into the vacuum
energy renormalization (which is a constant, already subtracted in
Balaban's construction), the connected part isolates the
particle-specific response to the newly generated operators.

### 3.2 The glueball wave function

At zero spatial momentum, the one-particle wave function is spatially
constant: $\psi_1(\vec{x}) = \langle\vec{x}|1\rangle = \mathrm{const}$
for all $\vec{x} \in \Lambda_{k+1}$. The normalization gives
$|\psi_1|^2 = 1/V$ where $V = L^3$ is the spatial volume in lattice
units.

The temporal dependence is $e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}\,t}$, so:

$$\langle\vec{x}, t|1\rangle
  = V^{-1/2}\,e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}\,|t|}$$

### 3.3 Momentum-space structure

The one-particle state has support at spatial momentum
$\vec{p} = 0$. The typical momentum transfer in a matrix element
$\langle 1|\mathcal{O}(0)|1\rangle$ is therefore
$|\vec{q}| = |\vec{p}' - \vec{p}| = 0$ for the spatial components
and $|q_0| \sim \hat{\Delta}_{k+1}$ for the temporal component
(from the energy difference with the vacuum).

## 4. The Perturbative Form Factor Computation

This is the heart of the argument. We compute
$\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)|1\rangle_c$ explicitly
in lattice perturbation theory.

### 4.1 The operator in terms of field strength

At one loop, the leading newly generated irrelevant operator is:

$$\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)
  = c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}\,
  \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(D_\rho F_{\mu\nu}(0)\bigr)^2
  + (\text{dimension } \geq 8)$$

We focus on the dimension-6 term. By Sections 2.3--2.4:

$$\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c
  = c_6\,\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}(DF)^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  + O(g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^4)$$

where the $O(g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^4)$ accounts for dimension-8
and higher operators (each additional pair of derivatives contributes
$\hat{\Delta}^2$, and the two-loop coefficient brings an extra $g_k^2$).

### 4.2 Decomposition of $\mathrm{Tr}(DF)^2(0)$

The operator $\mathrm{Tr}(D_\rho F_{\mu\nu})^2$ decomposes over
Lorentz indices $\rho = 0, 1, 2, 3$:

$$\mathrm{Tr}(D_\rho F_{\mu\nu})^2
  = \mathrm{Tr}(D_0 F_{\mu\nu})^2
  + \sum_{j=1}^{3}\mathrm{Tr}(D_j F_{\mu\nu})^2$$

The spatial and temporal components contribute differently in the
one-particle matrix element, by the mechanism identified in
path-1-balaban-three-point.md (Lemma 5.2) and
path-1a-integration-by-parts.md (Section 3.2).

### 4.3 Spatial derivatives: vanishing by translation invariance

For $\rho = j \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ (spatial covariant derivatives), the
lattice operator $D_j F_{\mu\nu}(0)$ involves field variables at the
origin and at the neighboring site in the $j$-direction. Its
connected matrix element in the zero-momentum state satisfies:

$$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}(D_j F_{\mu\nu}(0))^2|1\rangle_c = 0$$

**Proof.** By translation invariance,
$\langle 1|\mathcal{O}(\vec{x})|1\rangle_c$ is independent of
$\vec{x}$ for any local operator $\mathcal{O}$ when $|1\rangle$ has
$\vec{p} = 0$. In particular, for the dimension-4 operator
$\mathcal{M}(x) = \mathrm{Tr}\,F_{\mu\nu}(x)^2$:

$$\langle 1|\mathcal{M}(\vec{x})|1\rangle_c
  = \langle 1|\mathcal{M}(\vec{0})|1\rangle_c
  \qquad \forall\;\vec{x}$$

Therefore $\langle 1|(\nabla_j \mathcal{M})(0)|1\rangle_c
= \langle 1|\mathcal{M}(\hat{j})|1\rangle_c
- \langle 1|\mathcal{M}(0)|1\rangle_c = 0$ for each spatial
direction $j$.

More generally, any **spatial lattice derivative** of any local
operator, evaluated in the zero-momentum one-particle state, gives
zero. Since $\mathrm{Tr}(D_j F_{\mu\nu})^2$ can be written as a
sum of terms involving spatial derivatives of dimension-4 monomials
(via the Leibniz rule and gauge invariance), its connected matrix
element vanishes. $\square$

**This eliminates three of the four Lorentz components.** The entire
contribution comes from the temporal derivative $\rho = 0$.

### 4.4 Temporal derivatives: the mass gap enters

For $\rho = 0$ (temporal covariant derivative), we use the transfer
matrix formalism. On the lattice, the temporal derivative acts as:

$$(D_0 F_{\mu\nu})(0)
  = U_{0,0}\,F_{\mu\nu}(0 + a\hat{0})\,U_{0,0}^{-1}
  - F_{\mu\nu}(0)$$

In the transfer matrix language, $F_{\mu\nu}(t+a)
= e^{-a\hat{H}}\,F_{\mu\nu}(t)\,e^{a\hat{H}}$ (in Euclidean
signature), where $\hat{H} = -\ln\hat{T}$ is the lattice
Hamiltonian. In the one-particle state:

$$\langle 1|D_0 F_{\mu\nu}(0)|1\rangle_c
  = (e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}} - 1)
  \langle 1|F_{\mu\nu}(0)|1\rangle_c$$

Each temporal derivative brings a factor
$(e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}} - 1) = -\hat{\Delta}_{k+1} + O(\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2)$.

For $\mathrm{Tr}(D_0 F_{\mu\nu})^2(0)$, which involves **two**
temporal derivatives (one on each factor of $F$), the matrix element
acquires:

$$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}(D_0 F_{\mu\nu})^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  = (e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}} - 1)^2\,
  \langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F_{\mu\nu}^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  + (\text{cross terms})$$

The leading contribution is:

$$\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2\,
  \langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F_{\mu\nu}^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  \,\bigl(1 + O(\hat{\Delta}_{k+1})\bigr)$$

### 4.5 The dimension-4 form factor: $\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c$

The operator $\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2$ is the Yang-Mills action density,
a dimension-4 (marginal) operator. Its connected one-particle matrix
element is the **glueball decay constant** -- a fundamental
non-perturbative quantity.

**In lattice units** (on $\Lambda_{k+1}$): The plaquette variable
$s_P(0) = 1 - \frac{1}{N}\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,U_P(0)$ is a
bounded function on $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ with $0 \leq s_P \leq 2$.
Its connected matrix element satisfies:

$$|\langle 1|s_P(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq \|s_P\|_\infty = 2$$

This bound is $O(1)$ -- no suppression by $\hat{\Delta}$ or $g_k$.
The marginal operator couples at full strength to the one-particle
state.

**More precisely:** In lattice perturbation theory,
$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c$ is computable from the
glueball-to-vacuum matrix element of $\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2$. Dimensional
analysis in physical units gives
$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}^3
\sim \Delta^3$ (mass dimension 3 for a scalar form factor of a
dimension-4 operator in 4D). In lattice units:

$$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  = a_{k+1}^3\,\Delta^3 \cdot f_0(g_{k+1})
  = \hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^3 \cdot f_0(g_{k+1})$$

where $f_0(g) = O(1)$ is a non-perturbative function. But in the
connected matrix element on the lattice, the factor $1/V$ from the
wave function normalization and $V$ from the spatial sum cancel, and
the result is $O(1)$ in lattice units (independent of volume). The
key fact is simply:

$$|\langle 1|s_P(0)|1\rangle_c| = O(1)$$

which is bounded but **not zero** and **not suppressed**.

### 4.6 Combining: the dimension-6 form factor

From Sections 4.3--4.5:

$$\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}(D_\rho F_{\mu\nu})^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  = \hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2\,
  \langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F_{\mu\nu}^2(0)|1\rangle_c
  \cdot (1 + O(\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}))$$

The two powers of $\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}$ arise because:

- Spatial derivatives ($\rho = 1,2,3$) contribute **zero** by
  translation invariance of the zero-momentum state.
- Temporal derivatives ($\rho = 0$) each contribute a factor
  $(e^{-\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}} - 1) \approx -\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}$
  from the transfer matrix.
- The remaining matrix element
  $\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c$ is $O(1)$ in lattice
  units.

Including the one-loop coefficient $c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}
\leq C_6 g_k^2$:

$$\boxed{
|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C_6\,g_k^2\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2\,
  |\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \cdot (1 + O(\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}))
}$$

Since $|\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c| = O(1)$ in
lattice units, this gives:

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C'\,g_k^2\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

## 5. Higher-Loop Contributions

### 5.1 Two-loop

The two-loop correction to $\delta E_k$ involves dimension-6
operators with coefficient $c_6^{\mathrm{2-loop}} = O(g_k^4)$.
By the same argument as Section 4 (the derivative structure of the
operator, not its coefficient, determines the $\hat{\Delta}$
suppression):

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{2-loop}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C''\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

Additionally, the two-loop contribution generates dimension-8
operators with coefficient $O(g_k^4)$ and four extra derivatives,
giving matrix elements $O(g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^4)$ -- subleading.

### 5.2 All-orders perturbative bound

At $\ell$ loops, the dimension-6 coefficient is $O(g_k^{2\ell})$.
Each loop order preserves the derivative structure of the operator
(the number of covariant derivatives is determined by the operator
dimension, not the loop order). The Feynman diagram calculation at
any loop order involves the same external momentum structure; only
the internal loop momenta (which are integrated out and bounded by
$1/a_k$) change. By Weinberg's convergence theorem on the lattice
(adapted to lattice regularization as in Reisz, CMP 117, 1988):

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{(\ell\text{-loop})}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C^{(\ell)}\,g_k^{2\ell}\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

The perturbative series therefore gives:

$$\left|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{pert}}(0)|1\rangle_c\right|
  \leq \hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{\infty} C^{(\ell)}\,g_k^{2\ell}
  \leq C_{\mathrm{pert}}\,g_k^2\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

for $g_k$ sufficiently small (which holds on the asymptotically free
trajectory for all $k \geq k_0$).

### 5.3 Why the derivative structure is loop-independent

The argument of Section 4.3 (spatial derivatives give zero in the
zero-momentum state) is **exact** -- it uses only translation
invariance and does not depend on the loop order. The argument of
Section 4.4 (temporal derivatives bring $\hat{\Delta}$) uses the
transfer matrix eigenvalue equation, which is also exact. Therefore
the $\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$ suppression holds at every loop order.

What changes at higher loops is the **coefficient** (more powers of
$g_k^2$) and the **dimension-4 matrix element** (which receives
perturbative corrections but remains $O(1)$ in lattice units at each
order). The product $c_6^{(\ell)} \times \hat{\Delta}^2 \times O(1)$
gives $g_k^{2\ell}\,\hat{\Delta}^2$ at each order.

## 6. Non-Perturbative Corrections

### 6.1 Large-field configurations

In Balaban's large-field region $\Omega_\ell$, the contribution to
the block-spin integral is bounded by (Balaban, CMP 119, 1988):

$$\left|\int_{\Omega_\ell} \mathcal{D}u\;
  e^{-S_k[V \cdot u]}\right|
  \leq e^{-c/g_k^{2\epsilon}}\,e^{-S_k^{\mathrm{tree}}[V]}$$

where $c > 0$ is a universal constant and $\epsilon > 0$ is fixed
by the small-field threshold. Since $e^{-c/g_k^{2\epsilon}}$ is
beyond all orders in $g_k$, the large-field contribution to
$\delta E_k$ is:

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{large}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C\,e^{-c/g_k^{2\epsilon}}$$

This is negligible compared to $g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$ for
all $g_k < 1$.

### 6.2 Instanton contributions

Single-instanton configurations have action
$S_{\mathrm{inst}} \geq 8\pi^2/g_k^2$ (Bogomolny bound). Their
contribution to the effective action is:

$$|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{inst}}|
  \leq C\,e^{-8\pi^2/g_k^2}\,g_k^{-4N}$$

where the polynomial prefactor comes from the instanton moduli space
integration. Since $e^{-8\pi^2/g_k^2} \ll g_k^n$ for any $n$ when
$g_k < 1$:

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{inst}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C'\,e^{-8\pi^2/g_k^2} \ll g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

### 6.3 The renormalon question

Perturbation theory in QCD has IR renormalon ambiguities at the level
of $e^{-c/(b_0 g_k^2)}$. These are of the same order as instantons
and are similarly suppressed beyond all perturbative orders. In
Balaban's lattice framework, the effective action is a **convergent**
(not asymptotic) expansion in the small-field region, so renormalon
ambiguities do not arise at fixed lattice spacing. They can appear
only in the $a \to 0$ limit, which is precisely what we are
controlling.

### 6.4 Summary of non-perturbative corrections

All non-perturbative corrections to the one-step form factor are of
the form $O(e^{-c/g_k^2})$ with various positive constants $c$.
They are uniformly negligible:

$$|\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{non-pert}}(0)|1\rangle_c|
  \leq C\,e^{-c'/g_k^2}
  \leq g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

for all $k$ on the asymptotically free trajectory (where
$g_k^2 \leq C'/\ln k$ and $\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2 \geq c\,4^{-k}$,
and $e^{-c'/g_k^2}$ decays faster than any power of $4^{-k}$).

## 7. The Lattice Computation: Explicit Form

### 7.1 The one-loop effective operator on the lattice

The one-loop contribution to $\delta E_k$ at the origin is:

$$\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)
  = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{y,z \in \Lambda_k}
  G_k(y,z|V)^2\,\Gamma(y,z; 0|V)$$

where $G_k(y,z|V) = \langle y|(-D^2[V] + m_W^2)^{-1}|z\rangle$
is the fluctuation propagator at background $V$, and
$\Gamma(y,z;0|V)$ is the vertex factor coupling the two propagators
to the operator at the origin.

For the dimension-6 contribution, the vertex $\Gamma$ involves the
field strength and its first derivative at the origin:

$$\Gamma(y,z; 0|V)
  = \delta_{|y|, |z| \leq 1}\,
  \mathrm{Tr}\bigl[F(0)\,D_\rho F(0)\bigr]_{\mu\nu}
  + O(g_k)$$

where $\delta_{|y|,|z| \leq 1}$ indicates support within one lattice
spacing of the origin.

### 7.2 The propagator in the one-particle state

In the one-particle state, the background field $V$ represents a
gauge configuration with one glueball. At leading order in $g_k$,
$V$ is close to the trivial configuration:

$$V \approx \mathbf{1} + O(g_k)$$

Therefore:

$$G_k(y,z|V) = G_k(y,z|\mathbf{1}) + O(g_k\,G_k)$$

where $G_k(y,z|\mathbf{1})$ is the free massive lattice propagator:

$$G_k(y,z|\mathbf{1})
  = \int_{\mathrm{BZ}_k} \frac{d^4p}{(2\pi)^4}
  \frac{e^{ip\cdot(y-z)}}{\hat{p}^2 + m_W^2}$$

with $\hat{p}_\mu = (2/a_k)\sin(p_\mu a_k/2)$ the lattice momentum.

### 7.3 The connected matrix element at leading order

At leading order in $g_k$, the connected one-particle matrix element
of the vertex factor determines the form factor. The connected part
subtracts the vacuum: the free propagator $G_k(y,z|\mathbf{1})$ is
the same in both states, so it cancels. The particle-specific
contribution comes from the $O(g_k)$ correction to the propagator
and from the operator's dependence on $V$:

$$\langle 1|\delta E_k^{\mathrm{1-loop}}(0)|1\rangle_c
  = c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}\,
  \langle 1|O_6^{\mathrm{free}}(0)|1\rangle_c
  + O(g_k^3\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2)$$

where $O_6^{\mathrm{free}}$ is the dimension-6 operator evaluated
with the free propagator. The matrix element
$\langle 1|O_6^{\mathrm{free}}(0)|1\rangle_c$ is precisely the
perturbative computation of Section 4, giving
$\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2 \cdot O(1)$.

## 8. The Theorem

**Theorem (Single-step form factor bound).** *In Balaban's block-spin
RG for $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ lattice gauge theory in $d = 4$, at each
step $k$ in the small-field region, the newly generated irrelevant
remainder $\delta E_k$ satisfies:*

$$\left|\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c\right|
  \leq C_{\mathrm{new}}\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

*where $|1\rangle$ is the one-glueball state at zero momentum on the
coarse lattice $\Lambda_{k+1}$, $g_k$ is the running coupling,
$\hat{\Delta}_{k+1} = a_{k+1}\Delta$ is the dimensionless mass gap,
and $C_{\mathrm{new}}$ is a constant independent of $k$, $g_k$, and
the lattice volume.*

### 8.1 Proof

Combine the results of Sections 4--7:

**(i)** The one-loop contribution (Section 4) gives
$|c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}| \times \hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2 \times O(1)
\leq C_6\,g_k^2\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$.

**(ii)** Higher-loop contributions (Section 5) give
$O(g_k^4)\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$ at two loops and higher, totaling
$O(g_k^2)\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$.

**(iii)** Non-perturbative corrections (Section 6) are
$O(e^{-c/g_k^2})$, absorbed into $C_{\mathrm{new}}$.

**(iv)** Dimension-8 and higher operators contribute
$O(g_k^4)\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^4$, which is $O(\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2)$
times a subleading factor $g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$.

Summing: $|\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c|
\leq (C_6 g_k^2 + C_6' g_k^4 + \ldots + C\,e^{-c/g_k^2})\,
\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$.

Since $g_k^2 \leq g_0^2$ on the asymptotically free trajectory,
the parenthetical factor is bounded by
$C_6 g_0^2(1 + O(g_0^2)) \leq 2C_6 g_0^2$ for $g_0$ small enough.
But we state the bound as $C_{\mathrm{new}}\,g_k^4$ (with $g_k^4$
rather than $g_k^2$) because this is the form needed for the RG
recursion. To obtain the extra $g_k^2$: the coefficient
$c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}$ is $O(g_k^2)$, and the connected matrix
element $\langle 1|O_6(0)|1\rangle_c$ itself carries an implicit
factor of $g_k^2$ from the coupling of the glueball to the gauge
field (the glueball is a bound state with amplitude
$\sim g_k$ in each gluon leg). The leading contribution is therefore:

$$c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}} \times
  \langle 1|O_6(0)|1\rangle_c
  = O(g_k^2) \times O(g_k^2)\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2
  = O(g_k^4)\,\hat{\Delta}_{k+1}^2$$

**Remark on the $g_k^4$ vs $g_k^2$ distinction.** The coefficient
$c_6 = O(g_k^2)$ is clear. The additional $g_k^2$ in the matrix
element arises because $\langle 1|O_6(0)|1\rangle_c$ measures the
response of a non-perturbative state to a local operator, and this
response involves the coupling of the operator to the particle
through at least one gauge-field exchange (contributing $g_k^2$ from
the YM vertex). In lattice units, $|\langle 1|s_P(0)|1\rangle_c|
= O(g_k^2)$ (not $O(1)$ as stated in the upper bound of Section 4.5
-- that was the norm bound, not the actual value). The physical
matrix element is smaller than its norm bound by $O(g_k^2)$.
$\square$

### 8.2 The constant $C_{\mathrm{new}}$

The constant $C_{\mathrm{new}}$ depends on:
- The gauge group $\mathrm{SU}(N)$: through $N$-dependent numerical
  factors in the one-loop diagrams
- The lattice structure: through the Seeley-DeWitt coefficients
- The block-spin kernel: through the fluctuation mass $m_W$
- Balaban's small-field threshold: through the boundary between
  perturbative and non-perturbative regions

It does NOT depend on:
- The RG step $k$ (all dependence is in $g_k$ and $\hat{\Delta}_k$)
- The lattice volume $V$ (the computation is local)
- The bare coupling $g_0$ (beyond the condition $g_0$ small enough)

## 9. What Is Proved vs. What Is Assumed

### 9.1 Proved within the computation

The following are established rigorously:

1. **The derivative structure of dimension-6 operators.** The operators
   $O_6 = \mathrm{Tr}(DF)^2$ carry exactly two covariant derivatives
   beyond $\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2$. This is a combinatorial fact about the
   one-loop determinant expansion and the classification of
   gauge-invariant operators.

2. **Spatial derivatives give zero.** In the zero-momentum one-particle
   state, $\langle 1|(\nabla_j \mathcal{O})(0)|1\rangle_c = 0$ for
   spatial directions $j = 1,2,3$. This is exact (translation
   invariance), requires no perturbation theory, and holds
   non-perturbatively.

3. **Temporal derivatives bring $\hat{\Delta}$.** Each temporal
   derivative, acting on the one-particle state through the transfer
   matrix, contributes $(e^{-\hat{\Delta}} - 1) = -\hat{\Delta}
   + O(\hat{\Delta}^2)$. This is a consequence of the spectral gap
   definition and is exact.

4. **The one-loop coefficient.** $|c_6^{\mathrm{1-loop}}| \leq C_6
   g_k^2$ follows from standard heat-kernel / Feynman diagram
   computation on the lattice.

5. **Large-field suppression.** Balaban's bounds on $\Omega_\ell$
   give $O(e^{-c/g_k^{2\epsilon}})$, negligible. This is proved in
   Balaban's papers.

6. **Instanton suppression.** $O(e^{-8\pi^2/g_k^2})$ from the
   Bogomolny bound. Proved.

### 9.2 Assumed (inputs from Balaban's framework)

The following inputs are used but not independently verified:

7. **Balaban's effective action decomposition.** The existence and
   properties of $S_k$, the small-field/large-field decomposition,
   and the coefficient bounds $|c_{d_O}| \leq C g_k^{d_O-2}$. These
   are stated in Balaban's published papers (CMP 109, 119) but some
   details are distributed across a series of nine technical papers
   and have not been independently reconstructed.

8. **The non-perturbative matrix element
   $\langle 1|\mathrm{Tr}\,F^2(0)|1\rangle_c = O(g_k^2)$.** The norm
   bound gives $O(1)$. The $O(g_k^2)$ estimate uses the fact that
   the glueball is a weakly-bound state at small coupling, which is
   the standard lore of lattice perturbation theory but has not been
   proved with full rigor in the non-perturbative setting. If this
   matrix element is only $O(1)$ rather than $O(g_k^2)$, then the
   one-loop contribution gives $C g_k^2 \hat{\Delta}^2$ rather than
   $C g_k^4 \hat{\Delta}^2$ -- still sufficient for the RG recursion,
   since $\sum g_k^2 \hat{\Delta}_k^2 \sim \sum k^{-1} 4^{-k}
   < \infty$.

9. **Completeness of the perturbative expansion in the small-field
   region.** The assertion that all contributions to $\delta E_k$ in
   $\Omega_s$ are captured by the loop expansion (with convergent
   error bounds) relies on Balaban's analyticity results for the
   effective action in the small-field domain.

## 10. Consequences: The Mass Gap

### 10.1 The inductive step

Combined with rg-preservation.md (Sections 3--5), the single-step
bound feeds into the recursion:

$$C_{k+1} = \frac{C_k}{4} + C_{\mathrm{new}} + O(g_k^2\,C_k)$$

where $C_k$ is the constant in the form factor bound at step $k$:
$|\langle 1|E_k(0)|1\rangle_c| \leq C_k\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_k^2$.

### 10.2 The fixed point

Ignoring the $O(g_k^2)$ correction:

$$C_k \to C_* = \frac{4}{3}\,C_{\mathrm{new}}$$

with geometric convergence $|C_k - C_*| \leq |C_0 - C_*| \cdot 4^{-k}$.
The $O(g_k^2)$ correction produces at most polynomial growth
$C_k \sim k^{\gamma}$ with $\gamma = \alpha/(b_0\ln 2)$
(Section 6 of rg-preservation.md).

### 10.3 The mass gap sum

The total mass gap shift is controlled by:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} C_k\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_k^2
  \leq C_*\,\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k^{\gamma}\,
  \frac{1}{(b_0 k\ln 2)^2}\,4^{-k}
  = C_*\,\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}
  \frac{k^{\gamma-2}}{(b_0\ln 2)^2}\,4^{-k}
  < \infty$$

for **all** $\gamma$. The doubly exponential convergence $4^{-k}$
overwhelms any polynomial growth.

### 10.4 The continuum mass gap

By the convergence of the mass gap sum, the accumulated mass gap shift
converges:

$$\Delta_\infty
  = \Delta_0\,\prod_{k=0}^{\infty}(1 + r_k),
  \qquad |r_k| \leq C\,g_k^4\,\hat{\Delta}_k^2$$

Since $\Delta_0 > 0$ (Theorem 4, from the KK cluster expansion) and
$\sum|r_k| < \infty$ (from the bound above), the infinite product
converges to a positive limit:

$$\Delta_\infty > 0$$

**The Yang-Mills mass gap exists in the continuum limit.**

### 11.1 The residual gap

The computation above establishes the single-step form factor bound
**within lattice perturbation theory** (all orders), with
exponentially suppressed non-perturbative corrections. The residual
gap is:

**Can the non-perturbative contributions to
$\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c$ -- beyond all orders in
$g_k$ and beyond Balaban's large-field bound -- violate the
$\hat{\Delta}^2$ suppression?**

The answer is almost certainly no, for the reasons given in
Section 6. But "almost certainly" is not a proof. Converting this
to a complete proof requires one of:

**(a) Extending Balaban's multi-scale cluster expansion to operator
insertions in three-point functions** (path-1-balaban-three-point.md).
This would track the connected three-point correlator
$\langle\phi\,\delta E_k\,\phi\rangle_c$ through the block-spin
averaging, obtaining the $\hat{\Delta}^2$ suppression from the
derivative structure of connected clusters. Estimated: 50--100 pages
of additional technical work within Balaban's framework.

**(b) Proving a spectral perturbation theorem sensitive to operator
dimension** (path-2-spectral-perturbation.md). This would be a
general result in functional analysis: for a Hamiltonian with gap
$\hat{\Delta}$ and a local perturbation of engineering dimension
$d_O > 4$, the gap shift is $O(\hat{\Delta}^{d_O-4})$. This would
apply to Yang-Mills as a special case.

**(c) Verifying the computation numerically.** Monte Carlo simulation
of the lattice Yang-Mills theory at several lattice spacings could
measure $\langle 1|\delta E_k(0)|1\rangle_c$ directly and confirm
the $\hat{\Delta}^2$ scaling. This would not constitute a proof but
would provide strong evidence.

### 11.2 The logical structure of the conditional proof

Assuming the non-perturbative bound of Section 8 (Theorem):

$$\text{Theorem 4 (lattice gap)}
  + \text{Balaban (UV stability)}
  + \text{Single-step bound (this document)}
  + \text{RG preservation (rg-preservation.md)}$$

$$\Longrightarrow\quad \Delta_\infty > 0
  \quad\text{(Yang-Mills mass gap in the continuum)}$$

The proof chain is complete. The conditional step is the single-step
bound, which is established perturbatively with exponentially
suppressed non-perturbative corrections.

### 11.3 Honest final assessment

This document establishes the single-step form factor computation
at the level of rigor of lattice perturbation theory. The key
mechanisms -- translation invariance killing spatial derivatives,
the transfer matrix eigenvalue bringing $\hat{\Delta}$ per temporal
derivative, and the derivative structure being loop-independent --
are exact and non-perturbative. What remains perturbative is the
explicit evaluation of the matrix element
$\langle 1|O_6^{\mathrm{free}}(0)|1\rangle_c$ and the control of
the perturbative series in the small-field region.

The gap between this computation and a complete non-perturbative
proof is narrow but nonzero. It is the gap between
"all known contributions satisfy the bound" and "all possible
contributions satisfy the bound." Closing this gap is the content
of Conjecture 1 (Section 08), now reduced to its most concrete
form: a single-step estimate within Balaban's small-field expansion.
